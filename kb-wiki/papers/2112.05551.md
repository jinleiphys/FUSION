# Determination of the symmetry energy from the neutron star equation of state

> We analyze the uncertainties introduced in the determination of the neutron star matter proton fraction, in a range of densities close to the saturation density, if the cold $β$-equilibrium neutron star matter equation of state (EoS) is known. In particular, we discuss the effect of neglecting the muon contribution and of considering that the energy density of nuclear matter is well described by taking only terms until second order in the proton-neutron asymmetry. It is shown that two types of uncertainties may be associated with the extraction of the symmetry energy from the $β$-equilibrium equation of state: an overestimation if terms above the parabolic approximation on the asymmetry parameter are neglected, or an underestimation if the muon contribution is not considered. The effect of the uncertainty on the symmetric nuclear matter EoS on the determination of the proton fraction is discussed. It could be shown that the neutron star mass-radius curve is sensitive to the parabolic approximation on the asymmetry parameter.

## Key claim

The paper shows that extracting the symmetry energy from a known cold beta-equilibrium neutron star equation of state involves two opposing systematic uncertainties: an overestimation if terms above the parabolic approximation on the asymmetry parameter are neglected, and an underestimation if the muon contribution is not considered.

## Method

The authors start from the DD2 relativistic mean-field equation of state as a reference and derive a relation (Eq. final1) that connects the beta-equilibrium energy density, the lepton energy densities, the symmetric nuclear matter energy per particle, and the asymmetry parameter delta. They then solve this equation to extract the proton fraction and symmetry energy under different approximations: neglecting or including the fourth-order asymmetry term S4, neglecting or including muons, and using Taylor expansions of the symmetric nuclear matter energy per particle and symmetry energy around saturation density truncated at various orders.

## Key numbers

- At saturation density, neglecting muons in the extraction while the reference model includes them gives a relative error in the proton fraction of approximately 14% (dashed black curve in Fig. 1).
- Including the S4 term calculated with the effective mass reduces the error in the proton fraction at saturation to about 3% (blue dotted curve in Fig. 1).
- Including the S4 term calculated with the vacuum nucleon mass reduces the error at saturation to about 5% (red dotted curve in Fig. 1).
- Neglecting the fourth-order term S4 gives a deviation in the extracted symmetry energy of about +3% from the exact DD2 symmetry energy (top panel of Fig. 5).
- Including S4 calculated with the vacuum mass reduces the symmetry energy deviation to about 1% (bottom panel of Fig. 5).
- Using a second-order Taylor expansion for symmetric nuclear matter gives errors below 3% for the proton fraction in the density range approximately 0.5 rho0 to 1.5 rho0 (top panel of Fig. 2).
- Using a sixth-order Taylor expansion gives errors below 1% for the proton fraction in the range approximately 0.3 rho0 to 2 rho0 (top panel of Fig. 2).
- The DD2 model has saturation density rho0 = 0.149 fm^-3.

## Context

This work extends the methodology of Essick (2021) for extracting the proton fraction from a beta-equilibrium equation of state, and builds on the formalism of Bombaci (1991) and Vidana (2009) for expanding the nuclear matter energy per particle in powers of the asymmetry parameter. The analysis quantifies uncertainties that affect efforts to determine the density dependence of the symmetry energy from neutron star observations, which is essential for predicting neutron star composition including hyperon nucleation as studied by Providencia (2012, 2018) and Fortin (2020), and for understanding direct Urca cooling processes discussed by Horowitz (2002). The results enable more reliable extraction of symmetry energy parameters from future NICER radius measurements and gravitational wave constraints.
