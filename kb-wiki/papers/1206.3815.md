# Systematic analysis of the incoming quark energy loss in cold nuclear matter

> The investigation into the fast parton energy loss in cold nuclear matter is crucial for a good understanding of the parton propagation in hot-dense medium. By means of four typical sets of nuclear parton distributions and three parametrizations of quark energy loss, the parameter values in quark energy loss expressions are determined from a leading order statistical analysis of the existing experimental data on nuclear Drell-Yan differential cross section ratio as a function of the quark momentum fraction. It is found that with independence on the nuclear modification of parton distributions, the available experimental data from lower incident beam energy rule out the incident-parton momentum fraction quark energy loss. Whether the quark energy loss is linear or quadratic with the path length is not discriminated. The global fit of all selected data gives the quark energy loss per unit path length α = 1.21\pm0.09 GeV/fm by using nuclear parton distribution functions determined only by means of the world data on nuclear structure function. Our result does not support the theoretical prediction: the energy loss of an outgoing quark is three times larger than that of an incoming quark approaching the nuclear medium. It is desirable that the present work can provide useful reference for the Fermilab E906/SeaQuest experiment.

## Key claim

The global fit of all selected nuclear Drell-Yan data yields a quark energy loss per unit path length of α = 1.21 ± 0.09 GeV/fm when using HKM nuclear parton distribution functions, but this value is strongly dependent on the choice of nuclear parton distribution set, ranging from 0.23 ± 0.07 GeV/fm (EPS09) to 0.64 ± 0.09 GeV/fm (HKN07). The data rule out the incident-parton momentum fraction parametrization of quark energy loss but cannot discriminate between linear and quadratic dependence on path length.

## Method

The authors performed a leading-order perturbative QCD analysis of nuclear Drell-Yan differential cross section ratios as a function of the quark momentum fractions x₁ and x₂, using 269 data points from NA3 (150 GeV π⁻), NA10 (140 and 286 GeV π⁻), E772 (800 GeV p), and E866 (800 GeV p) experiments. They employed four sets of nuclear parton distribution functions (HKM, HKN07, nDS, EPS09) and three parametrizations of quark energy loss (linear Δx₁ = α⟨L⟩_A/E_h, quadratic Δx₁ = β⟨L⟩²_A/E_h, and incident-parton momentum fraction Δx₁ = κ x₁ A^{1/3}), extracting the parameters via χ² minimization.

## Key numbers

- Global fit with HKM nuclear PDFs gives α = 1.21 ± 0.09 GeV/fm (linear energy loss), with χ²/ndf = 1.07 and relative uncertainty δα/α ≈ 7%
- Global fit with HKM nuclear PDFs gives β = 0.19 ± 0.02 (quadratic energy loss), with χ²/ndf = 1.11 and δβ/β ≈ 10%
- Global fit with HKM nuclear PDFs gives κ = 0.0037 ± 0.0003 (incident-parton momentum fraction), with χ²/ndf = 1.21 and δκ/κ ≈ 8%
- Using HKN07 nuclear PDFs yields α = 0.64 ± 0.09 GeV/fm from the global fit
- Using nDS nuclear PDFs yields α = 0.73 ± 0.09 GeV/fm from the global fit
- Using EPS09 nuclear PDFs yields α = 0.23 ± 0.07 GeV/fm from the global fit
- Previous work by the same authors (Song et al., 2012) found outgoing quark energy loss per unit length = 0.38 ± 0.03 GeV/fm from semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering data

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analyses of quark energy loss in cold nuclear matter (Song et al., 2012) by including lower-energy data from CERN experiments NA3 (140-150 GeV) and NA10 (286 GeV) alongside the Fermilab E772 and E866 data (800 GeV) used earlier, covering target-quark momentum fractions from 0.01 to 0.45. The result that incoming quark energy loss (1.21 GeV/fm with HKM PDFs) is larger than the outgoing quark energy loss (0.38 GeV/fm) contradicts the theoretical prediction by Brodsky and Hoyer (1993) that outgoing quark energy loss should be three times larger than incoming. The analysis enables improved nuclear parton distribution function determinations and provides reference for the Fermilab E906/SeaQuest experiment, which can cover the x₂ range 0.12-0.45 where the different energy loss models show clear divergence.
