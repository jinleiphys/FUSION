# Initial Angular Momentum and Flow in High Energy Nuclear Collisions

> We study the transfer of angular momentum in high energy nuclear collisions from the colliding nuclei to the region around midrapidity, using the classical approximation of the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) picture. We find that the angular momentum shortly after the collision (up to times ~ 1/Q_s, where Q_s is the saturation scale) is carried by the "beta-type" flow of the initial classical gluon field, introduced by some of us earlier. beta^i ~ mu_1 nabla^i mu_2 - mu_2 nabla^i mu_1 (i=1,2) describes the rapidity-odd transverse energy flow and emerges from Gauss' Law for gluon fields. Here mu_1 and mu_2 are the averaged color charge fluctuation densities in the two nuclei, respectively. Interestingly, strong coupling calculations using AdS/CFT techniques also find an energy flow term featuring this particular combination of nuclear densities. In classical CGC the order of magnitude of the initial angular momentum per rapidity in the reaction plane, at a time 1/Q_s, is |dL_2/d eta| ~ R_A/Q_s^3 epsilon_0/2 at midrapidity, where R_A is the nuclear radius, and epsilon_0 is the average initial energy density. This result emerges as a cancellation between a vortex of energy flow in the reaction plane aligned with the total angular momentum, and energy shear flow opposed to it. We discuss in detail the process of matching classical Yang-Mills results to fluid dynamics. We will argue that dissipative corrections should not be discarded to ensure that macroscopic conservation laws, e.g. for angular momentum, hold. Viscous fluid dynamics tends to dissipate the shear flow contribution that carries angular momentum in boost-invariant fluid systems. This leads to small residual angular momentum around midrapidity at late times for collisions at high energies.

## Key claim

The initial angular momentum per rapidity in the reaction plane at midrapidity, at a time ~1/Q_s, emerges from a cancellation between a vortex of energy flow aligned with the total angular momentum and an opposing energy shear flow, leading to a net angular momentum opposite to the primordial total angular momentum of the colliding nuclei.

## Method

The authors used the classical approximation of the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) picture, specifically the McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model, to solve the Yang-Mills equations analytically via a power series expansion in proper time τ. They computed the event-averaged energy-momentum tensor of the classical gluon field up to second order in τ, then derived the angular momentum tensor components and integrated over the transverse plane to obtain the angular momentum per rapidity. The classical Yang-Mills results were matched to viscous fluid dynamics at a time τ₀ ~ 1/Q_s, and the subsequent fluid evolution was analyzed using the VIRAL viscous fluid code.

## Key numbers

- The order of magnitude of the initial angular momentum per rapidity in the reaction plane at midrapidity at time 1/Q_s is |dL₂/dη| ~ R_A Q_s⁻³ ε̄₀/2, where R_A is the nuclear radius and ε̄₀ is the average initial energy density.
- For Pb+Pb collisions at impact parameter b = 6 fm, the initial energy density ε₀ was chosen as 605 GeV/fm³ at τ = 0.2 fm/c at the center.
- The angular momentum per rapidity dL₂/dη at η = 0 at τ = 0.25 fm/c was computed as a function of impact parameter b for Pb+Pb collisions, showing zero at b = 0 and b >> R_A.
- The integral of β¹ over the transverse plane was approximated as ∫ d²r⊥ β¹ ≈ -4 ε̄₀ R_A for 0 < b < 2R_A.

## Context

This work extends the analytic CGC approach developed by Fries (2006), Chen (2013), and Chen (2015) to study angular momentum transfer in high-energy nuclear collisions. It provides a first-principles derivation of initial angular momentum that complements earlier phenomenological estimates based on transport models by Becattini (2007) and Csernai (2013). The results establish benchmarks for future numerical simulations of classical Yang-Mills systems and for matching to fluid dynamics, particularly relevant for understanding Λ and anti-Λ polarization observed by STAR at RHIC.
