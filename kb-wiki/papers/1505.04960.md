# Latest developments in anisotropic hydrodynamics

> We discuss the leading order of anisotropic hydrodynamics expansion. It has already been shown that in the (0+1) and (1+1)-dimensional cases it is consistent with the second order viscous hydrodynamics, and it provides a striking agreement with the exact solutions of the Boltzmann equation. Quite recently, a new set of equations has been proposed for the leading order of anisotropic hydrodynamics, which is consistent with the second order viscous hydrodynamics in the most general (3+1)-dimensional case, and does not require a next-to-leading treatment for describing pressure anisotropies in the transverse plane.

# Latest developments in anisotropic hydrodynamics

## Key claim
The leading order of anisotropic hydrodynamics can be extended to the full (3+1)-dimensional case using a generalized Romatschke-Strickland form, and this new formulation is consistent with second-order viscous hydrodynamics in the limit of small deviations from local equilibrium, without requiring a next-to-leading order treatment for transverse pressure anisotropies.

## Method
The author discusses the reorganization of the relativistic hydrodynamic expansion around a non-isotropic background, using a point-dependent Romatschke-Strickland form for the leading-order particle distribution function. For the (1+1)-dimensional case, the second moment of the Boltzmann equation was used alongside energy-momentum conservation to close the system. For the (3+1)-dimensional case, a generalized anisotropic background with an anisotropy tensor and a bulk scalar was employed, closing the system using the first moment of the Boltzmann equation and a geometrical argument for choosing equations from the zeroth and second moments.

## Key numbers
- In the (0+1)-dimensional case, anisotropic hydrodynamics shows "striking agreement" with exact solutions of the Boltzmann equation, while Israel-Stewart theory produces "unphysical vanishing longitudinal pressure" and significant deviations in transverse pressure evolution.
- For the (1+1)-dimensional case with massive particles, the new formulation (eaHydro) shows "large improvement" over the original prescription (saHydro) in reproducing the exact solution for shear evolution τΠη and bulk evolution τΠζ, though bulk evolution still shows some deviations.
- The new (3+1)-dimensional formulation is proven to be "fully consistent with the second order hydrodynamics in the limit of small deviation from local equilibrium."

## Context
This work extends the anisotropic hydrodynamics framework originally developed by Florkowski (2012, 2014), Martinez (2012), and Ryblewski (2012, 2013), which treated large momentum anisotropies non-perturbatively in (0+1) and (1+1) dimensions. The new formulation by Tinti (2014) and Nopoush (2014) generalizes the approach to (3+1)-dimensional flows without restrictions from boost invariance or cylindrical symmetry, enabling full three-dimensional simulations of heavy-ion collisions without requiring next-to-leading order corrections for transverse pressure anisotropies. This addresses limitations of standard second-order viscous hydrodynamics approaches such as Israel-Stewart theory and the DNMR formulation by Denicol (2012), which can become unreliable when pressure anisotropies are large.
