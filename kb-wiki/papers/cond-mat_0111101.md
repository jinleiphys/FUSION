# In Search of Exact Solutions

> Many-body systems, such as electrons flowing in a superconductor, are among the most difficult theoretical problems to study. A new family of exactly solvable models may offer some answers.

# In Search of Exact Solutions

## Key claim
A new family of exactly solvable pairing models, generalizing Richardson's 30-year-old solution, can be solved in any number of dimensions (one, two, three, or more), but the solutions are limited to finite-size systems at absolute zero temperature, though thermodynamic quantities at non-zero temperature can be obtained by thermal averaging.

## Method
Dukelsky and colleagues applied group-theoretical and algebraic methods, similar to those used by Gaudin (1976), to discover three classes of integrable pairing models: rational, trigonometric, and hyperbolic. They then applied the rational model to a two-dimensional system of 18 fermions with repulsive interactions, finding an exact numerical solution.

## Key numbers
- 18 fermions: the rational model was applied to a two-dimensional system of 18 fermions with repulsive interactions, for which an exact numerical solution was found.
- 30 years: Richardson's original exact solution of the pairing model was published more than 30 years prior to this work (Richardson, 1963).

## Context
This work extends the exact solution of the pairing model originally solved by Richardson (1963), a result that had passed almost unnoticed despite its potential impact on nuclear and condensed-matter physics. The new models apply to both boson and fermion systems in any dimension, in contrast to most previous exact solutions which applied only to one-dimensional systems (such as the Tomonaga-Luttinger solution). The results enable further applications to high-temperature superconductivity, Bose-Einstein condensation (Dukelsky and Schuck, 2001), and the interacting boson model of nuclear physics (Dukelsky and Pittel, 2001).
