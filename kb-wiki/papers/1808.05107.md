# Post breakup dynamics of fragments produced in nuclear multifragmentation

> The deexcitation of the primary hot fragments, produced in the breakup of an excited nuclear source, during their propagation under the influence of their mutual Coulomb repulsion is studied in the framework of a recently developed hybrid model. The latter is based on the Statistical Mul- tifragmentation Model (SMM), describing the prompt breakup of the source, whereas the particle emission from the hot fragments, that decay while traveling away from each other, is treated by the Weisskopf-Ewing evaporation model. Since this treatment provides an event by event descrip- tion of the process, in which the classical trajectories of the fragments are followed using molecular dynamics techniques, it allows one to study observables such as two-particle correlations and infer the extent to which the corresponding observables may provide information on the multifragment production mechanisms. Our results suggest that the framework on which these treatments are based may be considerably constrained by such analyses. Furthermore, they imply that information obtained from these model calculations may provide feedback to the theory of nuclear interferome- try. We also found that neutron deficient fragments should hold information more closely related to the breakup region than neutron rich ones, as they are produced in much earlier stages of the post breakup dynamics than the latter.

## Key claim

The hybrid model combining the Improved Statistical Multifragmentation Model (ISMM) with Weisskopf-Ewing evaporation and classical trajectory propagation suggests that neutron deficient fragments are produced on much shorter time scales than neutron rich ones, making them more reliable probes of the initial breakup configuration, but the framework's conclusions are subject to the specific parameterization choices for fusion cross-sections and momentum acceptance criteria.

## Method

The authors used a hybrid model where the prompt breakup of an excited nuclear source (A0=168, Z0=75, representing 75% of the 112Sn+112Sn system) was described by the canonical version of the Improved Statistical Multifragmentation Model (ISMM) with a breakup volume parameter χ=2. The primary hot fragments were then propagated under mutual Coulomb repulsion using Runge-Kutta-Cash-Karp molecular dynamics, while their sequential deexcitation was treated via the Weisskopf-Ewing evaporation model, allowing emission of fragments with mass number up to 20. A total of 10^8 events were generated, and two-particle correlation functions were calculated using the Pratt-Koonin formula with the Reid soft potential for proton-proton interactions.

## Key numbers

- The source parameters were A0=168 and Z0=75, corresponding to 75% of the 112Sn+112Sn system, assuming 25% pre-equilibrium emission.
- The breakup volume parameter was fixed to χ=2, giving V=(1+χ)V0 where V0 is the source volume at normal density.
- Approximately 10% of the final fragment yields were created within the first 10 fm/c after breakup, associated with the prompt source fragmentation.
- For two-proton correlations with Q=300 MeV/c, a tiny bump appeared near q=20 MeV/c, while two-deuteron correlations showed a noticeable peak around q≈25 MeV/c.
- With Q=500±20 MeV/c acceptance, the peak height increased substantially for both proton and deuteron pairs.
- For deuteron pairs selected with Q=500±20 MeV/c, a very small fraction kept their total momentum unchanged during post-breakup dynamics, and the asymptotic correlation function showed a rather flat behavior compared to the pronounced bump at creation time.
- Approximately 20% of proton pairs kept their momenta unchanged during the dynamics.

## Context

This work extends the hybrid model developed in Souza et al. (2018) (smmWeisskopf2018) and builds on the Improved Statistical Multifragmentation Model (ISMM) from earlier SMM formulations (Bondorf 1995). It addresses ambiguities in nuclear interferometry raised by studies such as those of Giuseppe et al. (2002, 2003) on disentangling fast and slow components of two-proton correlation functions. The findings enable improved interpretation of experimental correlation data by identifying which fragment species carry cleaner information about the breakup stage, and they highlight the need to account for momentum changes during post-breakup dynamics when applying the Pratt-Koonin formalism.
