# Higher order moments of multiparticle azimuthal correlations

> We introduce a general procedure to pave the road towards the ultimate goal of deriving analytic expressions for the probability density functions (p.d.f.'s) of multiparticle azimuthal correlations. All multiparticle azimuthal correlators can be expressed analytically in terms of the real and imaginary parts of $M$-particle $Q$-vectors. We derive the analytic results for the p.d.f.'s of single-particle $Q$-vectors in the most general case and demonstrate that they can be expressed solely in terms of Chebyshev polynomials of the first kind. This leads analytically to the expressions of the characteristic functions of $M$-particle $Q$-vectors in terms of Bessel functions of the first kind. From the obtained characteristics functions we calculate the higher order moments of the real and imaginary parts of $M$-particle $Q$-vectors and use them to obtain the higher order moments of multiparticle azimuthal correlators. Finally, these results are used to investigate the sensitivity of multiparticle azimuthal correlations and to illuminate requirements necessary for future anisotropic flow measurements.

## Key claim
The paper introduces a general procedure for deriving analytic expressions for the probability density functions (p.d.f.'s) of multiparticle azimuthal correlations, but only under the assumption that collective anisotropic flow is the sole source of correlations among produced particles and that the factorization of joint p.d.f.'s holds exactly, with nonflow correlations explicitly excluded from the present work.

## Method
The author derives analytic p.d.f.'s for the real and imaginary parts of single-particle Q-vectors by treating azimuthal angles as random observables with a Fourier-series p.d.f. and applying conservation of probability with generalizations for piecewise-defined functions and non-unique inverses. These single-particle results are then extended to M-particle Q-vectors via characteristic functions, using the property that the characteristic function of a sum of independent random observables is the product of individual characteristic functions. The higher order moments of the real and imaginary parts of M-particle Q-vectors are calculated from the obtained characteristic functions and used to derive higher order moments of isotropic multiparticle azimuthal correlations.

## Key numbers
- The p.d.f. for both real and imaginary parts of single-particle Q-vectors in the random walk case (all Fourier harmonics zero) is g(a) = 1/(π√(1-a²)) for a ∈ [-1,1), independent of harmonic m.
- For monochromatic flow with only cosine harmonic c_n, the p.d.f. of the real part of single-particle Q-vector evaluated in harmonic m is g(a) = (1+2c_n T_{n/m}(a))/(π√(1-a²)) when n/m is an arbitrary integer, where T_n is the nth Chebyshev polynomial of the first kind.
- For monochromatic flow with only sine harmonic s_n, the p.d.f. of the imaginary part of single-particle Q-vector evaluated in harmonic m is g(a) = (1+2s_n i^{n/m-1} T_{n/m}(a))/(π√(1-a²)) when n/m is an odd integer.
- The characteristic function for both real and imaginary parts of single-particle Q-vectors in the random walk case is φ_a(k) = J_0(k), where J_0 is the Bessel function of the first kind and order zero.
- For multichromatic flow, the characteristic function of the real part of single-particle Q-vector evaluated in harmonic m is φ_{Re u_m}(k) = J_0(k) + 2∑_{p=1}^∞ (-1)^p [c_{2p·m} J_{2p}(k) - i c_{(2p-1)·m} J_{2p-1}(k)].

## Context
This work extends the formalism of multiparticle azimuthal correlations introduced by Borghini (2000) and the exact evaluation framework developed by Bilandzic (2013), which replaced the non-exact generating function approach. The paper initiates the derivation of higher order moments and p.d.f.'s for these correlations, which were previously only known for first moments from Bhalerao (2011). The results enable future studies of event-by-event flow fluctuations and provide sensitivity estimates for designing detectors for anisotropic flow measurements, while the treatment of nonflow biases is deferred to subsequent work.
