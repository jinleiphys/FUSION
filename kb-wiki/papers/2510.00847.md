# Gluon Condensation as a Unifying Mechanism for Special Spectra of Cosmic Gamma Rays and Low-Momentum Pion Enhancement at the Large Hadron Collider

> Decoding the internal structure of the proton is a fundamental challenge in physics. Historically, any new discovery about the proton has fuelled advances in several scientific fields. We have reported that gluons inside the proton accumulate near the critical momentum due to chaotic phenomena, forming gluon condensation. Surprisingly, the pion distribution predicted by this gluon distribution for the production of high-energy proton collisions could answer two puzzles in astronomy and high-energy physics. We find that during ultrahigh-energy cosmic ray collisions, gluon condensation may abruptly produce a large number of low-momentum pions, whose electromagnetic decays have the typical breakout properties appearing in various cosmic gamma-ray spectra. On the other hand, the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), which is well below the cosmic ray energy scale, also shows weak but recognisable signs of gluon condensation, which had been mistaken for BEC pions. The connection between these two phenomena, which occur at different scales in the Universe, supports the existence of a new structure within the proton-gluon condensation.

## Key claim
Gluon condensation (GC), a chaotic accumulation of gluons near a critical momentum inside the proton, can simultaneously explain the broken power-law (BPL) shape observed in cosmic gamma-ray spectra and the low-momentum pion enhancement seen in LHC heavy-ion collisions, which had previously been attributed to Bose-Einstein condensation (BEC) of pions. The authors hedge that the GC effect at LHC energies is "weak but recognisable" and that the BPL interpretation is not unique, as inverse Compton scattering models can also produce similar spectral shapes.

## Method
The authors incorporate a gluon condensation distribution, derived from the Zhu-Shen-Ruan (ZSR) evolution equation, which includes chaotic nonlinear effects beyond the color glass condensate (CGC) framework, into the standard formula for gluon mini-jet production in hadron collisions. For cosmic gamma rays, they use energy conservation and relativistic covariance to bypass detailed hadronization, deriving an analytical BPL spectrum for gamma-ray emission from neutral-pion decay. For LHC heavy-ion collisions, they compare the rapidity and transverse momentum distributions of gluon mini-jets from the GC model to the pion data previously interpreted as BEC by Begun and Florkowski (2015).

## Key numbers
- The GC threshold for heavy-nucleus collisions is estimated at sqrt(s_GC) ≈ 1.4 TeV, corresponding to a critical pion energy E_pi^GC ≈ 100 GeV.
- The broken point in the GC gamma-ray spectrum is set at E_pi^GC = 100 GeV.
- The LHC Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV correspond to approximately 2 × sqrt(s_GC), placing them in the regime where GC effects begin to appear.
- Begun and Florkowski (2015) found pion coalescence of 0–2% in central collisions rising to 19% in peripheral collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV.
- The GC model predicts that at the upgraded LHC energy of sqrt(s_NN) = 5.36 TeV, low-momentum pions exceeding 19% may be detected in the forward rapidity region.
- The GC thresholds for O-O collisions at sqrt(s_NN) ≈ 10 TeV and p-p collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 300 TeV are E_pi^GC ≈ 1 TeV and 20 TeV, respectively.

## Context
This work extends the authors' prior development of the Zhu-Shen-Ruan (ZSR) equation (Zhu 2016) and their earlier proposal of gluon condensation (Zhu 2022), which goes beyond the color glass condensate (CGC) framework of McLerran and Venugopalan (1994) by incorporating chaotic dynamics that produce a sharp peak rather than a flat saturated distribution. The paper directly challenges the interpretation of low-momentum pion data from the ALICE collaboration, which Begun and Florkowski (2015) had attributed to BEC of pions, arguing instead that GC provides a simpler explanation without requiring the stringent BEC condition (mu_pi → m_pi). The GC spectrum is compared to inverse Compton (IC) models for gamma-ray bursts such as GRB 190114C and GRB 221009A, with the authors claiming that GRB 221009A favours the GC interpretation over leptonic models (Foffano 2024). Future tests are expected at the HE-LHC, FCC-hh, and SppC colliders.
