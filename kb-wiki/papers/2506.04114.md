# The Infrared Phase of QCD and Anderson Localization

> When Anderson localization entered the QCD landscape, it was almost immediately thought about in connection with thermal phases, namely as a factor in the chiral transition. However, recent developments revealed an additional structure that made Anderson-like features central to the genesis of the entirely new thermal phase: the IR phase. I will explain these developments.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Anderson-like localization of Dirac eigenmodes is not merely a factor in the chiral transition but is central to the existence of a newly identified thermal regime called the IR phase, which occurs at temperatures above the known crossover range and is characterized by a metal-to-critical scenario involving two mobility edges rather than a simple metal-to-insulator transition.

## Method

The author reviews lattice QCD simulations of SU(3) gauge theories with fundamental quarks, analyzing the spectral density of the overlap and staggered Dirac operators to characterize the deep-infrared behavior of Dirac modes. The analysis uses effective-dimension theory to compute the infrared spatial dimension of Dirac eigenmodes as a function of eigenvalue and temperature, identifying Anderson-like mobility edges from discontinuities in the dimension.

## Key numbers

- The IR phase transition temperature \(T_\fir\) in real-world QCD lies in the range 200 MeV < \(T_\fir\) < 230 MeV, based on \(N_f = 2+1\) studies at physical quark masses.
- The chiral crossover temperature is \(T_\cro^c \approx 155\) MeV.
- In pure-glue QCD, the transition occurs between \(T = 0.98 T_\fir\) and \(T = 1.12 T_\fir\), where the power-law behavior of the spectral density changes dramatically.
- The infrared spectral density in the IR phase follows a near-pure power law \(\rho(\lambda) \propto \lambda^p\) with \(p = -1 + \delta\) and \(\delta > 0\) very small, persisting for over three orders of magnitude in scale in the pure-glue case.
- The infrared dimension of near-zero critical modes in the IR phase is \(d_\fir = 2\), while exact zero modes have \(d_\fir = 3\).
- For \(N_f = 2+1\) real-world QCD, data at \(T = 234\) MeV (inside IR phase) shows \(d_\fir = 2\) for near-zero modes, while at \(T = 187\) MeV (outside IR phase) the dimension is different.

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal by Halasz and Verbaarschot (1995) that thermal fluctuations in QCD could induce Anderson localization of quark modes, and the subsequent identification of a mobility edge in hot QCD by Kovacs (2010) and Giordano (2013). It contradicts the simple metal-to-insulator scenario by showing that deep-infrared modes proliferate rather than deplete at high temperatures, as first reported by Alexandru et al. (2015). The paper builds on the formulation of the IR phase by Alexandru et al. (2019) and the metal-to-critical scenario by Alexandru et al. (2021), which introduced a second mobility edge at \(\lambda = 0\). The findings enable a new understanding of thermal QCD where the IR phase exhibits scale-invariant glue, infinite screening lengths, and a dimensional collapse of near-zero modes to \(d_\fir = 2\), with potential connections to the conformal window and the near-perfect fluid observed at RHIC and LHC.
