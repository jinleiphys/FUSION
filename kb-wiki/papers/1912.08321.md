# Lattice QCD Determination of $g_A$

> The nucleon axial coupling, $g_A$, is a fundamental property of protons and neutrons, dictating the strength with which the weak axial current of the Standard Model couples to nucleons, and hence, the lifetime of a free neutron. The prominence of $g_A$ in nuclear physics has made it a benchmark quantity with which to calibrate lattice QCD calculations of nucleon structure and more complex calculations of electroweak matrix elements in one and few nucleon systems. There were a number of significant challenges in determining $g_A$, notably the notorious exponentially-bad signal-to-noise problem and the requirement for hundreds of thousands of stochastic samples, that rendered this goal more difficult to obtain than originally thought. I will describe the use of an unconventional computation method, coupled with "ludicrously'" fast GPU code, access to publicly available lattice QCD configurations from MILC and access to leadership computing that have allowed these challenges to be overcome resulting in a determination of $g_A$ with 1% precision and all sources of systematic uncertainty controlled. I will discuss the implications of these results for the convergence of $SU(2)$ Chiral Perturbation theory for nucleons, as well as prospects for further improvements to $g_A$ (sub-percent precision, for which we have preliminary results) which is part of a more comprehensive application of lattice QCD to nuclear physics. This is particularly exciting in light of the new CORAL supercomputers coming online, Sierra and Summit, for which our lattice QCD codes achieve a machine-to-machine speed up over Titan of an order of magnitude.

## Key claim

The authors report a lattice QCD determination of the nucleon axial coupling \(g_A\) with 1% precision and all sources of systematic uncertainty controlled, using an unconventional Feynman-Hellmann method on publicly available MILC configurations. The abstract's claim of "all sources of systematic uncertainty controlled" is qualified by the actual result \(g_A = 1.2711(103)^s(39)^\chi(15)^a(04)^V(55)^M\), which includes separate uncertainties from statistics, chiral extrapolation, continuum extrapolation, volume extrapolation, and model averaging.

## Method

The calculation uses Möbius Domain-Wall Fermions on gradient-flowed HISQ gauge configurations generated by the MILC Collaboration, with calculations performed on 16 ensembles spanning lattice spacings \(0.09 \lesssim a \lesssim 0.15\) fm and pion masses \(130 \lesssim m_\pi \lesssim 400\) MeV. The matrix elements are extracted using a Feynman-Hellmann theorem-inspired method applied to the effective mass of two-point correlation functions, which provides additional suppression of excited-state contamination and allows access to many source-sink separations from a single calculation. The renormalized value of \(g_A\) is obtained from the ratio of bare axial to vector charges, exploiting the near-chiral symmetry of the action where \(Z_A = Z_V\) to one part in \(10^4\).

## Key numbers

- \(g_A = 1.2711(103)^s(39)^\chi(15)^a(04)^V(55)^M\) from the published model-average extrapolation, where the uncertainties are statistical (1.03%), chiral extrapolation (0.39%), continuum extrapolation (0.15%), volume extrapolation (0.04%), and model average (0.55%)
- The a12m130 ensemble (physical pion mass, \(a \sim 0.12\) fm) achieved 2.3% uncertainty in the published result, the most precise value at the physical pion mass at the time of publication
- Preliminary update with improved statistics on Sierra gives \(g_A^{\rm QCD} = 1.2642(93)\), a 0.74% uncertainty, obtained from 16 sources per configuration on a12m130 (compared to 3 sources in the published result) and 8 sources per configuration on a new a15m135XL ensemble
- The a12m130 ensemble with 16 sources was produced in 2.5 weekends on Sierra, compared to the published result which used 3 sources from the 2016 INCITE allocation on Titan

## Context

This work extends lattice QCD calculations of nucleon structure to the 1% precision level for \(g_A\), a benchmark quantity that was previously estimated to require near-exascale computing (such as Summit at OLCF) to achieve 2% uncertainty by 2020 (USQCD DOE report, 2016). The result provides the strongest constraint on right-handed beyond-Standard-Model currents (Alioli et al., 2017) and enables future sub-percent determinations that could discriminate the neutron lifetime puzzle at the 4-sigma level when combined with QED radiative corrections (Hayen et al., 2019; Seng et al., 2018). The analysis demonstrates that \(SU(2)\) heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory without explicit delta degrees of freedom has poor convergence even at the physical pion mass, with strong order-by-order cancellations observed in the chiral extrapolation.
