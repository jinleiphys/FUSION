# Statistical uncertainty estimation of higher-order cumulants with finite efficiency and its application in heavy-ion collisions

> We derive the general analytical expressions for the statistical uncertainties of cumulants up to fourth order including an efficiency correction. The analytical expressions have been tested with a toy Monte Carlo model analysis. An application to the study of particle multiplicity fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions is investigated. In this derivation, a mathematical proof is given that the validity of an averaged efficiency correction and the fluctuations induced by the non-uniformity of efficiency can be eliminated. The estimation of statistical uncertainties using the analytical formulas is found to be significantly faster than the commonly used bootstrap method. The simplicity and efficiency of using the analytical formulas may be useful for massive data analysis in many fields.

## Key claim

The paper derives general analytical formulas for the statistical uncertainties of efficiency-corrected cumulants up to fourth order, claiming that these formulas are significantly faster than the bootstrap method; however, the derivation assumes binomial efficiency and requires that the correcting efficiency be an average of the measuring efficiency within phase spaces meeting specific multinomial distribution conditions.

## Method

The authors start from known covariances of estimated cumulants in terms of population cumulants (Kendall 1994). They then apply the binomial efficiency correction formulas for cumulants up to fourth order (Nonaka 2017, Kitazawa 2017, Luo 2018) and propagate the variances using the delta method, expressing the variance of each efficiency-corrected cumulant as a sum of measured cumulants weighted by polynomial functions of the efficiency. The analytical results are tested against a toy Monte Carlo model and compared with the bootstrap method.

## Key numbers

- The variance of the estimated efficiency-corrected first-order cumulant is given by \( n \cdot \mathrm{Var}(\widehat{C}_1^{\mathrm{corr}}) = \frac{1}{\alpha^2} \langle x^2 \rangle_{\mathrm{c}} \), where \(\alpha\) is the efficiency and \(n\) the number of samples.
- The variance of the estimated efficiency-corrected second-order cumulant is \( n \cdot \mathrm{Var}(\widehat{C}_2^{\mathrm{corr}}) = \frac{1}{\alpha^4} (\langle x^4 \rangle_{\mathrm{c}} + 2\langle x^2 \rangle_{\mathrm{c}}^2) + \frac{2}{\alpha^2}(\frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{1}{\alpha^2})\langle x^3 \rangle_{\mathrm{c}} + (\frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{1}{\alpha^2})^2 \langle x^2 \rangle_{\mathrm{c}} \).
- The variance of the estimated efficiency-corrected fourth-order cumulant involves terms up to \(\langle x^8 \rangle_{\mathrm{c}}\) and includes 24 terms with coefficients such as \(1/\alpha^8\), \((-6/\alpha^4 + 6/\alpha^3)^2\), and \((11/\alpha^4 - 18/\alpha^3 + 7/\alpha^2)^2\).
- The covariance between the estimated efficiency-corrected first- and second-order cumulants is \( n \cdot \mathrm{Cov}(\widehat{C}_2^{\mathrm{corr}}, \widehat{C}_1^{\mathrm{corr}}) = \langle q_{(1,1)}^3 \rangle_{\mathrm{c}} + \langle q_{(1,1)} q_{(2,1)} \rangle_{\mathrm{c}} - \langle q_{(1,1)} q_{(2,2)} \rangle_{\mathrm{c}} \).

## Context

This work extends the efficiency correction formulas for cumulants (Nonaka 2017, Kitazawa 2017, Luo 2018, Si 2021) by providing the corresponding analytical statistical uncertainty formulas, which were previously unavailable. It directly addresses the need for fast uncertainty estimation in heavy-ion collision fluctuation analyses, such as those by the STAR Collaboration (STAR 2014, STAR 2017, STAR 2020) searching for the QCD critical point. The analytical method enables decomposition of variance components to understand how efficiency non-uniformity affects uncertainties, and it enables rapid uncertainty calculations for massive datasets where the bootstrap method (Efron 1979) would be computationally prohibitive.
