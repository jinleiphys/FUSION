# Berry's phase and chiral anomalies

> The basic materials of Berry's phase and chiral anomalies are presented to appreciate the phenomena related to those notions. As for Berry's phase, a general survey of the subject is presented using both Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formalisms. The canonical Hamiltonian formalism of the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, when applied to the anomalous Hall effect, can incorporate the gauge symmetry of Berry's connection but unable to incorporate the electromagnetic vector potential simultaneously. Transformed to the Lagrangian formalism with a time-derivative term allowed, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation can incorporate the electromagnetic vector potential simultaneously with Berry's connection, but the consistent canonical property is lost and thus becomes classical. The Lagrangian formalism can thus incorporate both gauge symmetries simultaneously but spoils the basic quantum symmetries, and thus results in classical anomalous Poisson brackets and the classical Nernst effect as in the conventional formalism. As for chiral anomalies, we present basic materials by the path integral formulation with an emphasis on fermions on the lattice. A chiral fermion defined by $γ_{5}$ on the lattice does not contain the chiral anomaly for the non-vanishing lattice spacing $a\neq0$. The idea of a spectral flow on the lattice does not lead to an anomaly for each species doubler separately but rather to a pair production in a general sense. We also mention that a specific construction called the Ginsparg-Wilson fermion, which is free of species doublers, may practically be useful. We discuss the representative applications of Berry's phase and chiral anomalies in nuclear physics and related fields to illustrate the use of these two basic notions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Berry's phase and chiral anomalies are fundamentally distinct phenomena despite superficial similarities: Berry's phase is topological only when the precise adiabatic condition is satisfied and does not cause level crossing, whereas the chiral anomaly is characterized by the inevitable failure of the adiabatic theorem and forced level crossing even in an ideal adiabatic process.

## Method

The authors present a systematic review using both Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formalisms, with the second quantized path integral approach as the primary tool for analyzing Berry's phase. For chiral anomalies, they employ the path integral formulation with an emphasis on fermions defined on the lattice with finite lattice spacing, examining the species doubling problem and the properties of the Ginsparg-Wilson fermion. The Bjorken-Johnson-Low (BJL) prescription is used to analyze equal-time commutation relations in systems with time-derivative couplings.

## Key numbers

- The adiabatic condition for Berry's phase is given by \(|\epsilon_{n\pm1}-\epsilon_{n}|\gg 2\pi\hbar/T\), where \(2\pi\hbar/T\) is the typical energy scale of the slower system and \(\epsilon_n\) is the n-th energy level of the fast system
- For a Weyl-type fermion described by \(H=-\mu \vec{p}(t)\cdot\vec{\sigma}\), the adiabatic condition becomes \(2\mu|\vec{p}| \gg 2\pi\hbar/T\)
- A chiral fermion defined by \(\gamma_5\) on the lattice does not contain the chiral anomaly for non-vanishing lattice spacing \(a\neq0\)
- In the Lorentz invariant 4-dimensional case, attempting to define a single species of chiral fermion on the lattice encounters 16 species of fermions (species doublers)

## Context

This review extends the authors' earlier observation (Fujikawa, 2006) that the chiral anomaly is characterized by the inevitable failure of the adiabatic theorem, contrasting with Berry's phase where topology is self-generated only when the adiabatic condition is satisfied. The work critically assesses proposals by Stone, Jackiw, Son-Yamamoto, and Stephanov that link Berry's phase to chiral anomalies, arguing these connections require careful distinction. The paper enables clearer assessment of applications in nuclear physics, particularly the chiral magnetic effect and the anomalous Hall effect, by providing the foundational formalism needed to evaluate whether specific phenomena genuinely reflect chiral anomalies or Berry's phase.
