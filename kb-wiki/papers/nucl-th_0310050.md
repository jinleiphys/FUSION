# Ultra peripheral heavy ion collisions and the energy dependence of the nuclear radius

> To estimate realistic cross sections in ultra peripheral heavy ion collisions we must remove effects of strong absorption. One method to eliminate these effects make use of a Glauber model calculation, where the nucleon-nucleon energy dependent cross sections at small impact parameter are suppressed. In another method we impose a geometrical cut on the minimal impact parameter of the nuclear collision ($b_{min} > R_1 + R_2$, where $R_i$ is the radius of ion "$i$"). In this last case the effect of a possible nuclear radius dependence with the energy has not been considered in detail up to now. Here we introduce this effect showing that for final states with small invariant mass the effect is negligible. However when the final state has a relatively large invariant mass, e.g. an intermediate mass Higgs boson, the cross section can decrease up to 50%.

## Key claim

Introducing an energy-dependent nuclear radius into the geometric-cut method for ultra peripheral heavy ion collisions causes cross sections to decrease; for final states with small invariant mass the effect is negligible (below 10%), but for a final state with large invariant mass such as an intermediate-mass Higgs boson (115 GeV) the cross section can decrease up to 50%.

## Method

The authors compute two-photon fusion cross sections for ultra peripheral heavy ion collisions using the equivalent-photon approximation (EPA) with a minimum impact parameter cut \(b_{min} > R_1 + R_2\). They replace the usual energy-independent nuclear radius \(R_{ion} = 1.2 A^{1/3}\) fm with an energy-dependent radius \(R(s)\) derived from a model by Barroso, Kodama, and Hama (1996) that describes the nuclear radius increase with energy. Cross sections for lepton pair production (\(\mu^+\mu^-\), \(\tau^+\tau^-\)) and resonance production (\(\eta_c\), Higgs boson) are computed for Au, Ca, and Pb ions at RHIC and LHC energies, comparing results with and without the energy-dependent radius.

## Key numbers

- For Au-Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s} = 0.2\) TeV/nucleon, the \(\mu^+\mu^-\) cross section decreases from 2.127 mbarn (constant radius) to 1.947 mbarn (energy-dependent radius), a ratio of 1.09.
- For Ca-Ca collisions at \(\sqrt{s} = 7.2\) TeV/nucleon, the \(\tau^+\tau^-\) cross section decreases from \(5.176 \times 10^{-3}\) mbarn to \(4.604 \times 10^{-3}\) mbarn, a ratio of 1.12.
- For Au-Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s} = 0.2\) TeV/nucleon, the \(\eta_c\) production cross section decreases from \(2.147 \times 10^{-3}\) mbarn to \(1.846 \times 10^{-3}\) mbarn, a ratio of 1.16.
- For Ca-Ca collisions at \(\sqrt{s} = 7.2\) TeV/nucleon, the Higgs boson (115 GeV) production cross section decreases from \(9.970 \times 10^{-10}\) mbarn to \(6.789 \times 10^{-10}\) mbarn, a ratio of 1.47 (nearly 50% decrease).
- For Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s} = 5.5\) TeV/nucleon, the Higgs boson cross section decreases from \(1.854 \times 10^{-8}\) mbarn to \(1.387 \times 10^{-8}\) mbarn, a ratio of 1.34.
- The energy-dependent nuclear radius values: Au at 0.2 TeV/nucleon gives \(R(s) = 7.29\) fm (vs. \(R_{ion} = 6.98\) fm); Pb at 5.5 TeV/nucleon gives \(R(s) = 7.61\) fm (vs. \(R_{ion} = 7.11\) fm).

## Context

This work extends the geometric-cut method for ultra peripheral heavy ion collisions, previously developed by Baur (1989, 1990) and Cahn and Jackson (1990), by incorporating the energy dependence of the nuclear radius that had not been considered in detail. The authors note that Glauber model calculations (Glauber, 1959) naturally include the energy dependence of nucleon-nucleon cross sections, and that Roldao (unpublished thesis) found small differences between the two methods. The energy-dependent radius model is taken from Barroso, Kodama, and Hama (1996). The results enable more realistic predictions for processes with large invariant masses (e.g., Higgs production) at RHIC and LHC, and suggest that high-precision measurements of ultra peripheral collisions could provide a new way to study the increase of nuclear radius with energy.
