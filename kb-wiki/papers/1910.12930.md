# The Skinny on Bulk Viscosity and Cavitation in Heavy Ion Collisions

> Relativistic heavy ion collisions generate nuclear-sized droplets of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) that exhibit nearly inviscid hydrodynamic expansion. Smaller collision systems such as p+Au, d+Au, and $^{3}$He+Au at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider, as well as p+Pb and high-multiplicity p+p at the Large Hadron Collider may create even smaller droplets of QGP. If so, the standard time evolution paradigm of heavy ion collisions may be extended to these smaller systems. These small systems present a unique opportunity to examine pre-hydrodynamic physics and extract properties of the QGP, such as the bulk viscosity, where the short lifetimes of the small droplets makes them more sensitive to these contributions. Here we focus on the influence of bulk viscosity, its temperature dependence, and cavitation effects on the dynamics in small and large systems using the publicly available hydrodynamic codes SONIC and MUSIC. We also compare pre-hydrodynamic physics in different frameworks including AdS/CFT strong coupling, IP-GLASMA weak coupling, and free streaming or no coupling.

## Key claim

The paper argues that currently used parameterizations of the bulk viscosity to entropy density ratio (ζ/s) in hydrodynamic simulations of heavy ion collisions lead to large space-time volumes with negative effective pressure (cavitation), placing the results outside the domain of validity of hydrodynamics; this conclusion is conditional on the specific parameterizations tested and the assumption that cavitation signals a breakdown of the hydrodynamic description.

## Method

The authors performed 2+1 dimensional relativistic viscous hydrodynamic simulations using the publicly available codes SONIC and MUSIC, initialized with a single Gaussian energy density profile (σ = 1.0 fm, central temperature T ≈ 370 MeV) as a proxy for small collision systems. They compared multiple temperature-dependent parameterizations of ζ/s (Param. I, Param. II, Param. III, and a Bayesian prior range) with a fixed shear viscosity η/s = 1/4π, varying the freeze-out temperature (145, 165, 180 MeV) and toggling the bulk δf correction in MUSIC on and off, then computed the ratio of bulk pressure Π to equilibrium pressure P₀ (Π/P₀) to identify cavitation regions.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of cavitation in heavy ion collisions first raised by Rajagopal (2009) and later studied by Denicol (2015) for large collision systems, applying it to small systems (p+Au, d+Au, ³He+Au at RHIC; p+Pb and high-multiplicity p+p at LHC) where short lifetimes make bulk viscosity effects more prominent. The paper contrasts the strongly-coupled AdS/CFT pre-hydrodynamic evolution used in SONIC (which requires only modest ζ/s ≈ 0.01, as in Weller (2017)) with the IP-GLASMA weak-coupling approach used in MUSIC (which requires large ζ/s to temper radial flow, as in Schenke (2019)), and argues that the large ζ/s parameterizations lead to cavitation that invalidates the hydrodynamic results. The findings enable future work to isolate which pre-hydrodynamic evolution and initial conditions necessitate large bulk viscosity, and to develop hydrodynamic codes that handle cavitation or to rule out such parameterizations via experimental constraints on freeze-out temperatures.
