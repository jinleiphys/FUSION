# Nucleon localization and fragment formation in nuclear fission

> An electron localization measure was originally introduced to characterize chemical bond structures in molecules. Recently, a nucleon localization based on Hartree-Fock densities has been introduced to investigate $α$-cluster structures in light nuclei. Compared to the local nucleonic densities, the nucleon localization function has been shown to be an excellent indicator of shell effects and cluster correlations. Using the spatial nucleon localization measure, we investigate the emergence of fragments in fissioning heavy nuclei. To illustrate basic concepts of nucleon localization, we employ the self-consistent energy density functional method with a quantified energy density functional optimized for fission studies. We study the particle densities and spatial nucleon localization distributions along the fission pathways of $^{264}$Fm, $^{232}$Th and $^{240}$Pu. We demonstrate that the fission fragments are formed fairly early in the evolution, well before scission. We illustrate the usefulness of the localization measure by showing how the hyperdeformed state of $^{232}$Th can be understood in terms of a quasimolecular state made of $^{132}$Sn and $^{100}$Zr fragments. Compared to nucleonic distributions, the nucleon localization function more effectively quantifies nucleonic clustering: its characteristic oscillating pattern, traced back to shell effects, is a clear fingerprint of cluster/fragment configurations. This is of particular interest for studies of fragment formation and fragment identification in fissioning nuclei.

## Key claim

The nucleon localization function (NLF) reveals that fission fragments in heavy nuclei are formed fairly early in the evolution, well before scission, based on self-consistent energy density functional calculations with the UNEDF1 functional; the abstract's claim of "fairly early" is qualified by the specific observation that the ring-like pattern of NLFs develops at an early stage of fission where the neck is hardly formed.

## Method

The authors employed the self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method using the Skyrme energy density functional UNEDF1, optimized for fission studies, with pairing treated via the Lipkin-Nogami approximation. They computed nucleonic densities and spatial nucleon localization functions (NLFs) along static fission pathways for three nuclei: 264Fm, 232Th, and 240Pu. The NLF, defined analogously to the electron localization function in chemistry, was calculated from canonical HFB orbits and normalized by the Thomas-Fermi kinetic energy density, with time-reversal symmetry conserved so that current densities vanish.

## Key numbers

- For 264Fm, the static symmetric fission pathway shows a neck emerging at mass quadrupole moment Q20 ≈ 145 b, with scission reached at Q20 ≈ 265 b.
- For 232Th, a hyperdeformed third minimum is predicted at quadrupole moments Q20 ≈ 150–200 b, associated with a quasimolecular configuration of 132Sn and 100Zr fragments.
- For 240Pu, the transition to a reflection-asymmetric pathway begins at Q20 ≈ 95 b.
- The ground state of 100Zr was calculated at a prolate configuration with Q20 = 10 b, corresponding to the lighter fission fragment predicted for 232Th.

## Context

This work extends the nucleon localization approach previously applied to alpha-cluster structures in light nuclei by Reinhard (2011) to heavy fissioning systems. It builds upon prior density functional theory studies of fission pathways for 264Fm (Staszczak 2009), 232Th (McDonnell 2013), and 240Pu (Sadhukhan 2016). The results demonstrate that NLFs provide a more selective signature of clustering than nucleonic densities alone, enabling future studies of clustering in medium-mass nuclei and identification of fission yields, as well as visualization of collective rotational motion when time-reversal symmetry is broken.
