# Nucleon Effective Masses in Neutron-Rich Matter

> Various kinds of isovector nucleon effective masses are used in the literature to characterize the momentum/energy dependence of the nucleon symmetry potential or self-energy due to the space/time non-locality of the underlying isovector strong interaction in neutron-rich nucleonic matter. The multifaceted studies on nucleon isovector effective masses are multi-disciplinary in nature. Besides structures, masses and low-lying excited states of nuclei as well as nuclear reactions, studies of the isospin dependence of short-range correlations in nuclei from scatterings of high-energy electrons and protons on heavy nuclei also help understand nucleon effective masses especially the so-called E-mass in neutron-rich matter. A thorough understanding of all kinds of nucleon effective masses has multiple impacts on many interesting issues in both nuclear physics and astrophysics. We review some of the significant progresses made in recent years by the nuclear physics community in resolving some of the hotly debated and longstanding issues regarding nucleon effective masses especially in dense neutron-rich matter.

## Key claim

This review paper synthesizes recent progress in understanding nucleon effective masses in neutron-rich matter, particularly the neutron-proton effective mass splitting, while acknowledging that the momentum/energy dependence of the nucleon isovector (symmetry) potential remains the most critical but still poorly known physics, with conflicting answers about whether neutrons have higher, equal to, or lower effective masses than protons in neutron-rich matter.

## Method

The authors review and compare results from multiple microscopic many-body theories and phenomenological models (including Skyrme-Hartree-Fock, Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, chiral perturbation theory, and quantum Monte Carlo) that have been used to calculate single-nucleon potentials and associated nucleon effective masses. They connect these theoretical approaches to experimental constraints from nucleon-nucleus scattering, heavy-ion collisions, nuclear structure studies, and astrophysical observations of neutron stars, using the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem and Migdal-Luttinger theorem as fundamental principles linking different aspects of isovector strong interactions.

## Key numbers

- The neutron-proton effective mass splitting at nuclear matter saturation density ρ₀ is proportional to the isospin asymmetry δ, with the linear splitting function s(ρ) defined such that m*ₙ₋ₚ ≈ s(ρ)δ
- The isoscalar effective mass M*ₛ/M = (1 + κₛ)⁻¹ and isovector effective mass M*ᵥ/M = (1 + κᵥ)⁻¹, where κₛ = (M/8ℏ²)ρΘₛ and κᵥ = (M/4ℏ²)ρΘᵥ in terms of Skyrme parameters
- The Landau Fermi-liquid theory relates isoscalar and isovector effective masses to Landau parameters via M*ₛ/M = 1 + F₁/3 and M*ᵥ/M = 1 + F′₁/3, with stability requiring F₁ > -3 and F′₁ > -3
- The total effective mass M*ⱼ/M = 1 - S₁ from the low-energy optical model dispersion relation E = k²/2M + U(E) with U(E) = S₀ + S₁E

## Context

This review extends earlier work by some of the same authors (LCK, 2008; LiChen, 2015; NST, 2016) that reviewed predictions from many-body theories about nucleon effective masses about 10 years ago. The paper connects to the broader effort to understand the nuclear symmetry energy and its density dependence, with implications for neutron star properties (including the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation), neutrino opacities as studied by Burrows (2006), transport properties and liquid-gas phase transitions in neutron-rich matter as studied by Xu (2015), and isospin-sensitive observables in heavy-ion collisions. The review identifies remaining key issues requiring further investigation in the era of high-precision experiments using advanced rare isotope beams.
