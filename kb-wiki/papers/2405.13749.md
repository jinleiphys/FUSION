# History of N(1680)

> This paper describes my personal appreciation for some of the great research achievements of Mitya Diakonov, Vitya Petrov, and Maxim Polyakov and how my own research career has followed the paths they opened. Among the topics where they have been the most influential have been the pursuit and study of the exotic pentaquark. The search for exotics may require a complementary approach, such as experimental and theoretical activity. Here, I would like to focus on a story of $N(1680)$ in which I was involved.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the non-strange unitary partner of the exotic pentaquark \(\Theta^+\), originally claimed as \(N(1710)\) by Diakonov, Petrov, and Polyakov (2004), may instead be a narrow state near \(1680\ \mathrm{MeV}\) that has eluded detection because standard partial-wave analyses (PWA) miss resonances with width \(\Gamma < 20\ \mathrm{MeV}\) by construction, and because the \(\pi N\) elastic data above the Roper resonance are dominated by diffraction, making the \(P_{11}\) amplitude poorly defined.

## Method

The author and collaborators used a modified partial-wave analysis (PWA) that inserts a Breit-Wigner structure into one partial amplitude and refits the full database, scanning over resonance mass \(M_R\), total width \(\Gamma_R\), and partial width to find changes in \(\chi^2\). This approach was applied to the existing \(KN\) database (Hyslop 1992) to search for \(\Theta^+\) in the \(P_{01}\) wave, and to the \(\pi N\) database (Arndt 2003) to search for a narrow \(N^*\) in the \(P_{11}\) wave. Later, high-statistics \(\pi^\pm p\) elastic scattering data from the EPECUR Collaboration at ITEP (2014) were analyzed to look for narrow structures near \(1680\ \mathrm{MeV}\).

## Key numbers

- The predicted \(\Theta^+\) mass from the Chiral Quark Soliton model (Diakonov 1997) is \(M_{\Theta^+} = 1.53\ \mathrm{GeV}\) with a width less than \(15\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
- The modified PWA of \(KN\) data (Arndt 2003) found that a \(\Theta^+\) in the \(P_{01}\) state with mass \(\sim 1545\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and width \(\Gamma(\Theta^+) \leq 0.5\ \mathrm{MeV}\) is possible.
- For the \(\pi N\) \(P_{11}\) wave, negative \(\Delta\chi^2\) (indicating possible resonance) appears near \(M_R = 1680\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and \(1730\ \mathrm{MeV}\), but only for \(\Gamma_{el} \le 0.5\ \mathrm{MeV}\) at \(1680\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and \(\Gamma_{el} \le 0.3\ \mathrm{MeV}\) at \(1730\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (Arndt 2003).
- The Chiral Quark Soliton model with SU(3)\(_F\) violation predicts for \(N(1680)\): \(\Gamma(N^* \to \pi\Delta) \approx 2.8\ \mathrm{MeV}\), \(\Gamma(N^* \to K\Lambda) \approx 0.70\ \mathrm{MeV}\), \(\Gamma(N^* \to \eta N) \approx 2\ \mathrm{MeV}\), \(\Gamma(N^* \to \pi N) \approx 2.1\ \mathrm{MeV}\), and total width \(\Gamma(N^* \to \mathrm{tot}) \approx 10\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
- The GRAAL Collaboration (2004) claimed evidence for a narrow (\(10\ \mathrm{MeV}\)) resonance at mass \(1675\ \mathrm{MeV}\) in the reaction \(\gamma n \to \eta n\).
- The EPECUR Collaboration (2014) measured \(\pi^\pm p\) elastic scattering from \(p = 800\) to \(1300\ \mathrm{MeV}/c\) (\(W = 1.55\) to \(1.83\ \mathrm{GeV}\)) with momentum resolution \(\sim 1\ \mathrm{MeV}/c\) and \(\sim 1\%\) statistical uncertainties, producing about 10,000 new data points.
- Analysis of EPECUR data (2016) found two narrow structures in elastic \(\pi^- p\) scattering: \(S_{11}(1686)\) and \(P_{11}(1720)\).

## Context

This work extends the search for exotic pentaquark partners predicted by the Chiral Quark Soliton model of Diakonov, Petrov, and Polyakov (1997), following the first experimental claims of \(\Theta^+\) by LEPS (2003) and DIANA (2003). It contradicts the earlier assignment of \(N(1710)\) as the non-strange member of the anti-decuplet (Polyakov 2004), which was not seen in standard \(\pi N\) PWA by the SAID group (Arndt 2003). The results enable future searches for narrow \(N^*\) states, particularly through the proposed J-PARC experiment P102 (Kohri 2024) measuring \(\pi^- p \to \eta n\) cross sections from \(p = 850\) to \(1200\ \mathrm{MeV}/c\) with a CsI(Tl) calorimeter.
