# Relativistic Quark-Antiquark Bound State Problem with Spin-dependent Interactions in Momentum Space

> The work described in this paper is the first step toward a relativistic three-quark bound-state calculation using a Hamiltonian consistent with the Wigner-Bargmann theorem and macroscopic locality. We give an explicit demonstration that we can solve the two-body problem in momentum space with spin-dependent interactions. The form of the potential is a combination of linear+Coulomb+spin-spin+% spin-orbit+tensor, which includes confinement and is of the general form consistent with rotation, space-reflection and time-reversal invariance. Comparison is made with previous calculations using an alternate technique and with the experimental meson mass spectrum. The results obtained suggest that the model is realistic enough to provide a two-body basis for the three quark baryon problem in which the Poincaré group representation is unitary and cluster separability is respected.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a relativistic two-body (quark-antiquark) bound-state problem with spin-dependent interactions (linear+Coulomb+spin-spin+spin-orbit+tensor) can be solved numerically in momentum space using a collocation method with cubic Hermite splines, and that the resulting meson mass spectrum can be fit to experimental data with an average deviation of 51 MeV across fifteen states. The hedge is that this is presented as a first step toward a three-quark calculation, and the model's parameters are freely varied phenomenologically rather than derived from first principles.

## Method

The authors solve a relativistic wave equation in momentum space for a quark-antiquark system, using a Hamiltonian consisting of a relativistic kinetic energy operator (sum of square roots of mass-squared plus momentum-squared) and a potential that includes a linear confining term plus a short-range spin-dependent interaction (Coulomb, spin-spin, spin-orbit, and tensor terms). The potential is expressed in momentum space with a Gaussian form factor at the quark-gluon vertex to regularize singularities, and each term is multiplied by an adjustable strength parameter. The wave function is expanded in cubic Hermite splines, and the equation is converted to a matrix eigenvalue problem via the collocation method, with eigenvalues giving meson masses.

## Key numbers

- Quark mass used: M = 360 MeV (for comparison with Carlson et al.) and M = 258 MeV (for the full seven-parameter fit).
- String tension: sqrt(sigma) = 0.197 (GeV)^2.
- For the comparison calculation: f_c * alpha_s = -0.5, constant energy offset M_q\bar{q} = 750 MeV.
- For the two-parameter fit: Lambda = 0.6426 GeV^{-1}, C_LS = 0.3236, constant energy offset M_q\bar{q} = 738.24 MeV; average deviation from fifteen experimental values is 69 MeV.
- For the full seven-parameter fit: M = 258 MeV, Lambda = 0.645 GeV^{-1}, C_L = 0.6704, C_C = 3.3824, C_SS = 0.155, C_LS = 0.0448, C_T = 0.1868; average deviation from eleven states is 28 MeV, and from all fifteen experimental values is 51 MeV.
- The authors' results are consistently 25–60 MeV higher than those of Carlson et al. (1991) for the same parameters, but cross-checked to agree within <0.5 MeV with an independent method.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic potential model approach to mesons by solving the two-body problem directly in momentum space with full spin-dependent interactions, which had previously been limited to spin-independent calculations (Jean et al., 1993; Maung et al., 1993). It provides a two-body wave function basis for solving the relativistic Faddeev equations for three-quark baryons, using the Bakamjian-Thomas construction to ensure consistency with the Wigner-Bargmann theorem and cluster separability (Keister, 1994). The results improve upon the variational coordinate-space calculations of Carlson et al. (1991) and the model of Stanley and Robson (1980), and the authors note that the momentum-space wave functions have power-law tails that may improve form factor calculations at high energy compared to harmonic oscillator basis models (Godfrey and Isgur, 1985).
