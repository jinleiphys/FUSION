# Neutrino energy reconstruction problems and neutrino oscillations

> We discuss the accuracy of the usual procedure for neutrino energy reconstruction which is based on the quasielastic kinematics. Our results are described in terms of a probability distribution for a real neutrino energy value. Several factors are responsible of the deviations from the reconstructed value. The main one is the multinucleon component of the neutrino interaction which in the case of Cherenkov detectors enters as a quasielastic cross section, increasing the mean neutrino energy which can differ appreciably from the reconstructed value. As an application we derive, for excess electron events attributed to the conversion of muon neutrinos, the true neutrino energy distribution based on the experimental one which is given in terms of the reconstructed value. The result is a reshaping effect. For MiniBooNE the low energy peak is suppressed and shifted at higher energies, which may influence the interpretation in terms of oscillation. For T2K at the Super Kamiokande far detector the reshaping translates into a narrowing of the energy distribution.

## Key claim

The standard neutrino energy reconstruction procedure, which assumes quasielastic kinematics on a nucleon at rest, systematically misestimates the true neutrino energy because multinucleon (np-nh) interactions produce a high-energy tail in the response that is misidentified as quasielastic; this reshaping effect can significantly alter the interpretation of oscillation signals in MiniBooNE and T2K.

## Method

The authors computed the probability distribution of the true neutrino energy for a given reconstructed energy by evaluating the double differential charged-current cross section on carbon along hyperbolas in the (energy transfer, momentum transfer) plane, using a nuclear response model that includes one-particle-one-hole (1p-1h) contributions treated in the random phase approximation (RPA) and multinucleon (np-nh) contributions. They then applied this smearing function to the experimental excess event distributions from MiniBooNE and T2K to obtain the true neutrino energy distributions.

## Key numbers

- For MiniBooNE with reconstructed energy \(\overline{E_\nu}=300\) MeV, the average true neutrino energy including np-nh is 546 MeV, compared to 335 MeV without np-nh.
- For T2K near detector with \(\overline{E_\nu}=300\) MeV, the average true neutrino energy including np-nh is 514 MeV, compared to 350 MeV without np-nh.
- For MiniBooNE with \(\overline{E_\nu}=600\) MeV, the average true neutrino energy including np-nh is 711 MeV; for \(\overline{E_\nu}=1000\) MeV it is 1024 MeV.
- For T2K near detector with \(\overline{E_\nu}=600\) MeV, the average true neutrino energy including np-nh is 638 MeV; for \(\overline{E_\nu}=1000\) MeV it is 937 MeV.
- The T2K far detector electron neutrino flux at Super Kamiokande was evaluated using \(|\Delta m_{32}^2| = 2.4 \times 10^{-3}\) eV\(^2\) and baseline \(L=295\) km.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior nuclear response model (Martini 2009, 2010, 2011) that successfully reproduced MiniBooNE double differential cross section data, and contrasts with earlier studies by Benhar and Meloni (2009) on spectral function effects and by Leitner and Mosel (2010) on pion absorption via transport equations. The results enable a more accurate conversion of reconstructed-energy event distributions into true-energy distributions for oscillation analyses, and suggest that the low-energy excess in MiniBooNE may be suppressed and shifted to higher energies when multinucleon contributions are included.
