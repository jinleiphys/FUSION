# Shadow poles in Brune parametrization of R-matrix theory

> Our collective knowledge of nuclear cross sections is recorded as resonance parameters in nuclear data libraries. To evaluate these parameters, campaigns of measurements are fitted with a parametric model of nuclear cross sections called R-matrix theory. In order to remove the arbitrary boundary parameters in the Wigner-Eisenbud R-matrix parametrization, the community is considering converting all nuclear data libraries to Brune parameters. In this article, we show there are more Brune parameters than previously thought. Below the channel threshold, we prove there exists two types of additional 'shadow poles' - branch shadow poles and analytic shadow poles - depending on how we continue R-matrix operators to complex wavenumbers (to do so we establish Mittag-Leffler expansions of R-matrix operators). This entails there are more Brune resonance energies than levels. Yet, we also prove that choosing any subset of Brune poles will yield the same cross sections than using the entire set of Brune poles, as long as it has at least the number of levels. In practice, this means that shadow Brune poles can be discarded from the new nuclear data libraries. Many isotopes are evaluated with the Reich-Moore approximation, introducing complex resonance energies to eliminate certain channels. We generalize Brune's parameterization to encompass the Reich-Moore approximation and the additional shadow poles, and show that all Brune parameters depend on what convention we choose to continue the R-matrix operators to complex wavenumbers. To convert nuclear data libraries to Brune parameters, the nuclear scientists community must thus first decide on such a convention. The authors argue in favor of analytic continuation. The first evidence of shadow poles in Brune's alternative parametrization of R-matrix theory is observed in isotope xenon-134, spin-parity group 1/2(-).

## Key claim

The paper proves that Brune's alternative parametrization of R-matrix theory contains more resonance poles than previously thought, specifically "shadow poles" below channel thresholds, but that any subset of Brune poles with at least the number of levels yields the same cross sections as the full set, meaning shadow poles can be discarded from nuclear data libraries in practice.

## Method

The authors establish Mittag-Leffler expansions of the reduced logarithmic derivative of the outgoing wave operator \(L_c(\rho_c)\) and use these to analyze the analytic continuation of R-matrix operators to complex wavenumbers. They distinguish two types of shadow poles, branch shadow poles and analytic shadow poles, depending on whether one uses the legacy Lane and Thomas definition or the analytic continuation definition for the shift and penetration functions. The analysis is generalized to the Reich-Moore approximation, and the first evidence of shadow poles is demonstrated in the isotope xenon-134 (\(^{134}\mathrm{Xe}\)) for the spin-parity group \(J^\pi = 1/2^{(-)}\).

## Key numbers

- The Mittag-Leffler expansion of \(L_c(\rho)/\rho\) is given as \(-\ell/\rho + \mathrm{i} + \sum_{n \geq 1} 1/(\rho - \omega_n)\), where \(\{\omega_n\}\) are the roots of the outgoing wavefunctions \(O_c(\rho)\).
- For neutral particles with angular momentum \(\ell = 0\) (s-wave), there are no roots: \(\{\omega_n^{\ell=0}\} = \{\emptyset\}\).
- For neutral particles with \(\ell = 1\) (p-wave), there is one root: \(\omega_1^{\ell=1} = -\mathrm{i}\).
- For neutral particles with \(\ell = 2\) (d-wave), there are two roots: \(\omega_{1,2}^{\ell=2} = \frac{1}{2}(-\sqrt{3} - 3\mathrm{i})\) and \(\frac{1}{2}(\sqrt{3} - 3\mathrm{i})\).
- For neutral particles with \(\ell = 3\) (f-wave), there are three roots, with \(\omega_1^{\ell=3} \approx -2.32219\mathrm{i}\) and \(\omega_{2,3}^{\ell=3} \approx \pm 1.75438 - 1.83891\mathrm{i}\).
- For neutral particles with \(\ell = 4\) (g-wave), there are four roots, with \(\omega_{1,2}^{\ell=4} \approx \pm 2.65742 - 2.10379\mathrm{i}\) and \(\omega_{3,4}^{\ell=4} \approx \pm 0.867234 - 2.89621\mathrm{i}\).
- The first evidence of shadow poles is observed in isotope \(^{134}\mathrm{Xe}\) spin-parity group \(J^\pi = 1/2^{(-)}\).

## Context

This work extends the alternative parametrization of R-matrix theory introduced by Brune (2002), which was designed to eliminate the arbitrary boundary condition parameters \(B_c\) present in the Wigner-Eisenbud parametrization (Wigner and Eisenbud, 1947; Bloch, 1957; Lane and Thomas, 1958). The discovery of shadow poles below threshold energies means that converting nuclear data libraries (ENDF, JEFF, JENDL, BROND, CENDL, TENDL) to Brune parameters requires the community to first agree on a convention for continuing R-matrix operators to complex wavenumbers; the authors argue in favor of analytic continuation in a follow-up article. The results enable the practical conversion of existing nuclear data libraries to Brune parameters while discarding shadow poles, and generalize the formalism to the Reich-Moore approximation used for many isotopes.
