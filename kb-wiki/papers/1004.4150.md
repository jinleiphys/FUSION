# Charged-Particle Thermonuclear Reaction Rates: IV. Comparison to Previous Work

> We compare our Monte Carlo reaction rates (see Paper II of this series) to previous results that were obtained by using the classical method of computing thermonuclear reaction rates. For each reaction, the comparison is presented using two types of graphs: the first shows the change in reaction rate uncertainties, while the second displays our new results normalized to the previously recommended reaction rate. We find that the rates have changed significantly for almost all reactions considered here. The changes are caused by (i) our new Monte Carlo method of computing reaction rates (see Paper I of this series), and (ii) newly available nuclear physics information (see Paper III of this series).

## Key claim

The Monte Carlo reaction rates presented in Paper II of this series differ substantially from previous classical rates for almost all reactions considered, but the authors caution that the two types of rates have qualitatively different statistical meanings and should not be compared as "apples with oranges."

## Method

The authors compared their new Monte Carlo reaction rates (Paper II) to previous classical reaction rates for each reaction using two types of graphs: one showing the change in reaction rate uncertainties and another displaying the new results normalized to the previously recommended rate. Previous results were adopted from the latest references that list recommended reaction rates in tabular form, primarily Angulo et al. (1999) and Iliadis et al. (2001), with numerical values obtained directly from original authors when only graphical results were available.

## Context

This paper (Paper IV) is the final installment of a series that introduces a Monte Carlo method for computing thermonuclear reaction rates (Paper I) and presents the resulting rates (Paper II) using newly available nuclear physics information (Paper III). It extends the work of Angulo et al. (1999) and Iliadis et al. (2001) by providing updated rates with statistically meaningful uncertainties, enabling stellar modelers to assess which reaction rates require updating in their simulations. The comparison graphs allow users to determine how much a particular rate has changed and whether previous classical rates were accurate, though the authors emphasize that the two methods produce qualitatively different results that cannot be directly compared in a straightforward statistical sense.
