# Non-empirical pairing energy functional in nuclear matter and finite nuclei

> We study 1S0 pairing gaps in neutron and nuclear matter as well as in finite nuclei on the basis of microscopic two-nucleon interactions. Special attention is paid to the consistency of the pairing interaction and normal self-energy contributions. We find that pairing gaps obtained from low-momentum interactions depend only weakly on approximation schemes for the normal self-energy, required in present energy-density functional calculations, while pairing gaps from hard potentials are very sensitive to the effective-mass approximation scheme.

## Key claim

Pairing gaps obtained from low-momentum interactions depend only weakly on approximation schemes for the normal self-energy, while pairing gaps from hard potentials are very sensitive to the effective-mass approximation scheme, even when both types of interaction reproduce the same low-energy scattering phase shifts.

## Method

The authors solved the gap equation for \(^1\)S\(_0\) neutron pairing in pure neutron matter and symmetric nuclear matter at lowest order in two different many-body expansion schemes: Hartree-Fock for soft low-momentum interactions (cutoff \(\Lambda_{\text{soft}} = 1.8\,\text{fm}^{-1}\)) and Brueckner-Hartree-Fock for hard interactions (cutoff \(\Lambda_{\text{hard}} = 6.0\,\text{fm}^{-1}\)), both derived from the Argonne \(v_{18}\) potential via renormalization group evolution. They compared the full momentum-dependent treatment of the normal self-energy with two momentum-independent approximations (point-evaluation at the Fermi surface and a weighted average over the Fermi surface) to assess the impact on the pairing gaps.

- For soft interactions in pure neutron matter, pairing gaps obtained with the full Hartree-Fock spectrum, the point-evaluated effective mass, and the averaged effective mass are essentially indistinguishable (Fig. 3, upper panel).
- For hard interactions in symmetric nuclear matter, the point-evaluated and averaged effective masses differ substantially; the total BHF effective mass at \(\kf = 1.2\,\text{fm}^{-1}\) shows a clear separation between the two averaging schemes (Fig. 2, lower panel).
- Pairing gaps from hard interactions in symmetric nuclear matter depend strongly on the averaging scheme for both the \(k\)-mass-only case (class \(\Lambda_{\text{hard}}^1\)) and the full case including \(e\)-mass and \(Z\)-factor (class \(\Lambda_{\text{hard}}^Z\)), as shown in Fig. 4.
- The microscopic effective mass from soft interactions in symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density \(\kf = 1.35\,\text{fm}^{-1}\) is well reproduced by the SLy4 Skyrme functional, but in pure neutron matter the SLy4 effective mass underestimates the microscopic predictions significantly (Fig. 2, upper panel).

## Context

This work extends the program initiated by Duguet et al. (2007) and Lesinski et al. (2008) to construct non-empirical energy-density functionals from low-momentum interactions, and contrasts with the results of Barranco et al. (2004) and Pastore et al. (2008) who used the hard Argonne \(v_{14}\) potential and found that two thirds of the observed pairing gap in \(^{120}\)Sn could be accounted for only after adding induced interactions and self-energy effects. By demonstrating that the sensitivity to effective-mass approximations originates from the intrinsic resolution scale of the interaction, the paper clarifies the origin of the quantitative mismatch between these two sets of finite-nucleus calculations and establishes that low-momentum interactions provide a more controlled starting point for consistent lowest-order pairing calculations.
