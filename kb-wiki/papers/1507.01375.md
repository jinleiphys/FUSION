# Reply to comment on "Searching for Topological Defect Dark Matter via Nongravitational Signatures"

> In the comment of Avelino, Sousa and Lobo [arXiv:1506.06028], it is argued, by comparing the kinetic energy of a topological defect with the overall energy of a pulsar, that the origin of the pulsar glitch phenomenon due to the passage of networks of topological defects through pulsars is faced with serious difficulties. Here, we point out that topological defects may trigger pulsar glitches within traditional scenarios, such as vortex unpinning. If the energy transfer from a topological defect exceeds the activation energy for a single pinned vortex, this may lead to an avalanche of unpinning of vortices and consequently a pulsar glitch, and therefore the source of angular momentum and energy required for a glitch event is provided by the pulsar itself. Indeed, the activation energy for such a process can be very small (essentially zero compared with the observed increase in the pulsar's rotational kinetic energy at the onset of a glitch). The unpinning of a vortex by a topological defect may occur through the passage of the defect into the core of the pulsar.

## Key claim

Topological defects may trigger pulsar glitches within traditional vortex-unpinning scenarios, where the defect provides only a trigger and the pulsar itself supplies the required angular momentum and energy, contrary to the argument in Avelino, Sousa and Lobo (2015) that the defect's kinetic energy is insufficient compared to the pulsar's total energy.

## Method

The authors analyze the energy requirements for a topological defect to unpin a single vortex in a pulsar's superfluid core, comparing the pinning energy of a vortex (approximately 1 MeV) to the rotational kinetic energy of a pulsar (10^56 to 10^64 eV). They consider two interaction regimes: attractive interaction, where the defect can directly penetrate the pulsar core, and repulsive interaction, where a domain wall may be stretched by the pulsar until elastic forces overcome repulsion and allow penetration.

## Key numbers

- Pinning energy for a single vortex: E_pin ~ 1 MeV
- Rotational kinetic energy of a pulsar: E_rot ~ 10^56 - 10^64 eV
- Typical pulsar glitch magnitude: delta Omega / Omega ~ 10^-11 to 10^-5
- Glitch recovery timescale: T_d ~ 1 day to 3 years
- Glitch onset temporal interval: at least ~ 30 s

## Context

This reply addresses the comment by Avelino, Sousa and Lobo (2015), who argued that topological defect kinetic energy is insufficient to cause pulsar glitches. The authors extend their original proposal in Stadnik and Flambaum (2014) by showing that defects can act as triggers within the established vortex-unpinning model (reviewed in Haskell 2015), rather than needing to supply the glitch energy directly. This work enables future detailed numerical simulations of defect-pulsar interactions and suggests correlated searches using pulsar networks, binary pulsar systems, atomic clock networks (Derevianko 2014), and laser interferometers (Stadnik and Flambaum 2015).
