# Observation of string-breaking dynamics in a quantum simulator

> Spontaneous particle-pair formation is a fundamental phenomenon in nature. It can, for example, appear when the potential energy between two particles increases with separation, as if they were connected by a tense string. Beyond a critical separation, new particle pairs can form, causing the string to break. String-breaking dynamics in quantum chromodynamics play a vital role in high-energy particle collisions and early universe evolution. Simulating string evolution and hadron formation is, therefore, a grand challenge in modern physics. Quantum simulators, well-suited for studying dynamics, are expected to outperform classical computing methods. However, the required experimental capabilities to simulate string-breaking dynamics have not yet been demonstrated, even for simpler models of the strong force. We experimentally probe, for the first time, the spatiotemporal dynamics of string-breaking in a (1+1)-dimensional $\mathbb{Z}_2$ lattice gauge theory using a fully programmable trapped-ion quantum simulator. We emulate external static charges and strings via site-dependent magnetic-field control enabled by a dual array of tightly focused laser beams targeting individual ions. First, we study how confinement affects isolated charges, finding that they freely spread without string tension but exhibit localized oscillations when tension is increased. Then, we observe and characterize string-breaking dynamics of a string stretched between two static charges after an abrupt increase in string tension. Charge pairs appear near the string edges and spread into the bulk, revealing a route to dynamical string-breaking distinct from the conventional Schwinger mechanism. Our work demonstrates that analog quantum simulators have achieved the necessary control to explore string-breaking dynamics, which may ultimately be relevant to nuclear and high-energy physics.

## Key claim

The authors experimentally observe string-breaking dynamics in a (1+1)-dimensional Z2 lattice gauge theory using a trapped-ion quantum simulator, but the observed mechanism differs from the conventional Schwinger mechanism: charge pairs form systematically at the string edges rather than uniformly in the bulk, and the characteristic time scale does not show the exponential sensitivity to Hamiltonian parameters expected from the Schwinger mechanism.

## Method

The authors map a (1+1)-dimensional Z2 lattice gauge theory onto a transverse-field Ising model with long-range interactions, implemented using a linear array of 13 Yb-171 ions. They use a dual array of tightly focused laser beams to achieve site-dependent magnetic-field control, enabling emulation of virtual static environments that create isolated charges or strings; a rapid quench of Hamiltonian parameters then drives non-equilibrium dynamics, which is observed with spatiotemporal resolution through charge density and electric field measurements.

## Key numbers

- System size: L = 13 spins (Yb-171 ions)
- Ising interaction decay exponent: beta ≈ 0.78 (exponentially decaying with distance, J_{i,j} = J e^{-beta(|i-j|-1)})
- Maximum charge propagation velocity: v_max = 2g (observed in the absence of string tension, h=0)
- Coherent oscillation amplitude: 2g/h (predicted by single-charge approximation)
- Coherent oscillation period: pi/h (in units of inverse J, predicted by single-charge approximation)
- Experimental measurements averaged over 300 repetitions per data point

## Context

This work extends prior theoretical studies of confinement and string dynamics in lattice gauge theories, including the Schwinger mechanism (Schwinger, 1951) and earlier theoretical predictions for resonant string breaking by Verdel et al. (2019) and Verdel et al. (2023). It builds on previous trapped-ion experiments demonstrating charge confinement (Martinez et al., 2016; Nguyen et al., 2022) and surpasses them by achieving the site-dependent control and programmability needed to observe spatiotemporally resolved string-breaking dynamics. The results enable future studies of string fragmentation and hadronization in quantum chromodynamics, and the virtual-static-region strategy can be adapted to other quantum simulation platforms such as two-dimensional ion crystals or neutral-atom arrays.
