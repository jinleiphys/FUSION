# Cross-checking the geometric effects in heavy-ion collisions at 1 GeV/nucleon

> Employing the isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck transport model, the 1 GeV/nucleon deformed uranium-uranium ultra-central collisions are simulated. Based on sensitive observables, mean square collective flow and pion meson multiplicity, the impacts of high-momentum tails caused by short-range correlations and the symmetry energy in high-density regions on geometric effects are discussed under different reaction orientations. Finally, the neural network model for identifying reaction orientations is also developed.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that geometric effects in deformed uranium-uranium collisions at 1 GeV/nucleon are robust against variations in symmetry energy and short-range correlation-induced high-momentum tails, but the latter can cause approximately 20% changes in mean square elliptic flow for body-body collisions, and the symmetry energy sensitivity is best probed via the neutron-to-proton elliptic flow ratio rather than the absolute flow values.

## Method

The authors employed the isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (IBUU) transport model to simulate ultra-central (impact parameter ≤ 1 fm) collisions of deformed ²³⁸U nuclei at 1 GeV/nucleon, with initial nucleon densities from relativistic mean-field plus BCS calculations (quadrupole deformation β = 0.287). They varied the nucleon momentum initialization among three forms: a Fermi gas model (IMD1), Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations with Av18 + three-body forces (IMD2), and an np-dominant short-range correlation picture (IMD3), while also varying the symmetry energy stiffness via parameter X from 0 (stiff) to 3 (soft). Six discrete reaction orientation configurations (body-body through tip-tip) were defined by Euler angles, and a convolutional neural network (COF) was trained on 10,000 simulated events to identify both θ and φ orientations from observables including anisotropic flows, pion multiplicities, and particle distributions.

## Key numbers

- Quadrupole deformation of ²³⁸U: β = 0.287 from RMF+BCS, consistent with experimental value β = 0.289
- Mean square elliptic flow ⟨v₂²⟩ for body-body collisions (case A): 27.585 × 10⁻⁵ (IMD1), 23.394 × 10⁻⁵ (IMD2), 21.716 × 10⁻⁵ (IMD3), approximately 20% reduction from HMTs
- Deformation-induced flow f(β) = ⟨v₂²⟩_{β=0.287} − ⟨v₂²⟩_{β=0}: 25.890 × 10⁻⁵ (IMD1), 21.620 × 10⁻⁵ (IMD2), 20.029 × 10⁻⁵ (IMD3)
- π⁻/π⁺ double ratio DR = (π⁻/π⁺)_{case Z} / (π⁻/π⁺)_{case A}: 1.035 (X=0), 1.042 (X=1), 1.076 (X=2), 1.123 (X=3), approximately 10% variation from stiff to soft symmetry energy
- Overall COF prediction accuracy for θ-orientation: 85.2% on training set, 81.0% on validation set
- Reaction time for tip-tip orientations is approximately 15% longer than for body-body orientations
- High-momentum nucleons (k > 1.52 fm⁻¹, corresponding to 300 MeV/c) account for 18% of nucleons in IMD2 and IMD3

## Context

This work extends the line of research on using heavy-ion collisions to probe nuclear deformation, building on the discovery by Zhang (2022) and Jia (2022) that mean square anisotropic flow in ultra-central collisions at relativistic energies has a robust linear response to initial deformation. It also follows the prior work by Yang (2024) which demonstrated that convolutional neural networks can identify reaction orientations in deformed heavy-ion collisions. The study systematically examines how two key nuclear physics inputs, the symmetry energy (which remains uncertain at suprasaturation densities per constraints from Li (2013) and others) and short-range correlations (where np pairs dominate by a factor of approximately 18 over pp/nn pairs per Subedi (2008)), affect the geometric signals, finding that the neutron-to-proton elliptic flow ratio under body-body collisions is the most promising probe for symmetry energy. The results enable future experimental analyses at facilities such as FRIB and RIBF, where the COF network could be applied to identify orientations and extract deformation and symmetry energy information from measured observables.
