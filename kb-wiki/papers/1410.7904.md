# Basic phenomenology for relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
This review presents the basic phenomenological concepts used to interpret soft-hadronic data from relativistic heavy-ion collisions at an elementary level, with the caveat that the quark-gluon plasma produced in current experiments likely does not correspond to the asymptotic state predicted by asymptotic freedom, but rather a strongly coupled fluid-like state whose properties are inferred from experimental and theoretical investigations at currently available energies.

## Method
The paper reviews the standard theoretical framework for soft-hadronic observables in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, covering: the Glauber model for initial energy density distributions using Woods-Saxon nuclear density profiles and nucleon-nucleon cross sections; Fourier decomposition of azimuthal momentum distributions into harmonic flow coefficients (directed, elliptic, triangular); relativistic hydrodynamics (perfect-fluid and viscous) for space-time evolution; and the Hagedorn statistical bootstrap model for the hadron mass spectrum. The review connects these phenomenological tools to experimental data from AGS, SPS, RHIC, and LHC.

## Key numbers
- Total charged particle multiplicity in central Au-Au collisions at RHIC at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV is about 5000
- Maximum RHIC energy: 200 GeV per nucleon pair
- LHC Pb-Pb energy: sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV (first run Nov.-Dec. 2010)
- Woods-Saxon parameters: r_0 = (1.12 A^{1/3} - 0.86 A^{-1/3}) fm, a = 0.54 fm, rho_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3}
- Upper limit on shear viscosity to entropy density ratio: 1 <= 4pi eta/s <= 3
- Diffractive processes contribute to about 10% of all inelastic collisions
- AGS Si beams at 14 GeV per nucleon; SPS O beams at 60 and 200 GeV per nucleon (1986), S beams at 200 GeV per nucleon (1987), Pb beams at 158 GeV per nucleon (1995)
- Au beams at BNL at 11 GeV per nucleon (1992)

## Context
This review extends the pedagogical tradition of textbooks on heavy-ion collisions and quark-gluon plasma by authors such as Csernai (1994), Wong (1995), Letessier (2002), Yagi (2005), and Florkowski (2010). It provides the conceptual foundation for understanding the transition from the Hagedorn limiting temperature concept (Hagedorn 1965) to modern hydrodynamic descriptions of the quark-gluon plasma as a nearly perfect fluid (Gyulassy 2004, Shuryak 2004). The work enables students and newcomers to connect the Glauber model (Bialas 1976) with event-by-event hydrodynamic calculations that extract elliptic and triangular flow coefficients (Alver 2010) to determine the shear viscosity of the quark-gluon plasma.
