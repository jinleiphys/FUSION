# Effects of symmetry breaking in finite quantum systems

> The review considers the peculiarities of symmetry breaking and symmetry transformations and the related physical effects in finite quantum systems. Some types of symmetry in finite systems can be broken only asymptotically. However, with a sufficiently large number of particles, crossover transitions become sharp, so that symmetry breaking happens similarly to that in macroscopic systems. This concerns, in particular, global gauge symmetry breaking, related to Bose-Einstein condensation and superconductivity, or isotropy breaking, related to the generation of quantum vortices, and the stratification in multicomponent mixtures. A special type of symmetry transformation, characteristic only for finite systems, is the change of shape symmetry. These phenomena are illustrated by the examples of several typical mesoscopic systems, such as trapped atoms, quantum dots, atomic nuclei, and metallic grains. The specific features of the review are: (i) the emphasis on the peculiarities of the symmetry breaking in finite mesoscopic systems; (ii) the analysis of common properties of physically different finite quantum systems; (iii) the manifestations of symmetry breaking in the spectra of collective excitations in finite quantum systems. The analysis of these features allows for the better understanding of the intimate relation between the type of symmetry and other physical properties of quantum systems. This also makes it possible to predict new effects by employing the analogies between finite quantum systems of different physical nature.

# Effects of symmetry breaking in finite quantum systems

## Key claim

While symmetry breaking can be rigorously defined only in the thermodynamic limit of macroscopic systems, mesoscopic systems with a sufficiently large but finite number of particles exhibit crossover transitions that become sharp enough to imitate true phase transitions and asymptotic symmetry breaking. The review argues that despite fundamental differences from bulk matter, finite quantum systems such as trapped atoms, quantum dots, atomic nuclei, and metallic grains share common symmetry-breaking phenomena that can be understood through analogous mathematical treatments.

## Method

The authors present a comprehensive review comparing symmetry-breaking phenomena across four classes of finite quantum systems: trapped atoms, quantum dots, atomic nuclei, and metallic grains. For each system class, they analyze the role of global gauge symmetry breaking (related to Bose-Einstein condensation and superconductivity), isotropy breaking (related to quantum vortex generation and stratification in multicomponent mixtures), and geometric shape transformations. The analysis draws on multiple theoretical frameworks including Landau phase-transition theory, the Bogolubov method of quasiaverages, mean-field approximations, and the renormalization group approach to Ginzburg-Landau-Wilson functionals.

## Key numbers

- Systems of about 100 particles (or even 10 particles) already form approximate thermodynamic phases with related symmetries and demonstrate symmetry changes associated with phase transitions, as shown by computer simulations cited from Strandburg (1988)
- The number of particles in trapped atomic clouds ranges from just a few up to millions of atoms
- Quantum dots considered in the review contain between two and approximately ten electrons
- The effective thermodynamic limit is defined by the condition that for an extensive observable A_N, the ratio A_N/N approaches a constant as N approaches infinity

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of symmetry breaking from macroscopic statistical systems, as developed by Landau (1937) and Bogolubov (1967, 1970), to mesoscopic finite systems where the thermodynamic limit cannot be strictly achieved. The work draws connections between the Weiss mean-field theory of ferromagnetism (1907), the Kirkwood method for describing crystals (1965), and the Landau theory of phase transitions, applying these concepts to modern mesoscopic systems. The review enables cross-fertilization between different subfields: for example, the description of rotating Bose-Einstein condensates is shown to be analogous to that of rotating nuclei or quantum dots in magnetic fields, and pairing effects for trapped fermionic atoms are nearly identical to those in atomic nuclei or metallic grains.
