# Chiral magnetic effect in heavy ion collisions and beyond

> Chirality is a ubiquitous concept in modern science, from particle physics to biology. In quantum physics, chirality of fermions is linked to topology of gauge fields by the chiral anomaly. While the chiral anomaly is usually associated with the short-distance behavior in field theory, in recent years it has been realized that it also affects the macroscopic behavior of systems with chiral fermions. In particular, the local imbalance between left- and right-handed fermions in the presence of a magnetic field induces non-dissipative transport of electric charge ("the Chiral Magnetic Effect", CME). In heavy ion collisions, there is an ongoing search for this effect at Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider, with results from a dedicated isobar run presented very recently. An observation of CME in heavy ion collisions could shed light on the mechanism of baryon asymmetry generation in the Early Universe. Recently, the CME has been discovered in Dirac and Weyl semimetals possessing chiral quasi-particles. This observation opens a path towards quantum sensors, and potentially a new kind of quantum computers.

## Key claim

The Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) is a non-dissipative electric current induced by a chiral chemical potential in the presence of a magnetic field, but it requires the chiral chemical potential to be dynamically generated through the chiral anomaly rather than being a static equilibrium property; a constant chiral chemical potential cannot produce a steady CME current without violating energy conservation.

## Method

The paper derives the CME from the chiral anomaly equation using the Atiyah-Singer index theorem, relating the number of chiral zero modes of the Dirac operator to the topological index of gauge fields. For heavy ion collisions, the author describes how topological sphaleron transitions in hot QCD plasma create chirally imbalanced domains, and how the resulting CME current produces charge separation relative to the reaction plane that can be measured via the gamma correlator observable proposed by Voloshin (2004). The experimental search at RHIC used a dedicated isobar run comparing collisions of Ru-96 and Zr-96 nuclei, with 3.8 billion events recorded and a blind analysis performed.

## Key numbers

* Magnetic field in heavy ion collisions: eB ~ Lambda_QCD^2, where Lambda_QCD is the typical QCD scale
* CME current formula: J = (e^2 / 2 pi^2) * mu_5 * B, where mu_5 is the chiral chemical potential
* The observed gamma correlator ratio gamma_Ru / gamma_Zr was significantly below 1, contrary to expectations
* After correcting for multiplicity differences, the gamma_Ru / gamma_Zr ratio exceeds the inverse-multiplicity baseline by (1-4) sigma, depending on analysis details
* The isobar run recorded data from 3.8 billion collision events

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of the chiral anomaly from its original formulation by Adler (1969) and Bell and Jackiw (1969) to macroscopic quantum transport, building on the earlier work of Vilenkin (1980) who first proposed the CME relation but without accounting for the anomaly's role in making the current physical. The paper discusses the STAR Collaboration's isobar run results (2021) which found gamma_Ru / gamma_Zr < 1, contradicting simple CME expectations, but which after multiplicity corrections show marginal evidence for CME. The work enables future searches at lower collision energies near the QCD critical point, planned for the RHIC beam energy scan and future facilities like NICA and FAIR, and connects to condensed matter applications in Dirac and Weyl semimetals where CME has been firmly established through magnetotransport studies by Li (2014) and Ong.
