# Hadron resonance gas and mean-field nuclear matter for baryon number fluctuations

> I give an estimate for the skewness and the kurtosis of the baryon number distribution in two representative models; i.e., models of a hadron resonance gas and relativistic mean-field nuclear matter. I emphasize formal similarity between these two descriptions. The hadron resonance gas leads to a deviation from the Skellam distribution if quantum statistical correlation is taken into account at high baryon density, but this effect is not strong enough to explain fluctuation data seen in the beam-energy scan at RHIC/STAR. In the calculation of mean-field nuclear matter the density correlation with the vector $ω$-field rather than the effective mass with the scalar $σ$-field renders the kurtosis suppressed at higher baryon density so as to account for the experimentally observed behavior of the kurtosis. We finally discuss the difference between the baryon number and the proton number fluctuations from correlation effects in isospin space. The numerical results suggest that such effects are only minor even in the case of complete randomization of isospin.

# Hadron resonance gas and mean-field nuclear matter for baryon number fluctuations

## Key claim

The hadron resonance gas model shows a deviation from the Skellam distribution when quantum statistical correlation is included at high baryon density, but this effect is not strong enough to explain the fluctuation data from the RHIC/STAR beam-energy scan. In the relativistic mean-field model, the density correlation with the vector ω-field (rather than the effective mass from the scalar σ-field) suppresses the kurtosis at higher baryon density, which can account for the experimentally observed behavior.

## Method

The author computed the skewness (Sσ) and kurtosis (κσ²) of the baryon number distribution from the grand canonical partition function using two approaches: a hadron resonance gas model with the THERMUS2.3 particle data package, and a relativistic mean-field σ-ω model (Walecka model) with parameters reproducing nuclear matter saturation properties. The chemical freeze-out line was parametrized as a function of collision energy √s_NN using established relations from Cleymans (2005), and the sensitivity was quantified by varying μB by ±10%. The author also analyzed the separate contributions of the scalar σ-field (effective mass) and vector ω-field (renormalized chemical potential) to the fluctuation observables.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter: 0.17 nucleons/fm³, with binding energy per nucleon of 16.3 MeV, reproduced using model parameters m_N = 939 MeV, m_σ = 550 MeV, m_ω = 783 MeV, g_s = 10.3, g_ω = 12.7
- Chemical freeze-out parametrization: T(μB) = a − b μB² − c μB⁴ with a = 0.166 GeV, b = 0.139 GeV⁻¹, c = 0.053 GeV⁻³; μB(√s_NN) = d/(1 + e√s_NN) with d = 1.308 GeV, e = 0.273 GeV⁻¹
- Maximum baryon density along the freeze-out line occurs at √s_NN ≈ 8 GeV, coinciding with the triple-point-like region from Andronic (2009)
- The liquid-gas critical point of nuclear matter in the RMF setup is located at T ≈ 21 MeV and μB ≈ 906 MeV
- The ratio χ_p^(4)/χ_B^(4) deviates from 1/2 by less than 10% at small √s_NN, with the deviation attributed to violation of the Boltzmann approximation at the 10% level

## Context

This work extends the approach of Floerchinger (2012), who demonstrated that the chemical freeze-out condition at low T and high μB could be sensitive to nuclear matter properties. The author shows that the RMF model can reproduce not only the freeze-out condition but also the fluctuation observables, whereas the HRG model (which works well near the deconfinement crossover region) cannot account for the suppression seen in the BES/STAR data. The analysis contradicts the claim in Kitazawa (2012) that isospin correlations could explain the suppression in the kurtosis; the author finds the effect is in the wrong direction and too small (10% versus the observed ~50% suppression). The work enables future studies of baryon number fluctuations using mean-field models that smoothly connect the HRG regime at high energy (√s_NN ≳ 10 GeV) to the nuclear matter regime at lower energies.
