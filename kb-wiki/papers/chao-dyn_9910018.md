# Response Function of an Irregular Oscillator

> Properties of the response functions for a two-dimensional quartic oscillator are studied based on the diagonalization of the Hamiltonian in a large model space. In particular, response functions corresponding to a given momentum transfer are studied for different values of the coupling parameter in the Hamiltonian. The latter controls regular or chaotic nature of the spectra and eigenstates of the system. Fluctuation properties of the energy-strength correlation of the response are investigated. Even when the statistical properties of the system indicate an almost completely chaotic character, there remains a typical structure in the response function similar to that in the regular system. The nature of this structure is studied in some detail.

# Response Function of an Irregular Oscillator

## Key claim

Even when the two-dimensional quartic oscillator system is tuned to a coupling parameter (k=0.6) where classical phase space is almost completely chaotic and the level statistics follow the Wigner distribution, the response function retains a regular, equidistant intermediate structure that cannot be explained by random-matrix theory. This persisting structure arises because the spreading width of specific doorway states (the |0,m) basis states) remains smaller than their level spacing, with the ratio γ/D ≈ 0.77 for k=0.6.

## Method

The Hamiltonian H = ½(p_x² + p_y²) + ½(x⁴ + y⁴) − k x² y² was diagonalized in a truncated harmonic oscillator basis with 0 ≤ n_x + n_y ≤ 300, yielding submatrices of dimension 5776 for the A₁ symmetry class. Response functions W(Ω) = Σ_j |⟨j|Q̂|i⟩|² δ(Ω − (E_j − E_i)) were computed for the probes x̂² and e^{iqx̂} (the latter at momentum transfers q = 10, 30, 50), using the ground state as the initial state. The coupling parameter was set to k = 0.2 (quasiintegrable) and k = 0.6 (chaotic), with the basis frequency ω₀ optimized to 7.51 and 7.13 respectively.

## Key numbers

- Coupling parameters studied: k = 0.2 (quasiintegrable, irregular orbits cover 25% of phase space) and k = 0.6 (chaotic, irregular orbits cover 90% of phase space)
- Basis truncation: 0 ≤ n_x + n_y ≤ 300; A₁ submatrix dimension = 5776; largest eigenvalue ≈ 3000; maximum reliable energy E = 1000–1500
- Optimized basis frequencies: ω₀ = 7.51 (k=0.2) and ω₀ = 7.13 (k=0.6)
- Ground state inverse length scales: x̄_gs⁻¹ = 1.64 (k=0.2) and 1.57 (k=0.6)
- Quasielastic peak energy: E_peak = q²/2; at q = 50, E_peak ≈ 1250
- For the x̂² probe: at k=0.2, two major states exhaust >60% of total strength; at k=0.6, they carry <50% and this fraction decreases with initial energy
- Lyapunov exponents for closed orbits at E=1/2: λ = 0.53 (k=0.2) and λ = 0.92 (k=0.6); period T ≈ 5.24; γ/D ratios: 0.44 (k=0.2) and 0.77 (k=0.6)
- Sum rule limiting values for the symmetrized probe: at q→∞, S̃₀ → 1/4 and S̃₁ → q²/8; these limits are already satisfied at q ≈ 10

## Context

This work extends studies of quantum chaos in the coupled quartic oscillator model previously investigated by Meyer (for classical phase space structure), Zimmerman et al. (for quantum eigenstates), and Pollak (for symmetry properties). It builds on the semiclassical response theory of Wilkinson and collaborators, which focused on long-wavelength probes, and on the fluctuation studies of strength functions by Aiba and Suzuki (1998). The finding that regular intermediate structure persists in the chaotic regime connects to the scar phenomenon of Heller (1984) and suggests that doorway-state concepts from nuclear reaction theory (Weisskopf) may apply to chaotic systems. The results enable future semiclassical studies of response functions based on periodic orbits, as developed by Eckardt, Muller, Mehlig, and Kon.
