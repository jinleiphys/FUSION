# Quantum Phase Transitions in Dense QCD

> Quantum chromodynamics (QCD) at finite temperature, $T$, and quark chemical potential, $μ$, has a rich phase structure: at low $T$ and low $μ$, the Nambu-Goldstone (NG) phase with nearly massless pions is realized by the dynamical breaking of chiral symmetry through condensation of quark-anti-quark pairs, while, at low $T$ and high $μ$, a Fermi liquid of deconfined quarks is expected to appear as a consequence of asymptotic freedom. Furthermore, in such a cold quark matter, condensation of quark-quark pairs leads to the color superconductivity (CSC). At high $T$ for arbitrary $μ$, all the condensates melt away and a quark-gluon plasma (QGP) is realized. The experimental exploration of thermal phase transition from the NG phase to QGP is being actively pursued in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions at RHIC (Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider), and will be continued in the future at LHC (Large Hadron Collider). The quantum phase transition from the NG phase to the CSC at low $T$ is also relevant to heavy-ion collisions at moderate energies, and is of interest in the interiors of neutron stars and possible quark stars. In this Chapter, after a brief introduction to the basic properties of QCD, the current status of the QCD phase structure and associated quantum phase transitions will be summarized with particular emphasis on the symmetry realization of each phase. Possible connection between the physics of QCD and that of ultracold atoms is also discussed.

## Key claim

This chapter reviews the current understanding of quantum phase transitions in dense QCD, emphasizing that the transition from the Nambu-Goldstone phase to color superconductivity at low temperature is a quantum phase transition relevant to heavy-ion collisions at moderate energies and to the interiors of neutron stars and possible quark stars, but the authors note that the phase structure depends on the symmetry realization of each phase and that the expansion by the strange quark mass does not necessarily work.

## Method

The authors provide a theoretical review of QCD phase structure by first introducing the fundamental symmetries of QCD, including chiral symmetry and its dynamical breaking, then summarizing lattice QCD Monte Carlo simulation results for the thermal transition at zero baryon density, and finally discussing the expected phase transitions at high baryon density using chemical equilibrium conditions and the Fermi gas model for neutron-star matter, hyperonic matter, and quark matter. The review also covers color superconductivity arising from quark-quark pairing via the color-magnetic interaction mediated by gluons.

## Key numbers

* The QCD scale parameter Lambda_QCD is approximately 200 MeV (2 x 10^8 eV)
* The pseudo-critical temperature for the chiral transition at zero baryon density is T_pc approximately 150-200 MeV (1.7-2.3 x 10^12 K)
* The chiral condensate at zero temperature is (1/2) <bar-u u + bar-d d>_0 approximately -(242(04) MeV)^3 at kappa = 2 GeV
* The critical quark chemical potential for percolation to quark matter is mu_c approximately 380-450 MeV, assuming non-interacting massless u and d quarks
* The central density of heavy atomic nuclei is rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3} = 0.16 x 10^{39} cm^{-3}
* The pion decay constant f_pi = 92.4 MeV and charged pion masses m_pi^pm approximately 140 MeV
* The effective masses of u,d quarks inside hadrons are M_u,d approximately 350 MeV and M_s approximately 550 MeV

## Context

This chapter extends the line of work on QCD phase structure that began with the discovery of asymptotic freedom and the proposal of a transition from hadronic matter to quark matter in neutron star cores by Collins and Perry (1975) and the strange quark star hypothesis by Witten (1984). It builds on lattice QCD simulations of the thermal transition by the MILC Collaboration (Cheng et al., 2007) and the Budapest-Wuppertal group (Aoki et al., 2009), and on reviews of color superconductivity by Rajagopal and Wilczek (2000) and Alford et al. (2007). The review enables future connections between QCD physics and ultracold atom systems, and provides a framework for interpreting observations of neutron star cooling and glitch phenomena.
