# Nuclear rainbow scattering and nucleus-nucleus potential

> Elastic scattering of alpha-particle and some tightly-bound light nuclei has shown the pattern of rainbow scattering at medium energies, which is due to the refraction of the incident wave by a strongly attractive nucleus-nucleus potential. This review gives an introduction to the physics of the nuclear rainbow based essentially on the optical model description of the elastic scattering. Since the realistic nucleus-nucleus optical potential (OP) is the key to explore this interesting process, an overview of the main methods used to determine the nucleus-nucleus OP is presented. The refractive rainbow-like structures observed in other quasi-elastic scattering reactions have also been discussed. Some evidences for the refractive effect in the elastic scattering of unstable nuclei are presented and perspectives for the future studies are discussed.

# Nuclear rainbow scattering and nucleus-nucleus potential

## Key claim
Nuclear rainbow scattering in elastic alpha-nucleus and light heavy-ion systems provides direct information on the nucleus-nucleus optical potential at short internuclear distances where the total density reaches up to twice nuclear matter saturation density, but only for systems where absorption is sufficiently weak that the farside scattering amplitude components with angular momenta below the rainbow angular momentum survive in the elastic channel.

## Method
The authors review the optical model description of elastic scattering for alpha-particle and tightly-bound light-ion systems (such as 12C+12C and 16O+16O) that exhibit refractive rainbow patterns at medium energies. They describe the use of nearside/farside decomposition of the scattering amplitude via Fuller's method to isolate the refractive farside component, and the application of the double-folding model with a density-dependent M3Y effective nucleon-nucleon interaction to calculate the real part of the optical potential. The imaginary (absorptive) part of the optical potential is treated with Woods-Saxon or Woods-Saxon-squared forms, and the resulting Airy interference pattern between the l_< and l_> branches of the farside deflection function is analyzed to extract the potential at small distances.

## Key numbers
- The incompressibility constant K of cold nuclear matter extracted from the folding analysis of rainbow systems is K ≈ 230-260 MeV.
- For elastic 16O+16O scattering at E_lab = 350 MeV, the mean free path λ_mfp reaches its asymptotic minimal value at R ≤ 5 fm, significantly smaller than the strong absorption radius R_sa ≈ 7-8 fm.
- The strong absorption radius parameterization gives r_0 ≈ 1.1 fm with surface separation Δ between 2-3 fm at energies of 10-20 MeV/nucleon, reducing to 1-2 fm at energies around 100 MeV/nucleon.
- The first Airy minimum (A1) in elastic 16O+16O scattering appears most favorably in the angular range Θ_c.m. ≈ 30°-60° for incident energies between 300 and 450 MeV.
- For elastic α+58Ni scattering at E_lab = 139 MeV, the unique selection of the real potential depth V_0 = 115 MeV was made possible by data extending beyond the rainbow angle.

## Context
This review extends the earlier observations of nuclear rainbow scattering in alpha-nucleus systems by Goldberg et al. (1972-1974) and in light heavy-ion systems such as 12C+12C by Stokstad and by Bohlen et al. (1982-1985) and 16O+16O by Stiliaris et al. (1989) and Bohlen et al. (1993). The work builds on the semiclassical trajectory analysis and Airy function formalism adapted from atmospheric optics by Airy (1838) and applied to nuclear scattering by McVoy and Brandan (1992). The folding model analysis using density-dependent M3Y interactions connects the rainbow scattering data to the nuclear matter equation of state, enabling future studies of refractive effects in elastic scattering of unstable nuclei and providing a test of the optical potential at densities up to twice saturation density.
