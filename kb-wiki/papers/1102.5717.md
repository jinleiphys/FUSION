# New applications of the renormalization group method in physics -- a brief introduction

> The renormalization group method developed by Ken Wilson more than four decades ago has revolutionized the way we think about problems involving a broad range of energy scales such as phase transitions, turbulence, continuum limits and bifurcations in dynamical systems. The theme issue provides articles reviewing recent progress made using the renormalization group method in atomic, condensed matter, nuclear and particle physics. In the following we introduce these articles in a way that emphasizes common themes and the universal aspects of the method.

## Key claim

The renormalization group (RG) method provides a universal language for problems involving broad ranges of energy scales, and this theme issue aims to foster communication across traditionally separate fields (atomic, condensed matter, nuclear, and particle physics) by presenting recent progress in each area, with the workshop that inspired it having 35 participants over five days.

## Method

The paper is an introductory overview of a theme issue, summarizing contributed articles that apply RG methods to condensed matter (non-relativistic fermions, graphene, density-matrix RG), nuclear physics (chiral effective field theory, Efimov physics, similarity renormalization group), particle physics (confinement, conformal windows, lattice gauge theory), and exact/functional RG equations (critical exponents, quantum gravity, BCS-BEC crossover). Each contributed article is introduced with its specific focus and key results.

## Key numbers

- For non-relativistic fermions with a circular Fermi surface in 2d, the BCS coupling flow equation is dV_m/dt = -V_m^2, with solution V(t) = V(0)/[1 + t V(0)] where t = ln(Lambda_0/Lambda); the critical scale for BCS pairing is Lambda_c ~ exp(-1/|V(0)|).
- In graphene, the dimensionless coupling constant g = e^2/(4 pi v_F) flows to the value of the fine structure constant alpha = 1/137 at a non-trivial infrared fixed point.
- For asymptotically free theories, the mass gap scales as m a proportional to exp(-A/g^2(a)), where a is the lattice spacing and g(a) the running coupling.
- The workshop had 35 participants and ran over five days.

## Context

This paper introduces a theme issue that grew out of a workshop at the Institute for Nuclear Theory (INT-10-45W), co-organized with Mike Birse. It extends the foundational RG work of Wilson (1974) and the textbook treatments by Goldenfeld (1992) and Cardy (1996). The issue enables cross-field communication by presenting recent RG applications in condensed matter (Shankar's review of non-relativistic fermions, Vozmediano's work on graphene, Schollwöck's density-matrix RG), nuclear physics (Birse on chiral EFT, Hammer and Platter on Efimov physics, Bogner et al. on V_low k and SRG), and particle physics (Ogilvie on confinement, deGrand on lattice gauge theory conformal windows), as well as exact RG methods (Blaizot on BMW approximation, Litim on asymptotic safety in quantum gravity, Scherer et al. on BCS-BEC crossover).
