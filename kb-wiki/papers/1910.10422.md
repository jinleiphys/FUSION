# On the boundary conditions for the neutron transport equation

> The solution of the linear transport equation used for the study of neutral particle fields requires the imposition of appropriate boundary conditions. The choice of the conditions to impose for an infinite medium is not straightforward. The question has been given different formulations in the literature with various justifications based on some physical reasoning. Some aspects of the question are here analysed, from both the mathematical and the physical point of view. It is concluded that the inspiring golden rule should be the establishment of conditions that do not require any reference to the properties of the specific medium being considered for their justification.

# On the boundary conditions for the neutron transport equation

## Key claim

The proper boundary condition for the linear Boltzmann (neutron transport) equation in an infinite spatial domain is the boundedness of the angular flux, not the vanishing condition often assumed in the literature. The authors argue that boundary conditions must be universally valid and not depend on specific medium properties such as the presence of absorption.

## Method

The authors analyze the mathematical and physical foundations of boundary conditions for the linear transport equation, comparing the standard vacuum boundary condition for finite convex systems (angular flux vanishes for incoming directions) with various formulations proposed for infinite media. They examine the limiting process of pushing the bounding surface to infinity, consider the transport equation in vacuum where all macroscopic cross sections are zero, and discuss the implications for both the direct and adjoint transport equations. The analysis includes examples from diffusion theory and the Peierls integral form of the transport equation.

## Key numbers

- No specific numerical results are presented; the paper is a theoretical and conceptual analysis of boundary conditions.

## Context

This work extends the discussion initiated by Davison (1957) on the proper treatment of boundary conditions at infinity for the neutron transport equation. It challenges the consensus that emerged during the 1960s, as represented by Case and Zweifel, Mika (1966), and Gibbs and Larsen (1977), that the angular flux should vanish at infinity. The authors instead align with the weaker condition of boundedness, as previously applied in diffusion theory by Meghreblian and Holmes. The paper enables a re-examination of the functional spaces used for solving transport problems in infinite media and clarifies the distinction between conditions that follow from the equation itself versus those imposed by assumptions about the medium.
