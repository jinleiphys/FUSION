# Dynamical spectral structure of density fluctuation near QCD critical point

> The expression for the dynamical spectral structure of the density fluctuation near the QCD critical point has been derived using linear response theory within the purview of Israel-Stewart relativistic viscous hydrodynamics. The change in spectral structure of the system as it moves toward critical end point has been studied. The effects of the critical point have been introduced in the system through a realistic equation of state and the scaling behaviour of various transport coefficients and thermodynamic response functions. We have found that the Brillouin and the Rayleigh peaks are distinctly visible when the system is away from critical point but the peaks tend to merge near the critical point. The sensitivity of structure of the spectral function on wave vector ($k$) of the sound wave has been demonstrated. It has been shown that the Brillouin peaks get merged with the Rayleigh peak because of the absorption of sound waves in the vicinity of the critical point.

## Key claim

Using linear response theory within Israel-Stewart relativistic viscous hydrodynamics, the dynamical spectral structure of density fluctuation near the QCD critical point shows that Brillouin and Rayleigh peaks are distinctly visible away from the critical point but tend to merge near it, with the merging attributed to absorption of sound waves in the vicinity of the critical point rather than solely to critical slowing down.

## Method

The authors derived the dynamical spectral structure of density fluctuation by linearizing the Israel-Stewart relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations in the Eckart frame and applying Fourier-Laplace transformation to obtain the density-density correlator in wave vector-frequency space. The effects of the QCD critical point were introduced through a realistic equation of state constructed by mapping the 3D Ising model onto the QCD phase diagram, combined with scaling laws for the critical behavior of transport coefficients (shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, thermal conductivity) and thermodynamic response functions (isothermal compressibility, specific heats, speed of sound, volume expansivity). The spectral function was evaluated numerically for points near and away from the assumed critical endpoint at (T_c, mu_c) = (154 MeV, 367 MeV).

## Key numbers

- The critical endpoint is assumed at (T_c, mu_c) = (154 MeV, 367 MeV) with critical region extensions (Delta T_c, Delta mu_c) = (0.1 GeV, 0.2 GeV) and elongation parameter B = 2.
- Away from the critical point at r = 0.2 (reduced temperature) and wave vector k = 0.1 fm^{-1}, three distinct peaks are visible: a central Rayleigh peak and symmetric Brillouin doublet.
- Near the critical point at r = 0.01 and k = 0.1 fm^{-1}, the Rayleigh peak height increases by more than an order of magnitude and the Brillouin peaks vanish.
- For k = 0.02 fm^{-1} near the critical point (r = 0.01), the Rayleigh peak becomes larger and sharper compared to k = 0.1 fm^{-1}.
- Away from the critical point (r = 0.2), the Brillouin frequency varies linearly with k with slope 0.28, close to the speed of sound c_s = 0.25 obtained from the equation of state at the same temperature and chemical potential.
- Critical exponents used: alpha = 1.8, gamma' = 1.2, a_k = 0.63 for the scaling of thermodynamic quantities and transport coefficients.

## Context

This work extends the study of spectral structure of density fluctuations near the QCD critical point by Kunihiro (prior work) by incorporating a realistic equation of state containing the critical point, which was absent in that earlier treatment. The authors show that the equation of state plays a vital role in determining the behavior of the spectral function, changing the Rayleigh peak strength by several orders of magnitude. The results enable future interpretation of Mach-cone attenuation in heavy-ion collisions, suggesting that mode-dependent speed of propagation away from the critical point and vanishing speed of sound at the critical point must be considered, rather than attributing attenuation solely to the critical point as suggested in prior work.
