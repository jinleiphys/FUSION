# Finite-nuclear-size effect in hydrogen-like ions with relativistic nuclear structure

> The finite-nuclear-size (FNS) effect has a large contribution to the atomic spectral properties especially for heavy nuclei. By adopting the microscopic nuclear charge density distributions obtained from the relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory, we systematically investigate the FNS corrections to atomic energy levels and bound-electron $g$ factors of hydrogen-like ions with nuclear charge up to $118$. The comparison of the present numerical calculations with the predictions from empirical nuclear charge models, the non-relativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations, and the results based on experimental charge densities indicate that both the nuclear charge radius and the detailed shape of charge density distribution play important roles in determining the FNS corrections. The variation of FNS corrections to energy levels and $g$ factors with respect to the nuclear charge are investigated for the lowest several bound states of hydrogen-like ions. It is shown that they both increase by orders of magnitude with increasing the nuclear charge, while the ratio between them has a relatively weak dependence on the nuclear charge. The FNS corrections to the $s_{1/2}$ and $p_{1/2}$ bound state energies from the RCHB calculations are generally in good agreement with the analytical estimations by Shabaev [J. Phys. B, 26, 1103 (1993)] based on the homogeneously charged sphere nuclear model, with the discrepancy indicating the distinct contribution of microscopic nuclear structure to the FNS effects.

## Key claim

The finite-nuclear-size (FNS) corrections to atomic energy levels and bound-electron g factors calculated using microscopic nuclear charge densities from relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory generally agree with analytical estimations based on a homogeneously charged sphere model, but the discrepancies reveal that the detailed shape of the nuclear charge density distribution, not just the charge radius, contributes measurably to the FNS effect.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) equations with the PC-PK1 density functional to obtain point proton and neutron densities for even-even nuclei with Z from 8 to 118, then constructed nuclear charge densities by including nucleon form factors and spin-orbit contributions. The electron Dirac equation was solved using the kinetically balanced generalized pseudospectral (KB-GPS) method to obtain atomic energy levels and bound-electron g factors for finite-size nuclei, and FNS corrections were taken as the difference from the point-nucleus values.

## Key numbers

- For the 1s1/2 state of hydrogen-like 208Pb (Z=82, A=208), the FNS correction to the energy level is 67.278 eV and to the g factor is 4.5358e-4.
- For the 1s1/2 state of hydrogen-like 40Ca (Z=20, A=40), the FNS correction to the energy level is 1.4543e-2 eV and to the g factor is 1.1294e-7.
- For the 1s1/2 state of hydrogen-like 294Og (Z=118, A=294), the FNS correction to the energy level is 3583.1 eV and to the g factor is 1.8472e-2.
- For the 2s1/2 state of 208Pb, the FNS correction to the energy level is 11.682 eV and to the g factor is 7.8695e-5.
- For the 2p1/2 state of 208Pb, the FNS correction to the energy level is 1.0007 eV and to the g factor is 6.7787e-6.
- The root-mean-square charge radius for 208Pb from RCHB is 5.5055 fm; for 40Ca it is 3.4750 fm; for 294Og it is 6.2719 fm.

## Context

This work extends the study of FNS effects beyond empirical nuclear charge models (homogeneously charged sphere, Gaussian, and two-parameter Fermi distributions) and beyond the non-relativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations performed by Valuev et al. (2020) for a few selected ions. By using the relativistic RCHB method, the authors provide a systematic survey across the entire periodic table up to Z=118, enabling more accurate predictions for tests of quantum electrodynamics in highly charged ions and for determinations of fundamental constants from high-precision spectroscopy.
