# Towards a Deeper Understanding of How Experiments Constrain the Underlying Physics of Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Recent work has provided the means to rigorously determine properties of super-hadronic matter from experimental data through the application of broad scale modeling of high-energy nuclear collisions within a Bayesian framework. These studies have provided unprecedented statistical inferences about the physics underlying nuclear collisions by virtue of simultaneously considering a wide range of model parameters and experimental observables. Notably, this approach has been used to constrain both the QCD equation of state and the shear viscosity above the quark-hadron transition. Although the inferences themselves have a clear meaning, the complex nature of the relationships between model parameters and observables have remained relatively obscure. We present here a novel extension of the standard Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo approach that allows for the quantitative determination of how inferences of model parameters are driven by experimental measurements and their uncertainties. This technique is then applied in the context of heavy ion collisions in order to explore previous results in greater depth. The resulting relationships are useful for identifying model weaknesses, prioritizing future experimental measurements, and most importantly: developing an intuition for the role that different observables play in constraining our understanding of the underlying physics.

## Key claim

The authors present a novel extension of the standard Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo approach that allows quantitative determination of how inferences of model parameters are driven by experimental measurements and their uncertainties, applied to a 14-parameter analysis of heavy-ion collisions where the uncertainties for each observable were all set to 6% of the experimental value rather than derived from detailed model studies.

## Method

The authors used a 14-parameter model consisting of initial state generation, 2-dimensional hydrodynamics, and a hadronic cascade at a transition temperature of 165 MeV, with parameters describing the equation of state, shear viscosity, and initial conditions for both RHIC Au+Au collisions at 100A GeV + 100A GeV and LHC Pb+Pb collisions at 1.38A TeV + 1.38A TeV. They trained a Gaussian process emulator on 1200 full-model runs (1000 from latin hyper-cube sampling and 200 chosen to be consistent with the likelihood) to replace the computationally expensive model, then performed MCMC with several million random steps using the Metropolis algorithm. They developed three measures of sensitivity from a single MCMC trace: linear response derivatives of observables with respect to parameters, the effect of reducing observable uncertainties on posterior widths, and the decomposition of posterior covariance into contributions from individual observables.

## Key numbers

* Shear viscosity at T0 = 165 MeV, parameterized as (eta/s)_0 with range 0.02 to 0.5, and temperature dependence eta' with range 0.0 to 3.0
* Equation of state parameters: R from -0.9 to 2.0, X' from 0.5 to 5.0
* Initial time tau_0 = 0.8 fm/c for all model runs
* 30 observables total: 15 from RHIC (Au+Au) and 15 from LHC (Pb+Pb), each with uncertainties set to 6% of experimental value
* 14 principal components analyzed from the 30 observables, matching the 14-dimensional parameter space
* 1200 full-model runs used to train the emulator, with 1000 from latin hyper-cube sampling and 200 additional runs
* RHIC pion yield in 0-5% centrality: 422 pions in pt range 0.2 to 1.2 GeV/c (PHENIX)
* LHC pion yield in 0-5% centrality: 1258 pions in pt range 0.2 to 1.2 GeV/c (ALICE)
* RHIC v2 for pions in 20-30% centrality: 8.14% (STAR), reduced by 9% to account for smooth initial conditions
* LHC v2 for pions in 20-30% centrality: 9.56% (ALICE), reduced by 9% to account for smooth initial conditions

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian inference framework previously applied to heavy-ion collisions by Novak et al. (2014) and Pratt et al. (2015), which provided the first quantitative constraints on the QCD equation of state and shear viscosity using Gaussian process emulators. The new sensitivity analysis techniques allow researchers to understand which experimental measurements drive constraints on specific parameters, addressing the limitation that previous Bayesian analyses provided posterior distributions but not insight into how individual observables contribute. This enables identification of model weaknesses, prioritization of future experimental measurements, and development of intuition for how different observables constrain the underlying physics of super-hadronic matter.
