# Radial oscillations in neutron stars from unified hadronic and quarkyonic equation of states

> We study radial oscillations in non-rotating neutron stars by considering the unified equation of states (EoSs), which support the 2 M$_\odot$ star criterion. We solve the Sturm-Liouville problem to compute 20 lowest radial oscillation modes and their eigenfunctions for neutron star modelled with eight selected unified EoSs from distinct Skyrme-Hartree Fock, Relativistic Mean-Field and quarkyonic models. We compare the behavior of the computed eigenfrequency for NS modelled with hadronic to that with quarkyonic EoSs while varying central densities. The lowest order, f-mode frequency varies substantially between the two classes of the of EoS at 1.4 M$_\odot$ but vanishes at their respective maximum masses, consistent with the stability criterion $\partial M/\partialρ_c > 0$. Moreover, we also computed large frequency separation and discovered that higher-order mode frequencies are significantly reduced by incorporating crust in the EoS.

# Radial oscillations in neutron stars from unified hadronic and quarkyonic equation of states

## Key claim

The fundamental mode (f-mode) frequency of radial oscillations in neutron stars varies substantially between hadronic and quarkyonic equations of state at 1.4 solar masses, but vanishes at each model's respective maximum mass, consistent with the stability criterion that the derivative of mass with respect to central density must be positive. The authors qualify that this comparison holds for the eight selected unified equations of state and that higher-order mode frequencies are significantly reduced when the crust is incorporated into the equation of state.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for hydrostatic equilibrium in general relativity to obtain neutron star mass and radius for eight unified equations of state, including five hadronic models (NL3, IOPB, DD2, DDME2, SLy4) and three quarkyonic models (Q1, Q2, Q3). They then formulated the radial oscillation problem as a Sturm-Liouville eigenvalue problem, solving a system of two first-order differential equations for the displacement function and the Lagrangian pressure perturbation using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method with logarithmic step sizes, and finding eigenfrequencies via the bisection method.

## Key numbers

- The f-mode frequency at 1.4 solar masses ranges from 2.59 kHz (NL3 hadronic) to 5.61 kHz (Q1 quarkyonic), with hadronic values between 2.59 and 3.25 kHz and quarkyonic values between 3.45 and 5.61 kHz
- The 20 lowest radial oscillation modes were computed for each equation of state, with frequencies up to 44.88 kHz (Q2, n=19) at 1.4 solar masses
- The transition density for quarkyonic models is 0.3 fm^{-3} (Q1, Q2) or 0.4 fm^{-3} (Q3), with slope parameter L of 30 MeV (Q1, Q2) or 50 MeV (Q3) and dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda of 1400 MeV (Q1, Q3) or 800 MeV (Q2)
- The first large separation Delta f_0 at 1.4 solar masses is larger than subsequent separations for all equations of state, with uneven fluctuations appearing when the crust is included

## Context

This work extends the study of radial oscillations in neutron stars first investigated by Chandrasekhar (1964) and later by Harrison et al. (1965), Chanmugam (1977), and Glass and Lindblom (1983), whose numerical results were corrected by Vath and Chanmugam (1992) and re-examined by Kokkotas and Ruoff (2001). The authors compare five hadronic equations of state (NL3, IOPB, DD2, DDME2, SLy4) with three quarkyonic equations of state (Q1-Q3) from Zhao and collaborators (2020), finding that the quarkyonic models produce multiple peaks in higher-order mode frequencies due to a narrow peak in the adiabatic index above the transition density, which could serve as a signature of phase transitions if observed. The results enable future work connecting radial oscillations to gravitational wave emission through coupling with non-radial modes, as suggested by Passamonti and collaborators (2006), and to modulation of short gamma-ray bursts from hyper-massive neutron star mergers.
