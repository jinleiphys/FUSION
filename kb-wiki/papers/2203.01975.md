# Nudged elastic band approach to nuclear fission pathways

> The nuclear fission process is a dramatic example of the large-amplitude collective motion in which the nucleus undergoes a series of shape changes before splitting into distinct fragments. This motion can be represented by a pathway in the many-dimensional space of collective coordinates. The collective action along the fission pathway determines the spontaneous fission half-lives as well as mass and charge distributions of fission fragments. We study the performance and precision of various methods to determine the minimum action and minimum-energy fission trajectories in the collective space. We apply the nudged elastic band method (NEB), grid-based methods, and Euler Lagrange approach to the collective action minimization in two and three dimensional collective spaces. The performance of various approaches to the fission pathway problem is assessed by studying the collective motion along both analytic energy surfaces and realistic potential energy surfaces obtained with the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory. The uniqueness and stability of the solutions is studied. The NEB method is capable of efficient determination of the exit points on the outer turning surface that characterize the most probable fission pathway and constitute the key input for fission studies. This method can also be used to accurately compute the critical points (i.e., local minima and saddle points) on the potential energy surface of the fissioning nucleus that determine the static fission path. The NEB method is the tool of choice for finding the least-action and minimum energy fission trajectories. It will be particularly useful in large-scale fission calculation of superheavy nuclei and neutron-rich fissioning nuclei contributing to the astrophysical r-process recycling.

## Key claim

The nudged elastic band (NEB) method is the tool of choice for finding least-action and minimum-energy fission trajectories, but it converges to a local stationary path rather than guaranteeing the global minimum; the dynamic programming method also performs well and can provide initial conditions for NEB calculations in many-dimensional cases.

## Method

The authors applied the nudged elastic band method, grid-based methods (dynamic programming and Dijkstra's algorithm), and the Euler-Lagrange approach to minimize the collective action integral in two- and three-dimensional collective spaces. They benchmarked these methods on analytic energy surfaces (symmetric and asymmetric camel-back potentials and the Muller-Brown potential) and on realistic potential energy surfaces obtained from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory for 232U and 240Pu, using the SkM* functional and mixed density-dependent pairing force.

## Key numbers

- For the symmetric camel-back surface, the NEB-LAP action integral was 5.518, compared to 5.524 (DPM), 5.536 (EL), and 5.563 (DA)
- For the asymmetric camel-back surface, the NEB-LAP action integral was 6.404, compared to 6.405 (DPM), 6.407 (EL), and 6.886 (DA); the NEB-MEP gave 6.793
- For the Muller-Brown surface, the NEB-LAP action integral was 22.875, compared to 22.909 (DPM), 22.871 (EL), and 23.427 (DA); the NEB-MEP gave 28.491
- The grid spacing used for analytic surfaces was Delta x = 0.1 along the x-axis and Delta y = 0.005 along the y-axis

## Context

This work extends prior grid-based approaches to least-action paths in fission, such as the dynamic programming method of Baran (1981) used in numerous energy density functional calculations by Sadhukhan (2013, 2014, 2016, 2017) and others, and the method of characteristics approach of Schmid (1986) and Eckern (1992). The NEB method, originally formulated for molecular systems by Mills (1994, 1995) and Jonsson (1998), is shown to be particularly useful for large-scale fission calculations of superheavy nuclei and neutron-rich fissioning nuclei contributing to astrophysical r-process recycling, where computational efficiency is critical.
