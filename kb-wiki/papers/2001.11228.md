# Astronuclear Physics: a Tale of the Atomic Nuclei in the Skies

> A century ago, nuclear physics entered astrophysics, giving birth to a new field of science referred to as "Nuclear Astrophysics". With time, it developed at an impressive pace into a vastly inter- and multidisciplinary discipline bringing into its wake not only astronomy and cosmology, but also many other sub-fields of physics, especially particle, solid-state and computational physics, as well as chemistry, geology and even biology. The present Astronuclear Physics review focusses primarily on the facets of nuclear physics that are of relevance to astronomy and astrophysics, the theoretical aspects being of special concern here.

## Key claim

This review argues that nuclear astrophysics has advanced dramatically over the past century through the interplay of multi-messenger observations, stellar modeling, and nuclear theory, but that many long-standing problems remain unsolved, particularly regarding the robustness of supernova explosion simulations and the determination of thermonuclear reaction cross sections for exotic nuclei, and that the connection between nuclear data and astrophysical modeling is often presented without adequate discussion of the severe uncertainties pervading both domains.

## Method

The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical review of nuclear physics phenomena relevant to astrophysics, covering nuclear masses, structure properties, weak and strong interaction decays, thermonuclear reactions, and the equation of state of dense matter. They survey the observational foundations of nucleosynthesis theory, including electromagnetic spectra, solar system abundances, cosmic ray composition, neutrino astronomy, and gravitational wave detections, and review the major nucleosynthesis processes for heavy elements (s-process, r-process, p-process, and i-process). The review emphasizes theoretical modeling over experimental details and explicitly avoids extensive discussion of the connections between nuclear data and astrophysical observations or abundance modeling.

## Key numbers

* The Sun burns approximately 2 × 10^19 kg (10^-11 solar masses) of protons per year via the p-p chain, releasing about 6 MeV per proton.
* The Hoyle state in 12C is a 0+ excited state at 7.7 MeV, predicted as a resonance in the 8Be + alpha reaction and later confirmed experimentally.
* The solar system formed about 4.6 Gyr ago, and its bulk composition shows abundance peaks at the iron peak (centered around mass number 56), at alpha-element mass numbers (multiples of 4), in the A ≈ 80–90 region, and double peaks at A = 130–138 and A = 195–208.
* The review covers literature published up to July 1, 2019.

## Context

This work is an update and extension of a previous review by Arnould (1999) and surveys the same broad interdisciplinary field that originated with the foundational works of Burbidge (1957), Cameron (1957), and Hoyle (1946, 1954). It extends the standard nuclear astrophysics narrative by giving special emphasis to theoretical nuclear physics challenges, such as the equation of state for neutron star matter and reaction rates for nuclei far from stability, while explicitly not reviewing in depth the connections between nuclear data and astronomical observations, which the authors consider a highly uncertain and often underappreciated topic. The review enables future work by cataloging open questions in supernova explosion modeling, the r-process site identification, and the determination of reaction cross sections for the "world of almost no event" and the "world of exoticism."
