# Neutron drip line of $Z=9-11$ isotopic chains

> A recent experimental breakthrough identified the last bound neutron-rich nuclei in fluorine and neon isotopes. Based on this finding, we perform a theoretical study of $Z=9, 10, 11, 12$ isotopes in the relativistic mean field (RMF) model. The mean field parameters are assumed from the PK1 parameterization, and the pairing correlation is described by the particle number conservation BCS (FBCS) method recently formulated in the RMF model. We show that the FBCS approach plays an essential role in reproducing experimental results of fluorine and neon isotopes. Furthermore, we predict $^{39}$Na and $^{40}$Mg to be the last bound neutron-rich nuclei in sodium and magnesium isotopes.

## Key claim
The particle-number-conserving BCS (FBCS) method within the relativistic mean field (RMF) model is essential for reproducing the experimentally observed neutron drip line of fluorine (31F) and neon (34Ne), whereas the standard BCS method fails; the authors predict 39Na and 40Mg as the last bound isotopes for sodium and magnesium, but note that the FBCS result for 31F is bound by only a few hundred keV.

## Method
The authors employed the relativistic mean field (RMF) model with the PK1 parameterization for the mean-field effective interactions, using a harmonic oscillator basis expansion (12 shells for fermions, 20 for mesons) that allows for axial deformation. Pairing correlations were treated with both the standard BCS method and the recently developed particle-number-conserving BCS (FBCS) method, using a density-independent contact delta interaction with pairing strengths fitted to odd-even mass staggerings of stable nuclei (e.g., 20-24F, 22-26Ne). The neutron drip line was determined by computing one- and two-neutron separation energies for each isotopic chain.

## Key numbers
* Fluorine drip line: RMF+FBCS yields 31F (N=22) as the last bound isotope, consistent with the experimental result from PhysRevLett.123.212501; the RMF+BCS approach incorrectly yields N=20. The pairing energy for 31F is approximately 2.5 MeV in FBCS versus 0.2 MeV in BCS.
* Neon drip line: RMF+FBCS predicts 34Ne (N=24) as the last bound isotope, matching the experimental result from PhysRevLett.123.212501; the BCS method predicts N=26. The experimental S2N for 34Ne almost vanishes.
* Sodium drip line: Both FBCS and BCS methods indicate 39Na as the last bound sodium isotope, though the S2N for 39Na almost vanishes in the BCS method. The RIBF experiment detected one event for 39Na.
* Magnesium drip line: RMF+FBCS predicts 40Mg as the last bound magnesium isotope; the BCS approach yields 42Mg.
* Pairing strengths (V0 in MeV fm3) for FBCS: fluorine 380, neon 480, sodium 380, magnesium 380.

## Context
This work extends the experimental breakthrough at RIKEN RIBF (PhysRevLett.123.212501) that identified 31F and 34Ne as the last bound neutron-rich nuclei for fluorine and neon, moving the drip line from Z=8 (24O, determined twenty years ago) to Z=10. The study contrasts with earlier theoretical predictions that varied widely (e.g., 29F to 33F for fluorine, 30Ne to 42Ne for neon) and with the WS4 mass formula (Wang 2014) which correctly predicted 24O and 34Ne but gave 37Na for sodium. The FBCS approach, recently formulated in the RMF model (An 2019), is shown to be critical for reproducing the experimental drip lines, and the authors note that future work should apply the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc) to account for continuum effects, while also implementing proper particle-number conservation.
