# Symmetry background of the stability of the second 0^+ state of 4^He

> It was found in this paper that the dominant component of the wave function of the second 0^+ state of 4^He and the corresponding component of the 3^H+p channel have different spatial permutation symmetries. This fact will hinder the wave function to extend from the interior region to the outgoing channel and will lead to a narrower width.

# Symmetry background of the stability of the second 0⁺ state of ⁴He

## Key claim
The dominant spatial permutation symmetry component of the second 0⁺ state of ⁴He (λ = {211}) and the corresponding component of the ³H+p channel (λ = {31}) are different, and their parities also differ; this mismatch hinders the wave function from extending from the interior region into the outgoing channel, leading to a narrower width than would otherwise be expected.

## Method
The paper decomposes the interior wave function of ⁴He excited states into components classified by spatial permutation symmetry λ (representations of the S₄ group) using results from prior work on inherent nodal surfaces (INS) and dynamical nodal surfaces (DNS). The channel wave functions for the ³H+p, ³He+n, and d+d channels are similarly decomposed, and the matching of λ and parity between interior and channel components is compared to determine whether a state is hindered from decaying into a given channel.

## Key numbers
- The second 0⁺ state of ⁴He lies at 20.21 MeV.
- The ³H+p channel threshold is at 19.815 MeV.
- The ³He+n channel threshold is at 20.578 MeV.
- The d+d channel threshold is at 23.848 MeV.
- The d+n+p channel threshold is at 26.072 MeV.
- The 2n+2p channel threshold is at 28.30 MeV.
- The second 0⁺ state has a width of about 390 keV.

## Context
This work extends the analysis of inherent nodal surfaces (INS) and dynamical nodal surfaces (DNS) developed by Bao (1992, 1998) to explain why the second 0⁺ state of ⁴He is narrow while other excited states are broad. It contradicts a purely dynamical explanation of resonance widths by showing that spatial permutation symmetry and parity constraints can hinder wave function extension into decay channels. The analysis enables predictions about which higher-lying states (e.g., the k=9 2⁺ state) might be more easily discriminated experimentally, and the method is stated to be generalizable to other few-body systems as in Bao and Liu (1999).
