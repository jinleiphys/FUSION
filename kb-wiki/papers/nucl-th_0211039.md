# Effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian and continuum shell model

> The intrinsic dynamics of a system with open decay channels is described by an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian which at the same time allows one to find the external dynamics, - reaction cross sections. We discuss ways of incorporating this approach into the shell model context. Several examples of increasing complexity, from schematic models to realistic nuclear calculations (chain of oxygen isotopes), are presented. The approach is capable of describing a multitude of phenomena in a unified way combining physics of structure and reactions. Self-consistency of calculations and threshold energy dependence of the coupling to the continuum are crucial for the description of loosely bound states.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that incorporating continuum effects into the shell model via an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian produces qualitative new phenomena, such as the segregation of many-body states into short-lived and long-lived groups, but only under the condition that the continuum coupling strength exceeds the level spacing and that threshold energy dependence is properly included for loosely bound systems.

## Method

The authors construct an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian by separating the full Hilbert space into intrinsic states and continuum channels using projection operators, then eliminating the continuum. The resulting Hamiltonian contains a Hermitian shell-model part, a real energy-dependent shift from virtual continuum processes, and an imaginary factorized term from open decay channels. They diagonalize this complex Hamiltonian for systems of fermions on discrete single-particle levels coupled to one or two decay channels, using random two-body interactions to study generic behavior, and also apply the approach to a chain of oxygen isotopes.

## Key numbers

* For a system of 4 fermions on 8 equidistant single-particle levels (spacing 0.5 energy units) with one decaying level, the total number of many-body states is 70, divided evenly into 35 short-lived and 35 long-lived states at large decay width.
* The segregation of states into short-lived (occupation number near 1) and long-lived (occupation number near 0) groups begins when the single-particle width gamma becomes comparable to the level spacing D.
* For two unstable single-particle levels with widths gamma and gamma/2, the 70 states separate into four groups: 15 short-lived states with width approximately 3 gamma/2, 20 states with width approximately gamma, 20 states with width approximately gamma/2, and 15 quasistable states.
* The trace of the effective Hamiltonian is conserved, giving sum over states of widths Gamma(Psi) = (Omega-1)!/((Omega-N)!(N-1)!) * gamma, which for 4 particles on 8 levels equals 35 * gamma.

## Context

This work extends the effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian formalism developed by Feshbach (1958) and Mahaux and Weidenmuller (1969), and builds on the shell model embedded in the continuum revived by Bennaceur (1999), Michel (2002), and Betan (2002). The paper demonstrates that the approach unifies structure and reaction physics for loosely bound nuclei near the drip line, and the segregation effect is analogous to Dicke superradiance (1954) in optics and to the doorway-state picture of Sokolov and Zelevinsky (1988, 1992). The method enables self-consistent calculations of resonance energies and widths for nuclei far from stability, with threshold energy dependence being crucial for correct description.
