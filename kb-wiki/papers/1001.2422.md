# Random Matrices and Chaos in Nuclear Physics: Nuclear Reactions

> The application of random-matrix theory (RMT) to compound-nucleus (CN) reactions is reviewed. An introduction into the basic concepts of nuclear scattering theory is followed by a survey of phenomenological approaches to CN scattering. The implementation of a random-matrix approach into scattering theory leads to a statistical theory of CN reactions. Since RMT applies generically to chaotic quantum systems, that theory is, at the same time, a generic theory of quantum chaotic scattering. It uses a minimum of input parameters (average S-matrix and mean level spacing of the CN). Predictions of the theory are derived with the help of field-theoretical methods adapted from condensed-matter physics and compared with those of phenomenological approaches. Thorough tests of the theory are reviewed, as are applications in nuclear physics, with special attention given to violation of symmetries (isospin, parity) and time-reversal invariance.

## Key claim

Random-matrix theory (RMT) provides a generic statistical theory of compound-nucleus (CN) reactions that applies to all chaotic quantum scattering, using only the average S-matrix and mean level spacing as input parameters, but the theory is limited to energy windows between threshold energies where the number of open channels is fixed.

## Method

The authors review the implementation of RMT into nuclear scattering theory by combining the shell-model approach to resonances with a supersymmetric generating functional borrowed from condensed-matter physics. They compare the resulting statistical theory with phenomenological approaches including the Hauser-Feshbach formula, the Weisskopf estimate for resonance widths, and Ericson's model of cross-section fluctuations. The theory is tested against experimental data on compound-nucleus reactions, with particular attention to symmetry violations in isospin, parity, and time-reversal invariance.

## Key numbers

- The first particle threshold for nucleon emission typically occurs at an excitation energy of 6 or 8 MeV in a nucleus with mass number A
- In 17O, the spacing between the lowest (d5/2) and highest (d3/2) single-particle state is 5.08 MeV
- At neutron threshold, resonance spacings are of order 10 eV, with about 10^4 to 10^6 levels between the ground state and the isolated resonances seen in slow-neutron scattering
- At neutron threshold, where d is of order 10 eV and threshold spacing is several 100 keV, the number of resonances per energy window is approximately 10^4
- At 15 MeV above neutron threshold (the Ericson regime), the number of resonances per window is estimated to be one or two orders of magnitude smaller
- The Weisskopf estimate gives the average total width as Gamma = (d / 2 pi) times the sum over channels of transmission coefficients T_c

## Context

This review completes the earlier review by two of the same authors on random matrices and chaos in nuclear structure (Weidenmueller 2009). It extends the foundational work of Wigner (1955, 1957) and Dyson (1962) on RMT, the Hauser-Feshbach formula (1952), and Ericson's prediction of statistical fluctuations (1960, 1963). The theory also applies to electron transport through disordered mesoscopic samples (Beenakker 1997, Alhassid 2000, Imry 2002) and to electromagnetic wave propagation through microwave cavities (Fyodorov 2005). The review establishes the connection between RMT, CN scattering theory, and chaotic quantum scattering that emerged from the Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit conjecture (1984) and the first papers by Verbaarschot (1984, 1985) and Weidenmueller (1984).
