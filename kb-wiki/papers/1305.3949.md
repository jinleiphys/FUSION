# Out-of-Equilibrium Chiral Magnetic Effect at Strong Coupling

> We study the charge transports originating from triangle anomaly in out-of-equilibrium conditions in the framework of AdS/CFT correspondence at strong coupling, to gain useful insights on possible charge separation effects that may happen in the very early stages of heavy-ion collisions. We first construct a gravity background of a homogeneous mass shell with a finite (axial) charge density gravitationally collapsing to a charged blackhole, which serves as a dual model for out-of-equilibrium charged plasma undergoing thermalization. We find that a finite charge density in the plasma slows down the thermalization. We then study the out-of-equilibrium properties of Chiral Magnetic Effect and Chiral Magnetic Wave in this background. As the medium thermalizes, the magnitude of chiral magnetic conductivity and the response time delay grow. We find a dynamical peak in the spectral function of retarded current correlator, which we identify as an out-of-equilibrium chiral magnetic wave. The group velocity of the out-of-equilibrium chiral magnetic wave is shown to receive a dominant contribution from a non-equilibrium effect, making the wave moving much faster than in the equilibrium, which may enhance the charge transports via triangle anomaly in the early stage of heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

Using AdS/CFT at strong coupling, the authors find that the out-of-equilibrium chiral magnetic wave propagates much faster than its equilibrium counterpart, with the group velocity receiving a dominant contribution from non-equilibrium effects; this enhancement may increase charge transport via the triangle anomaly in the very early stages of heavy-ion collisions, but the results rely on a quasi-static approximation that becomes unreliable as the system approaches thermalization.

## Method

The authors construct a holographic dual of out-of-equilibrium charged plasma by modeling a thin, homogeneous mass shell with finite axial charge density that gravitationally collapses from pure AdS into an AdS-Reissner-Nordstrom black hole, solving the Israel junction conditions to obtain the shell trajectory. They then compute the frequency-dependent chiral magnetic conductivity and the spectral function of the retarded current correlator in this time-dependent background using a quasi-static approximation, treating the shell as stationary at each instant. The initial shell position is set by the saturation scale Q_s (0.87 GeV for RHIC, 1.23 GeV for LHC), and the late-time equilibrium temperature is taken as 300 MeV (RHIC) or 400 MeV (LHC).

## Key numbers

* Thermalization occurs mostly by t less than about 1 fm for both RHIC and LHC conditions.
* For RHIC (T=300 MeV), the horizon radius z_H is 0.209 fm for mu_A=50 MeV, 0.208 fm for mu_A=100 MeV, and 0.206 fm for mu_A=200 MeV.
* For LHC (T=400 MeV), the horizon radius z_H is 0.157 fm for mu_A=50 MeV, 0.156 fm for mu_A=100 MeV, and 0.155 fm for mu_A=200 MeV.
* The sphaleron relaxation time is estimated as tau_R approximately 10 fm for alpha_s=0.2, and 1.3 fm for alpha_s=0.3, at T=250 MeV.
* The charge susceptibility at T=250 MeV is given as chi approximately 1.0 T^2, about 0.06 GeV^2.
* The lattice result for the energy density at high temperature is epsilon/T^4 approximately 13, which fixes the 5D Newton constant G_5 approximately 0.45.

## Context

This work extends previous equilibrium studies of the Chiral Magnetic Effect at strong coupling (Yee 2009) and the Chiral Magnetic Wave (Kharzeev, Yee, and others 2010-2012) into out-of-equilibrium and non-hydrodynamic regimes, motivated by the early-stage dynamics of heavy-ion collisions where magnetic fields are strongest. The falling mass shell geometry generalizes the neutral-shell construction of Lin and others (2008) by including finite axial charge density, and the results suggest that non-equilibrium effects could significantly enhance anomalous charge transport before thermalization, providing a foundation for more quantitative modeling of early-time charge separation in heavy-ion collisions.
