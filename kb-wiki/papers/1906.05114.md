# Impact of electron capture rates on nuclei far from stability on core-collapse supernovae

> The impact of electron-capture (EC) cross sections on neutron-rich nuclei on the dynamics of core-collapse during infall and early post-bounce is studied performing spherically symmetric simulations in general relativity using a multigroup scheme for neutrino transport and full nuclear distributions in extended nuclear statistical equilibrium models. We thereby vary the prescription for EC rates on individual nuclei, the nuclear interaction for the EoS, the mass model for the nuclear statistical equilibrium distribution and the progenitor model. In agreement with previous works, we show that the individual EC rates are the most important source of uncertainty in the simulations, while the other inputs only marginally influence the results. A recently proposed analytic formula to extrapolate microscopic results on stable nuclei for EC rates to the neutron rich region, with a functional form motivated by nuclear-structure data and parameters fitted from large scale shell model calculations, is shown to lead to a sizable (16%) reduction of the electron fraction at bounce compared to more primitive prescriptions for the rates, leading to smaller inner core masses and slower shock propagation. We show that the EC process involves $\approx$ 130 different nuclear species around 86 Kr mainly in the N = 50 shell closure region, and establish a list of the most important nuclei to be studied in order to constrain the global rates.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a physically motivated analytic formula for electron-capture rates on neutron-rich nuclei, which incorporates isospin dependence and odd-even effects fitted to large-scale shell-model calculations, leads to a 16% reduction of the electron fraction at bounce compared to the standard Langanke parameterization, thereby reducing inner core masses and slowing shock propagation; this effect is shown to be the dominant source of uncertainty in core-collapse simulations, outweighing variations in the nuclear equation of state, mass model, or progenitor choice.

## Method

Spherically symmetric general-relativistic core-collapse supernova simulations were performed using two codes (CoCoNuT with multigroup neutrino transport and ACCEPT with a leakage scheme), employing extended nuclear statistical equilibrium models for the full nuclear distribution. The authors varied three prescriptions for electron-capture rates on individual nuclei (Bruenn's independent-particle model, the Langanke parameterization LMP(0), and the improved isospin-dependent parameterization LMP(3) from Raduta (2017)), two nuclear equations of state (DD2 with Hempel NSE and SLy4 with Gulminelli NSE), two mass models (Duflo-Zuker DZ10 and HFB-24), and different progenitor models, all starting from a 15 solar mass progenitor from Woosley (2002).

## Key numbers

* Electron fraction at bounce was reduced by 16% when using the LMP(3) prescription compared to the LMP(0) prescription, leading to smaller inner core masses and slower shock propagation.
* The electron-capture process involves approximately 130 different nuclear species around Kr-86, mainly in the N=50 shell closure region.
* The unknown binding energies of nuclei far from stability and possible shell quenching can increase the total electron-capture rate by up to 30%, as shown in Raduta (2016).
* The improved LMP(3) parameterization can reduce individual electron-capture rates on neutron-rich nuclei by two orders of magnitude or more compared to LMP(0) under certain thermodynamic conditions, leading to up to one order of magnitude reduction in the average rate summed over the nuclear distribution.
* The two extrapolation procedures for the LMP(3) parameter at extreme temperatures and densities (beyond the weak-rate table coverage) produced electron fraction differences of less than 3.5%.

## Context

This work extends the systematic study by Sullivan (2016), which showed that uncertainties in individual electron-capture rates induce stronger modifications to the inner core mass at bounce and neutrino luminosity peak than progenitor model or equation of state variations, but where rates were globally scaled by arbitrary factors of 2 to 10. The present paper uses physically motivated rate modifications from Raduta (2017) and performs self-consistent simulations, confirming that the N=50 shell closure region nuclei (identified by Sullivan (2016) and Titus (2017)) are the most important. The results enable future experimental and theoretical studies to focus on the approximately 130 most relevant nuclei around Kr-86, and the authors provide total electron-capture rates and neutrino-nucleus scattering opacities in the Compose database for use in other simulations.
