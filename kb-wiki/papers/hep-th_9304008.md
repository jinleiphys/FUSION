# Normalization of Scattering States, Scattering Phase Shifts and Levinson's Theorem

> We show that the normalization integral for the Schrödinger and Dirac scattering wave functions contains, besides the usual delta-function, a term proportional to the derivative of the phase shift. This term is of zero measure with respect to the integration over momentum variables and can be discarded in most cases. Yet it carries the full information on phase shifts and can be used for computation and manipulation of quantities which depend on phase shifts. In this paper we prove Levinson's theorem in a most general way which assumes only the completeness of states. In the case of a Dirac particle we obtain a new result valid for positive and negative energies separately. We also make a generalization of known results, for the phase shifts in the asymptotic limit of high energies, to the case of singular potentials. As an application we consider certain equations, which arise in a generalized interaction picture of quantum electrodynamics. Using the above mentioned results for the phase shifts we prove that any solution of these equations, which has a finite number of bound states, has a total charge zero. Furthermore, we show that in these equations the coupling constant is not a free parameter, but rather should be treated as an eigenvalue and hence must have a definite numerical value.

## Key claim

The normalization integral for Schrödinger and Dirac scattering wave functions contains a term proportional to the derivative of the phase shift, which is of zero measure with respect to momentum integration and can usually be discarded, yet carries full information on phase shifts and can be used to prove Levinson's theorem in a general way assuming only completeness of states.

## Method

The author derives exact expressions for the normalization integrals of radial Schrödinger and Dirac scattering wave functions, showing they contain a delta-function term plus a term proportional to the derivative of the phase shift. Using these normalization relations, the paper proves Levinson's theorem for both Schrödinger and Dirac particles, extends the high-energy asymptotic phase shift formula to singular potentials (including 1/r behavior at the origin), and applies these results to a set of equations arising in a generalized interaction picture of quantum electrodynamics.

## Key numbers

- For the Schrödinger case, the normalization integral contains the term Δ(k,k') = 2η'_l(k) when k=k', where η'_l(k) is the derivative of the phase shift with respect to momentum.
- For the Dirac case, the normalization integral contains the same Δ(k,k') term with the relativistic phase shift η_{εκ}(k) replacing η_l(k).
- Levinson's theorem for Schrödinger: η_l(0) = n_lπ, modified to η_0(0) = (n_0 + 1/2)π for l=0 with a zero-energy resonance, with additional possible solutions η_0(0) = (n_0 + 1/4)π.
- For Dirac particles, the new result gives η_{mκ}(0) = n_l^+π (without threshold resonance) and η_{-mκ}(0) = n_{l̅}^-π, with modifications for l=0 threshold resonances.
- High-energy asymptotic phase shift for Schrödinger: η_l(k) → k∫_0^∞ dr [√(1 - 2mV(r)/k²) - 1], which reduces to -(m/k)∫_0^∞ dr V(r) for potentials less singular than 1/r.
- High-energy asymptotic phase shift for Dirac: η_{εκ}(k) → k∫_0^∞ dr [√(1 - 2εV(r)/k²) - 1], which reduces to -(ε/k)∫_0^∞ dr V(r) for potentials less singular than 1/r.

## Context

This work extends and generalizes Levinson's theorem (Levinson 1949) and its modifications by Newton (Newton 1960) and by Ni and Ma (Ni, Ma) for the Schrödinger case, and by Ma and Ni (Ma and Ni) for the Dirac case. The paper also generalizes high-energy asymptotic phase shift results from Newton (Newton) and Parzen (Parzen) to singular potentials including 1/r behavior at the origin. The results enable analysis of equations arising in a generalized interaction picture of quantum electrodynamics, proving that any solution with a finite number of bound states has total charge zero and that the coupling constant must be treated as an eigenvalue with a definite numerical value.
