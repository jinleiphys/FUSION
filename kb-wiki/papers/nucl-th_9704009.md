# Compression and Expansion in Central Collisions

> Dynamics of central collisions of heavy nuclei in the energy range from few tens of MeV/nucleon to a couple of GeV/nucleon is discussed. As the beam energy increases and/or the impact parameter decreases, the maximum compression increases. It is argued that the hydrodynamic behavior of matter sets in the vicinity of balance energy. At higher energies shock fronts are observed to form within head-on reaction simulations, perpendicular to beam axis and separating hot compressed matter from cold. In the semicentral reactions a weak tangential discontinuity develops in-between these fronts. The hot compressed matter exposed to the vacuum in directions parallel to the shock fronts begins to expand collectively into these directions. The expansion affects particle angular distributions and mean energy components and further shapes of spectra and mean energies of particles emitted into any one direction. The variation of slopes and the relative yields measured within the FOPI collaboration are in a general agreement with the results of simulations. As to the FOPI data on stopping, they are consistent with the preference for transverse over the longitudinal motion in the head-on Au + Au collisions. Unfortunately, though, the data cannot be used to decide directly on that preference due to acceptance cuts. Tied to the spatial and temporal changes in the reactions are changes in the entropy per nucleon.

## Key claim

The hydrodynamic behavior of nuclear matter in central heavy-ion collisions sets in at the balance energy, which demarks the transition from attractive to repulsive flow; however, the FOPI data on stopping cannot directly confirm the predicted preference for transverse over longitudinal motion in head-on Au + Au collisions due to acceptance cuts.

## Method

Transport simulations based on the Boltzmann equation with a density- and isospin-dependent mean field, free NN cross-sections, and composite production were performed for Au + Au collisions at various beam energies and impact parameters. The dynamics were analyzed using baryon-density contour plots, radial velocity profiles, and entropy per baryon along and perpendicular to the beam axis, with comparisons to Rankine-Hugoniot shock-wave predictions and to FOPI experimental data on energy ratio (ERAT) distributions and light-particle spectra.

## Key numbers

- Balance energy in the 40Ar + 45Sc system rises with increasing reduced impact parameter b̂, as measured by Pak (1996) and calculated by Sofianos (1995).
- For symmetric systems, balance energy is higher for light systems and lower for heavy systems; a crude estimate gives E_bal ≈ 740/A^(2/3) + 160/A^(1/3) - 0.21 A^(2/3) MeV, where A is total mass.
- In 400 MeV/nucleon Au + Au head-on collisions, the transverse collective energy freezes out at about 40 MeV/nucleon, with about 10 MeV/nucleon of longitudinal collective energy, so net collective energy constitutes about 50% of the kinetic energy in the center-of-mass frame.
- The width of the tangential discontinuity at b = 7 fm in 400 MeV/nucleon Au + Au is δ ≈ 1.7 fm at t = 13.5 fm/c, consistent with δ = √(η t / m_N ρ).
- For protons from 400 MeV/nucleon reactions, the upper limit on the flow slope from expansion anisotropy alone is 0.22, while measured slopes reach values twice as high.
- The FOPI data at 250 MeV/nucleon allow only a small reduction (ν ≈ 0.05-0.15) in NN cross-sections in simulations with a stiff equation of state, and hardly any reduction with a soft equation of state; values ν ≥ 0.4 are required for transparency to prevail over transverse expansion in b = 0 Au + Au collisions.

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic description of heavy-ion collisions from the balance energy studies of Pak (1996), Westmeier (1993), and Buta (1995) to higher energies where shock fronts and tangential discontinuities develop. The simulations build on earlier transport-model calculations by Klakow (1993) and Danielewicz (1995), and the comparisons to FOPI data from Reisdorf (1995), Wessels (1993, 1994), Poggi (1995), and Tsang (1996) test the hydrodynamic scenario against experimental observables. The results enable future Phase II FOPI measurements to directly observe ERAT distributions above 2, which would confirm the predicted transverse expansion dominance in head-on heavy-ion collisions.
