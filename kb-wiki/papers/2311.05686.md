# Floquet insulators and lattice fermions beyond naive time discretization

> Periodically driven quantum systems known as Floquet insulators can host topologically protected bound states known as "$π$ modes" that exhibit response at half the frequency of the drive. Such states can also appear in undriven lattice field theories when time is discretized as a result of fermion doubling, raising the question of whether these two phenomena could be connected. Recently, we demonstrated such a connection at the level of an explicit mapping between the spectra of a continuous-time Floquet model and a discrete-time undriven lattice fermion model. However, this mapping relied on a symmetry of the single-particle spectrum that is not present for generic drive parameters. Inspired by the example of the temporal Wilson term in lattice field theory, in this paper we extend this mapping to the full drive parameter space by allowing the parameters of the discrete-time model to be frequency-dependent. The spectra of the resulting lattice fermion models exactly match the quasienergy spectrum of the Floquet model in the thermodynamic limit. Our results demonstrate that spectral features characteristic of beyond-equilibrium physics in Floquet systems can be replicated in static systems with appropriate time discretization.

## Key claim

The authors extend a previously established mapping between the spectrum of a continuous-time Floquet insulator and a discrete-time undriven lattice fermion model from a restricted line in parameter space (where the quasienergy spectrum exhibits \(\pi\) pairing) to the full drive parameter space, by allowing the parameters of the discrete-time model to be frequency-dependent, inspired by the temporal Wilson term in lattice field theory.

## Method

The authors construct a modified Wilson-Dirac (MWD) action in Euclidean spacetime with frequency-dependent mass \(m(p_0)\) and Wilson parameter \(R_1(p_0)\), and demand that the sine-transformed quasienergy eigenvalues of the Floquet model match the eigenvalues of the MWD operator when evaluated at the corresponding frequencies. They impose an additional condition, Eq. (imp), to obtain unique expressions for \(m(p_0)\) and \(R_1(p_0)\), and extrapolate these solutions over the full frequency range \(-\pi/T \leq p_0 < \pi/T\). The mapping is also performed for a modified Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) model with frequency-dependent hopping parameters, as discussed in an appendix.

## Key numbers

* The temporal lattice spacing is set to \(a_0 = T\), the drive period of the Floquet model, and the spatial lattice spacing to \(a_1 = 1\).
* The quasienergy gap closes along the lines \(\eta = |\beta|\) in the phase diagram defined by \(\beta = t_0/T - \pi/4\) and \(\eta = t_1/T - \pi/4\).
* For the \(\beta=0\) line, the SSH model parameters that match the Floquet spectrum are \(u = (1 + \sin(2\eta))/(2T)\) and \(v = 1/T - u\).
* For the \(\beta=0\) line, the Wilson-Dirac model parameters that match the Floquet spectrum are \(m = -\sin(2\eta)/T\) and \(R_1 = 1/(2T) - m/2\).
* The quantity \(2R_1(R_1 + m)\) is fixed to \(\cos^2(2\eta)/(2T^2)\) for all \(\beta\) and \(\eta\).
* The frequency-dependent mass is given by \(m(p_0) = \pm (1/T)\sqrt{\sin^2(2\eta) + \delta(p_0)}\), where \(\delta(p_0) = (\tan(2\beta)\cos(p_0T) - \sin(2\eta)/\cos(2\beta))^2 - \sin^2(2\eta)\).

## Context

This work extends the mapping between Floquet insulators and lattice fermions previously established by the same authors (Iadecola, Sen, Sivertsen, 2023) for the \(\beta=0\) line, where the quasienergy spectrum is \(\pi\)-paired. It draws inspiration from the temporal Wilson term in lattice field theory, which introduces a frequency-dependent mass to lift the degeneracy of fermion doublers, as reviewed in the context of the Wilson-Dirac operator. The results enable the replication of spectral features characteristic of Floquet systems, including boundary zero and \(\pi\) modes, in static systems with appropriate time discretization, and provide a framework for studying Floquet symmetry-protected topological phases using discrete-time lattice models.
