# On the nature of spin, inertia and gravity of a moving canonical particle

> It is suggested that a moving canonical particle interacts with a vacuum regarded as a "soft" cellular space. The interaction results into the emergence of elementary excitations of space - inertons - surrounding the particle. It is assumed that such a motion leads not only to the spatial oscillation of the particle along a path but to the oscillation of the particle centre-of-mass as well. This phenomenon culminating in the anisotropic pulsation of the particle is associated with the notion of spin. The particle-space interaction is treated as the origin of the matter waves, which are identified with the particle inertia and inertons surrounding the moving particle are considered as carriers of its inert properties. Inertons are also identified with real carriers of the gravitational interaction and the range of the particle gravitational potential is evaluated by the inerton cloud amplitude $Λ=λc/v$, where $λ$ is the de Broglie wavelength, $c$ and $v$ are the velocity of light and the particle respectively. The nature of the phase transition that occurs in a quantum system when one should pass from the description based on the Schroedinger formalism to that of resting on the Dirac one is explained in detail.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a moving canonical particle's interaction with a "soft" cellular vacuum generates elementary excitations called inertons, and that the particle's anisotropic pulsation (oscillation of its centre-of-mass) constitutes the physical origin of spin. The claim is conditional on the particle being treated as a solid, elastic object (like a drop) moving through a fine-grained space, and the spin arises specifically from the two possible phase states of these radial pulsations (forward or backward relative to translational motion).

## Method

The author constructs a Lagrangian formalism for a relativistic particle interacting with a vacuum medium, introducing separate spatial and intrinsic (pulsation) coordinates for both the particle and its emitted inerton cloud. Euler-Lagrange equations are solved for the intrinsic variables, yielding oscillatory solutions for the particle's centre-of-mass displacement and the inerton cloud's intrinsic motion. The Hamiltonian is then derived and reduced to a form describing a "bare" particle with spatial and intrinsic harmonic oscillator terms, from which Hamilton-Jacobi equations and action-angle variables lead to de Broglie relations and a wave equation for spin.

## Key numbers

- The range of the particle's gravitational potential is evaluated by the inerton cloud amplitude: $\Lambda = \lambda c / v_0$, where $\lambda$ is the de Broglie wavelength, $c$ is the speed of light, and $v_0$ is the particle velocity.
- The mean velocity of the particle along its trajectory is $\bar v = v_0 (1 - 2/\pi)$ from Eq. (24).
- The spatial oscillation period is $2T$, and the frequency $\nu = 1/2T$ yields the quantum relation $E = h\nu$ (Eq. 48).
- The action increments over one oscillation period give $J = p_0 \lambda = h$ and $\iota_\alpha = \pi_{\alpha 0} \lambda_\alpha = h$, leading to the de Broglie relation $h/\lambda = M v_0$ (Eq. 47).
- The inerton cloud extends $\lambda$ along the particle path and $2\Lambda/\pi$ in transverse directions.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous submicroscopic mechanics (Krasnoholovets, 1997-2000) which derived the Schrodinger equation from particle-space interaction, now showing how the Dirac equation formalism can emerge from the same framework. It builds on earlier spin models including Schrodinger's Zitterbewegung (1930), Frenkel's proper mechanical moment (1926), Barut and Zanghi's real Zitterbewegung (1984), and Oudet's fluid-mass electron model (1994). The inerton concept is presented as a substitute for gravitons in general relativity, and the paper claims experimental manifestation of inerton clouds surrounding electrons has been demonstrated (Krasnoholovets, 2000). The approach enables a unified treatment of spin, inertia, and gravity within a single vacuum-medium model.
