# Decay of superheavy nuclei based on the random forest algorithm

> How nuclides decay in the superheavy region is key information for investigating new elements beyond oganesson and the island of stability. The Random Forest algorithm is applied to study the competition between different decay modes in the superheavy region, including $α$ decay, $β^-$ decay, $β^+$ decay, electron capture and spontaneous fission. The observed half-lives and dominant decay mode are well reproduced. The dominant decay mode of 96.9 % nuclei beyond $^{212}$Po is correctly described. $α$ decay is predicted to be the dominant decay mode for isotopes in new elements $Z = 119 - 122$, except for spontaneous fission in some even-even ones because of the odd-even staggering effect. The predicted half-lives show the existence of a long-lived spontaneous fission island at the southwest of $^{298}$Fl caused by the competition of nuclear deformation and Coulomb repulsion. More understanding of spontaneous fission, especially beyond $^{286}$Fl, is crucial to search for new elements and the island of stability.

## Key claim

The Random Forest algorithm can reproduce the dominant decay mode for 96.9% of nuclei beyond 212Po, but the prediction of spontaneous fission remains the key uncertainty for extrapolating to new elements, especially because the odd-even staggering effect makes spontaneous fission competitive with alpha decay in even-even isotopes of Z=120 and 122.

## Method

The authors calculated partial half-lives for alpha decay, beta-minus decay, beta-plus decay, electron capture, and spontaneous fission using semi-empirical formulas (Universal Decay Law for alpha, a three-parameter SF3 formula for spontaneous fission, and a Gamow-Teller transition formula for beta decays). They then trained a Random Forest algorithm with 100,000 trees on the residuals of these formulas, using features Z, N, A, the odevity of Z and N, and decay energy (or fission barrier for spontaneous fission), with 11 leaves per tree. The trained model was applied to predict decay modes for nuclei with Z greater than or equal to 84 and N greater than or equal to 128, using experimental masses where available and theoretical masses from WS4 and UNEDF0 models for unmeasured nuclei.

## Key numbers

- 96.9% of 445 nuclei beyond 212Po had their dominant decay mode correctly described when using experimental reaction energies (431 out of 445)
- RMSE of log10(T1/2) for the dominant decay mode was 0.62 when using WS4 energies and 0.67 when using UNEDF0 energies
- The consistency rate of dominant decay mode between using WS4 and UNEDF0 energies decreased from 82.2% to 66.2% during extrapolation
- When calculated energies replaced all experimental ones, the number with consistent decay mode reduced to 72.6% (WS4) and 64% (UNEDF0), with RMSE increasing to 2.07 and 2.64 respectively
- The predicted half-life of 252Cm is 1.43 days (compared to an upper limit of 2 days from 1966)
- The SF3 formula for spontaneous fission achieved RMSE of 3.070 for Z less than 104 and 1.267 for Z greater than or equal to 104 before RF training, improving to 1.195 and 0.825 after RF training

## Context

This work extends machine learning approaches to nuclear physics that previously used neural networks for nuclear mass residuals (Utama 2016, Niu 2018) and Decision Trees for binding energy residuals (Carnini 2020), but is the first to apply the Random Forest algorithm to study competition between multiple decay modes in the superheavy region. The results enable predictions for elements Z=119-122 and identify a long-lived spontaneous fission island at the southwest of 298Fl, while highlighting that spontaneous fission beyond 286Fl requires better understanding for future searches at facilities such as CAFE2 and SHANS2 in Lanzhou.
