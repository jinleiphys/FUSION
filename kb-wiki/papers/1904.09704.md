# The Role of Lattice QCD in Searches for Violations of Fundamental Symmetries and Signals for New Physics

> This document is one of a series of whitepapers from the USQCD collaboration. Here, we discuss opportunities for Lattice Quantum Chromodynamics (LQCD) in the research frontier in fundamental symmetries and signals for new physics. LQCD, in synergy with effective field theories and nuclear many-body studies, provides theoretical support to ongoing and planned experimental programs in searches for electric dipole moments of the nucleon, nuclei and atoms, decay of the proton, $n$-$\overline{n}$ oscillations, neutrinoless double-$β$ decay of a nucleus, conversion of muon to electron, precision measurements of weak decays of the nucleon and of nuclei, precision isotope-shift spectroscopy, as well as direct dark matter detection experiments using nuclear targets. This whitepaper details the objectives of the LQCD program in the area of Fundamental Symmetries within the USQCD collaboration, identifies priorities that can be addressed within the next five years, and elaborates on the areas that will likely demand a high degree of innovation in both numerical and analytical frontiers of the LQCD research.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD, in synergy with effective field theories and nuclear many-body studies, provides essential theoretical support to ongoing and planned experimental programs searching for violations of fundamental symmetries, but many of the required calculations are classified as "challenging" or "extremely challenging" and will demand at least an order of magnitude increase in computational resources or new theoretical and algorithmic innovations to reach the required precision within the next five years.

## Method

The USQCD collaboration's whitepaper surveys the role of Lattice Quantum Chromodynamics (LQCD) in multiple areas of fundamental symmetries research, including electric dipole moments (EDMs), proton decay, neutron-antineutron oscillations, neutrinoless double-beta decay, muon-to-electron conversion, precision weak decays, isotope-shift spectroscopy, and dark matter detection. For each area, the authors assess the current status of LQCD calculations, categorize the difficulty of near-term goals as "straightforward," "challenging," or "extremely challenging," and outline the computational resources and methodological innovations needed to achieve results that can be matched to experimental observables through multi-scale effective field theory frameworks.

## Key numbers

- Current limit on neutron EDM: |d_n| < 3 × 10^{-26} e·cm (90% CL), from Baker (2006) and Afach (2015)
- Planned neutron EDM experiments aim for sensitivity of approximately 5 × 10^{-28} e·cm, an improvement of 1-2 orders of magnitude
- Storage-ring proton EDM experiment potential sensitivity: 10^{-29} e·cm, from Anastassopoulos (2015)
- Proton lifetime constraints: τ_p > 8.2 × 10^{33} years for p → π^0 e^+ (Nishino, 2009) and τ_p > 1.4 × 10^{34} years (Babu, 2013)
- Proton lifetime constraint for p → K^+ ν^-: τ_p > 5.9 × 10^{33} years (Abe, 2014)
- Target precision for proton decay matrix elements in next 5 years: less than 10%

## Context

This whitepaper is one of seven from the USQCD collaboration (2018) that update the collaboration's previous whitepapers from 2013, and it complements companion whitepapers on quark and lepton flavor physics (Lehner, 2018) and neutrino-nucleus scattering (Kronfeld, 2018). The document extends the program of LQCD calculations that previously constrained the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix and CP violation in the kaon sector, now addressing the broader fundamental symmetries frontier identified as a primary thrust in the 2007 and 2015 NSAC Long Range Plans. The work enables interpretation of next-generation experiments including DUNE, Hyper-Kamiokande, and ton-scale neutrinoless double-beta decay experiments by providing nonperturbative QCD inputs that connect high-scale new-physics models to low-energy nuclear observables.
