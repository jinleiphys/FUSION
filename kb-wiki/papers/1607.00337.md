# Late Time Emission of Prompt Fission Gamma Rays

> The emission of prompt fission $γ$ rays within a few nanoseconds to a few microseconds following the scission point is studied in the Hauser-Feshbach formalism applied to the deexcitation of primary excited fission fragments. Neutron and $γ$-ray evaporations from fully accelerated fission fragments are calculated in competition at each stage of the decay, and the role of isomers in the fission products, before $β$-decay, is analyzed. The time evolution of the average total $γ$-ray energy, average total $γ$-ray multiplicity, and fragment-specific $γ$-ray spectra, is presented in the case of neutron-induced fission reactions of $^{235}$U and $^{239}$Pu, as well as spontaneous fission of $^{252}$Cf. The production of specific isomeric states is calculated and compared to available experimental data. About 7% of all prompt fission $γ$ rays are predicted to be emitted between 10 nsec and 5 $μ$sec following fission, in the case of $^{235}$U and $^{239}$Pu $(n_{\rm th},f)$ reactions, and up to 3% in the case of $^{252}$Cf spontaneous fission. The cumulative average total $γ$-ray energy increases by 2 to 5% in the same time interval. Finally, those results are shown to be robust against significant changes in the model input parameters.

## Key claim

About 7% of all prompt fission gamma rays are predicted to be emitted between 10 nsec and 5 microsec following fission for thermal neutron-induced fission of 235U and 239Pu, and up to 3% for 252Cf spontaneous fission, with the cumulative average total gamma-ray energy increasing by 2 to 5% in the same time interval; these results are claimed to be robust against significant changes in model input parameters.

## Method

The authors used the Monte Carlo Hauser-Feshbach code CGMF to simulate the statistical decay of primary excited fission fragments following neutron-induced fission of 235U and 239Pu and spontaneous fission of 252Cf. Neutron and gamma-ray emissions from fully accelerated fission fragments were calculated in competition at each stage of the decay, using known nuclear structure data including isomeric states and their half-lives to determine whether decay occurs within a chosen time coincidence window. The time evolution of average total gamma-ray energy, average total gamma-ray multiplicity, and fragment-specific gamma-ray spectra was computed by randomly sampling the exponential decay law according to an input time window in coincidence with the fission event.

## Key numbers

- About 7% of all prompt fission gamma rays are emitted between 10 nsec and 5 microsec following fission for 235U and 239Pu (n_th,f) reactions
- Up to 3% of all prompt fission gamma rays are emitted between 10 nsec and 5 microsec following fission for 252Cf spontaneous fission
- Cumulative average total gamma-ray energy increases by 2 to 5% in the same time interval (10 nsec to 5 microsec)
- For 252Cf (sf), average prompt gamma-ray multiplicity increases from 7.79 at 1 nsec to 8.27 at 5 microsec (more than 5% increase)
- CGMF-calculated average total gamma-ray energies at 10 nsec: 6.32 MeV for 235U, 6.55 MeV for 239Pu, and 6.73 MeV for 252Cf (sf)
- ENDF/B-VII.1 values for comparison: 6.60 MeV for 235U and 6.74 MeV for 239Pu
- Experimental value for 252Cf (sf) from Oberstedt et al.: 6.65 +/- 0.10 MeV
- About 10% of prompt gamma rays are emitted after 1 nsec for thermal neutron-induced fission of 235U and 239Pu
- The 162 nsec, 1.69 MeV, 6+ isomer in 134Te decays via three gamma lines at 115, 297, and 1279 keV

## Context

This work extends the Hauser-Feshbach statistical treatment of prompt fission gamma-ray emission by explicitly modeling the time dependence of emissions from isomeric states in fission fragments, building on earlier experimental studies of isomeric gamma rays by Sund (1974) for 235U and 239Pu and by John (1970) for 252Cf spontaneous fission. The calculations use the CGMF code previously developed by Kawano (2010) and Talou (2011), and the results are compared to experimental data from Billnert (2013), Chyzh (2013, 2014), and Verbinski (1973), as well as evaluated data from the ENDF/B-VII.1 library. The work enables improved treatment of prompt gamma-ray timing in transport simulations and better interpretation of experiments measuring prompt fission neutron spectra and average neutron multiplicities, where late isomeric decays can introduce systematic biases.
