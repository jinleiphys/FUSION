# Quantum simulation of non-equilibrium dynamics and thermalization in the Schwinger model

> We present simulations of non-equilibrium dynamics of quantum field theories on digital quantum computers. As a representative example, we consider the Schwinger model, a 1+1 dimensional U(1) gauge theory, coupled through a Yukawa-type interaction to a thermal environment described by a scalar field theory. We use the Hamiltonian formulation of the Schwinger model discretized on a spatial lattice. With the thermal scalar fields traced out, the Schwinger model can be treated as an open quantum system and its real-time dynamics are governed by a Lindblad equation in the Markovian limit. The interaction with the environment ultimately drives the system to thermal equilibrium. In the quantum Brownian motion limit, the Lindblad equation is related to a field theoretical Caldeira-Leggett equation. By using the Stinespring dilation theorem with ancillary qubits, we perform studies of both the non-equilibrium dynamics and the preparation of a thermal state in the Schwinger model using IBM's simulator and quantum devices. The real-time dynamics of field theories as open quantum systems and the thermal state preparation studied here are relevant for a variety of applications in nuclear and particle physics, quantum information and cosmology.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that non-equilibrium dynamics and thermalization of a quantum field theory (the Schwinger model) can be simulated on a digital quantum computer by treating the system as an open quantum system coupled to a thermal environment, but only in the quantum Brownian motion limit where the environment temperature is much larger than the characteristic energy gap of the system and the Markovian approximation holds.

## Method

The authors discretize the 1+1 dimensional Schwinger model (a U(1) gauge theory) on a spatial lattice using the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian formulation with staggered fermions and a finite-dimensional truncation of the gauge field Hilbert space. They couple this system to a thermal scalar field environment via a Yukawa-type interaction, trace out the environment, and derive a Lindblad equation in the Markovian limit. The non-unitary time evolution is simulated on IBM's quantum devices using the Stinespring dilation theorem with ancillary qubits, implementing the Lindblad evolution through a quantum circuit with unitary operators for the system Hamiltonian and the Lindblad operator.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing set to \(a=1\), with fermion mass \(m=0.1/a\), charge \(e=1/a\), and inverse temperature \(\beta=0.1a\) (i.e., \(T=10/a\))
- For \(N=2\) spatial lattice sites, the simulation uses 2 qubits for the system plus 1 ancilla qubit, with \(N_{\rm cycle}=200\) time steps
- One cycle of the quantum algorithm consists of 7-13 CNOT gates and approximately 100 single-qubit gates
- Numerical solutions (RK4) for \(\langle E^2 \rangle\) are shown up to \(N=8\) spatial lattice sites, with results beginning to converge as \(N \rightarrow 8\)
- The quantum device results (ibmq_montreal) use up to \(N_{\rm cycle}=4\) cycles and show good agreement with noiseless simulations up to \(t\approx 6\)

## Context

This work extends prior quantum simulations of the Schwinger model as a closed system (Muschik 2016, Martinez 2016, Klco 2018, Chakraborty 2020, Shaw 2020) and recent studies of thermalization dynamics on analog quantum computers (Zhou 2021). The open quantum system formalism connects to approaches used for heavy quark bound states and jets in quark-gluon plasma studies (Young 2010, Akamatsu 2011, Brambilla 2017, Yao 2018). The results enable future simulations of non-equilibrium dynamics of non-Abelian gauge theories in higher spatial dimensions and demonstrate a method for preparing thermal states on quantum computers for finite-temperature field theory applications.
