# The Evolution of Neutrino Astronomy

> How did neutrino astronomy evolve? Are there any useful lessons for astronomers and physicists embarking on new observational ventures today? We answer the first question; the reader can can decide if there are any useful parallels for other fields.

# The Evolution of Neutrino Astronomy

## Key claim

The authors argue that the historical development of solar neutrino astronomy, from the first chlorine experiment through the gallium detectors and water Cherenkov instruments, demonstrates that measuring something new with reasonable accuracy can lead to important discoveries, often not what was originally sought. The paper presents this as a personal retrospective, acknowledging that all history is incomplete and distorted by the passage of time and fading memories.

## Method

The authors recount the sequence of experimental and theoretical developments in solar neutrino research from 1958 through the late 1990s, based on their direct involvement as the proposers of the Homestake chlorine experiment. They describe the radiochemical detection technique using perchloroethylene (C₂Cl₄) to capture solar neutrinos via the reaction ³⁷Cl(νₑ, e⁻)³⁷Ar, the subsequent development of gallium detectors (SAGE and GALLEX) sensitive to pp neutrinos, and the emergence of electronic detectors such as Kamiokande and SuperKamiokande using water Cherenkov technology.

## Key numbers

- The total capital expenditure for the Homestake chlorine experiment was $0.6 million (in approximately 1965 dollars), covering excavation of the cavity in the Homestake Gold Mine, construction of the tank, and purchase of the liquid.
- The chlorine detector contained 100,000 gallons of perchloroethylene.
- The ⁸B neutrinos are produced in only 2 of every 10⁴ terminations of the basic pp fusion chain.
- The ⁸B neutrino flux depends upon approximately the 25th power of the solar central temperature.
- Solar neutrinos are observed in the energy range below 15 MeV.
- Radiochemical experiments detected typically 25 to 50 events per year, while newer electronic experiments measure several thousand events per year.
- The gallium experiments (SAGE and GALLEX) required about three times the world's annual production of gallium.
- The Russian SAGE experiment secured 60 tons of gallium free for the duration of a solar neutrino experiment.
- The SuperKamiokande detector contains 50,000 tons of pure water.
- Backgrounds in radiochemical experiments can be as low as one event in three months.

## Context

This paper extends the authors' earlier reviews (Bahcall and Davis 1976, 1982) of the solar neutrino problem, which began with the 1968 discrepancy between chlorine measurements and standard solar model predictions. The work describes how the MSW effect (Mikheyev and Smirnov 1985, building on Wolfenstein) and the Gribov-Pontecorvo (1969) neutrino oscillation hypothesis became the consensus explanation for the deficit. The paper enables future neutrino astronomy beyond the solar system, particularly detection of very high energy (∼10¹⁵ eV) neutrinos from gamma-ray bursts using km²-scale detectors such as AMANDA, ICECUBE, ANTARES, and NESTOR.
