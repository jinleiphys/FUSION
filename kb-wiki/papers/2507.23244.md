# Uncovering the nature of low-lying dipole states with QRPA calculations: is Z=42 the answer?

> The pygmy dipole resonance (PDR), marked by enhanced electric dipole strength near particle emission energies, offers a unique perspective on the collective dynamics of nuclear structure. Its precise nature, particularly its degree of collectivity, remains a topic of debate. In this study, we investigate low-energy dipole excitations in spherical Mo isotopes ($^{82}$Mo to $^{98}$Mo) using a fully consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) and quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) framework. We observe that an enhancement in dipole strength near particle emission energies is closely correlated with the development of either neutron or proton skins. To further understand the nature of this enhancement, we examine the behavior of proton and neutron transition densities. Our analysis shows that these (low-lying dipole) states exhibit distinct characteristics involving in-phase oscillations within the nucleus and neutron- or proton-dominated oscillations at the surface, while the primary contributor to this enhancement displays an intricate underlying structure. We also investigate the collectivity of these excitations by analyzing two-quasiparticle fragmentations and relative energy shifts. Our findings reveal that skin oscillation states exhibit moderate collectivity, as indicated by substantial configuration mixing, but limited coherence, whereas the GDR states exhibit strong coherence and large energy shifts characteristic of fully developed collective motion. This study paves the way for future investigations into the collective nature of low-energy dipole states in the enhancement region, particularly in deformed nuclei, where nuclear shape effects may play a crucial role in their excitation dynamics.

## Key claim
The paper claims that the enhancement of electric dipole strength near particle emission energies in spherical Mo isotopes is closely correlated with the development of either neutron or proton skins, but the primary contributor to this enhancement displays an intricate underlying structure rather than simple collective motion, and skin oscillation states exhibit only moderate collectivity with limited coherence.

## Method
The authors performed fully consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) and quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) calculations using the Gogny D1M parametrization for spherical even-even Mo isotopes from $^{82}$Mo to $^{98}$Mo. They analyzed the electric dipole response using both isovector and isoscalar dipole operators, examined radial transition densities to characterize neutron and proton oscillations, and quantified collectivity by computing fragmentation numbers (counting two-quasiparticle configurations exceeding the average weight) and energy shifts relative to unperturbed two-quasiparticle energies.

## Key numbers
- Neutron skin thickness $\delta_{np}$ ranges from -0.102 fm in $^{82}$Mo (proton skin) to +0.096 fm in $^{98}$Mo (neutron skin), with $^{90}$Mo having $\delta_{np} = 0.002$ fm as the near-zero reference
- HFB energy for $^{84}$Mo with $n=10$ basis is -703.80 MeV, differing by less than 0.2% from the $n=16$ value of -705.20 MeV
- HFB energy for $^{96}$Mo with $n=10$ basis is -835.91 MeV, compared to -837.28 MeV for $n=16$
- Charge radius $R_{\text{ch}}$ for $^{84}$Mo is 4.26 fm and for $^{96}$Mo is 4.32 fm, stable across basis sizes from $n=10$ to $n=16$
- Total number of two-quasiparticle configurations $N_{\mathrm{2qp}} = 12152$ for $K^{\pi}=0^{-}$ in the chosen basis spanning 11 major oscillator shells

## Context
This work extends previous theoretical studies of the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) in Mo isotopes, which had been investigated experimentally via photon scattering (Rusev 2006, 2009), $(\alpha,\alpha'\gamma)$ scattering (Derya 2013), and nuclear resonance fluorescence (Roming 2013), as well as theoretically using the Gogny D1M+QRPA gamma-ray strength function model (Heim 2021) and interacting boson approximation calculations (Pascu 2012). The study enables future investigations into the collective nature of low-energy dipole states in deformed nuclei, where nuclear shape effects may play a crucial role in excitation dynamics.
