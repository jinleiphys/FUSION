# Muonic-Atom Spectroscopy and Impact on Nuclear Structure and Precision QED Theory

> Recent progress in laser and x-ray spectroscopy of muonic atoms offers promising long-term possibilities at the intersection of atomic, nuclear and particle physics. In muonic hydrogen, laser spectroscopy measurements will determine the ground-state hyperfine splitting (HFS) and additionally improve the Lamb shift by a factor of 5. Precision spectroscopy with cryogenic microcalorimeters has the potential to significantly improve the charge radii of the light nuclei in the $Z=3-8$ range. Complementary progress in precision should be achieved on the theory of nucleon- and nuclear-structure effects. The impact of this muonic-atom spectroscopy program will be amplified by the upcoming results from H and He$^+$ spectroscopy, simple molecules such as HD$^+$ and Penning trap measurements. In this broader context, one can test ab-initio nuclear theories, bound-state QED for two- or three-body systems, and determine fundamental constants, such as the Rydberg ($R_\infty$) and the fine-structure ($α$) constants.

## Key claim

Laser and x-ray spectroscopy of muonic atoms can determine nuclear charge radii and test bound-state QED, but the full impact depends on complementary progress in the theory of nucleon- and nuclear-structure effects, as well as upcoming results from hydrogen, He\(^+\), HD\(^+\), and Penning trap measurements.

## Method

The paper reviews three experimental approaches: laser spectroscopy of muonic hydrogen (muH) for ground-state hyperfine splitting (HFS) and Lamb shift measurements; x-ray spectroscopy with cryogenic microcalorimeters for 2P-1S transitions in light muonic atoms (Z=3-8); and crystal spectrometer measurements of muonic carbon transitions. These are combined with theoretical calculations using chiral effective field theory (chiEFT), dispersive frameworks, and ab-initio nuclear theory to extract nuclear charge radii and test bound-state QED.

## Key numbers

- Laser spectroscopy of muonic hydrogen will improve the Lamb shift measurement by a factor of 5
- The ground-state HFS in muonic hydrogen has a transition wavelength of 6.8 micrometers
- The laser fluence needed for the HFS experiment is 4 orders of magnitude larger than used for the 2S-2P transition measurement in muonic hydrogen
- A 5 ppm precise determination of mu\(^{12}\)C and mu\(^{13}\)C 2P-1S transition energies yielded a charge radius accuracy of 2 x 10\(^{-3}\) fm
- Cryogenic microcalorimeters have demonstrated energy resolutions of a few to a few tens of eV in the 1-400 keV range
- Line centers can be determined to better than 10 ppm with appropriate calibration
- The 1S-2S transition measurement in hydrogen combined with the muonic hydrogen proton radius determines Rydberg constant R\(_\infty\) with relative accuracy 8 x 10\(^{-13}\)
- Hydrogen theory has been verified at the 1 x 10\(^{-12}\) level
- HD\(^+\) theory has been verified at the 2 x 10\(^{-11}\) level
- Bound electron g-factors can be tested at the 4 x 10\(^{-11}\) level of accuracy
- Most charge radii for stable isotopes in the Z=3-8 range have accuracies spanning 0.3-1.4%
- The two-photon-exchange contribution to the mu\(^4\)He\(^+\) Lamb shift is E\(^{\langle 2PE \rangle A+N}_{2P-2S}\) = 9.34(20)_N(11)_A meV

## Context

This paper extends the work of the CREMA Collaboration (Pohl 2010, Antognini 2013) that measured the proton charge radius via muonic hydrogen spectroscopy, which created the "proton-radius puzzle." It builds on subsequent muonic atom measurements of the deuteron and \(^{3,4}\)He radii (Pohl 2016, Krauth 2021) and proposes extending these methods to the Z=3-8 range using cryogenic microcalorimeters. The program enables tests of ab-initio nuclear theories (as developed by the chiEFT community for A=3 to A=18), bound-state QED tests in two- and three-body systems, and determination of fundamental constants including the Rydberg constant and fine-structure constant.
