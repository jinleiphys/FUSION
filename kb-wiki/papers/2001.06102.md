# Gravitational waves or deconfined quarks: what causes the premature collapse of neutron stars born in short gamma-ray bursts?

> We infer the collapse times of long-lived neutron stars into black holes using the X-ray afterglows of 18 short gamma-ray bursts. We then apply hierarchical inference to infer properties of the neutron star equation of state and dominant spin-down mechanism. We measure the maximum non-rotating neutron star mass $M_\mathrm{TOV} = 2.31 ^{+0.36}_{-0.21} M_{\odot}$ and constrain the fraction of remnants spinning down predominantly through gravitational-wave emission to $η= 0.69 ^{+0.21}_{-0.39}$ with $68 \%$ uncertainties. In principle, this method can determine the difference between hadronic and quark equation of states. In practice, however, the data is not yet informative with indications that these neutron stars do not have hadronic equation of states at the $1σ$ level. These inferences all depend on the underlying progenitor mass distribution for short gamma-ray bursts produced by binary neutron star mergers. The recently announced gravitational-wave detection of GW190425 suggests this underlying distribution is different from the locally-measured population of double neutron stars. We show that $M_\mathrm{TOV}$ and $η$ constraints depend on the fraction of binary mergers that form through a distribution consistent with the locally-measured population and a distribution that can explain GW190425. The more binaries that form from the latter distribution, the larger $M_\mathrm{TOV}$ needs to be to satisfy the X-ray observations. Our measurements above are marginalised over this unknown fraction. If instead, we assume GW190425 is not a binary neutron star merger, i.e the underlying mass distribution of double neutron stars is the same as observed locally, we measure $M_\mathrm{TOV} = 2.26 ^{+0.31}_{-0.17} M_{\odot}$.

## Key claim

The paper infers that the maximum non-rotating neutron star mass is $M_\mathrm{TOV} = 2.31^{+0.36}_{-0.21} M_\odot$ and that approximately 70% of neutron stars born in short gamma-ray bursts spin down predominantly through gravitational-wave emission, but these results depend on the unknown mixing fraction between two progenitor mass distributions (the locally-observed double neutron star population and a population that can explain GW190425), and the data only indicates a preference against hadronic equations of state at the $1\sigma$ level rather than providing a definitive distinction.

## Method

The authors fit a collapsing magnetar model to the X-ray afterglow lightcurves of 18 short gamma-ray bursts observed by the Swift telescope, measuring the collapse time of each neutron star into a black hole. They then apply hierarchical Bayesian inference to infer hyperparameters describing the neutron star equation of state and dominant spin-down mechanism, marginalizing over the unknown post-merger remnant mass distribution and initial spin period. The progenitor mass distribution is modeled as a mixture of two Gaussian distributions representing the galactic double neutron star population and a broader neutron star population that can explain GW190425, with the mixing fraction $\epsilon$ treated as a free parameter.

## Key numbers

- Maximum non-rotating neutron star mass $M_\mathrm{TOV} = 2.31^{+0.36}_{-0.21} M_\odot$ (68% uncertainties), marginalized over the unknown mixing fraction between progenitor populations
- Fraction of remnants spinning down predominantly through gravitational-wave emission $\eta = 0.69^{+0.21}_{-0.39}$ (68% uncertainties)
- Assuming $\epsilon = 0$ (mass distribution consistent with local double neutron stars but not GW190425): $M_\mathrm{TOV} = 2.26^{+0.31}_{-0.17} M_\odot$
- Assuming $\epsilon = 0.5$: $M_\mathrm{TOV} = 2.30^{+0.38}_{-0.19} M_\odot$
- Bayes factors for the collapsing magnetar model versus the fireball-shock model range from $\ln BF_{M/F} = 3$ (GRB150120A) to $\ln BF_{M/F} = 1874$ (GRB160821B) across the 18 bursts

## Context

This work extends the approach of Ravi and Lasky (2014) who derived a theoretical collapse-time distribution assuming vacuum dipole spin-down and found observed collapse times to be shorter than expected, and of Fan and collaborators (2013) who suggested gravitational-wave emission could explain the discrepancy. The analysis also builds on the mass distribution work of Alsing and collaborators (2018) and the hierarchical inference framework of Thrane and Talbot (2019). The results enable future gravitational-wave observations to constrain the mixing fraction $\epsilon$ between progenitor populations, which would then allow a more precise measurement of $M_\mathrm{TOV}$ by taking a slice through the two-dimensional posterior.
