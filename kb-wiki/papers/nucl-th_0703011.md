# Structure of low-lying 12C-resonances

> The hyperspherical adiabatic expansion is combined with complex scaling and used to calculate low-lying nuclear resonances of $^{12}$C in the $3α$-model. We use Ali-Bodmer potentials and compare results for other potentials $α-α$ with similar $^{8}$Be-properties. A three-body potential is used to adjust the $^{12}$C-resonance positions to desired values extending the applicability of the method to many-body systems decaying into three $α$-particles. For natural choices of three-body potentials we find 14 resonances below the proton separation threshold, i.e. two $0^{+}$, three $2^{+}$, two $4^{+}$, one of each of $1^{\pm}$, $2^{-}$, $3^{\pm}$, $4^{-}$, and $6^{+}$. The partial wave decomposition of each resonance is calculated as function of hyperradius. Strong variation is found from small to large distance. Connection to previous experimental and theoretical results are discussed and agreements as well as disagreements are emphasized.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a three-alpha cluster model using the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion with complex scaling can systematically describe all low-lying resonances of 12C below the proton separation threshold, but the model systematically overestimates widths by about a factor of two for most states, indicating preformation factors at the surface of roughly 1/2, and fails dramatically for the 1+ states where the computed widths are orders of magnitude too large.

## Method

The authors solved the three-body problem for 12C as three alpha particles using the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method combined with complex scaling of coordinates and a Faddeev decomposition. They employed Ali-Bodmer alpha-alpha potentials and added a diagonal three-body Gaussian potential with range b = 6 fm, adjusting its strength separately for each angular momentum and parity to reproduce one known resonance energy per Jpi, then computing the other resonances and all widths without further fitting.

## Key numbers

- 14 resonances found below the proton separation threshold at 15.96 MeV excitation energy: two 0+, three 2+, two 4+, one each of 1+, 1-, 2-, 3+, 3-, 4-, and 6+
- First 0+ resonance: theoretical energy ER = 0.38 MeV, width Gamma = 0.0625 keV (experimental: ER = 0.38 MeV, Gamma = 0.0085 keV)
- Second 0+ resonance: theoretical ER = 3.95 MeV, Gamma = 1000 keV (experimental: ER = 4.3 MeV, Gamma = 3490 keV)
- 1- resonance: theoretical ER = 3.61 MeV, Gamma = 475 keV (experimental: ER = 3.57 MeV, Gamma = 315 keV)
- 3- resonance: theoretical ER = 2.33 MeV, Gamma = 68 keV (experimental: ER = 2.37 MeV, Gamma = 34 keV)
- 2- resonance: theoretical ER = 4.53 MeV, Gamma = 452 keV (experimental: ER = 4.55 MeV, Gamma = 260 keV)
- 4- resonance: theoretical ER = 5.98 MeV, Gamma = 1035 keV (experimental: ER = 6.08 MeV, Gamma = 375 keV)
- 1+ (T=0) resonance: theoretical ER = 5.42 MeV, Gamma = 48.6 keV (experimental: ER = 5.43 MeV, Gamma = 0.0177 keV); width overestimated by factor 2.6 x 10^3
- 1+ (T=1) resonance: theoretical ER = 7.70 MeV, Gamma = 948 keV (experimental: ER = 7.84 MeV, Gamma = 0.0018 keV); width overestimated by factor 5.3 x 10^5
- Ground state 0+: theoretical rms radius = 2.59 fm (experimental charge radius = 2.4829 +/- 0.0019 fm)
- Three-body potential strengths: approximately -20 MeV for even-parity states (0+, 2+, 3+, 4+, 6+), approximately -2 MeV for negative-parity states (1-, 2-, 3-, 4-), and approximately -80 to -92 MeV for 1+ states

## Context

This work extends the three-body hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method previously applied to systems like 6He, 11Li, and 17Ne (Garrido et al., 2005; Garrido et al., 2002; Garrido et al., 2003) to provide a comprehensive survey of all low-lying 12C resonances within a single model, addressing open questions from experiments on 0+ and 2+ resonances above 7.6 MeV (Kurokawa and Katayama, 2004) and recent beta-decay studies of 12N and 12B (Diget et al., 2005). The results enable calculations of energy distributions after three-alpha decay and provide a foundation for comparing with microscopic approaches such as the no-core shell model (Navratil et al., 2000), stochastic variational method (Matsumoto et al., 2004), and Greens function Monte Carlo (Pieper et al., 2005), which have not yet provided satisfactory continuum descriptions.
