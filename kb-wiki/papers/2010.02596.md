# Correspondence between Israel-Stewart and first-order causal and stable hydrodynamics for Bjorken-expanding baryon-rich systems with vanishing particle masses

> We obtain an exact correspondence between the dynamical equations in Israel-Stewart (IS) theory and first-order causal and stable (FOCS) hydrodynamics for a boost-invariant system with an ideal gas equation of state at finite baryon chemical potential. Explicit expressions for the temperature and chemical potential dependence of the regulators in the FOCS theory are given in terms of the kinetic coefficients and constant relaxation time of the IS theory. Using the correspondence between the IS and FOCS theory, stability conditions for a charged fluid which are known in the FOCS approach are applied and one finds that the IS theory considered is unstable.

## Key claim

For a boost-invariant, Bjorken-expanding system with an ideal gas equation of state at finite baryon chemical potential, the Israel-Stewart (IS) theory can be exactly mapped onto first-order causal and stable (FOCS) hydrodynamics only when the IS parameter β equals 1/3; using this correspondence, the IS theory is found to be unstable under the stability conditions known from the FOCS approach.

## Method

The authors derived the second-order differential equation for energy density evolution from the IS theory under the Bjorken flow, using a constant relaxation time approximation in the shear sector. They then compared this equation term-by-term with the corresponding FOCS evolution equation for energy density, matching coefficients of the second-order, first-order, and derivative-independent terms to express the FOCS regulator coefficients (ε₁, ε₂, ε₃, π₁, π₂, π₃) in terms of the IS relaxation time τ_R and thermodynamic quantities. The matching also required comparing the first-order baryon number conservation equation from IS with the second-order FOCS number current equation, which forced the FOCS coefficients ν₁ and ν₃ to vanish and ν₂ to be τ-independent.

## Key numbers

- The IS parameter β must equal 1/3 for a unique determination of the FOCS regulator ε₂; this is derived from comparing coefficients of dT/dτ and dμ/dτ in the matched equations.
- The FOCS regulator ε₂ is given by τ_R(ε+p) = (4/3)τ_R ε for β = 1/3.
- The FOCS regulator ε₁ equals 3τ_R(ε+p) = 4τ_R ε for β = 1/3.
- The FOCS regulator ε₃ equals τ_R T (∂ε/∂μ) = 3τ_R T n for β = 1/3.
- The FOCS regulator π₂ equals (1/3)τ_R(ε+p) = (4/9)τ_R ε for β = 1/3.
- The stability condition ε₁ν₃ − ε₃ν₁ > 0 cannot be satisfied because ν₁ = ν₃ = 0 while ε₁ ≥ 0 and ε₃ ≥ 0, indicating instability in the fluid rest frame.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous exact matching between IS and FOCS theories for boost-invariant systems with vanishing baryon number (Das 2020) to the case of finite baryon chemical potential. It builds on the FOCS formalism developed by Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, and Kovtun (2017, 2019) and Kovtun (2019), and on the IS theory as formulated by Jaiswal (2013, 2015). The correspondence enables transferring stability conditions known from the FOCS approach to analyze the IS theory, which had not been fully explored for nonlinear causality and stability in the charged fluid case. The instability found here for the Bjorken flow does not necessarily imply acausality, and the authors note that a complete analysis of causality and stability in a Lorentz-boosted frame for the case with non-vanishing ε₃ and π₃ remains an open question.
