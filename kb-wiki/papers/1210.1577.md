# Baryon Annihilation and Regeneration in Heavy Ion Collisions

> The role of baryon-antibaryon annihilation during the hadronic stage of a relativistic heavy ion collision is explored by simulating the chemical evolution of a hadron gas. Beginning with a chemically equilibrated gas at an initial temperature of 170 MeV, the chemical composition of a representative hydrodynamic cell is followed throughout the hadronic stage. The cell's volume changes with time according to a parameterization that mimics a three-dimensional hydrodynamic expansion. The chemical evolution includes both annihilation and regeneration of baryons, consistent with detailed balance. During the hadronic stage, the number of baryons drops by approximately $40\%$ for the case where there is no net baryonic charge. When the calculations are performed without the baryon regenerating processes, e.g. $5π\rightarrow p\bar{p}$, the loss of baryons was found to be closer to $50\%$. After accounting for annihilation, yields are consistent with measurements from the ALICE Collaboration at the LHC. Baryon annihilation is shown to alter the extracted chemical breakup temperature by significantly changing the $p/π$ ratio. Assuming that annihilation cross sections are independent of the strangeness and isospin of the annihilating baryon and anti-baryon, the loss of strange baryons from annihilation is found to be similar.

## Key claim

Baryon-antibaryon annihilation during the hadronic stage of a relativistic heavy ion collision reduces final baryon yields by approximately 40% when regeneration processes (e.g., \(5\pi\rightarrow p\bar{p}\)) are included via detailed balance, but the exact reduction depends on the assumption that the system begins in chemical equilibrium at an initial temperature of 170 MeV and that annihilation cross sections are independent of strangeness and isospin.

## Method

The chemical evolution of a single hydrodynamic cell was simulated, starting from a chemically equilibrated hadron gas at \(T_0 = 170\) MeV with zero net baryonic charge. The cell volume expanded according to a parameterization mimicking three-dimensional hydrodynamic expansion, with \(\tau_0 = 8\) fm/\(c\) and \(\tau_\perp = 6.5\) fm/\(c\). The evolution included 319 hadronic resonances (masses up to 2.25 GeV/\(c^2\)), with baryon-antibaryon annihilation and regeneration rates calculated using a simplified \(p\bar{p}\) annihilation cross section \(\sigma = 67\,P_{\mathrm{lab}}^{-0.7}\) mb, assumed identical for all baryon-antibaryon pairs, and with all annihilations proceeding through a \(5\pi\) final state.

## Key numbers

- Baryon number dropped by approximately **40%** during the hadronic stage when both annihilation and regeneration were included, for the case of zero net baryonic charge.
- When regeneration processes (e.g., \(5\pi\rightarrow p\bar{p}\)) were omitted, baryon loss was closer to **50%**.
- The \(p/\pi^+\) ratio measured by ALICE at the LHC is \(0.46 \pm 10\%\); without annihilation this ratio would correspond to a chemical freeze-out temperature of **145 MeV**, but with annihilation any initial temperature \(\gtrsim 150\) MeV becomes acceptable.
- The average \(\langle \sigma v_{\mathrm{rel}} \rangle\) for \(p\bar{p}\) annihilation was near **5 fm\(^2\cdot c\)**.
- Initial temperature was set to **170 MeV** and initial time to \(\tau_0 = 8\) fm/\(c\).
- Without annihilation, decays increased proton yield by approximately **50%**, mainly through delta decays.
- Annihilation increased pion yields by approximately **10%** and kaon yields by approximately **5%**.

## Context

This work extends the chemical rate approach of Rapp (2000) and Huovinen (2003) by including a larger number of resonances (319) and following chemical evolution until interactions fully cease, rather than assuming freeze-out at a fixed temperature like 120 MeV as in Huovinen (2003). The results help explain why hybrid hydrodynamic/cascade models (Song 2011, Novak 2013) overpredict the \(p/\pi\) ratio compared to ALICE and PHENIX measurements, and show that baryon annihilation significantly alters the inferred chemical freeze-out temperature. The study also highlights that regeneration cancels annihilation only for the first 1–2 fm/\(c\) and becomes negligible afterward, justifying its omission in some microscopic models (e.g., URQMD) if 20% accuracy is sufficient, but requiring its inclusion for 10% precision.
