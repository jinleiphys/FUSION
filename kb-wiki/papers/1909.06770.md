# Influence of electromagnetic fields in proton-nucleus collisions at relativistic energy

> We study proton-gold collisions at RHIC energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV within the Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics (PHSD) off-shell transport approach, investigating the influence of the intense electromagnetic fields generated in these small systems. We show the space-time evolution of the magnetic and electric components, emphasizing the huge values of the latter one, in particular the electric field $E_x$ along the impact parameter direction whose magnitude is comparable to the magnetic field $B_y$ perpendicular to the reaction plane. We find a fair agreement of the charged particle pseudorapidity density of the high-multiplicity events with respect to the experimental result of the PHENIX Collaboration. Focusing on the most central collision, we show rapidity distributions and spectra as well as the flow coefficients $v_1$ and $v_2$ and we discuss the impact of the electromagnetic fields on identified particle observables. We compute the directed flow $v_1$ of $π^+$, $π^-$, $K^+$, $K^-$ for collisions at fixed impact parameter and predict that an electromagnetically-induced splitting in the $v_1$ of positively and negatively charged particles is generated in the Au-going side of p+Au reaction mainly driven by the huge $E_x$ component. We find that this effect is stronger for the strange mesons and increases for increasing impact parameter. Furthermore, we highlight the amount of directed flow generated in the deconfined phase, finding that it constitutes the main contribution in the central rapidity region, especially for kaons. Thus, we support the idea that the directed flow is a promising probe for the electromagnetic fields generated in relativistic nuclear collisions and show that in proton-induced reactions the electric component along the impact parameter axis is the primary origin of a charge-odd $v_1$ of pions and kaons.

## Key claim

The authors predict that in proton-gold collisions at RHIC energy, the electric field component \(E_x\) along the impact parameter direction is comparable in magnitude to the magnetic field \(B_y\) perpendicular to the reaction plane, and that this electric field is the primary origin of a charge-odd directed flow \(v_1\) of pions and kaons, with the effect being stronger for strange mesons and increasing with impact parameter.

## Method

The authors used the Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics (PHSD) off-shell transport approach, which includes the dynamical formation and evolution of retarded electromagnetic fields via Liénard-Wiechert potentials and the Lorentz force on quasiparticles. They simulated proton-gold collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\) GeV, computing the space-time evolution of electromagnetic fields and final-state particle observables for fixed impact parameters and for minimum bias events with centrality selection based on charged particle multiplicity.

## Key numbers

- The magnetic field component \(|eB_y|\) reaches approximately \(4\,m_\pi^2\) in Au+Au collisions at \(b=7\) fm, while in p+Au collisions at \(b=4\) fm both \(|eB_y|\) and \(|eE_x|\) reach values of approximately \(2\,m_\pi^2\).
- The electromagnetic fields decrease very fast, becoming close to zero after approximately 0.25 fm/\(c\) from the first nucleon-nucleon collisions.
- The maximum number of nucleons the proton can hit inside the gold nucleus is about 50, and for a given number of participating nucleons the number of charged particles produced can vary from zero to about 20 at midrapidity.
- The charged-particle pseudorapidity density for the 0-5% most central p+Au collisions shows an enhancement in the Au-going side (backward rapidity) and agrees with experimental data from the PHENIX Collaboration.

## Context

This work extends previous PHSD studies of electromagnetic fields in symmetric Au+Au collisions (Voronyuk 2011) and asymmetric Cu+Au collisions (Voronyuk 2014) to the small system of p+Au, where the field distribution is essentially that produced by the heavy gold nucleus alone. The study supports the idea that directed flow is a promising probe for electromagnetic fields in relativistic nuclear collisions, and it enables future comparisons with experimental results from the STAR and ALICE Collaborations on charge-dependent directed flow in different collision systems and energies.
