# Stability studies of first order spin-hydrodynamic frameworks

> We study the stability of first-order dissipative spin-hydrodynamic frameworks. We considered two different first-order dissipative spin-hydrodynamic frameworks. The first one considers the spin chemical potential ($ω^{αβ}$) to be first order ($\mathcal{O}(\partial)$) in the hydrodynamic gradient expansion. The hydrodynamic gradient ordering of the spin chemical potential is a debatable issue within the frameworks of spin hydrodynamics. Therefore as a second choice, we also consider the spin hydrodynamic equations with $ω^{αβ}\sim\mathcal{O}(1)$. We find that for both frameworks, at the level of linear perturbations some spin modes can be unstable. To remove these generic instabilities we consider the Frenkel condition. We argue that Frenkel condition helps get rid of the unstable solutions in both cases, but with a physical drawback for the case where $ω^{μν}\sim\mathcal{O}(\partial)$.

## Key claim

The paper shows that first-order dissipative spin-hydrodynamic frameworks generically contain unstable spin modes when linear perturbations are analyzed around a global equilibrium state, regardless of whether the spin chemical potential is taken as \(\mathcal{O}(1)\) or \(\mathcal{O}(\partial)\) in the hydrodynamic gradient expansion. The instabilities are traced to the boost degrees of freedom \(\omega^{0i}\) and can be removed by imposing the Frenkel condition, but this comes with a physical drawback when \(\omega^{\mu\nu}\sim\mathcal{O}(\partial)\).

## Method

The authors perform linear mode analysis around a hydrostatic global equilibrium (with \(u^\mu_{(0)}=(1,0,0,0)\), \(\omega^{\mu\nu}_{(0)}=0\), \(S^{\mu\nu}_{(0)}=0\)) for two distinct first-order spin-hydrodynamic frameworks. The first framework treats the spin chemical potential as \(\mathcal{O}(\partial)\) in the gradient expansion (based on Hattori (2019) and Fukushima (2020)), while the second treats it as \(\mathcal{O}(1)\) (based on She (2021)). For each framework, the linearized equations of motion for energy density, momentum density, and spin density perturbations are solved in Fourier space, yielding dispersion relations. The analysis is then repeated after imposing the Frenkel condition to eliminate the \(\omega^{0i}\) degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- For the \(\omega^{\mu\nu}\sim\mathcal{O}(\partial)\) framework, unstable modes appear with dispersion relations \(\omega = +2i D_b\) (two modes) and \(\omega = 2i D_b - 2i c_s^2 \lambda' k_z^2 + \mathcal{O}(k_z^4)\), where \(D_b = 4\lambda/\chi_b\) and \(\chi_b = \partial S^{i0}/\partial \omega^{i0}\).
- For the \(\omega^{\mu\nu}\sim\mathcal{O}(\partial)\) framework, stable spin modes include \(\omega = -2i D_s\) and \(\omega = -2i D_s - i\gamma' k_z^2 + \mathcal{O}(k_z^4)\), where \(D_s = 4\widetilde{\gamma}/\chi_s\) and \(\chi_s = \partial S^{ij}/\partial \omega^{ij}\).
- For the \(\omega^{\mu\nu}\sim\mathcal{O}(\partial)\) framework, sound modes are \(\omega = \pm c_s k_z - (i/2)\gamma_{||} k_z^2 + \mathcal{O}(k_z^3)\) and shear modes are \(\omega = -i\gamma_\perp k_z^2 + \mathcal{O}(k_z^4)\), with \(\gamma_{||} = (\zeta + 4\eta/3)/(\varepsilon_{(0)}+p_{(0)})\) and \(\gamma_\perp = \eta/(\varepsilon_{(0)}+p_{(0)})\).
- The unstable modes arise for \(D_b > 0\), which corresponds to \(\chi_b > 0\) in the equation of state relating spin density to spin chemical potential.

## Context

This work extends the stability analysis of first-order relativistic hydrodynamics (Kovtun (2019), Bemfica (2019)) to spin-hydrodynamic frameworks, where the spin chemical potential's gradient ordering is debated. It confirms and generalizes the instability found by Sarwar (2022) for the \(\omega^{\mu\nu}\sim\mathcal{O}(\partial)\) framework. The Frenkel condition, originally proposed by Frenkel (1926), is shown to remove the unstable boost modes in both frameworks, but for the \(\omega^{\mu\nu}\sim\mathcal{O}(\partial)\) case this introduces a physical drawback. The results enable future work on selecting physically consistent spin-hydrodynamic formulations for heavy-ion collision phenomenology.
