# Complete Glauber calculations for proton-nucleus inelastic cross sections

> We perform a parameter-free calculation for the high-energy proton-nucleus scattering based on the Glauber theory. A complete evaluation of the so-called Glauber amplitude is made by using the factorization of the single-particle wave functions. The multiple-scattering or multistep processes are fully taken into account within the Glauber theory. We demonstrate that proton- $^{12}$C, $^{20}$Ne, and $^{28}$Si elastic and inelastic scattering ($J^π=0^+ \to 2^+$ and $0^+ \to 4^+$) processes are very well described in a wide range of the incident energies from $\sim$50 MeV to $\sim$ 1 GeV. We evaluate the validity of a simple one-step approximation andfind that the approximation works fairly well for the inelastic $0^+ \to 2^+$ processes but not for $0^+ \to 4^+$ where the multistep processes become more important. As an application, we quantify the difference between the total reaction and interaction cross sections of proton-$^{12}$C, $^{20}$Ne, and $^{28}$Si collisions.

# Complete Glauber calculations for proton-nucleus inelastic cross sections

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a parameter-free Glauber theory calculation, using wave functions constrained only by static structure information (charge radius and B(E2) value), can reproduce proton-nucleus elastic and inelastic scattering cross sections for ¹²C, ²⁰Ne, and ²⁸Si across incident energies from ~50 MeV to ~1 GeV, but the one-step distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) fails for 0⁺ → 4⁺ transitions where multistep processes are important.

## Method

The authors evaluate the complete Glauber amplitude for proton-nucleus scattering by factorizing the Slater determinant wave functions, reducing the A-fold multidimensional integration to three-dimensional single-particle coordinate integrals. They use axially-symmetric deformed harmonic-oscillator (DHO) wave functions with parameters fixed to reproduce measured charge radii and B(E2; 2⁺ → 0⁺) values, then compute elastic and inelastic (0⁺ → 2⁺ and 0⁺ → 4⁺) differential cross sections for ¹²C, ²⁰Ne, and ²⁸Si without any adjustable reaction parameters.

## Key numbers

- Incident energy range: ~50 MeV to ~1 GeV for proton-¹²C, ²⁰Ne, and ²⁸Si scattering
- For ¹²C: theoretical point-proton radius 2.33 fm vs experimental 2.327 ± 0.009 fm; B(E2) theoretical 8.24 e²fm⁴ vs experimental 8.30 ± 1.19 e²fm⁴; quadrupole deformation β₂ = −0.443
- For ²⁰Ne: theoretical point-proton radius 2.89 fm vs experimental 2.889 ± 0.009 fm; B(E2) theoretical 77.8 e²fm⁴ vs experimental 77.7 ± 12.2 e²fm⁴; β₂ = 0.572
- For ²⁸Si (oblate): theoretical point-proton radius 3.01 fm vs experimental 3.010 ± 0.009 fm; B(E2) theoretical 67.1 e²fm⁴ vs experimental 66.9 ± 10.2 e²fm⁴; β₂ = −0.339
- For ²⁸Si (prolate): theoretical point-proton radius 3.01 fm; B(E2) theoretical 66.9 e²fm⁴; β₂ = 0.336
- DHO parameters: ¹²C ω₀ = 0.0953 c/fm, ε = −0.594; ²⁰Ne ω₀ = 0.0719 c/fm, ε = 0.484; ²⁸Si (oblate) ω₀ = 0.0675 c/fm, ε = −0.250; ²⁸Si (prolate) ω₀ = 0.0666 c/fm, ε = 0.152
- Angular momentum projection uses 20 points each for θ₁ and θ₂, giving 400 Slater determinants per mesh point

## Context

This work extends the complete Glauber calculation approach of Abgrall (1976), who performed such calculations for proton-¹²C at ~1 GeV but used analytically integrable harmonic-oscillator wave functions limited in applicability. The present method generalizes the factorization technique to accept multi-Slater determinant wave functions from shell model, mean-field, or molecular dynamics models. The authors compare their full calculations against the optical-limit approximation (OLA) and the eikonal distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) from Saudinos (1974) and Alexander (1974), finding that while OLA works well for elastic scattering, DWIA fails for 0⁺ → 4⁺ transitions. The work enables future analysis of proton inelastic scattering on exotic nuclei measured in inverse kinematics, and demonstrates that ⁴⁺ state cross sections can distinguish oblate versus prolate shapes in ²⁸Si, complementing the alpha-scattering measurement of Peach (2016).
