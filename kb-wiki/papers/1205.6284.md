# Core-Collapse Supernovae as Supercomputing Science: a status report toward 6D simulations with exact Boltzmann neutrino transport in full general relativity

> This is a status report on our endeavor to reveal the mechanism of core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe) by large-scale numerical simulations. Multi-dimensionality of the supernova engine, general relativistic magnetohydrodynamics, energy and lepton number transport by neutrinos emitted from the forming neutron star as well as nuclear interactions there, are all believed to play crucial roles in repelling infalling matter and producing energetic explosions. These ingredients are nonlinearly coupled with one another in the dynamics of core-collapse, bounce, and shock expansion. Serious quantitative studies of CCSNe hence make extensive numerical computations mandatory. Since neutrinos are neither in thermal nor in chemical equilibrium in general, their distributions in the phase space should be computed. This is a six dimensional (6D) neutrino transport problem and quite a challenge even for those with an access to the most advanced numerical resources such as the "K computer". To tackle this problem, we have embarked on multi-front efforts. In particular we report in this paper our recent progresses in the treatments of multi-dimensional (multi-D) radiation-hydrodynamics. We are currently proceeding on two different paths to the ultimate goal; in one approach we employ an approximate but highly efficient scheme for neutrino transport and treat 3D hydrodynamics and/or general relativity rigorously; some neutrino-driven explosions will be presented and comparisons will be made between 2D and 3D models quantitatively; in the second approach, on the other hand, exact but so far Newtonian Boltzmann equations are solved in two and three spatial dimensions; we will show some demonstrative test simulations. We will also address the perspectives of exa-scale computations on the next generation supercomputers.

## Key claim

The authors report progress toward the ultimate goal of 3D core-collapse supernova simulations with exact Boltzmann neutrino transport in full general relativity, but acknowledge that such "dream simulations" will likely only become feasible on next-generation exa-scale supercomputers; the present work provides intermediate results using two complementary approaches that each sacrifice some physical completeness.

## Method

The authors pursue two parallel paths: (1) 3D hydrodynamical simulations with approximate neutrino transport using the isotropic diffusion source approximation (IDSA) scheme in a ray-by-ray approach, applied to both Newtonian and fully general relativistic frameworks; (2) exact Boltzmann neutrino transport solved in two and three spatial dimensions but under Newtonian gravity, using implicit finite differencing of the Boltzmann equations with efficient large-matrix inversion. The 3D Newtonian simulations with spectral neutrino transport use 300 logarithmically spaced radial zones and 64 polar by 32 azimuthal uniform mesh points covering a sphere of radius 5000 km.

## Key numbers

- Explosion obtained for a non-rotating 11.2 solar mass progenitor (Woosley 2002 model) in 3D simulations with IDSA transport, with average shock radius exceeding 1000 km at around 300 ms after bounce
- 3D computational grid consists of 300 logarithmically spaced radial zones and 64 polar (theta) and 32 azimuthal (phi) uniform mesh points covering the entire sphere with a radius of 5000 km
- Time scale ratio tau_res/tau_heat reaches approximately 2 in the gain region at t=125 ms after bounce, indicating successful shock revival by neutrino heating
- Canonical explosion energy for type II-plateau supernovae is roughly 10^51 erg, compared to available gravitational binding energy of proto-neutron stars of approximately 10^53 erg
- State-of-the-art supernova simulations require more than 10^18 operations and typically several months for a single model on current best supercomputing platforms
- The LS180 equation of state (incompressibility K=180 MeV) cannot support a 2 solar mass cold neutron star; recent experiments suggest a stiffer EOS with K=240 plus or minus 20 MeV
- Type Ic-BL supernovae (hypernovae) show kinetic energies of 2 to 5 times 10^52 erg

## Context

This status report extends the multi-dimensional modeling efforts following the failure of spherically symmetric (1D) neutrino-driven explosions (Kitaura 2006) and the subsequent successes in axisymmetric 2D simulations by Buras (2006), Bruenn (2009), and Suwa (2010). The work directly addresses the controversy between Nordhaus (2010), who claimed 3D dynamics makes shock revival easier than 2D, and Hanke (2011), who found little difference in critical neutrino luminosity between 2D and 3D; the authors find that shock expansion is faster in 2D than in 3D, contrary to the Nordhaus claim. The paper also describes ongoing efforts to expand the inventory of nuclear equations of state, including work by Shen (1998, 2011), Lattimer and Swesty (1991), and new tables incorporating hyperons (Ishizuka 2008) and quark matter (Nakazato 2010), and enables future exa-scale simulations that will combine full Boltzmann neutrino transport with 3D general relativistic magnetohydrodynamics.
