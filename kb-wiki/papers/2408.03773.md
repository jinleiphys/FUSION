# Fundamental physical constants, operation of physical phenomena and entropy increase

> Approaching the problem of understanding fundamental physical constants (FPCs) started with discussing the role these constants play in high-energy nuclear physics and astrophysics. Condensed matter physics was relatively unexplored in this regard. More recently, it was realised that FPCs set lower or upper bounds on key condensed matter properties. Here, we discuss a much wider role played by FPCs in condensed matter physics: at given environmental conditions, FPCs set the observability and operation of entire physical effects and phenomena. We discuss structural and superconducting phase transitions and transitions between different states of matter, with implications for life processes. We also discuss metastable states, transitions between them, chemical reactions and their products. A byproduct of this discussion is that the order of magnitude of the transition temperature can be calculated from FPCs only. We show that the new states emerging as a result of various transitions increase the phase space and entropy. Were FPCs to take different values, these transitions would become inoperative at our environmental conditions and the new states due to these transitions would not emerge. This suggests that the current values of FPCs, by enabling various transitions and reactions which give rise to new states, promote entropy increase. Based on this entropy increase and the associated increase of statistical probability, we conjecture that entropy increase is a selection principle for FPCs considered to be variable in earlier discussions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that fundamental physical constants (FPCs) set not just bounds on condensed matter properties, but the very observability and operation of entire physical phenomena such as phase transitions and transitions between states of matter; the author then conjectures that entropy increase, enabled by the current values of FPCs, may serve as a selection principle for those constants.

## Method

The author examines structural phase transitions (using silica polymorphs quartz and cristobalite as a model), superconducting transitions, and first-order transitions between solids, liquids, and gases. For each case, the transition temperature is expressed in terms of FPCs (Planck constant, electron mass and charge, proton-to-electron mass ratio), and the consequences of varying those constants are evaluated at typical environmental temperatures (10^2–10^3 K). The entropy increase from the emergence of new phases is calculated using standard statistical mechanics formulas for solids and gases.

## Key numbers

* The upper bound on maximal phonon frequency is ω_m^u ≈ 3680 K, derived from FPCs with the proton mass as the atomic mass (Eq. f2).
* The structural phase transition temperature T_c is estimated as T_c ≈ 500 K, using the average atomic mass factor A^(1/2) ≈ 8 and ω_m^u = 3680 K (Eq. tcval1).
* The upper limit to superconducting temperature T_c^u is in the range 10^2–10^3 K, based on the Migdal-Eliashberg theory with coupling constant λ ≈ 1–2 (Eq. tc).
* The Bohr radius a_B ≈ 0.5 Å and Rydberg energy E_R ≈ 13.6 eV set the characteristic scales of condensed matter (Eqs. bohr, rydberg).

## Context

This work extends the author’s prior research on FPCs setting bounds on viscosity, diffusion, and superconducting temperature (Trachenko, 2023–2024) and contrasts with earlier high-energy and astrophysical discussions of FPC fine-tuning (Barrow, 2003; Adams, 2019). It enables a new perspective where the values of FPCs are linked to the emergence of multiple states of matter and life-supporting conditions, and it proposes entropy increase as a potential selection principle for variable constants, a concept previously discussed in the context of cosmological and biological evolution (Smolin, 1997).
