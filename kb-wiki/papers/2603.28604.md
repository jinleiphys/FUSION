# Hadron Structure from lattice QCD in the context of the Electron-Ion Collider

> Hadron structure calculations using lattice Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) have advanced significantly in recent years. Results for charges, form factors, and lower Mellin moments can be obtained to high precision, generalized parton distributions can now be computed either directly or reconstructed from moments, and transverse-momentum-dependent distributions can be accessed through direct lattice calculations. Together, these quantities provide detailed and complementary insights into the internal structure of hadrons. These theoretical developments are highly relevant to the experimental program of the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) and of other facilities. We review the most pertinent lattice QCD results for hadron structure that inform the EIC scientific agenda, with particular emphasis on the pion, kaon, and nucleon.

# Hadron Structure from lattice QCD in the context of the Electron-Ion Collider

## Key claim
Lattice QCD can now provide percent-level precision for nucleon charges, form factors, and lower Mellin moments, and can extract Bjorken-x dependent PDFs and GPDs using approaches like LaMET, but these results are currently reliable only in controlled kinematic regions (x between approximately 0.2 and 0.8) and require boosts P_z of 1.5-2 GeV with perturbative matching up to NNLO to yield results consistent with phenomenology.

## Method
The authors review lattice QCD calculations of hadron structure quantities relevant to the EIC, including Mellin moments computed from matrix elements of local operators (up to the fourth moment), direct extraction of PDFs using the quasi-distribution approach within LaMET and the pseudo-distribution approach using short-distance factorization, and computation of form factors and generalized form factors from ensembles with N_f=2+1 and N_f=2+1+1 dynamical fermions at physical pion mass, lattice spacings as small as a=0.05 fm, and spatial volumes L greater than 6 fm. Calculations employ hadron boosts P_z of 1.5-2 GeV, nonperturbative renormalization, and perturbative matching up to NNLO.

## Key numbers
- Lattice spacings as small as a=0.05 fm and spatial volumes L greater than 6 fm are used for state-of-the-art calculations
- Hadron boosts P_z of 1.5-2 GeV are employed in LaMET calculations of PDFs and GPDs
- The BNL/JLab group reached Q^2 as large as 2.5 GeV^2 for the pion vector form factor using one ensemble with m_pi=140 MeV and a=0.076 fm
- The ratio of u-quark to s-quark momentum fractions in the kaon is 0.810(11), the ratio of second moments is 0.647(8), and the ratio of third moments is 0.632(67), computed using one N_f=2+1+1 TMF ensemble with m_pi=140 MeV and a=0.08 fm
- RQCD used 47 CLS ensembles at six lattice spacings a in (0.038-0.098) fm with pion masses from 480 to 130 MeV for nucleon charge calculations
- ETMC used four ensembles at lattice spacings a in (0.08-0.05) fm with m_pi approximately 140 MeV for nucleon charge continuum extrapolations
- For the third moment of the nucleon helicity PDF, RQCD finds for the proton: <x^2>_Delta p = 0.035(3)(8) and for the neutron: <x^2>_Delta n = 0.0034(17)(41) in the MSbar scheme at 2 GeV
- Gluons contribute about 45% to the total momentum and spin sums of the nucleon from ETMC preliminary continuum extrapolated results

## Context
This review extends the line of lattice QCD hadron structure calculations that began with Wilson (1974) and Creutz (1980), and directly addresses the theory program for the Electron-Ion Collider as articulated in the EIC Yellow Report by AbdulKhalek (2021) and Aschenauer (2019). The work builds on the LaMET framework developed by Ji (2013, 2020) and the pseudo-distribution approach by Radyushkin (2017), and provides cross-checks with phenomenological analyses from JAM (Barry 2021, 2025), FANTO (Kotz 2025), and xFitter (Novikov 2020). The results enable improved extraction of transversity PDFs when LQCD tensor charge inputs are used (Cocuzza 2023), and provide crucial inputs for neutrino experiments such as MINERvA, NOvA, T2K, and DUNE, as well as parity-violation experiments including Q-weak, G0, and HAPPEX at JLab.
