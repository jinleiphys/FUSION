# Chapman-Enskog calculation of the shear viscosity of quark-gluon plasma including all $2\leftrightarrow 2$ scatterings at finite temperature

> We use the Chapman-Enskog method to investigate the shear viscosity of the quark-gluon plasma with a focus on its relation to parton cross sections. We use the recently obtained analytical expression for the shear viscosity $η$ of a massless quark-gluon gas at chemical equilibrium with Boltzmann statistics and all $2\leftrightarrow 2$ scatterings with arbitrary cross sections. Here we apply this general expression to cross sections at finite temperature that are based on perturbative-QCD and screened with scaled thermal masses $\sqrtκ\,m_D$ and $\sqrtκ\,m_F$. We find that the Chapman-Enskog results on $η\, g^4/T^3$ versus $m_D/T$ at $κ=1$ are qualitatively similar to but higher than the corresponding leading-order results from the AMY framework. We then find that using $κ=0.4$ allows the Chapman-Enskog results to match well the corresponding AMY results as it includes the effect of using thermal masses (instead of self-energies) to screen the cross sections. In addition, we show that the shear viscosity-to-entropy density ratio $η/s$ is very sensitive to the choice of momentum scale $Q$ in the strong coupling, where the choice of $Q=3T$ leads to $η/s \sim 0.15$ for $N_f=0$ or 3 at the QCD phase transition temperature $T_c$. These results lay the foundation for mapping parton cross sections to given shear viscosity in parton transport models and QCD effective kinetic theory.

## Key claim

The Chapman-Enskog method applied to a massless quark-gluon gas with Boltzmann statistics and all $2\leftrightarrow 2$ scatterings yields shear viscosity results that match the leading-order AMY framework only when a screening coefficient $\kappa=0.4$ is used, because the simpler thermal-mass screening (with $\kappa=1$) overestimates the shear viscosity compared to the AMY results that use momentum-dependent self-energies.

## Method

The authors apply a recently derived analytical Chapman-Enskog expression for the shear viscosity of a multi-species massless quark-gluon gas in chemical equilibrium, using Boltzmann statistics and all $2\leftrightarrow 2$ elastic and inelastic scatterings with arbitrary cross sections. They compute the shear viscosity using perturbative-QCD-based cross sections at finite temperature, where the matrix elements are screened with scaled thermal masses $\sqrt{\kappa}\,m_D$ and $\sqrt{\kappa}\,m_F$, and extra screenings are added to remove unphysical negative values and divergences in the original matrix elements. The strong coupling $\alpha_s$ is evaluated at a momentum scale $Q$, taken by default as $Q=3T$.

## Key numbers

- At $\kappa=0.4$ and $Q=3T$, the shear viscosity-to-entropy density ratio $\eta/s \sim 0.15$ for $N_f=0$ or $3$ at the QCD phase transition temperature $T_c \simeq 156$ MeV.
- The thermally averaged $gg$ elastic cross section at $T_c$ is as large as $\sim 34$ mb, while the thermally averaged $q\bar{q}\rightarrow q^{\prime}\bar{q}^{\prime}$ cross section is only $\sim 1.0$ mb.
- The decrease of shear viscosity due to inelastic $2\leftrightarrow 2$ scatterings ranges from 4% to about 8% for $N_f=1,2,3$.
- The extra screenings added to the original matrix elements change the shear viscosity by less than 1% for $N_f=3$.
- The fit function for $\eta\,g^4/T^3$ over a narrow temperature range $T\in[0.15,0.6]$ GeV is given by $24.7\,(1+2.63 N_f)^{0.596} + (45.5-28.3 e^{-0.659 N_f}) (m_D/T)^{0.808+0.384\ln(N_f+5.91)}$.

## Context

This work extends the authors' recent analytical Chapman-Enskog expression for shear viscosity (Ohanaka, 2026) by applying it to finite-temperature pQCD cross sections, and it provides a direct comparison to the leading-order AMY framework (Arnold, 2000; Arnold, 2003) which uses parton self-energies for screening and includes both $2\leftrightarrow 2$ and $1\leftrightarrow 2$ processes. The results enable mapping of parton cross sections to shear viscosity in parton transport models such as ZPC (Zhang, 1997), AMPT (Lin, 2004), BAMPS (Xu, 2007), and QCD effective kinetic theory (Kurkela, 2018), and provide fit functions for $\eta/s$ that can be used in phenomenological studies of heavy-ion collisions.
