# Quarkyonic Percolation and deconfinement at finite density and number of colors

> We examine the interplay between the percolation and the deconfinement phase transitions of Yang-Mills matter at finite temperature, quark chemical potential $μ_Q$ and number of colors $N_c$. We find that, whereas the critical $N_c$ for percolation goes down with density, the critical $N_c$ for confinement generally goes up. Because of this, Yang-Mills matter falls into two qualitatively different regimes: the "low-$N_c$ limit", where percolation does not occur because matter deconfines before it percolates, and the high-$N_c$ limit, where there are three distinct phases characterizing Yang-Mills matter at finite temperature and density: confined, deconfined and confined but percolating matter. The latter can be thought of as the recently conjectured "quarkyonic phase". We attempt to estimate the critical $N_c$, to see if the percolating phase can occur in our world. We find that, while percolation will not occur at normal nuclear density as in the large-$N_c$ limit, a sliver of the phase diagram in $N_c$, energy density and baryonic density where percolation occurs while confinement persists is possible. We conclude by speculating on the phenomenological properties of such percolating "quarkyonic" matter, suggest avenues to study it quantitatively, and look for it in experiment.

## Key claim

The paper argues that percolation (overlap of quark wavefunctions from different baryons) and deconfinement are distinct phase transitions in Yang-Mills matter, and that a "quarkyonic" phase, confined but percolating, can exist in a sliver of the phase diagram, but only if non-perturbative mechanisms keep color localized while quarks delocalize; the authors note this is not an inevitable conclusion and depends on whether such mechanisms are realized in nature.

## Method

The authors model baryonic matter as a cubic lattice of hard spheres (radius 1/Λ_QCD) representing baryons, with quarks randomly positioned inside each sphere. They compute baryon-to-baryon exchange probabilities using two representative propagator forms (a step function in coordinate space and a screened sine-squared form in momentum space) that depend on the 't Hooft coupling λ, the confinement scale r_T, and the number of colors N_c. They then apply Monte Carlo renormalization group blocking on cells of size b^3 to map the multi-bond probabilities onto a single effective bond probability, which is compared to the 3D cubic-lattice bond-percolation threshold p_c ≈ 0.2488 to determine the critical N_c^* for percolation as a function of baryon density.

## Key numbers

- The 3D cubic-lattice bond-percolation threshold used is p_c ≈ 0.2488.
- The universal crossing probability for the R_1 rule in 3D percolation is Π_1 = 0.265.
- The 3D percolation critical exponent ν ≈ 0.8765.
- The reference baryon density is ρ̄_0 = Λ_QCD^3/8 (one baryon per baryon size).
- The density range explored corresponds to ε from 0.8 to 1.4, giving densities from approximately 0.046 Λ_QCD^3 to 0.244 Λ_QCD^3.
- For the F_T propagator with ε=0.8, λ=0.7, and r_T=0.6, the critical N_c^* values for b=3 to 7 are {27.9, 27.6, 27.9, 27.6, 27.5}, showing substantial independence of cell size.
- The critical N_c for percolation is found to follow the parametrization N_c^*(ρ̄) = A/(ρ̄^Z + B).

## Context

This work extends the percolation model introduced by the same authors in Lottini and Torrieri (2011) from fixed density to variable density and non-zero temperature, building on the "quarkyonic matter" conjecture of McLerran and Pisarski (2007) and subsequent work by Kojo et al. The paper aims to determine whether a percolating yet confined phase exists at N_c=3, which would be relevant for experimental searches in low-energy heavy-ion collisions and neutron star astrophysics. The results contrast with the bag model picture where deconfinement occurs at μ_Q ≈ Λ_QCD, and the authors note that percolation may be linked to the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition and the failure of the large-N_c picture for nuclear matter as discussed in Torrieri and Lottini (2011).
