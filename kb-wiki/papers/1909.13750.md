# Combining nuclear reactions and structure with the dispersive optical model

> A review of recent applications of the nonlocal dispersive optical model (DOM) is presented that allows a simultaneous description of nuclear structure and nuclear reactions. An assessment of the quality of the resulting potentials for $^{40}$Ca and $^{48}$Ca is discussed for the description of the $(e,e'p)$ reaction to valence hole states and the possibility of interpreting the data in terms of absolute spectroscopic factors. The relevance of these results in the context of conflicting interpretations between transfer and knockout reactions is pointed out as well as the importance of proton reaction cross sections for isotopes with neutron excess. Application of the nonlocal DOM to $^{48}$Ca incorporates the effect of the 8 additional neutrons and allows for an excellent description of elastic scattering data of both protons and neutrons. The corresponding neutron distribution constrained by all available data generates a prediction for the neutron skin that is larger than most mean-field and available \textit{ab initio} results. Results are presented for the most recent nonlocal DOM analysis of $^{208}$Pb.

## Key claim

The nonlocal dispersive optical model (DOM) can simultaneously describe nuclear reactions and structure, yielding absolute spectroscopic factors from \((e,e'p)\) reactions that are consistent with the canonical suppression around 0.7 for \(^{40}\)Ca, but the extracted values depend critically on including high-energy proton reaction cross-section data and on accounting for fragmentation not yet captured by the DOM self-energy.

## Method

The authors apply the nonlocal DOM, which uses a subtracted dispersion relation to relate the nucleon self-energy to elastic scattering data, reaction cross sections, and structure information. For \(^{40}\)Ca and \(^{48}\)Ca, they generate proton distorted waves, overlap functions, and normalizations entirely from the DOM self-energy, then use the distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) via the DWEEPY code to describe \((e,e'p)\) knockout data without adjusting any ingredients to those data. Spectroscopic factors are renormalized by incorporating experimentally observed low-energy fragmentation that the DOM self-energy does not yet include.

## Key numbers

* Spectroscopic factor for the \(0\mathrm{d}_{3/2}\) orbital in \(^{40}\)Ca: \(0.71 \pm 0.04\) (from DOM, renormalized).
* Spectroscopic factor for the \(1\mathrm{s}_{1/2}\) orbital in \(^{40}\)Ca: \(0.60 \pm 0.03\) (from DOM, renormalized).
* Spectroscopic factor for the \(0\mathrm{d}_{3/2}\) orbital in \(^{48}\)Ca: \(0.58 \pm 0.03\) (from DOM, renormalized).
* Spectroscopic factor for the \(1\mathrm{s}_{1/2}\) orbital in \(^{48}\)Ca: \(0.55 \pm 0.03\) (from DOM, renormalized).
* Neutron skin of \(^{48}\)Ca: \(\Delta r_{np} = 0.249 \pm 0.023\) fm (DOM prediction).
* Unrenormalized DOM spectroscopic factors for \(^{40}\)Ca: \(0.71 \pm 0.04\) (\(0\mathrm{d}_{3/2}\)) and \(0.74 \pm 0.03\) (\(1\mathrm{s}_{1/2}\)).
* Unrenormalized DOM spectroscopic factors for \(^{48}\)Ca: \(0.60 \pm 0.03\) (\(0\mathrm{d}_{3/2}\)) and \(0.64 \pm 0.03\) (\(1\mathrm{s}_{1/2}\)).

## Context

This work extends the nonlocal DOM framework of Mahzoon et al. (2014, 2017) and Dickhoff et al. (2017) by incorporating new higher-energy proton reaction cross-section data for \(^{40}\)Ca and extrapolated data for \(^{48}\)Ca, and by applying the resulting self-energy to \((e,e'p)\) reactions. The results support the canonical spectroscopic-factor suppression near 0.7 established by the Nikhef group (Lapikas, 1993; Kramer, 1989, 2001) and show a reduction in proton spectroscopic factors when eight neutrons are added to \(^{40}\)Ca, consistent with the trend in knockout reactions reported by Gade (2014) but with a reduced slope. The neutron-skin prediction for \(^{48}\)Ca (0.249 fm) is larger than most mean-field results and the coupled-cluster calculation of Hagen et al. (2016), and the ongoing PREX and CREX parity-violating electron scattering experiments at Jefferson Lab will test this prediction.
