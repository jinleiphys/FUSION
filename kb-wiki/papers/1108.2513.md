# Low-energy effective field theory for finite-temperature relativistic superfluids

> We derive the low-energy effective action governing the infrared dynamics of relativistic superfluids at finite temperature. We organize our derivation in an effective field theory fashion-purely in terms of infrared degrees of freedom and symmetries. Our degrees of freedom are the superfluid phase ψ, and the comoving coordinates for the volume elements of the normal fluid component. The presence of two sound modes follows straightforwardly from Taylor-expanding the action at second order in small perturbations. We match our description to more conventional hydrodynamical ones, thus linking the functional form of our Lagrangian to the equation of state, which we assume as an input. We re-derive in our language some standard properties of relativistic superfluids in the high-temperature and low-temperature limits. As an illustration of the efficiency of our methods, we compute the cross-section for a sound wave (of either type) scattering off a superfluid vortex at temperatures right beneath the critical one.

## Key claim

The paper derives a low-energy effective field theory for finite-temperature relativistic superfluids using only infrared degrees of freedom (the superfluid phase and comoving coordinates of the normal fluid) and symmetries, without invoking thermodynamics or hydrodynamical equations as input; the derivation assumes non-dissipative dynamics and requires the equation of state as an external input for matching to standard hydrodynamical descriptions.

## Method

The author constructs an effective action from four scalar fields: the superfluid phase ψ (with a U(1) shift symmetry) and three comoving coordinates φ^I for the normal fluid (with shift, SO(3), and volume-preserving diffeomorphism symmetries). At lowest order in derivatives, the Lagrangian is a generic function F(b, X, y) of three invariants: b (the norm of the entropy current), X = ∂_μ ψ ∂^μ ψ, and y = u^μ ∂_μ ψ (where u^μ is the normal fluid four-velocity). The stress-energy tensor and U(1) current are derived via metric variation and Noether's theorem, and thermodynamic identifications (temperature T = -F_b, chemical potential μ = y, entropy density s = b) are imposed to match standard hydrodynamics.

## Key numbers

- The quadratic Lagrangian for small perturbations yields two propagating sound modes with eigenfrequencies determined by the secular equation det( [[K_N ω^2 - G_N k^2, M ω k], [M ω k, K_S ω^2 - G_S k^2]] ) = 0, where coefficients K_N, G_N, K_S, G_S, M are defined in terms of derivatives of F evaluated at background values b_0, y_0, and X = -y_0^2.
- Above the critical temperature T_c, where F_X = 0, the system reduces to a single propagating sound mode with squared speed c_1^2 = [(F_{by} b_0 - F_y)^2 - F_{yy} F_{bb} b_0^2] / [F_{yy} (F_y y_0 - F_b b_0)].
- The pressure is identified as p = F - F_b b, and the thermodynamic relation ρ + p = Ts + μ n is satisfied with the identifications μ = y, s = b, T = -F_b.

## Context

This work extends previous effective field theory approaches to ordinary fluids (Dubovsky et al., 2006; Endlich et al., 2010) and zero-temperature superfluids (Son, 2002) to the finite-temperature relativistic case, building on the two-fluid model of Landau. The formalism mirrors the hydrodynamical analysis of Son (2005) and Herzog et al. (2009) but derives the same equations from a variational principle rather than from constitutive relations. The paper enables systematic computation of observables such as sound wave scattering off superfluid vortices via standard Feynman rules, and provides a framework for studying relativistic superfluid systems like neutron stars.
