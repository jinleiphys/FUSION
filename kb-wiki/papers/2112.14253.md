# Nuclear level densities: from empirical models to microscopic methods

> The level density is among the most important statistical nuclear properties. It appears in Fermi's golden rule for transition rates and is an important input to the Hauser-Feshbach theory of compound nucleus reactions. We discuss empirical models of level densities and summarize the main experimental methods used to determine them. The microscopic calculation of level densities in the presence of correlations is a challenging many-body problem. We review recent microscopic approaches to calculate level densities. Mean-field and combinatorial methods have been applied across the nuclear chart, but often need to be augmented with empirical collective enhancement factors. The moment method and the auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFMC) method are formulated in the context of the configuration-interaction shell model approach, and include correlations beyond the mean-field approximation.

# Nuclear level densities: from empirical models to microscopic methods

## Key claim
Microscopic methods that include correlations beyond the mean-field approximation, such as the auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFMC) method and the moment method, can achieve excellent agreement with experimental level densities without adjustable parameters, but mean-field and combinatorial approaches still require empirical collective enhancement factors whose energy dependence remains poorly understood.

## Method
The paper reviews the thermodynamic approach to level densities via the canonical and grand-canonical ensembles, where the level density is obtained as the inverse Laplace transform of the partition function evaluated in the saddle-point approximation. Empirical models including the back-shifted Fermi gas, constant-temperature, and composite Gilbert-Cameron formulas are summarized alongside experimental methods such as level counting, neutron resonance data, particle evaporation spectra, and the Oslo method. Microscopic approaches covered include mean-field Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov with combinatorial counting, spectral averaging theory (moment method), and the AFMC method based on the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation, which reduces the interacting many-body problem to an average over non-interacting systems in external auxiliary fields.

## Key numbers
- Bethe's formula for the state density of non-interacting fermions with both protons and neutrons: $\rho(E_x) = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{12} a^{-1/4} E_x^{-5/4} e^{2 \sqrt{a E_x}}$, where $a = \frac{\pi^2}{6}[ g_p(\epsilon^{(p)}_F) + g_n(\epsilon^{(n)}_F)]$
- For a free Fermi gas in a box: $a \approx A / 15\;{\rm MeV}^{-1}$; for an isotropic harmonic oscillator: $a \approx A / 10\;{\rm MeV}^{-1}$; for a Woods-Saxon potential in medium-mass nuclei: $a \approx A/10.7$ MeV$^{-1}$
- The spin-cutoff parameter $\sigma$ is related to the thermal moment of inertia $I$ by $\sigma^2 = I T / \hbar^2$
- The ratio of negative- to positive-parity partition functions for an even-even nucleus is given by $Z_- / Z_+ = \tanh f$, where $f$ is the total average occupation of the $\pi$-parity orbitals
- AFMC level densities for nickel isotopes $^{59-64}$Ni show excellent agreement with experimental data without adjustable parameters
- The rotational enhancement factor in deformed $^{162}$Dy decays to 1 near the mean-field shape transition at $E_x \sim 30$ MeV
- In samarium isotopes, the spherical region dominates the state density in $^{148}$Sm, while in deformed $^{152}$Sm and $^{154}$Sm the prolate region dominates at lower excitation energies but the spherical density becomes comparable at higher excitations

## Context
This review extends the long line of work on nuclear level densities from Bethe's original Fermi gas model (Bethe 1936) through the Gilbert-Cameron composite formula (Gilbert 1965) to modern microscopic approaches. It contrasts mean-field and combinatorial methods (Demetriou 2001, Goriely 2008) that require empirical collective enhancement factors with the AFMC method (Lang 1993, Alhassid 1994) and the moment method (Mon 1975, Kota 2010) that include correlations beyond mean-field within the configuration-interaction shell model. The AFMC results for spin distributions benchmark the spin-cutoff model (Bethe 1937, Ericson 1960) and reveal staggering effects in even-even nuclei at low excitation energies, while the deformation-dependent level densities (Mustonen 2018) enable future modeling of shape dynamics such as fission without invoking a mean-field approximation.
