# Optimal neutron-star mass ranges to constrain the equation of state of nuclear matter with electromagnetic and gravitational-wave observations

> Exploiting a very large library of physically plausible equations of state (EOSs) containing more than $10^{7}$ members and yielding more than $10^{9}$ stellar models, we conduct a survey of the impact that a neutron-star radius measurement via electromagnetic observations can have on the EOS of nuclear matter. Such measurements are soon to be expected from the ongoing \textit{Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer} mission and will complement the constraints on the EOS from gravitational-wave detections. Thanks to the large statistical range of our EOS library, we can obtain a first quantitative estimate of the commonly made assumption that the high-density part of the EOS is best constrained when measuring the radius of the most massive, albeit rare, neutron stars with masses $M\gtrsim2.1\,M_\odot$. At the same time, we find that radius measurements of neutron stars with masses $M\simeq1.7-1.85\,M_\odot$ can provide the strongest constraints on the low-density part of the EOS. Finally, we quantify how radius measurements by future missions can further improve our understanding of the EOS of matter at nuclear densities.

## Key claim

The authors provide a first quantitative estimate that the high-density part of the nuclear equation of state (EOS) is best constrained by measuring the radius of the most massive neutron stars (M ≳ 2.1 M⊙), while radius measurements of stars with masses M ≃ 1.7–1.85 M⊙ can provide the strongest constraints on the low-density part of the EOS, a finding that depends on using a sufficiently large and convergent EOS library rather than a Bayesian analysis with specific priors.

## Method

The authors constructed a library of approximately 2.5×10⁷ physically plausible hadronic EOSs by combining chiral effective field theory calculations at low densities with perturbative QCD calculations at high densities, modeling the intermediate region with piecewise polytropes. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to generate about 3.8×10⁹ stellar models, then applied constraints from the GW170817 event (maximum mass ≲ 2.2 M⊙ and tidal deformability Λ₁.₄ between 290 and 580) to obtain a reduced “constraint-satisfying” set. They defined a “degree of constraint” (DOC) as χ = 1 − A/A₀, where A₀ is the area spanned by constraint-satisfying EOSs in the pressure-chemical potential plane and A is the area after imposing a hypothetical mass-radius measurement represented as a square box in the (M,R) plane.

## Key numbers

- EOS library contained approximately 2.5×10⁷ physically plausible EOSs, yielding approximately 3.8×10⁹ stellar models.
- After applying GW170817 constraints, about 20% of the complete EOS set remained (the “constraint-satisfying” set).
- The tidal deformability constraint used was 290 < Λ₁.₄ < 580 for a 1.4 M⊙ star.
- The maximum mass constraint used was M_TOV ≲ 2.2 M⊙ (with a comparison case of ≲ 2.3 M⊙).
- The low-density regime was defined for chemical potential μ ∈ [0, 1.12 GeV]; the high-density regime for μ ∈ [1.12 GeV, 1.44 GeV].
- The highest number density in the sample was approximately 6.5 n₀, where n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³.
- For Case 1 (PSR J0437-4715): mass 1.44 M⊙, radius 12.28 ± 0.31 km, with 5% relative error on radius and ±0.07 M⊙ mass uncertainty.
- For Case 2: second star mass 2.0 M⊙, radius 11.58 ± 0.29 km.
- For Case 3: second star mass 1.2 M⊙, radius 12.37 ± 0.31 km.
- For a 1.44 M⊙ star with radius R₁.₄₄ ≲ 12 km, the DOC reached χ ∼ 0.67.
- At least approximately 5×10⁶ EOSs were necessary for convergent results with the chosen parameterization.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Most (2018) and Kurkela (2014) by using a much larger EOS library to make general statements independent of prior assumptions, complementing Bayesian analyses such as those by Fasano (2019) and Greif (2019). The results anticipate constraints from the NICER mission (Arzoumanian 2014) and can be applied to future gravitational-wave detections and electromagnetic observations to further constrain the EOS; the EOS library is publicly available for this purpose.
