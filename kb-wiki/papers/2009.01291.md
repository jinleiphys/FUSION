# Proton spin after 30 years: what we know and what we don't?

> More than three decades has passed since the European Muon Collaboration published the first surprising result on the spin structure of the proton. Much theoretical and experimental progress has been made in understanding the origins of the proton spin. In this review, we will discuss what we have learned so far, what are still missing, and what we shall expect to learn from the upcoming experiments including JLab 12 GeV and Electron-Ion Collider. In particular, we focus on first principles calculations and experimental measurements of the total gluon helicity $ΔG$, and quark and gluon orbital angular momenta.

## Key claim

The proton spin can be decomposed into contributions from quarks and gluons via two established sum rules (the frame-independent Ji sum rule and the infinite-momentum-frame Jaffe-Manohar sum rule), but the precise decomposition remains incomplete: the quark helicity contributes about 30-40% of the total spin, while the gluon helicity and orbital angular momentum contributions are still subject to significant uncertainties that upcoming experiments at JLab 12 GeV and the Electron-Ion Collider aim to resolve.

## Method

The authors review theoretical and experimental progress on proton spin structure, focusing on two complementary spin sum rules: the frame-independent Ji sum rule (ΔΣ/2 + L_q + J_g = ħ/2) derived from the gauge-invariant energy-momentum tensor, and the infinite-momentum-frame Jaffe-Manohar sum rule (ΔΣ/2 + ΔG + ℓ_q + ℓ_g = ħ/2) derived from canonical angular momentum operators. They discuss lattice QCD calculations using large-momentum effective theory (LaMET) to compute parton helicity and orbital angular momentum distributions, and summarize experimental constraints from polarized deep-inelastic scattering, RHIC-spin experiments, and prospects for JLab 12 GeV and the Electron-Ion Collider.

## Key numbers

- EMC measurement of quark helicity contribution: ΔΣ(Q²=10.7 GeV²) = 0.060 ± 0.047 ± 0.069, consistent with zero
- Lattice QCD (ETMC collaboration, physical pion mass, MS scheme at μ=2 GeV): total quark helicity contribution = 38.2(3.1)% of proton spin; total quark orbital angular momentum = 18.8(10.1)%; total gluon contribution = 37.5%
- Lattice QCD (χQCD collaboration): ΔG(μ=√10 GeV) = 0.251(47)(16), corresponding to 50(9)(3)% of proton spin
- Lattice QCD (quenched approximation): total quark angular momentum J_q = 0.30 ± 0.07 (60% of proton spin)

## Context

This review extends the discussions in earlier reviews by Aidala et al. (2012) and Deur et al. (2018), focusing specifically on the origins of proton spin rather than providing a comprehensive survey of hadron spin physics. It synthesizes three decades of work since the European Muon Collaboration's 1987 discovery that quarks carry far less of the proton spin than expected from the quark model, and outlines how the upcoming Electron-Ion Collider and JLab 12 GeV program will provide high-precision measurements of gluon helicity ΔG and gluon angular momentum J_g and ℓ_g, while lattice QCD with LaMET offers first-principles calculations to complement experimental results.
