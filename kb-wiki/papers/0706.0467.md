# Effects of geometric constraints on the nuclear multifragmentation process

> We include in statistical model calculations the facts that in the nuclear multifragmentation process the fragments are produced within a given volume and have a finite size. The corrections associated with these constraints affect the partition modes and, as a consequence, other observables in the process. In particular, we find that the favored fragmenting modes strongly suppress the collective flow energy, leading to much lower values compared to what is obtained from unconstrained calculations. This leads, for a given total excitation energy, to a nontrivial correlation between the breakup temperature and the collective expansion velocity. In particular we find that, under some conditions, the temperature of the fragmenting system may increase as a function of this expansion velocity, contrary to what it might be expected.

## Key claim

Including geometric constraints (finite fragment size and confinement within the breakup volume) in Statistical Multifragmentation Model calculations strongly suppresses the collective flow energy compared to unconstrained calculations, and under certain conditions the breakup temperature may increase with collective expansion velocity rather than decrease.

## Method

The authors modified the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) to require that each fragment lies entirely inside the spherical breakup volume, using a radial velocity field u(r) = γ r/R. They calculated the average kinetic energy of fragments accounting for the reduced accessible volume for larger fragments, and solved the energy conservation equation to determine breakup temperatures for each partition. Secondary deexcitation of primary fragments was treated via a Weisskopf statistical decay approach with Monte Carlo sampling of decay channels.

## Key numbers

- For a system with A0=168, Z0=75 at breakup density χ=2 (three times normal nuclear density) and E*/A = 6 MeV, the maximum average flow energy is approximately E_flow/A = 1 MeV at β_flow ≈ 0.105
- Without geometric constraints, the flow energy per particle ε_flow would give E_flow = ε_flow × A0; with constraints, heavy fragments are more affected than light ones
- For the simulation with E*/A = 6.0 MeV and ε_flow = 2.0 MeV, the average kinetic energy of primary fragments versus atomic number Z was fitted as E_Z = 12.3 + 1.4Z MeV
- The actual simulation temperature was T = 5.9 MeV, while neglecting geometric constraints would lead to an inferred T = 8.2 MeV and ε_flow = 0.7 MeV
- After secondary decay, the kinetic energy fit became E_Z ≈ 16.8 + 2.4Z MeV

## Context

This work extends the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) of Bondorf (1995) and the Improved Statistical Multifragmentation Model (ISMM) by incorporating finite-size corrections to radial flow calculations. It addresses criticisms by DasGupta (2001) regarding the assumption that fragments can coalesce despite non-zero relative velocities between different regions. The results suggest that interpretations based on statistical calculations where flow energy is simply subtracted from total energy (as done in flow studies by Schussler (1992) and others) should be reviewed, and that other mechanisms beyond radial flow are needed to explain experimentally observed slopes in fragment energy spectra.
