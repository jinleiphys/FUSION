# No-core shell model for 48-Ca, 48-Sc and 48-Ti

> We report the first no-core shell model results for $^{48}Ca$, $^{48}Sc$ and $^{48}Ti$ with derived and modified two-body Hamiltonians. We use an oscillator basis with a limited $\hbarΩ$ range around $45/A^{1/3}-25/A^{2/3} = 10.5 MeV$ and a limited model space up to $1\hbarΩ$. No single-particle energies are used. We find that the charge dependence of the bulk binding energy of eight A=48 nuclei is reasonably described with an effective Hamiltonian derived from the CD-Bonn interaction while there is an overall underbinding by about 0.4 MeV/nucleon. However, the resulting spectra exhibit deficiencies that are anticipated due to: (1) basis space limitations and/or the absence of effective many-body interactions; and, (2) the absence of genuine three-nucleon interactions. We then introduce additive isospin-dependent central terms plus a tensor force to our Hamiltonian and achieve accurate binding energies and reasonable spectra for all three nuclei. The resulting no-core shell model opens a path for applications to the double-beta ($ββ$) decay process.

## Key claim

The first no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations for A=48 nuclei are reported, but the authors acknowledge that the ab initio approach with a two-body effective Hamiltonian derived from the CD-Bonn interaction underbinds by about 0.4 MeV/nucleon and produces spectra with deficiencies due to basis space limitations and the absence of three-nucleon interactions; only after adding phenomenological isospin-dependent central terms plus a tensor force do they achieve accurate binding energies and reasonable spectra.

## Method

The authors performed no-core shell model calculations for 48-Ca, 48-Sc, and 48-Ti using an oscillator basis with a limited model space up to 1 hbar-Omega and a harmonic oscillator frequency near 10.5 MeV. They derived a two-body effective Hamiltonian from the CD-Bonn NN interaction using the Lee-Suzuki-Okamoto unitary transformation at the two-body cluster level, then added phenomenological finite-range central and tensor potentials with strengths fitted to ground state energies and selected excited states. The m-scheme basis dimensionalities were 12022 for 48-Ca, 139046 for 48-Sc, and 634744 for 48-Ti in the 0 hbar-Omega model spaces, with 2921360 for 48-Ca in the 1 hbar-Omega model space.

## Key numbers

- Underbinding of about 20 MeV (0.4 MeV/nucleon) for A=48 nuclei when using the ab initio CD-Bonn effective Hamiltonian
- Harmonic oscillator frequency of 10.5 MeV, derived from the formula 45/A^(1/3) - 25/A^(2/3)
- Point proton rms radii of 3.51 fm (48-Ca), 3.55 fm (48-Sc), and 3.59 fm (48-Ti); point neutron rms radii of 3.75 fm (48-Ca), 3.73 fm (48-Sc), and 3.71 fm (48-Ti)
- Phenomenological central strengths V0 = -14.40 MeV-fm^2 and V1 = -22.61 MeV-fm^2 with range R = 1.5 fm, and tensor strength Vt = -52.22 MeV-fm^3
- First excited 2+ state of 48-Ca at 3.832 MeV excitation experimentally; the ab initio NCSM produced compressed spectra relative to experiment
- Lowest negative parity excitation in 48-Ca at 6.9 MeV when using the 1 hbar-Omega effective Hamiltonian strategy

## Context

This work extends the no-core shell method, previously applied to light nuclei with A less than or equal to 17, to the A=48 region for the first time, motivated by the need for precision treatments of nuclear matrix elements involved in double-beta decay of 48-Ca. It contrasts with conventional shell model calculations by Caurier et al. (1990s) who used a 40-Ca core with the KB3 effective interaction and experimental single-particle energies, and with other pf-shell calculations that employed truncated model spaces. The results demonstrate that current NCSM capabilities with two-body cluster approximations and limited model spaces are insufficient for predictive calculations of double-beta decay rates, and that larger basis spaces and genuine three-nucleon forces are needed, opening a path for future work with chiral effective field theory interactions as explored by Nogga et al. (2006) and Navratil et al. (2007).
