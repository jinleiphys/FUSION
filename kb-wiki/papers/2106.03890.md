# Extreme Matter meets Extreme Gravity: Ultra-heavy neutron stars with crossovers and first-order phase transitions

> The speed of sound of the matter within neutron stars may contain non-smooth structure related to first-order phase transitions or or crossovers. Here we investigate what are the observable consequences of structure, such as bumps, spikes, step functions, plateaus, and kinks. One of the main consequences is the possibility of ultra-heavy neutron stars, i.e.~stars with masses significantly heavier than two solar masses. These stars pass all observational and theoretical constraints, including those imposed by recent LIGO/Virgo gravitational-wave observations and NICER X-ray observations. We thoroughly investigate other consequences of this structure in the speed of sound to develop an understanding of how non-smooth features affect astrophysical observables, such as stellar radii, tidal deformability, moment of inertia, and Love number. Our results have important implications to future gravitational wave and X-ray observations of neutron stars and their impact in nuclear astrophysics.

## Key claim
If one assumes that the light companion of GW190814 (mass 2.5-2.67 solar masses) was a slowly-rotating neutron star rather than a black hole, then neutron stars with masses significantly above 2.5 solar masses can exist without violating any current observational or theoretical constraints, including those from LIGO/Virgo and NICER, provided the speed of sound in dense matter contains non-smooth structure such as bumps, spikes, or plateaus.

## Method
The authors constructed a large set of equations of state by parameterizing the functional form of the speed of sound squared as a function of baryon density, allowing for features such as bumps, spikes, step functions, plateaus, kinks, and wells (where the speed of sound drops to zero to simulate first-order phase transitions). They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to generate mass-radius sequences and computed tidal deformabilities, moments of inertia, and Love numbers for each equation of state, then applied observational constraints from GW170817, GW190814, and NICER measurements of PSR J0740+6620.

## Key numbers
- The absolutely maximum mass of a causal neutron star could be in the range of M = 2.5-3.6 solar masses and R = 11-16 km without violating any observational constraints.
- If the light companion of GW190814 (mass 2.5-2.67 solar masses) was a neutron star, the maximum central baryon density reached is approximately 6 times nuclear saturation density (n_sat); without this assumption, the maximum central baryon density is less than about 8 n_sat.
- For an equation of state reaching masses up to 2.91 solar masses (the mass of the V723 Mon companion), the tidal deformability would be as low as Lambda approximately 2.5.
- The I-Love-Q relations (inter-relations between moment of inertia, Love number, and quadrupole moment) show relative fractional variability below 1.5% across all equations of state with structure in the speed of sound.

## Context
This work extends the study of neutron star equations of state with phase transitions, building on the parameterization approach of Tan (2020) and addressing the interpretation of the GW190814 event as possibly containing a neutron star rather than a black hole, contrary to arguments by Most (2020) and others. The paper investigates whether first-order phase transitions can produce mass twins in heavy neutron stars (above 2 solar masses), extending earlier work on twin stars by Christian (2020) and Blaschke (2020). The results enable future gravitational wave and X-ray observations to distinguish between different types of phase transitions (first-order versus crossover) in neutron star interiors.
