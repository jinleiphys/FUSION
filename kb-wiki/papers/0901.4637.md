# Gravitational radiation from crystalline color-superconducting hybrid stars

> The interiors of high mass compact (neutron) stars may contain deconfined quark matter in a crystalline color superconducting (CCS) state. On a basis of microscopic nuclear and quark matter equations of states we explore the internal structure of such stars in general relativity. We find that their stable sequence harbors CCS quark cores with masses M_core \le (0.78-0.82)M_{sun} and radii R_core \le 7 km. The CCS quark matter can support nonaxisymmetric deformations, because of its finite shear modulus, and can generate gravitational radiation at twice the rotation frequency of the star. Assuming that the CCS core is maximally strained we compute the maximal quadrupole moment it can sustain. The characteristic strain of gravitational wave emission $h_0$ predicted by our models are compared to the upper limits obtained by the LIGO and GEO 600 detectors. The upper limits are consistent with the breaking strain of CCS matter σ\le 10^{-4} and large pairing gaps Δ\sim 50 MeV, or, alternatively, with σ\sim 10^{-3} and small pairing gaps Δ\sim 15 MeV. An observationally determined value of the characteristic strain h_0 can pin down the product σΔ^2. On the theoretical side a better understanding of the breaking strain of CCS matter will be needed to predict reliably the level of the deformation of CCS quark core from first principles.

## Key claim

The characteristic strain of gravitational wave emission from hybrid stars with crystalline color-superconducting (CCS) quark cores is consistent with current LIGO and GEO 600 upper limits only under specific combinations of the breaking strain and pairing gap: either a breaking strain σ ≤ 10⁻⁴ with large gaps Δ ∼ 50 MeV, or σ ∼ 10⁻³ with small gaps Δ ∼ 15 MeV.

## Method

The authors constructed hybrid compact star models using microscopic equations of state: Dirac-Bruckner-Hartree-Fock theory for nuclear matter and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model for quark matter, with a Maxwell construction for the deconfinement phase transition. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations in general relativity to obtain equilibrium sequences of nonrotating configurations, then computed the maximal quadrupole moment of the CCS quark core by assuming it is maximally strained and using the shear modulus of CCS matter from Mannarelli (2006). The characteristic strain of gravitational wave emission was calculated for rotation at the Crab pulsar frequency and compared to LIGO upper limits.

## Key numbers

- CCS quark core masses: M_core ≤ (0.78–0.82) M_⊙ for stable configurations; the full range from microscopic models is 0 ≤ M_core/M_⊙ ≤ 0.75–0.88 for central densities 1.3 × 10¹⁵ ≤ ρ_c ≤ 2 × 10¹⁵ g cm⁻³
- CCS quark core radii: R_core ≤ 7 km; hybrid star radii are approximately constant at R = 12 km within the stability range
- Maximum total stellar mass: M_max ≃ 2 M_⊙ at central density 2 × 10¹⁵ g cm⁻³
- Shear modulus of CCS matter: μ = 2.47 MeV fm⁻³ (Δ/10 MeV)² (μ_q/400 MeV)²
- Current LIGO upper limit on gravitational wave strain from the Crab pulsar: h = 3.4 × 10⁻²⁵ (95% confidence)
- The current upper limit implies σ_max Δ² ∼ 0.25 MeV² under the assumptions of this model

## Context

This work extends earlier estimates by Lin (2007) and Haskell et al. (2007) for uniform-density incompressible models by using microscopic equations of state for both nuclear and quark matter, building on the equilibrium and stability study of hybrid configurations by Ippolito et al. (2007). The authors find that the uniform-density incompressible approximation systematically overestimates the quadrupole moment and strain amplitude, though the error is not large. The results enable future observational determination of the product σΔ² from measured gravitational wave strain, while highlighting the need for first-principles calculations of the breaking strain of CCS matter.
