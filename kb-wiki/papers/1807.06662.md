# Actinide Production in Neutron-Rich Ejecta of a Neutron Star Merger

> The rapid-neutron-capture ("r") process is responsible for synthesizing many of the heavy elements observed in both the solar system and Galactic metal-poor halo stars. Simulations of r-process nucleosynthesis can reproduce abundances derived from observations with varying success, but so far fail to account for the observed over-enhancement of actinides, present in about 30% of r-process-enhanced stars. In this work, we investigate actinide production in the dynamical ejecta of a neutron star merger and explore if varying levels of neutron richness can reproduce the actinide boost. We also investigate the sensitivity of actinide production on nuclear physics properties: fission distribution, beta-decay, and mass model. For most cases, the actinides are over-produced in our models if the initial conditions are sufficiently neutron-rich for fission cycling. We find that actinide production can be so robust in the dynamical ejecta that an additional lanthanide-rich, actinide-poor component is necessary in order to match observations of actinide-boost stars. We present a simple actinide-dilution model that folds in estimated contributions from two nucleosynthetic sites within a merger event. Our study suggests that while the dynamical ejecta of a neutron star merger is a likely production site for the formation of actinides, a significant contribution from another site or sites (e.g., the neutron star merger accretion disk wind) is required to explain abundances of r-process-enhanced, metal-poor stars.

## Key claim

The dynamical ejecta of a neutron star merger can overproduce actinides relative to lanthanides when initial conditions are sufficiently neutron-rich for fission cycling, but matching observations of actinide-boost stars requires an additional lanthanide-rich, actinide-poor component from another site such as the accretion disk wind.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis calculations using the PRISM nuclear network code on a 1.4-1.4 solar mass neutron star merger trajectory from Rosswog (2013), varying the initial electron fraction between 0.005 and 0.250. They used FRDM2012 nuclear masses with self-consistent Hauser-Feshbach rates for neutron capture and neutron-induced fission, and systematically replaced fission fragment distributions, beta-decay rates (comparing Moller+18 with Marketin 2016), and mass models to test sensitivity of actinide production.

## Key numbers

- About 30% of r-process-enhanced stars show an actinide boost (over-enhancement of thorium and uranium relative to lanthanides)
- At the baseline electron fraction of Ye=0.035, approximately 78% of mass above A=230 leaves the heavy region via fission between freezeout and about 8 seconds
- By 1 Gyr after the r-process event, approximately 10% of the original A>230 mass ends up in either 232Th or 238U
- Europium production reaches a maximum at Ye=0.185 (Ye^L), while thorium production is maximized at Ye=0.125 (Ye^A)
- Sensitivity of final abundances to choices of theoretical spontaneous fission and alpha-decay rates is less than 0.1%
- The Marketin (2016) beta-decay rates are between two and several thousand times faster than the Moller+18 rates above A~190

## Context

This work extends previous r-process nucleosynthesis studies that failed to account for the actinide boost observed in about 30% of r-process-enhanced stars (Mashonkina 2014). The authors build on neutron star merger simulations following the GW170817 event (Abbott 2017) and prior work by Korobkin (2012) and Rosswog (2013). The study enables a new actinide-dilution model that combines contributions from dynamical ejecta and accretion disk wind to explain actinide-boost stars, and provides updated initial production ratios for Th/Eu, U/Eu, and U/Th cosmochronometry that can be applied to metal-poor stars.
