# Uncertainty Quantification and Propagation in Nuclear Density Functional Theory

> Nuclear density functional theory (DFT) is one of the main theoretical tools used to study the properties of heavy and superheavy elements, or to describe the structure of nuclei far from stability. While on-going efforts seek to better root nuclear DFT in the theory of nuclear forces [see Duguet et al., this issue], energy functionals remain semi-phenomenological constructions that depend on a set of parameters adjusted to experimental data in finite nuclei. In this paper, we review recent efforts to quantify the related uncertainties, and propagate them to model predictions. In particular, we cover the topics of parameter estimation for inverse problems, statistical analysis of model uncertainties and Bayesian inference methods. Illustrative examples are taken from the literature.

## Key claim

This review argues that while nuclear density functional theory (DFT) is the only microscopic approach capable of describing heavy and superheavy nuclei, its semi-phenomenological energy functionals depend on parameters adjusted to experimental data, and rigorous uncertainty quantification and propagation methodology is essential because it is "by no means guaranteed that the theory will be capable of converging to the exact result."

## Method

The paper reviews statistical methods applied to nuclear energy density functionals (EDFs), focusing on parameter estimation for inverse problems using chi-squared minimization with derivative-free optimization algorithms (specifically the POUNDERS algorithm), covariance analysis for estimating statistical uncertainties, and Bayesian inference methods for propagating uncertainties to model predictions. The review covers both the self-consistent mean-field approach (using Skyrme or Gogny effective interactions) and the strict Kohn-Sham EDF approach, discussing how the choice of experimental dataset, objective function weights, and optimization starting points affect the resulting parameterizations.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear binding energies are computed within approximately 500 keV in state-of-the-art DFT calculations, representing a relative error of 0.05% or less for nuclei with mass A > 100
- The UNEDF2 functional used 5 data types and 130 data points, while UNEDF0 used 3 data types and 108 points
- For the UNEDF1 optimization, root-mean-square deviations for deformed masses ranged from 0.776 MeV to 2.596 MeV depending on the choice of standard deviation for odd-even staggering data
- The POUNDERS algorithm converged to a chi-squared value of 67.9 from the SLy4 starting point (235 function evaluations) and 67.6 from the SkM* starting point (150 function evaluations)
- The largest differences between optimizations starting from different initial points occurred for L^{NM}_{sym} (slope of symmetry energy at saturation) and C^{rho nabla J}_1 (isovector spin-orbit coupling constant)

## Context

This review extends the work of parameter estimation for nuclear EDFs that began with full regression analysis by Toivanen (2008) and covariance matrix computation by Kluepfel (2009), and it builds on the UNEDF collaboration's systematic optimization protocols (Kortelainen 2010, 2012, 2014). The paper enables future work by identifying that systematic uncertainties remain largely unstudied, that the impact of objective function weights on optimization results can be significant (as shown in Schunck 2015), and that nonlinearity in observables such as fission isomer excitation energies limits the validity of simple covariance techniques.
