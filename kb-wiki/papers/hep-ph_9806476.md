# A Four-Fermi Model in 0+1 Dimensions in Matter

> The results of a number of constituent quark models in matter may be understood in the mean-field approximation by using a simple four-fermi model in 0+1 dimensions.

# A Four-Fermi Model in 0+1 Dimensions in Matter

## Key claim

The thermodynamics and phase structure of many constituent quark models (such as the instanton model and NJL model) can be captured by a simple four-fermi model in 0+1 dimensions, but only in the mean-field (large N) approximation and only for the case of a two-level quark system.

## Method

The authors construct a Lagrangian with chiral scalar and vector four-fermi interactions for a quark field with color and flavor indices, then analyze it on a circle of radius β = 1/T in one dimension. They derive the pressure per particle and gap equations in the mean-field (large N) limit, solve for the phase structure at finite temperature and chemical potential, and construct the quark condensate and spectral sum rules for both quenched and unquenched approximations.

## Key numbers

- Critical chemical potential for phase change at m = T = 0: μ_c = 1/(4Σ) + 1/(4α)
- Tricritical point temperature: T_{3c} = m_Q/3, where m_Q is the constituent quark mass
- Tricritical point density: ρ_{3c} = n/√3, where n = N/V_3
- Tricritical point chemical potential: μ_{3c} = m_Q log(2 + √3)/3
- Second order transition temperature at μ = 0: T_* = m_Q/2
- For constituent mass m_Q ∼ 300 MeV: μ_* = T_* ∼ 150 MeV, μ_{3c} ∼ T_{3c} ∼ 100 MeV
- In absence of matter: n = |⟨q̄q⟩| ∼ (200 MeV)^3
- Ratio of tricritical density to nuclear matter density: ρ_{3c}/ρ_0 ∼ 10/3, where ρ_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3}
- Critical chemical potential in quenched approximation: μ_* ≈ 0.5277

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier analysis (Janik et al., 1998) of a four-fermi model without vector coupling, and relates to NJL model studies (All, various years) and Walecka-type models (Kapusta, various years) that exhibit liquid-gas transitions at low nucleonic matter density. The model enables systematic study of how microscopic spectral sum rules respond to temperature and chemical potential changes, including phase transitions, and is intended to be useful for interpreting lattice QCD simulations with matter.
