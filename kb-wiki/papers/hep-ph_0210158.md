# A statistical approach to the QCD phase transition --A mystery in the critical temperature

> We study the QCD phase transition based on the statistical treatment with the bag-model picture of hadrons, and derive a phenomenological relation among the low-lying hadron masses, the hadron sizes and the critical temperature of the QCD phase transition. We apply this phenomenological relation to both full QCD and quenched QCD, and compare these results with the corresponding lattice QCD results. Whereas such a statistical approach works well in full QCD, it results in an extremely large estimate of the critical temperature in quenched QCD, which indicates a serious problem in understanding of the QCD phase transition. This large discrepancy traces back to the fact that enough number of glueballs are not yet thermally excited at the critical temperature T_c \simeq 280 MeV in quenched QCD due to the extremely small statistical factor as exp(-m_G/T_c) \simeq 0.00207. This fact itself has a quite general nature independent of the particular choice of the effective model framework. We are thus arrive at a mystery, namely, what is really the trigger of the deconfinement phase transition.

## Key claim

The statistical bag-model approach, which reproduces the full-QCD critical temperature remarkably well, fails catastrophically in quenched QCD because glueballs are too heavy to be thermally excited at the expected transition temperature, leading to an estimated critical temperature of about 827 MeV versus the lattice QCD result of about 280 MeV.

## Method

The authors model the QCD phase transition using a closed-packing picture where hadrons are treated as spherical bags; the critical temperature is found by solving r_V(T_c) = 1, where r_V(T) is the spatial occupation ratio of thermally excited bags. For full QCD, the sum runs over pions and low-lying vector mesons; for quenched QCD, it runs over glueball states with masses and radii taken from lattice QCD calculations.

## Key numbers

- In full QCD with only pions (m_pi = 140 MeV, R_pi ~ 1 fm, degeneracy 3), the critical temperature is estimated as T_c ~ 183 MeV.
- The quenched lattice QCD result for the critical temperature is T_c = 0.629(3) sqrt(sigma) ~ 280 MeV (with sqrt(sigma) = 450 MeV).
- Using only the lightest 0++ glueball (mass 1730 MeV, radius 0.4 fm, degeneracy 1), the statistical approach gives T_c ~ 827 MeV.
- At T = 280 MeV, the statistical factor for the lightest glueball is e^{-m_G/T_c} ~ 0.00207.
- Including all 15 low-lying glueball states up to 5 GeV (assuming typical hadron size 1 fm) still gives T_c ~ 395 MeV.
- At T = 280 MeV, the inter-particle distance of glueballs is estimated as l ~ 5 fm, corresponding to a density of about 1/(4.0 fm)^3.

## Context

This work extends the Hagedorn (1968) picture of a hadronic phase transition and the bag model of Chodos, Jaffe, Johnson and Thorn (1974) to compare full QCD and quenched QCD. The success in full QCD (consistent with lattice results from Karsch, Laermann and Peikert (2001) and Lutgemeier (1998)) contrasts sharply with the failure in quenched QCD, where the glueball spectrum from Morningstar and Peardon (1999) cannot provide enough thermal excitation. The authors argue this is a model-independent puzzle that challenges the understanding of what drives the deconfinement transition in quenched QCD, and they suggest that future work must address what the actual trigger of the phase transition is.
