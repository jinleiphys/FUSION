# Nonflow suppression in flow analysis with a maximum likelihood estimator

> We show that the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) is an effective tool for mitigating non-flow effects in flow analysis. To this end, one constructs two toy models that simulate non-flow contributions corresponding to particle decay and momentum conservation, respectively. The performance of MLE is analyzed by comparing it against standard approaches such as particle correlation and event plane methods. For both cases, MLE is observed to provide a reasonable estimate of the underlying flow harmonics, and in particular, its performance can be further improved when the specific form of the likelihood in the presence of non-flow can be assessed. The dependencies of extracted flow harmonics on the multiplicity of individual events and the total number of events are analyzed. Additionally, it is shown that the proposed approach performs efficiently in addressing deficiencies in detector acceptance. These findings suggest MLE as a compelling alternative to standard methods for flow analysis.

## Key claim

The maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) provides a reasonable estimate of underlying flow harmonics in the presence of non-flow effects from particle decay and momentum conservation, and its performance can be further improved when the specific form of the likelihood in the presence of non-flow can be assessed. The authors note that for the triangular flow $v_3$ in the particle decay toy model, MLE estimation was slightly inferior compared to the event-plane and particle correlation methods.

## Method

Two toy models were constructed to simulate non-flow contributions: one mimicking particle decay where first-generation particles split into two daughter particles with angular separation drawn from a Gaussian distribution, and another mimicking momentum conservation where transverse momentum conservation is enforced for individual subsets of particles. The performance of MLE was analyzed by comparing it against standard approaches (particle correlation and event-plane methods) using Monte Carlo generated events with known input flow harmonics up to fourth order, with event planes randomized for each event.

## Key numbers

- For toy model I (particle decay) with $M_0=3000$ first-generation particles, $P_\mathrm{dcy}=1/3$, and $\sigma=15^\circ$, MLE estimated $v_2=0.187$ compared to input $v_2=0.2$, while particle correlation gave $0.186$ and event-plane gave $0.186$
- For toy model I with $P_\mathrm{dcy}=1/2$ and $\sigma=15^\circ$, MLE estimated $v_2=0.144$ compared to input $v_2=0.2$, while particle correlation gave $0.142$ and event-plane gave $0.143$
- For toy model I with $\sigma=\pi/24$ and $P_\mathrm{dcy}=1/3$, MLE estimated $v_2=0.199$ compared to input $v_2=0.2$, while particle correlation gave $0.196$ and event-plane gave $0.196$
- For toy model I with $M_0=300$ and $v_3$ input $0.15$, MLE estimated $v_3=0.133$ while particle correlation gave $0.130$ and event-plane gave $0.134$
- For toy model I with $M_0=300$ and $v_4$ input $0.25$, MLE estimated $v_4=0.199$ while particle correlation gave $0.197$ and event-plane gave $0.199$

## Context

This work extends the MLE-based flow analysis framework proposed by the same group (Ye et al., 2024) by systematically studying non-flow suppression, which had been conjectured but not previously demonstrated. The study contrasts MLE with standard approaches including the event-plane method (Poskanzer and Voloshin, 1998) and particle correlation/cumulant methods (Borghini, Dinh, and Ollitrault, 2001). The results suggest MLE as a compelling alternative for flow analysis, particularly because its flexibility allows incorporating additional parameters (such as decay probability and angular spread) into the likelihood function, which is not straightforward for other methods.
