# Cross-sections of neutron rich nuclei from projectile fragmentation: canonical thermodynamic model estimates

> A remarkably simple dependence of fragmentation cross-section on average binding energy has been established in experimental data. This dependence was empirically parametrised leading to a very useful formula for extrapolation. We find that the canonical thermodynamic model, which has been used in the past for successful computations of many observables resulting from multifragmentation, reproduces the salient fetures of fragmentation cross-sections of very neutron rich nuclei very well. This helps towards a theoretical understanding of the observed data.

# Cross-sections of neutron rich nuclei from projectile fragmentation: canonical thermodynamic model estimates

## Key claim
The canonical thermodynamic model reproduces the salient features of fragmentation cross-sections for very neutron rich nuclei, including the rapid decrease and even-odd straggling, but only for the bulk of the distribution; the agreement degrades at the extreme tails (largest mass numbers) where cross-sections become very small.

## Method
The authors compute production cross-sections for silicon isotopes from projectile fragmentation of ⁴⁸Ca and copper isotopes from ⁸⁶Kr using the canonical thermodynamic model, which provides closed-form expressions for average fragment multiplicities via recursion relations for the canonical partition function. The model includes experimental binding energies for the isotopes of interest, a liquid-drop formula for other nuclei, a temperature-dependent surface energy, and a Wigner-Seitz approximation for Coulomb interactions. The cross-section is taken as σ = C ⟨n⟩, where C is a normalization constant fixed to one experimental data point, and the temperature is set to 9.5 MeV.

## Key numbers
- Silicon isotope cross-sections from ⁴⁸Ca on ⁹Be at 140 MeV per nucleon span about seven orders of magnitude in the experimental data.
- Copper isotope cross-sections from ⁸⁶Kr fragmentation span more than eight decades in the experimental data.
- The temperature used in the canonical model calculations is 9.5 MeV.
- The freeze-out volume is set to V = 6 V₀, where V₀ is the normal volume of the source nucleus.

## Context
This work extends the empirical parametrization of fragmentation cross-sections based on average binding energy, developed by Mocko et al. (2006) and Tsang et al. (2006), by providing a theoretical foundation using the canonical thermodynamic model. The model has been previously applied to multifragmentation observables by Das Gupta et al. (2001) and is related to the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) of Bondorf et al. (1995) and microcanonical simulations by Gross and Randrup, but unlike those Monte-Carlo-based approaches, the canonical model can compute very small cross-sections accurately. The results enable predictions for production cross-sections of intermediate mass fragments and properties of the largest fragment after multifragmentation, as noted by Chaudhuri et al. (2007).
