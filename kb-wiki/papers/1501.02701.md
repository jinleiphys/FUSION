# Beta decay of 252Cf on the way to scission from the exit point

> Upon increasing significantly the nuclear elongation, the beta-decay energy grows. This paper investigates within a simple yet partly microscopic approach, the transition rate of the beta decay of the 252Cf nucleus on the way to scission from the exit point for a spontaneous fission process. A rather crude classical approximation is made for the corresponding damped collective motion assumed to be one dimensional. Given these assumptions, we only aim in this paper at providing the order of magnitudes of such a phenomenon. At each deformation the energy available for beta decay, is determined from such a dynamical treatment. Then, for a given elongation, transition rates for the allowed (Fermi) beta decay are calculated from pair correlated wave functions obtained within a macroscopic-microscopic approach and then integrated over the time corresponding to the whole descent from exit to scission. The results are presented as a function of the damping factor (inverse of the characteristic damping time) in use in our classical dynamical approach. For instance, in the case of a descent time from the exit to the scission points of about $10^{- 20}$ second, one finds a total rate of beta decay corresponding roughly to 20 events per year and per milligram of 252Cf. The inclusion of pairing correlations does not affect much these results.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a nucleus like ²⁵²Cf, which is beta-stable in its ground state, becomes beta-unstable near the fission exit point, and that the total rate of beta decay during the descent from exit to scission is of order 20 events per year per milligram of ²⁵²Cf for a descent time of about 10⁻²⁰ second; however, the authors stress that these are only order-of-magnitude estimates based on a crude classical approximation for the damped collective motion.

## Method

The authors used a macroscopic-microscopic approach with the Lublin-Strasbourg Drop (LSD) model and Yukawa folded potential, including seniority pairing correlations, to compute single-particle levels and Q-values for ²⁵²Cf as a function of nuclear elongation ρ = R₁₂/R₀. They modeled the collective descent from the exit point to scission as a one-dimensional, damped classical motion with constant mass parameter (reduced mass of fragments) and linear potential, solving the equation of motion with friction force -k·dq/dt. For each deformation, they calculated allowed Fermi 0⁺ → 0⁺ beta-decay transition rates from pair-correlated wavefunctions, integrated over the descent time, and summed over all neutron-to-proton single-particle transitions satisfying K-quantum-number matching and energy conditions.

## Key numbers

- The Q(β⁻) value for ²⁵²Cf varies from -1.2 MeV at sphericity to approximately +2.5 MeV around the scission point (ρ ≈ 2.9).
- The exit point on the LDM fission barrier is located at exactly 4.8 MeV below the saddle point.
- The total energy gain from exit to scission is ΔE_max ≈ 28 MeV.
- The descent time from exit to scission ranges from ≈ 3.5 × 10⁻²¹ s (for low damping, k/μ = 1 in units of MeV/ℏ ≈ 1.5 × 10²¹ s⁻¹) to ≈ 18 × 10⁻²¹ s (for high damping, k/μ = 6).
- Pre-scission kinetic energy ranges from a couple hundred keV (k/μ = 6) to ≈ 5 MeV (k/μ = 1).
- For a descent time of about 10⁻²⁰ s, the total beta-decay rate is roughly 20 events per year per milligram of ²⁵²Cf.
- The number of beta decays per year per milligram ranges from ≈ 6 (k/μ = 1) to ≈ 46 (k/μ = 6).
- Including pairing correlations increases the decay numbers by only ≈ 2% for k/μ = 6.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of beta-decay stability under extreme deformation, building on liquid-drop model calculations of mass excess for A=252 isobars (Pomorski and collaborators, using the LSD model) and macroscopic-microscopic fission barrier calculations. The authors note that the beta-stability valley shifts from Californium to Nobelium as deformation increases from spherical to scission configurations. The results provide a first estimate of beta-decay probabilities during fission, which could serve as a probe of pre-scission dynamics (e.g., average pre-scission kinetic energy and reduced friction parameter) if experimentally accessible, though the authors acknowledge that detection with current devices for tractable amounts of ²⁵²Cf is questionable.
