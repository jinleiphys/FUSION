# Pairing vibrations in the interacting boson model based on density functional theory

> We propose a method to incorporate the coupling between shape and pairing collective degrees of freedom in the framework of the interacting boson model (IBM), based on the nuclear density functional theory. To account for pairing vibrations, a boson-number non-conserving IBM Hamiltonian is introduced. The Hamiltonian is constructed by using solutions of self-consistent mean-field calculations based on a universal energy density functional and pairing force, with constraints on the axially-symmetric quadrupole and pairing intrinsic deformations. By mapping the resulting quadrupole-pairing potential energy surface onto the expectation value of the bosonic Hamiltonian in the boson condensate state, the strength parameters of the boson Hamiltonian are determined. An illustrative calculation is performed for $^{122}$Xe, and the method is further explored in a more systematic study of rare-earth $N=92$ isotones. The inclusion of the dynamical pairing degree of freedom significantly lowers the energies of bands based on excited $0^+$ states. The results are in quantitative agreement with spectroscopic data, and are consistent with those obtained using the collective Hamiltonian approach.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method to incorporate pairing vibrations into the interacting boson model (IBM) by introducing a boson-number non-conserving Hamiltonian, but the method is restricted to axially-symmetric shapes and the mapping procedure is carried out only in the neighbourhood of the global minimum, as this region is most relevant for low-energy excitations.

## Method

The authors performed constrained self-consistent mean-field (SCMF) calculations using the relativistic mean-field plus BCS (RMF+BCS) model with the PC-PK1 energy density functional and a separable pairing force, imposing constraints on the axial quadrupole deformation β and the intrinsic pairing deformation α. They then introduced a boson-number non-conserving IBM Hamiltonian that mixes three boson subspaces differing in boson number by one, and determined the Hamiltonian's strength parameters by mapping the SCMF (β, α) potential energy surface onto the expectation value of the bosonic Hamiltonian in a boson condensate state. The Hamiltonian was diagonalized in an M-scheme basis comprising a direct sum of the three boson-number configurations.

## Key numbers

- For ¹²²Xe, the global minimum of the SCMF potential energy surface is found at β ≈ 0.32 and α ≈ 10.
- The strength parameters of the boson Hamiltonian for ¹²²Xe are: ε_s⁰ = 2.19 MeV, ε_d = 0.611 MeV, κ = −0.102 MeV, χ = −0.4, κ' = −0.029 MeV, t_s = 0.18 MeV, C_β = 2.75, C_α = 0.045.
- The B(E2; 2⁺₁ → 0⁺₁) value in ¹²²Xe is 79 W.u. in the pv-IBM calculation, compared to the experimental value of 78(4) W.u.
- The B(E2; 4⁺₁ → 2⁺₁) value in ¹²²Xe is 114 W.u. in the pv-IBM calculation, compared to the experimental value of 114(6) W.u.
- The B(E2; 6⁺₁ → 4⁺₁) value in ¹²²Xe is 124 W.u. in the pv-IBM calculation, compared to the experimental value of 1.1×10²(4) W.u.
- The B(E2; 2⁺_{K=0⁺₂} → 0⁺_{K=0⁺₂}) value in ¹²²Xe increases from 46 W.u. in the standard IBM to 79 W.u. in the pv-IBM.
- The B(E2; 4⁺_{K=0⁺₂} → 2⁺_{K=0⁺₂}) value in ¹²²Xe increases from 57 W.u. in the standard IBM to 111 W.u. in the pv-IBM.
- The B(E2; 6⁺_{K=0⁺₂} → 4⁺_{K=0⁺₂}) value in ¹²²Xe increases from 64 W.u. in the standard IBM to 119 W.u. in the pv-IBM.

## Context

This work extends the DFT-to-IBM mapping procedure developed by Nomura (2008) by adding a pairing degree of freedom, which was previously treated only through shape degrees of freedom in the standard sd-IBM. The method is consistent with the quadrupole-plus-pairing collective Hamiltonian approach of Xiang (2020) based on relativistic mean-field theory, but the current implementation is restricted to axially-symmetric shapes. The inclusion of dynamical pairing significantly lowers the energies of excited 0⁺ states in ¹²²Xe and in rare-earth N=92 isotones, enabling future studies of pairing vibrations in nuclear spectroscopy, including applications to neutrinoless ββ decay and spontaneous fission.
