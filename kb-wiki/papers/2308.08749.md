# Novel approach to the removal of the Pauli-forbidden states in the orthogonality condition model: A case of multi-$α$ systems

> We propose to use a basis function constructed based on the microscopic cluster model for an efficient description of multi-cluster systems with the orthogonality condition originating from the Pauli principle. The basis function is expressed analytically by a superposition of correlated Gaussian functions. We demonstrate the power of this approach by taking an example of a $3α$ system, $^{12}$C. A comparison with the conventional pseudopotential method using the projection operator is made. The present method offers efficient and numerically stable computations as the number of basis functions is significantly reduced compared to the conventional method. We show that the present basis function includes reasonably small components of the Pauli-forbidden states, allowing us to discuss simply the structure of the first excited $0^+$ state, Hoyle state.

# Novel approach to the removal of the Pauli-forbidden states in the orthogonality condition model: A case of multi-α systems

## Key claim

The transformed correlated Gaussian (TCG) basis function, derived from the microscopic cluster model, contains components of the Pauli-forbidden states that are reasonably small (on the order of 10^{-5} to 10^{-3}) for multi-α systems, allowing the orthogonality condition model to be solved without the pseudopotential method and its associated numerical instability. The method achieves energy convergence with approximately 100 basis functions for the 3α system, compared to about 1000 basis functions required by the conventional pseudopotential method.

## Method

The authors construct a basis function for the orthogonality condition model by analytically transforming a correlated Gaussian function into the spectroscopic amplitude of a microscopic multi-cluster wave function, following the formalism of Matsumura (2004). For the 3α system (¹²C), they solve the Schrödinger equation using the stochastic variational method with the TCG basis, comparing results against the conventional symmetrized correlated Gaussian basis with a pseudopotential (projection operator) of strength λ = 10³ MeV. The Hamiltonian includes 2α and Coulomb interactions from Fukatsu (1992) and a 3α interaction from Ohtsubo (2013), with oscillator parameter ν = 0.26 fm⁻².

## Key numbers

- Energy of the ground state (0₁⁺) of ¹²C measured from the 3α threshold: −7.259 MeV with 120 TCG basis functions, compared to −7.269 MeV with 1000 SCG basis functions
- Component of the Pauli-forbidden states for the TCG ground state: 0.0025 (dimensionless overlap expectation value)
- Component of the Pauli-forbidden states for the SCG ground state: approximately 10⁻⁷ (dimensionless overlap expectation value)
- For the 2α system, the TCG basis function has PFS component of 0.031 for b₁ = 2.0 fm and 0.006 for b₁ = 4.0 fm, while the SCG basis has 0.76 and 0.21 respectively
- Maximum overlap of a single TCG basis with the Hoyle state (0₂⁺): 0.90 at (b₁, b₂) = (2.30, 7.69) fm (isosceles triangle configuration)
- Overlap of the equilateral triangle TCG basis with the Hoyle state: 0.83 at (b₁, b₂) = (5.32, 4.61) fm
- Spectroscopic factor of the Hoyle state in the microscopic 3α wave function: 0.918 (from Matsumura, 2004)
- Number of terms in the TCG superposition: 5 for 2α, 120 for 3α, 10147 for 4α, and 2224955 for 5α systems

## Context

This work extends the orthogonality condition model approach of Saito (1968, 1969, 1977) for multi-cluster systems, addressing the numerical instability of the pseudopotential method introduced by Kukulin (1978). The TCG basis builds on the correlated Gaussian expansion method of Varga (1995) and the analytical transformation developed by Matsumura (2004). The method enables more efficient computation than the pseudopotential approach, which required a huge number of basis functions for the 4α system (Funaki, 2008; Orabi, 2011) and has not been feasible for the 5α system. The authors demonstrate that the geometrical shape of the Hoyle state is determined primarily by the Pauli principle rather than the intercluster interaction, consistent with the interpretation of Tohsaki (2001) and Funaki (2003). Future applications include 4α and 5α systems, as well as heavier cluster systems such as ¹⁶O+α+n (²¹Ne) and ¹⁶O+α+α (²⁴Mg).
