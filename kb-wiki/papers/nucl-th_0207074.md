# The freeze-out mechanism and phase-space density in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We explore the consequences of a freeze-out criterion for heavy-ion collisions, based on pion escape probabilities from the hot and dense but rapidly expanding collision region. The influence of the expansion and the scattering rate on the escape probability is studied. The temperature dependence of this scattering rate favors a low freeze-out temperature of ~100 MeV. In general, our results support freeze-out along finite four-volumes rather than sharp three-dimensional hypersurfaces, with high-pt particles decoupling earlier from smaller volumes. We compare our approach to the proposed universal freeze-out criteria using the pion phase-space density and its mean free path.

# The freeze-out mechanism and phase-space density in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The freeze-out of pions from the hot and dense fireball in heavy-ion collisions occurs gradually over a finite four-volume rather than on a sharp three-dimensional hypersurface, with the temperature dependence of the scattering rate favoring a low freeze-out temperature of approximately 100 MeV; however, this conclusion depends on the specific choice of escape probability threshold and the assumed transverse flow gradient, and the authors note that a full dynamical simulation is needed to go beyond their simplified model calculations.

## Method

The authors calculate pion escape probabilities using opacity integrals that integrate the scattering rate along the particle trajectory, where the scattering rate is computed from a thermal model with Breit-Wigner resonance cross-sections for pion interactions with nucleons, antinucleons, pions, kaons, rhos, and deltas. Chemical potentials for SPS at sqrt(s) = 17 A GeV and RHIC at sqrt(s) = 130 A GeV are extracted from experimental data on particle yields and phase-space densities at three different temperatures (90, 100, and 120 MeV). The escape probability is then related to the competition between the scattering rate and the expansion rate, using two analytic models for the fireball expansion: a power-law density fall-off and a constant transverse rapidity gradient model.

## Key numbers

- The freeze-out temperature favored by the temperature dependence of the scattering rate is approximately 100 MeV.
- For a realistic transverse flow gradient of xi = 0.08 fm^{-1} and Bjorken freeze-out time tau_0 = 10 fm/c, achieving an escape probability of at least 1/3 requires the opacity integral to be smaller than -ln(1/3) ≈ 1.1, corresponding to a scattering rate less than 0.18 (fm/c)^{-1} for the power-law model or 0.26 (fm/c)^{-1} for the constant gradient model.
- At a temperature of 120 MeV, an escape probability of 1/3 is possibly reached at RHIC for pion momentum p_pi > 0.4 GeV/c and at SPS for p_pi > 0.3 GeV/c.
- For an escape probability of 1/2, the scattering rate must be smaller than 0.11 or 0.16 (fm/c)^{-1} depending on the expansion model, requiring temperature to drop to 100 MeV for particles with p_pi < 0.4 GeV/c.
- Approximately 10% of pions are decoupled at T = 120 MeV, but approximately half of them escape at local temperatures below 100 MeV.
- The momentum-averaged scattering rate from scattering on nucleons and antinucleons at T = 100 MeV is approximately 0.2 (fm/c)^{-1}.

## Context

This work extends the earlier suggestion by Garpman et al. (1975) that freeze-out occurs when the dilution rate becomes larger than the collision rate, and provides a quantitative framework for the gradual freeze-out picture of Grassi et al. (1996) and Sinyukov et al. (2002). The authors compare their approach to the universal freeze-out criterion based on pion mean free path proposed by Ceres (2002) and the suggestion by Ferenc et al. (1999) that pion phase-space density at freeze-out should be a universal quantity, finding that the latter fails because it neglects the contribution of other particle species to the pion scattering rate. The results enable future work using full hydrodynamic simulations to account for finite-momentum effects and to evaluate the general freeze-out criterion based on the condition that the four-divergence of the escaped particle current is positive.
