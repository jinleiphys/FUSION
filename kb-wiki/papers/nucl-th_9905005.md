# Quark-Gluon Plasma

> An elementary introduction to the physics of quark-gluon plasma is given. We start with a sketchy presentation of the Quantum Chromodynamics which is the fundamental theory of strong interactions. The structure of hadrons built up of quarks and gluons is briefly discussed with a special emphasis on the confinement hypothesis. Then, we explain what is the quark-gluon plasma and consider why and when the hadrons can dissolve liberating the quarks and gluons. The heavy-ion collisions at high-energies, which provide a unique opportunity to get a droplet of the quark-gluon plasma in the terrestrial conditions, are described. We also consider the most promising experimental signatures of the quark-gluon plasma produced in nucleus-nucleus collisions. At the end, the perspectives of the quark-gluon plasma studies at the future accelerators are mentioned.

## Key claim

This paper provides an elementary introduction to quark-gluon plasma physics, arguing that while the existence of the deconfinement phase transition is supported by lattice QCD and simple thermodynamic models, the question of whether quark-gluon plasma has already been produced at currently operating heavy-ion accelerators "is right now vigorously debated."

## Method

The paper reviews Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) as the fundamental theory of strong interactions, describes the structure of hadrons in terms of quarks and gluons, and explains the confinement hypothesis through the string model and bag model. It then presents a thermodynamic analysis of the deconfinement phase transition using ideal gas models of pions and of quarks and gluons, applying the Gibbs criterion to compute the critical temperature. Finally, it discusses heavy-ion collision scenarios and two proposed experimental signatures of quark-gluon plasma: strangeness enhancement and J/ψ suppression.

## Key numbers

- The QCD scale parameter Λ_QCD ≈ 200 MeV.
- The normal nuclear density ρ_0 = 0.16 fm⁻³; the density where inter-nucleon separation is smaller than the nucleon radius (1 fm) is ρ ≈ 1 fm⁻³ ≈ 6 ρ_0.
- The inter-pion separation becomes smaller than 1 fm for temperature T > 219 MeV, based on the pion density formula ρ_π ≈ 0.73 T³.
- The critical temperature for deconfinement is T_c ≈ 0.72 B^{1/4}; taking B^{1/4} = 200 MeV gives T_c = 144 MeV.
- The pressure of the pion gas is p_π ≈ 0.33 T⁴, while that of the quark-gluon plasma is p_qg ≈ 4.1 T⁴.
- Lattice QCD results indicate the deconfinement phase transition occurs at T ≈ 180 MeV.
- RHIC will provide collisions at 200 GeV per nucleon in the center-of-mass frame; LHC will provide 6 000 GeV per nucleon, compared to 20 GeV currently available in beam-target systems.

## Context

This paper extends the pedagogical tradition of review collections such as Hwa (1990) and Hwa (1995) and the regular "Quark Matter" conference proceedings (1993, 1995, 1996). It provides a conceptual foundation for understanding the deconfinement phase transition and heavy-ion collision experiments, enabling readers to engage with the ongoing debate about whether quark-gluon plasma has been observed at CERN's SPS energies (around 200 GeV per nucleon) and to anticipate the definitive tests expected at RHIC and LHC.
