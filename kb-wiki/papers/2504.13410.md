# Refinement of an analytical capture cross section formula

> An analytical formula with high accuracy is proposed for a systematic description of the capture cross sections at near-barrier energies from light to superheavy reaction systems. Based on the empirical barrier distribution (EBD) method, three key input quantities are refined by introducing nuclear surface correction to the Coulomb parameter $z$ for calculating the barrier height, incorporating the reaction $Q$-value and shell correction into the barrier distribution width calculations, and considering the deep inelastic scattering effects of superheavy systems on the barrier radius. With these refinements, not only the accuracy of the calculated barrier height but also the accuracy of the predicted capture cross sections, is substantially improved. The average deviation (in logarithmic scale) between the predicted cross sections and the experimental data for a total of 426 reaction systems with $ 35 < Z_1 Z_2 < 2600$ is sharply reduced from 3.485 to 0.113.

# Refinement of an analytical capture cross section formula

## Key claim

The proposed EBD2 formula reduces the average logarithmic deviation between predicted and experimental capture cross sections from 3.485 to 0.113 across 426 reaction systems, but only when three specific refinements are simultaneously applied: nuclear surface correction to the Coulomb parameter, inclusion of reaction Q-value and shell correction in the barrier distribution width, and deep inelastic scattering effects on the barrier radius for superheavy systems.

## Method

The author modifies the empirical barrier distribution (EBD) method by reparameterizing three input quantities: the barrier height V_B, barrier radius R_B, and barrier distribution width W. The barrier height is expressed as a quadratic function of a surface-corrected Coulomb parameter z = (Z1 Z2)/(A1^{1/3}+A2^{1/3}) * F_S, where F_S = 1 - Z1^{-1/3} Z2^{-1/3}, plus a shell correction term ΔB. The barrier radius includes a deep inelastic scattering factor F_DIS = 1 + exp(X_B - X_0) for superheavy systems. The width W incorporates a Q-value-dependent term w_0 = (V_B + Q)/c_2 to account for dynamical deformation effects. The capture cross section is then calculated using the standard EBD analytical formula with these refined parameters.

## Key numbers

- Average logarithmic deviation for 426 reaction systems (35 < Z1 Z2 < 2600): reduced from 3.485 (EBD) to 0.113 (EBD2)
- Mean relative deviation of barrier heights for 382 extracted barriers: 1.65% with EBD2, compared to 2.04% with EBD and 1.95% with the MCW potential
- Barrier radius coefficient r_0 = 1.10 fm in Eq.(4)
- Dimensionless parameter X_0 = 10.2 for the deep inelastic scattering factor F_DIS
- For 16O+16O: EBD predicted V_B = 11.02 MeV and R_B = 5.85 fm, while EBD2 gives V_B = 10.04 MeV and R_B = 7.39 fm; extracted R_B = 8.52 ± 0.24 fm from fusion data and 8.63 ± 0.32 fm from elastic scattering
- For 132Sn+40Ca: W = 3.83 MeV; for 132Sn+48Ca: W = 2.33 MeV
- For 132Sn+48Ca: barrier height increased by ΔB = 1.28 MeV due to shell-isospin competition
- Q-values: -52.13 MeV for 132Sn+40Ca, -75.78 MeV for 132Sn+48Ca
- Parameters: c_0 = 0.63 MeV, c_1 = 0.015 fm^{-1}, c_2 = 33.0 MeV·fm^{-1}, r_0 = 1.10 fm

## Context

This work extends the empirical barrier distribution method originally developed by Siwek-Wilczynska and Wilczynski (2004) and implemented in the KEWPIE2 code, which had demonstrated reasonable success for heavy fusion systems but showed diminished accuracy for light nuclei. The refinements address limitations identified in prior studies: Wen et al. (2022) found barrier heights approximately linear in the Coulomb parameter; Yao et al. (2024) observed Q-value effects on barrier distribution width; and Wang et al. (2025) and Kozulin et al. (2016) noted deep inelastic scattering influences on barrier radius for superheavy systems. The improved formula enables more reliable predictions of capture cross sections for fusion reactions from light to superheavy systems, which can subsequently reduce uncertainties in calculating evaporation residual cross sections for superheavy nuclei synthesis.
