# Surface Emission from Neutron Stars and Implications for the Physics of their Interiors

> Neutron stars are associated with diverse physical phenomena that take place in conditions characterized by ultrahigh densities as well as intense gravitational, magnetic, and radiation fields. Understanding the properties and interactions of matter in these regimes remains one of the challenges in compact object astrophysics. Photons emitted from the surfaces of neutron stars provide direct probes of their structure, composition, and magnetic fields. In this review, I discuss in detail the physics that governs the properties of emission from the surfaces of neutron stars and their various observational manifestations. I present the constraints on neutron star radii, core and crust composition, and magnetic field strength and topology obtained from studies of their broadband spectra, evolution of thermal luminosity, and the profiles of pulsations that originate on their surfaces.

## Key claim

This review argues that photons emitted from neutron star surfaces provide the most powerful direct probe of their interior composition, radii, and magnetic fields, but the constraints obtained (e.g., radii of 9–12 km) depend critically on detailed atmospheric models and the specific evolutionary state of each source class (isolated cooling stars, accreting quiescent sources, thermonuclear bursters, and magnetars).

## Method

The paper reviews the physics of neutron star surface emission by first describing the energy sources (residual heat, accretion, nuclear reactions, magnetic field decay, rotational energy) and then detailing the atmospheric models that shape the emergent spectrum and anisotropy. It compares these theoretical models to observations across multiple source classes, isolated cooling neutron stars, quiescent accreting systems, thermonuclear bursters, and magnetars, to extract constraints on radii, core and crust composition, and magnetic field strength and topology.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star radii constrained to 9 km < R < 12 km from quiescent and thermonuclear burst observations, disfavoring equations of state predicting radii as large as 15 km (Özel, Baym, & Güver 2010; Steiner, Lattimer, & Brown 2010).
- Core temperature of a proto-neutron star immediately after supernova is ~10^11 K.
- The boundary between the stellar interior and the heat blanketing envelope is at a density of ~10^10 g cm^-3.
- The envelope depth is approximately 100 m.
- For magnetic fields B > 10^11–10^12 G, the effective temperature is slightly enhanced for a given boundary temperature.

## Context

This review synthesizes and extends the work of Gudmundsson et al. (1982, 1983) on the T_b–T_eff relation in neutron star envelopes, Yakovlev & Pethick (2004) on neutron star cooling, and Page et al. (2011) and Shternin et al. (2011) on evidence for superfluidity in the Cas A supernova remnant. It enables future comparisons with higher signal-to-noise X-ray data from new satellites and with gravitational wave and neutrino observations to further constrain neutron star structure.
