# Electron screening in molecular fusion reactions

> Recent laboratory experiments have measured fusion cross sections at center-of-mass energies low enough for the effects of atomic and molecular electrons to be important. To extract the cross section for bare nuclei from these data (as required for astrophysical applications), it is necessary to understand these screening effects. We study electron screening effects in the low-energy collisions of Z=1 nuclei with hydrogen molecules. Our model is based on a dynamical evolution of the electron wavefunctions within the TDHF scheme, while the motion of the nuclei is treated classically. We find that at the currently accessible energies the screening effects depend strongly on the molecular orientation. The screening is found to be larger for molecular targets than for atomic targets, due to the reflection symmetry in the latter. The results agree fairly well with data measured for deuteron collisions on molecular deuterium and tritium targets.

# Electron screening in molecular fusion reactions

## Key claim

The screening energy in low-energy fusion reactions depends strongly on molecular orientation, and for Z=1 nuclei colliding with hydrogen molecules, the screening is larger for molecular targets than for atomic targets, contrary to the general expectation that molecular screening should be smaller, due to the reflection symmetry present in the atomic d+D system but absent in the molecular case.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations of the electron wavefunctions during head-on collisions of a Z=1 projectile nucleus with a hydrogen molecule, while treating the nuclear motion classically via Newton's laws. The molecular screening potential was obtained by integrating the relative force between projectile and target nuclei along the collision trajectory, with the screening energy defined as the negative of this potential at the classical turning point. Collisions were studied for various orientation angles θ between the projectile velocity and the molecular axis, and the results were angle-averaged using 5-point Gaussian quadrature to compare with experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The Hartree-Fock ground state of the hydrogen molecule was found at nuclear separation R = 0.78 Å with energy -30.8 eV, in good agreement with experiment.
- Projectile velocities studied ranged from 0.2 αc to 10 αc, corresponding to center-of-mass energies E = 1 keV to 2.5 MeV for the d+D₂ system.
- The uniform Cartesian grid used a separation of 0.26 Å with (20,20,40) points in (x,y,z) directions.
- At extreme back angles (θ ≥ 160°) and energies near 25-30 keV, the screening energy becomes negative, producing "anti-screening" (depletion of fusion cross section).
- For E ≤ 3 keV, the screening energy becomes nearly energy-independent but still depends noticeably on molecular orientation.
- The angle-averaged screening energy Ūₑ changes from approximately 20 eV at E = 15 keV to 40 eV at E = 2 keV.
- The kinetic energy transferred to the spectator nucleus was calculated as ΔE_kin ≈ 0.1 eV for a collision at E = 25 keV and θ = 170°.
- The molecular rotation angle during collision is at most 0.005 radians (for E = 1 keV, E_rot = 0.025 eV).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous TDHF study of screening in atomic targets (Shoppa, 1995) and addresses the unexplained observation that experimentally determined screening energies sometimes exceed adiabatic limits, as noted by NMR (1994). The calculations test the dynamical picture proposed by Prati (1994) that molecular screening is reduced by bond-breaking energy and spectator recoil, but find that the spectator gains negligible kinetic energy (≈0.1 eV) before fusion occurs, contradicting that assumption. The results enable comparison with experimental data from Greife (1995) on d(d,p) and d(d,n) reactions down to 1.6 keV, and with Brown (1990) on t(d,n)α reactions, showing that the calculated energy-dependent screening energies reproduce the observed cross section enhancements. The authors plan future studies of helium collisions on hydrogenic targets to test whether the larger molecular screening found here is specific to systems with reflection symmetry in the atomic case.
