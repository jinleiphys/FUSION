# On the EFT of Dyon-Monopole Catalysis

> Monopole-fermion (and dyon-fermion) interactions provide a famous example where scattering from a compact object gives a cross section much larger than the object's geometrical size. This underlies the phenomenon of monopole catalysis of baryon-number violation because the reaction rate is much larger in the presence of a monopole than in its absence. It is sometimes claimed to violate the otherwise generic requirement that short distance physics decouples from long-distance observables -- a property that underpins the general utility of effective field theory (EFT) methods. Decoupling in this context is most simply expressed using point-particle effective field theories (PPEFTs) designed to capture systematically how small but massive objects influence their surroundings when probed only on length scales large compared to their size. These have been tested in precision calculations of how nuclear properties affect atomic energy levels for both ordinary and pionic atoms. We adapt the PPEFT formalism to describe low-energy $S$-wave dyon-fermion scattering with a view to understanding whether large catalysis cross sections violate decoupling (and show why they do not). We also explore the related but separate issue of the long-distance complications associated with polarizing the fermion vacuum exterior to a dyon and show in some circumstances how PPEFT methods can simplify calculations of low-energy fermion-dyon scattering in their presence. We propose an effective Hamiltonian governing how dyon excitations respond to fermion scattering in terms of a time-dependent vacuum angle and outline open questions remaining in its microscopic derivation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the large cross sections in dyon-fermion scattering do not violate the decoupling principle of effective field theory, because the scale-invariant S-wave cross section arises from a kinematic peculiarity (only one independent radial mode exists for S-wave fermions near a dyon, unlike the two modes for spinless particles) rather than from a failure of short-distance physics to decouple.

## Method

The authors adapt the point-particle effective field theory (PPEFT) formalism to low-energy S-wave dyon-fermion scattering, treating the dyon as a first-quantized compact object and relating its effective world-line action to near-source boundary conditions on the relativistic fermion fields. They analyze the renormalization group evolution of these boundary conditions as the radius of a Gaussian pillbox around the dyon is varied, and incorporate the polarization of the fermion vacuum exterior to the dyon through long-wavelength effective couplings. The dyon's internal excitations are treated within a Born-Oppenheimer approximation using a collective coordinate for the unbroken U(1) gauge rotation.

## Key numbers

* The S-wave dyon-fermion scattering cross section is scale invariant and given by sigma_s ~ 2*pi/k^2, where k is the relativistic fermion energy (no explicit numerical prefactor is given beyond 2*pi).
* The dyon carries magnetic charge q_M = 1/e and electric charge q_E = Q, with the Dirac quantization condition requiring 2*q_M*q to be an integer for any electric charge q (the minimal fermion charge is q_psi = +/- e/2).
* The dyon mass M ~ (4*pi*beta*mu/e^2)*F(beta, v/mu) ~ O(m_g/alpha), where m_g ~ beta*mu is the gauge boson mass and alpha = e^2/(4*pi) is the fine-structure constant.
* The dyon rotor kinetic term gives moment of inertia I ~ 1/(alpha*m_g), with energy level spacing delta_E_n = (2n+1)/I ~ (e^2*m_g)/(4*pi) << m_g.
* In the Prasad-Sommerfield limit, the dyon functions are given explicitly: K(r) = c*r/sinh(c*r), J(r) = sinh(varpi)*[1 - c*r*coth(c*r)], H(r) = cosh(varpi)*[c*r*coth(c*r) - 1], with c = beta*mu.

## Context

This work extends the PPEFT formalism previously tested on nuclear effects in atomic energy levels (Burgess 2020, Zalavari 2020) and gravitational back-reaction (Burgess 2008, Bayntun 2009) to the monopole catalysis problem originally studied by Rubakov (1981) and Callan (1982). It addresses the claim that monopole catalysis violates decoupling by showing that the large cross sections are consistent with EFT reasoning, and it proposes an effective Hamiltonian for dyon excitations in terms of a time-dependent vacuum angle, while noting that a full microscopic derivation of this Hamiltonian remains an open question.
