# Recollision induced nuclear excitation of $^{229}$Th

> Previously we proposed a new approach of exciting the $^{229}$Th nucleus using laser-driven electron recollision [W. Wang et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 127, 052501 (2021)]. The current article is aimed to elaborate the method by explaining further theoretical details and presenting extended new results. The method has also been improved by adopting the electronic excitation cross sections calculated recently by Tkalya [E. V. Tkalya, Phys. Rev. Lett. 124, 242501 (2020)]. The new cross sections are obtained from Dirac distorted-wave calculations instead of from Dirac plane-wave calculations as we used previously. The distorted-wave cross sections are shown to be 5 to 6 orders of magnitude higher than the plane-wave results. With the excitation cross sections updated, the probability of isomeric excitation of $^{229}$Th from electron recollision is calculated to be on the order of $10^{-12}$ per nucleus per (femtosecond) laser pulse. Dependency of the excitation probability on various laser parameters is calculated and discussed, including the laser intensity, the laser wavelength, and the laser pulse duration.

## Key claim

The probability of isomeric excitation of $^{229}$Th via laser-driven electron recollision is calculated to be on the order of $10^{-12}$ per nucleus per femtosecond laser pulse, but this result depends on adopting the recently calculated Dirac distorted-wave electronic excitation cross sections, which are 5 to 6 orders of magnitude higher than the plane-wave cross sections used in the authors' prior work (Wang et al., 2021).

## Method

The authors model the recollision induced nuclear excitation (RINE) process by combining classical trajectory simulations of laser-driven electron dynamics with nuclear excitation cross sections. Tunneling ionization rates are calculated using the Ammosov-Delone-Krainov (ADK) formula, and electron trajectories are propagated classically under the combined influence of the laser electric field and the Green-Sellin-Zachor (GSZ) effective ion-core potential. The nuclear excitation probability is obtained by integrating the product of the recolliding electron flux density and the electronic excitation cross section over time, using Dirac distorted-wave cross sections from Tkalya (2020) rather than the plane-wave Born approximation used previously.

## Key numbers

* The distorted-wave excitation cross sections are 5 to 6 orders of magnitude higher than the plane-wave values; the plane-wave cross sections are on the order of $10^{-8}$ barn (or $10^{-32}$ cm$^2$), while the distorted-wave cross sections are on the order of $10^{-3}$ to $10^{-2}$ barn.
* The probability of isomeric excitation of $^{229}$Th from electron recollision is on the order of $10^{-12}$ per nucleus per femtosecond laser pulse.
* There is a threshold laser intensity around $3\times 10^{13}$ W/cm$^2$ below which nuclear excitation cannot occur via recollision, for a pulse duration of 30 fs and wavelength of 800 nm.
* The maximum recollision energy in the Simpleman model (ignoring the ion-core potential) is $3.17 U_p$, where $U_p = E_0^2/4\omega^2$ is the ponderomotive potential.
* The critical recollision radius $R_c$ for nuclear excitation is between 3.0 and 3.5 a.u. for 10 eV electrons, about 2.0 a.u. for 50 eV electrons, and about 1.5 a.u. for 100 eV electrons.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously proposed RINE approach (Wang et al., 2021) by incorporating the more accurate Dirac distorted-wave electronic excitation cross sections calculated by Tkalya (2020), which dramatically increase the predicted excitation probability compared to the plane-wave results used earlier. The method combines strong-field atomic physics (tunneling ionization and electron recollision, established by Kulander (1993), Schafer (1993), and Corkum (1993)) with nuclear Coulomb excitation theory. The approach offers a potentially controllable and efficient alternative to existing excitation methods such as direct vacuum-ultraviolet light excitation (attempted by Jeet (2015), Yamaguchi (2015), and Stellmer (2018) without positive results) and indirect synchrotron radiation excitation (Masuda, 2019), which requires narrowband high-photon-energy light sources not easily accessible.
