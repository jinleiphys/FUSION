# The Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo Method for Nuclear Physics and Nuclear Astrophysics

> In this thesis, I discuss the use of the Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo method to compute the ground state of nuclear Hamiltonians, and I show several applications to interesting problems both in nuclear physics and in nuclear astrophysics. In particular, the AFDMC algorithm is applied to the study of several nuclear systems, finite, and infinite matter. Results about the ground state of nuclei ($^4$He, $^8$He, $^{16}$O and $^{40}$Ca), neutron drops (with 8 and 20 neutrons) and neutron rich-nuclei (isotopes of oxygen and calcium) are discussed, and the equation of state of nuclear and neutron matter are calculated and compared with other many-body calculations. The $^1S_0$ superfluid phase of neutron matter in the low-density regime was also studied.

## Key claim

The Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) method can accurately compute ground-state properties of nuclear systems, including nuclei up to ⁴⁰Ca, neutron drops, neutron-rich isotopes, and infinite nuclear and neutron matter, using realistic Hamiltonians, but the accuracy of the three-nucleon force (Illinois TNI) for pure neutron matter remains questionable because those forces were fitted without input from nuclear matter calculations.

## Method

The AFDMC method solves the many-body Schrödinger equation by projecting out the ground state from a trial wave function via imaginary-time evolution, using a stochastic procedure that handles spin and isospin operators through auxiliary fields. The algorithm propagates walkers according to a diffusion Green's function combined with a weight term that incorporates the potential energy, using importance sampling to reduce fluctuations. The method is applied to finite nuclei, neutron drops in periodic boxes, and infinite matter using Argonne AV18 two-nucleon interactions supplemented with Urbana IX or Illinois three-nucleon forces.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Neutron star central density: 7 to 10 times nuclear saturation density (ρ₀)
- Neutron star typical mass: ~1.5 solar masses (M⊙) with radius ~12 km
- Pion-nucleon coupling constant: f²_πNN/4π = 0.075 ± 0.002
- Three-nucleon force contributes ~15-20% of total potential energy in heavier nuclei
- AFDMC applied to nuclei: ⁴He, ⁸He, ¹⁶O, and ⁴⁰Ca
- Neutron drops studied: 8 and 20 neutrons
- Crust-core interface density: ρ ≈ ρ₀/3
- Proton fraction in neutron star crust: x ~ 0.1 to 0.2

## Context

This thesis extends Quantum Monte Carlo methods, previously limited to nuclei up to A=12 with Green's Function Monte Carlo (Pieper, 2005) and to neutron matter with 14 neutrons in a periodic box (Carlson, 2003), to heavier systems including ⁴⁰Ca and infinite matter. The AFDMC method, originally invented by Schmidt and Fantoni (1999), enables calculations that were previously inaccessible to exact methods like Faddeev-Yakubovsky (limited to A=4) and no-core shell model (extended to A=40 by Roth, 2007 but with uncontrolled approximations). The results enable improved neutron star structure calculations via the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations and allow study of the ¹S₀ superfluid phase in neutron matter relevant to neutron star cooling and glitch phenomena.
