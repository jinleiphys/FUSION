# Quarkyonic Mean Field Theory

> We discuss mean field theory of Quarkyonic matter at zero temperature. We treat the nucleons with contact interactions in mean field approximation, discussing both vector and scalar mean field interactions. We treat the quarks without mean field vector interactions, but allow mass terms to be generated consistent from a scalar mean field consistent with the additive quark model for quark masses. Quarkyonic matter is composed of a shell of nucleons that under-occupy the total available phase space associated with the underlying quark degrees of freedom. The fully occupied Fermi sphere beneath this shell of nucleons at high densities is thought of as quarks, but when this fully occupied distribution of states first appears, although the phase space is filled, the matter is at low density. For the transition between this low density and high density saturated matter, we advocate a dual description of the fully filled Fermi sea in terms of hadrons, and make a phenomenological hypothesis for the equation of state of this matter. We then proceed to an example where the mean field interactions are all vector and only associated with the nucleons, ignoring the effects of mass change associated with the scalar interactions. Except for the effects of Pauli blocking, the nucleons and quarks do not interact. To get a reasonable transition to Quarkyonic matter the interaction of the quarks among themselves are assumed to be non-perturbative, and a simple phenomenological relation between quark Fermi energy and density is introduced.

# Quarkyonic Mean Field Theory

## Key claim

The authors propose a mean-field description of Quarkyonic matter where nucleons and quarks coexist without double-counting phase space, but they explicitly state that their goal is not to provide a phenomenologically viable model, only to explore how Quarkyonic matter might appear in a field theory with both degrees of freedom. The transition between nuclear matter and Quarkyonic matter can be continuous rather than first-order, provided that quark interactions are modeled phenomenologically with a linear density dependence at low densities.

## Method

The authors construct a mean-field theory at zero temperature with nucleons interacting via contact vector and scalar interactions, while quarks are treated without vector mean fields but with scalar mass terms consistent with the additive quark model. Ghost fields are introduced to prevent double-counting of states where quarks inside nucleons would occupy the same phase space as free quarks. The quark chemical potential is related to density through a phenomenological low-density expansion that transitions to a free-gas form at high densities, and the energy density is minimized with respect to quark density at fixed total baryon number.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density $\rho_0$ is used as the reference scale throughout the calculations.
- For the free quark gas with $\Delta M = 70$ MeV, the nucleon mass is taken as $M_N = 939$ MeV, the vector meson mass as $M_v = 783$ MeV, and the vector coupling as $g_v = 8$.
- For the interacting quark model, the parameters are $a = 1.2$, $\Delta M = 70$ MeV, and $g_v = 6$.
- The Quarkyonic phase appears around $n_B \sim 2.2\rho_0$ in the interacting quark model.
- The continuous transition occurs between $2\rho_0$ and $3\rho_0$, where a global minimum appears in the energy density as a function of quark density.

## Context

This work extends the Quarkyonic matter concept originally proposed by McLerran (2007) and builds on the field-theoretic formulation with nucleon, ghost, and quark degrees of freedom developed by Duarte (2021). It follows the momentum-space picture elaborated by Kojo (2021) and is related to earlier studies by McLerran (2018), Jeong (2019), Sen (2020), Cao (2020), and Kovensky (2020). The authors contrast their Quarkyonic configuration with the alternative "Baryquark matter" scenario proposed by Koch (2022), where the Fermi sea is filled with confined baryons surrounded by deconfined quarks. The work enables future transport modeling and applications to neutron star equations of state, where the rapid rise of sound velocity extracted from observations by Drischler (2021), Tews (2018), Steiner (2017), and Bedaque (2014) motivates a continuous transition at relatively low densities around $n_B \simeq 2.0\rho_0$.
