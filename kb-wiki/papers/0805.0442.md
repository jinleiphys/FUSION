# Instability of Boost-invariant hydrodynamics with a QCD inspired bulk viscosity

> We solve the relativistic Navier-Stokes equations with homogeneous boost-invariant boundary conditions, and perform a stability analysis of the solution. We show that, if the bulk viscosity has a peak around $T_c$ as inferred from QCD-based arguments, the background solution "freezes" at $T_c$ to a nearly constant temperature state. This state is however highly unstable with respect to certain inhomogeneous modes. Calculations show that these modes have enough time to blow up and tear the system into droplets. We conjecture that this is how freeze-out occurs in the QGP created in heavy ion collisions, and perhaps similar transitions in the early universe.

## Key claim
If the bulk viscosity has a sharp peak around the QCD critical temperature \(T_c\) as inferred from QCD-based arguments, the boost-invariant background solution "freezes" at \(T_c\) to a nearly constant temperature state, but this state is highly unstable with respect to certain inhomogeneous modes that have enough time to blow up and tear the system into droplets.

## Method
The authors solved the relativistic Navier-Stokes equations with homogeneous boost-invariant boundary conditions, using a parametrization of the speed of sound from Chojnacki (2007) and the "sCFT limit" for shear viscosity (\(\eta = s/4\pi\)). They modeled the bulk viscosity \(\zeta\) as a Gaussian peak centered at \(T_c\) with width \(\sigma = 0.01 T_c\) plus a small perturbative QCD baseline, then performed a linear stability analysis of the background solution against Fourier modes in rapidity space with wavenumber \(k\).

## Key numbers
- Bulk viscosity parametrization: \(\zeta = s \left( z_{pQCD} + \frac{z_0}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma} \exp\left[-\frac{(T-T_c)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right] \right)\) with \(\sigma = 0.01 T_c\) and \(z_{pQCD} \sim 10^{-3}\); calculations used \(z_0 = 0, 0.1, 1\; T_c\).
- Initial conditions: temperature \(T = 0.3\) GeV and comoving time \(\tau = 0.6\) fm.
- Perturbation growth: for \(z_0 = 1\; T_c\), unstable modes grow as power laws \(\vec{x}(\tau) \sim (\tau/\tau_{ini})^{\lambda_{max}}\) with \(\lambda_{max}\) becoming large near \(T_c\); perturbations seeded at scale \(X \sim 10^{-1}\) at rate \(\sim \text{fm}^{-4}\) reach \(X \ge 1\) a few fm after \(T \sim T_c\).
- Eigenvalues \(\lambda_{min}\) and \(\lambda_{max}\) were computed for wavenumbers \(k = 2\) and \(k = 8\); the direction of the unstable eigenvector remains approximately constant while the system traverses the viscosity peak.

## Context
This work extends the stability analysis of boost-invariant hydrodynamics by combining the conjectured peak in bulk viscosity near \(T_c\) (inferred from arguments by Kharzeev et al. (2007) and others) with an earlier stability study by Bozek (2007). It aims to explain the freeze-out process in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, where standard hydrodynamics fails to reproduce particle interferometry data unless decoupling occurs at unrealistically high temperatures near \(T_c\). The scenario differs from earlier approaches by Mishustin et al. (2005), Majumder (2007), Rafelski (2007), and Kapusta (2007) in that instabilities are generated purely through hydrodynamic evolution without requiring a first-order phase transition or discontinuities in the equation of state.
