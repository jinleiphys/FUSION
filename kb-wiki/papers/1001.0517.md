# Reduced neutron widths in the nuclear data ensemble: Experiment and theory do not agree

> I have analyzed reduced neutron widths (Γ_{n}^0) for the subset of 1245 resonances in the nuclear data ensemble (NDE) for which they have been reported. Random matrix theory (RMT) predicts for the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) that these widths should follow a \c{hi}^2 distribution having one degree of freedom (ν=1) - the Porter Thomas distribution (PTD). Careful analysis of the Γ_{n}^2 values in the NDE rejects the validity of the PTD with a statistical significance of at least 99.97% (ν=0.801\pm0.052). This striking disagreement with the RMT prediction is most likely due to the inclusion of significant p-wave contamination to the supposedly pure s-wave NDE. When an energy dependent threshold is used to remove the p-wave contamination, the PTD is still rejected with a statistical significance of at least 98.17% (ν=1.217\pm0.092). Furthermore, examination of the primary references for the NDE reveals that many resonances in most of the individual data sets were selected using methods derived from RMT. Therefore, using the full NDE data set to test RMT predictions seems highly questionable. These results cast very serious doubt on claims that the NDE represents a striking confirmation of RMT.

# Reduced neutron widths in the nuclear data ensemble: Experiment and theory do not agree

## Key claim

The reduced neutron widths in the nuclear data ensemble (NDE) reject the Porter-Thomas distribution (PTD) predicted by random matrix theory (RMT) for the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) with a statistical significance of at least 99.97%, but this disagreement is most likely due to p-wave contamination in the supposedly pure s-wave data set rather than a fundamental failure of RMT. Even after applying an energy-dependent threshold to remove p-wave contamination, the PTD is still rejected with a statistical significance of at least 98.17%, and the author argues that using the full NDE to test RMT is highly questionable because many resonances were selected using methods derived from RMT itself.

## Method

The author reconstructed the NDE from primary references, obtaining 1245 neutron resonances in 24 nuclides for which reduced neutron widths had been reported. Separate maximum-likelihood (ML) analyses were performed for each nuclide using energy-dependent thresholds of the form T = a * E_n, with results combined via weighted averaging and Fisher's and Stouffer's methods to estimate the chi-squared degrees-of-freedom parameter nu and test the PTD prediction of nu = 1.

## Key numbers

- The weighted average of ML results at minimum threshold for 24 NDE nuclides is nu = 0.801 +/- 0.052, which is 3.8 standard deviations from the PTD prediction of nu = 1, rejecting the PTD with a statistical significance of at least 99.97%
- After applying p-wave-free thresholds, the weighted average is nu = 1.217 +/- 0.092, rejecting the PTD with a statistical significance of at least 98.17%
- The NDE contains 1245 neutron resonances in 24 nuclides, with individual nuclides contributing between 14 and 178 resonances
- For 14 of 24 NDE nuclides, p-wave or unknown-parity resonances account for 5% or more of the total; for 10 of 24 they are at least 10%; in the three worst cases about 35%
- For 232Th, 36 of 178 resonances had to be selected as s-wave using GOE-derived measures from 98 possible resonances, yielding approximately 10^27 possible s-wave sets

## Context

This work extends earlier analyses by Bohigas (1983) and Haq (1982) who assembled the NDE and claimed it provided striking confirmation of RMT predictions for energy-level fluctuations. The author identifies specific problems with the original analysis of neutron widths in Bohigas (1983), including errors in the reported number of resonances and the use of RMT-based selection criteria in the primary references from the Columbia University group (Liou 1972, 1973, 1974, 1975; Rahn 1972, 1974). The results cast doubt on the NDE as a test of RMT and enable future tests using improved experimental techniques for determining resonance spins, as demonstrated in Koehler (2007, 2010) for individual nuclides.
