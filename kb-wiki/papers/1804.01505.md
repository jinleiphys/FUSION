# Linear Response on a Quantum Computer

> The dynamic linear response of a quantum system is critical for understanding both the structure and dynamics of strongly-interacting quantum systems, including neutron scattering from materials, photon and electron scattering from atomic systems, and electron and neutrino scattering by nuclei. We present a general algorithm for quantum computers to calculate the dynamic linear response function with controlled errors and to obtain information about specific final states that can be directly compared to experimental observations.

## Key claim

The paper presents a general quantum algorithm for computing the dynamic linear response function of a quantum system, but the method is restricted to the zero-temperature (T=0) ground state and requires access to three black-box quantum oracles (ground-state preparation, time evolution under the excitation operator, and time evolution under the system Hamiltonian) whose practical implementation for general Hamiltonians remains a significant challenge.

## Method

The algorithm first prepares the state proportional to the excitation operator acting on the ground state by applying a unitary operator \( \hat{U}^\gamma_S = e^{-i\gamma \hat{O}\otimes\sigma_y} \) to an ancilla qubit and the ground state, then post-selecting on the ancilla being in state \( \lvert0\rangle \). The response function is then estimated by applying the standard Phase Estimation Algorithm (PEA) to this prepared state using \( W \) ancilla qubits, where the Hamiltonian is shifted and scaled to map its spectrum to the interval [0,1]. The probability distribution of the measured phase register yields an approximation to the scaled dynamic response function, with the Fejer kernel providing a representation of the delta function with width determined by the number of ancilla qubits.

## Key numbers

- The success probability of the state preparation routine is \( P_{success} = \mathcal{O}\left(\delta_S \frac{\langle\hat{O}^2\rangle_0}{\|\hat{O}\|^2}\right) \), where \( \delta_S \) is the target precision.
- The number of ancilla qubits required is \( W = \log_2\left(\Delta H / \Delta\omega\right) \), where \( \Delta H = E_{max} - E_0 \) is the total energy range and \( \Delta\omega \) is the desired energy resolution.
- The maximum time evolution needed is \( t_{max} = 2\pi / \Delta\omega \).
- The number of repetitions required to achieve precision \( \delta_S \) with probability \( 1-\epsilon \) is \( N_{rep} \approx \ln\left(\frac{2}{\epsilon}\right) \frac{1}{2\delta_S^2} \), independent of the target resolution \( \Delta\omega \).
- The parameter \( \gamma \) for state preparation must scale as \( \gamma \lessapprox C \frac{\sqrt{\delta}}{\|\hat{O}\|} \) to obtain negligible bias.
- The classical simulation example used a 2D Hubbard model with \( A=2 \) "nucleons", \( M=31^2 \) lattice sites, and \( U/t = -2 \).

## Context

This work extends earlier quantum algorithms for computing time-dependent correlation functions (Terhal 2000, Ortiz 2001, Somma 2002) and chemical reaction rates (Lidar 1999, Kassal 2008) by providing a completely general approach that does not require simplifying assumptions on the excitation operator and requires only a polynomial number of measurements instead of exponential. The method improves on algorithms for evaluating energy spectra (Somma 2002, Wang 2012) that require resolution of individual excited states, which grows exponentially for large systems. The approach is particularly suited for lattice-based simulations of cold atoms (Bulgac 2006, Carlson 2011) and nuclear systems (Lee 2004, Lee 2009), and enables direct comparison with experimental scattering data including neutrino-nucleus scattering experiments.
