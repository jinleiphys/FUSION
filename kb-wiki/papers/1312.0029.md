# Astrophysical Constraints on Dense Matter in Neutron Stars

> Ever since the discovery of neutron stars it has been realized that they serve as probes of a physical regime that cannot be accessed in laboratories: strongly degenerate matter at several times nuclear saturation density. Existing nuclear theories diverge widely in their predictions about such matter. It could be that the matter is primarily nucleons, but it is also possible that exotic species such as hyperons, free quarks, condensates, or strange matter may dominate this regime. Astronomical observations of cold high-density matter are necessarily indirect, which means that we must rely on measurements of quantities such as the masses and radii of neutron stars and their surface effective temperatures as a function of age. Here we review the current status of constraints from various methods and the prospects for future improvements.

## Key claim

This review argues that astronomical observations of neutron star masses, radii, and cooling rates can constrain the properties of cold supranuclear matter at several times nuclear saturation density, but these constraints are necessarily indirect and subject to systematic errors, meaning that no single measurement can definitively rule out entire classes of dense matter models (such as nucleonic-only versus hyperonic or quark matter).

## Method

The paper reviews current methods for measuring neutron star properties: mass measurements from binary pulsar timing (using Keplerian and post-Keplerian parameters including pericenter precession, Einstein delay, orbital decay, and Shapiro delay), radius measurements from various astrophysical techniques, and cooling rate inferences from surface temperature observations. It also discusses how these observables map onto the equation of state of cold dense matter via the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation, and compares predictions from different classes of dense matter models including nucleonic, hyperonic, quark, and strange matter equations of state.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: ρ_s ≈ 2.6 × 10^14 g cm^-3
- Central densities of realistic neutron stars range from ~5 × 10^14 g cm^-3 to ~few × 10^15 g cm^-3
- Neutron drip density: ρ_nd ≈ 4 × 10^11 g cm^-3
- PSR J1614-2230 mass: M = 1.97 ± 0.04 M_⊙ (from Shapiro delay measurement)
- PSR J0348+0432 mass: M = 2.01 ± 0.04 M_⊙ (from white dwarf optical spectroscopy)
- PSR B1957+20 best-fit mass: M = 2.40 ± 0.12 M_⊙ (but could be as low as 1.66 M_⊙ at extreme inclination)
- PSR J1311-3430 mass: M = 2.7 M_⊙ for simple heated light curves, with no viable solutions below 2.1 M_⊙
- Neutron skin thickness of 208Pb: 0.33^+0.16_-0.18 fm (PREX team measurement)
- Maximum density in a neutron star: ρ_max ≈ 1.5 × 10^16 g cm^-3 (M_⊙/M_max)^2
- Canonical neutron star: M = 1.4 M_⊙, R = 10 km, average number density n = 4 × 10^38 cm^-3, Fermi energy E_F ≈ 100 MeV
- Relativistic heavy-ion collisions reach 2 to 4.5 times nuclear saturation density

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes work on neutron star equation of state constraints from multiple observational methods, building on prior reviews by Lattimer and Prakash (2012), Oertel (2013), and others. It discusses how mass measurements of ~2 M_⊙ neutron stars (Demorest et al. 2010; Antoniadis et al. 2013) have already ruled out some soft equations of state, while noting that hyperonic models can still accommodate such masses (Lopes et al. 2006). The paper enables future work by identifying that gravitational wave detections from neutron star mergers and improved radius measurements (potentially to ~10% precision as noted by Lattimer and Prakash 2001) will provide the strongest constraints, and that cooling measurements from isolated neutron stars (Yakovlev et al. 2006) offer complementary information on microphysics like superconducting gaps.
