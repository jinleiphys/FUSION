# Hydrodynamics: Fluctuating Initial Conditions and Two-particle Correlations

> Event-by-event hydrodynamics (or hydrodynamics with fluctuating initial conditions) has been developed in the past few years. Here we discuss how it may help to understand the various structures observed in two-particle correlations.

## Key claim
Event-by-event hydrodynamics with fluctuating initial conditions may explain the near-side ridge and the away-side double-hump structure observed in two-particle correlations, provided that longitudinal high-energy-density tubes in the initial state deflect background matter into two symmetrical directions during hydrodynamic expansion.

## Method
The SPheRIO group generated fluctuating initial conditions using the NeXus code and solved the perfect-fluid conservation equations with the Smooth Particle Hydrodynamics method in 3+1 dimensions. They also constructed a simplified 2+1 boost-invariant model containing a single high-energy tube on a smoothed background to isolate the mechanism producing the correlation structures. Two different elliptic-flow subtraction methods (ZYAM and event-plane alignment) were applied as cross-checks.

## Key numbers
- Central Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV per nucleon pair were simulated; trigger transverse momentum \(p_t^{trig} > 2.5\) GeV and associated \(p_t^{assoc} > 1.5\) GeV were used for the correlation plots.
- In the simplified one-tube model, the freeze-out temperature was set to 0.14 GeV.
- Temporal evolution of the tube was shown at times 1.0, 2.9, and 6.6 fm.
- For non-central collisions, a 20–30% centrality window was used; the tube was placed either in the reaction plane or at 60° relative to it.

## Context
This work extends the event-by-event hydrodynamic approach pioneered by the SPheRIO collaboration (Andrade, Grassi, Hama, Qian, 2008) and contrasts with smooth-initial-condition calculations (Hirano, Nonaka, 2000s). It provides a unified hydrodynamic explanation for the ridge and away-side structures that had previously been attributed to Mach cones (Renk, 2008) or to jet-medium interactions in the AMPT model (Ma, 2009). The results enable future tests using three-particle correlations and 2+1 correlations to distinguish between competing scenarios.
