# Benchmarking projected Hartree-Fock as an approximation

> We benchmark angular-momentum projected{-after-variation} Hartree-Fock calculations as an approximation to full configuration-interaction results in a shell model basis. For such a simple approximation we find reasonably good agreement between excitation spectra, including for many odd-$A$ and odd-odd nuclides. We frequently find shape coexistence, in the form of multiple Hartree-Fock minima; {mixing in shape coexistence, the first step beyond single-reference projected Hartree-Fock}, demonstrably improves the spectrum in the $sd$- and $pf$-shells. The complex spectra of germanium isotopes present a challenge: for even $A$ the spectra are only moderately good and those of odd $A$ bear little resemblance to the configuration-interaction results. Despite this failure we are able to broadly reproduce the odd-even staggering of ground state binding energies, save for germanium isotopes with $N > 40$. To illustrate potential applications, we compute the spectrum of the recently measured dripline nuclide $^{40}$Mg. All in all, projected Hartree-Fock often provides a better description of low-lying nuclear spectra than one might expect. Key to this is the use of gradient descent and unrestricted shapes.

## Key claim

Angular-momentum projected Hartree-Fock (PHF) provides a surprisingly good description of low-lying nuclear excitation spectra across many nuclides including odd-A and odd-odd systems, provided one uses gradient descent to find stable Hartree-Fock minima and allows unrestricted (triaxial) shapes; the agreement with full configuration-interaction results is often better than expected, though germanium isotopes with N > 40 present a significant challenge.

## Method

The authors performed angular-momentum projected-after-variation Hartree-Fock (PHF) calculations in shell-model spaces using the same interactions as full configuration-interaction (FCI) benchmarks, with separate proton and neutron Slater determinants. Hartree-Fock minima were found by gradient descent rather than iterated diagonalization, and the authors checked stability via the Hartree-Fock condition and the Tamm-Dancoff approximation eigenvalues. For nuclei exhibiting shape coexistence (multiple Hartree-Fock minima), the authors mixed the primary and secondary minima by solving a generalized eigenvalue problem for the projected Hamiltonian and norm kernels.

## Key numbers

- Average root-mean-square error in PHF excitation spectra relative to FCI for 114 nuclei across the sd- and pf-shells and 8 even-A germanium isotopes: 0.85 MeV
- Average RMS error for even-even nuclides: 0.67 MeV; for odd-odd nuclides: 0.68 MeV; for odd-A nuclides: 1.05 MeV
- Average RMS error for germanium isotopes: 0.38 MeV
- Average difference in Hartree-Fock energy between gradient descent minima and diagonalization solutions across all sd-shell nuclides: approximately 4 MeV
- Average lowering of the ground state energy when mixing in a second minimum: 0.22 MeV for the 21 plotted nuclides; 0.16 MeV in the sd-shell, 0.18 MeV in the pf-shell, and about 0.3 MeV for germanium isotopes
- Specific ground state energy lowerings from adding a second minimum: 0.63 MeV for 30Si, 0.44 MeV for 46Ti, 0.73 MeV for 70Ge, and 1.17 MeV for 71Ge
- Hartree-Fock energy of the primary minimum for 20Ne: -36.404 MeV with beta = 0.45, gamma = 0 degrees; secondary minimum: -31.831 MeV with beta = 0.23, gamma = 60 degrees
- Hartree-Fock energy of the primary minimum for 30P from gradient descent: -148.912 MeV; PHF ground state energy: -151.188 MeV; from diagonalization: HF energy -122.141 MeV, PHF ground state -126.493 MeV

## Context

This work provides the widest systematic benchmarking of projected Hartree-Fock against full configuration-interaction shell model calculations, extending earlier tests by Whitehead (1971, 1972) and others to include odd-A and odd-odd nuclides systematically. The study demonstrates that gradient descent reliably finds stable Hartree-Fock minima where iterated diagonalization often fails, particularly for odd numbers of particles, and that mixing shape-coexisting minima (the first step beyond single-reference PHF) improves spectra in the sd- and pf-shells. The results enable the use of simple PHF as a starting point for describing low-lying nuclear spectra, including for dripline nuclides such as 40Mg, and suggest that more sophisticated beyond-mean-field methods (generator coordinate method, Monte Carlo shell model) may often be built upon an already reasonable approximation.
