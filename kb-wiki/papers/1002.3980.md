# Very-High-Energy Gamma-Ray Signal from Nuclear Photodisintegration as a Probe of Extragalactic Sources of Ultrahigh-Energy Nuclei

> It is crucial to identify the ultrahigh-energy cosmic-ray (UHECR) sources and probe their unknown properties. Recent results from the Pierre Auger Observatory favor a heavy nuclear composition for the UHECRs. Under the requirement that heavy nuclei survive in these sources, using gamma-ray bursts as an example, we predict a diagnostic gamma-ray signal, unique to nuclei - the emission of de-excitation gamma rays following photodisintegration. These gamma rays, boosted from MeV to TeV-PeV energies, may be detectable by gamma-ray telescopes such as VERITAS, HESS, and MAGIC, and especially the next-generation CTA and AGIS. They are a promising messenger to identify and study individual UHE nuclei accelerators.

## Key claim

Under the condition that ultrahigh-energy (UHE) nuclei survive photodisintegration in their sources (so that the heavy composition favored by Pierre Auger Observatory data can be reproduced), the de-excitation gamma-ray signal from nuclear photodisintegration provides a diagnostic that is unique to nuclei and may dominate over other hadronic and leptonic components at TeV-PeV energies for nearby sources, though the detection is limited to sources within roughly 100 Mpc due to low efficiency and cosmic infrared background/CMB attenuation.

## Method

The authors performed numerical calculations of photodisintegration, photomeson, and Bethe-Heitler processes for iron nuclei in gamma-ray burst (GRB) internal shock models, using detailed cross sections and cooling timescales. They adopted a heavy composition of 50% iron and 50% proton, with a cosmic-ray spectrum of \(E_A^{-2.3}\) normalized to the UHECR energy input rate of \(10^{44}\) erg Mpc\(^{-3}\) yr\(^{-1}\) at \(10^{19}\) eV, and used low-luminosity GRB parameters (bulk Lorentz factor \(\Gamma=10\), emission radius \(r=10^{15.8}\) cm, photon luminosity \(L_\gamma^b=10^{46.2}\) erg s\(^{-1}\), break energy \(\varepsilon_{\rm ob}^b=5\) keV). The de-excitation gamma-ray fluence was evaluated using the giant dipole resonance approximation and compared with photomeson, Bethe-Heitler, ion-synchrotron, and synchrotron self-Compton components, including gamma-ray attenuation by pair creation with the cosmic infrared background and CMB.

## Key numbers

- De-excitation efficiency for iron: \(f_{\rm deex} \simeq 2.8 \times 10^{-5} \frac{L_{\gamma, 46.2}^b (A/56)^{0.21}}{r_{15.8} \Gamma_1^2 \varepsilon_{\rm ob, 5~keV}^b}\) at the resonance energy \(E_{A b}^{(A \gamma)} \simeq 9.6 \times 10^{15}~{\rm eV}~(A/56)^{0.79} (\varepsilon_{\rm ob, 5~keV}^b)^{-1} \Gamma_1^2\)
- Observed de-excitation gamma-ray energy from iron: \(E_{\gamma}^{(\rm deex)} \approx 1.5~{\rm TeV}~(A/56)^{-1} E_{A,16.5}\)
- De-excitation gamma-ray fluence at break: \(E_{\gamma}^2 \phi_{\gamma}^{(\rm deex)} \sim 5 \times 10^{-8}~{\rm erg}~{\rm cm}^{-2}\) at \(E_{\gamma b}^{(\rm deex)} \simeq 0.5~{\rm TeV}~(\varepsilon_{\rm ob, 5~keV}^b)^{-1} \Gamma_1^2\) for a low-luminosity GRB at 100 Mpc with \(\tilde{\mathcal E}_{\rm HECR}^{\rm iso}=10^{50.5}\) erg
- Maximum iron energy: \(E_{A}^{\rm max} \simeq (Z/26) 10^{20.2}~{\rm eV}\) for the adopted GRB parameters
- Photomeson production efficiency at resonance: \(f_{\rm mes} (E_{Ab}^{(\rm mes)}) \simeq 20 (A/56)^{-0.21} f_{\rm deex} (E_{Ab}^{(A \gamma)})\)
- Event rate of transient sources within 100 Mpc: \(\sim 1.3~(\tilde{\mathcal E}_{\rm HECR,50.5}^{\rm iso})^{-1}~{\rm yr}^{-1}\)

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclear photodisintegration gamma-ray production, previously applied to Milky Way sources by K. K. et al. (1994) and Anchordoqui et al. (2007), to extragalactic UHECR sources, motivated by the Pierre Auger Observatory data favoring a heavy nuclear composition. It contrasts with most prior work on gamma-ray and neutrino signals from UHECR sources, which focused on proton sources (e.g., Waxman and Bahcall 1997; Murase 2007; Ando et al. 2009). The paper enables a new observational strategy: using very-high-energy gamma-ray telescopes (VERITAS, HESS, MAGIC, and next-generation CTA and AGIS) to identify individual UHE nuclei accelerators, which is difficult with cosmic-ray nuclei due to large magnetic deflections (Tinyakov and Sigl 2009) and with neutrinos due to low fluxes (Murase et al. 2008; Murase and Beacom 2010).
