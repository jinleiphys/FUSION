# VarP-GP: cost-efficient Bayesian emulation of quark-gluon plasma modeling with variable statistical precision

> We present VarP-GP, a new cost-efficient Bayesian emulator for expensive computational models with variable statistical precision. We focus on the interpretation of measurements of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) generated in high-energy nuclear collisions, through comparison to numerical models using Bayesian Inference. Such inference calculations are computationally expensive and require surrogate model emulation, which is commonly implemented using Machine Learning (ML)--based Gaussian processes (GPs). Emulator training data are generated by Monte Carlo simulations whose numerical precision depends on the computational resources utilized; improved precision entails greater computational cost. This study utilizes JETSCAPE simulations of inclusive hadron and jet measurements in nuclear collisions at RHIC and the LHC. The VarP-GP emulator combines information from multiple simulation runs with varying precision across the model parameter space, taking advantage of the smoothness in that space of QCD-driven processes. Comparison to a traditional emulator approach shows a marked reduction in emulator uncertainty at fixed computational cost, indicating that knowledge of the overall contours of the parameter design space is more important for precise emulation than detailed information at a more limited number of design points. As an initial application of VarP-GP, a computationally-expensive model parameter sensitivity study of jet quenching data is reported. The VarP-GP emulator enables new multi-model and many-observable calibrations of QGP data and modeling, which would otherwise not be possible with achievable computing resources.

## Key claim

VarP-GP reduces emulator uncertainty at fixed computational cost compared to traditional homoskedastic Gaussian process emulators by pooling information from simulation runs with varying statistical precision across the parameter space, leveraging the smoothness of QCD-driven processes. The key condition is that knowledge of the overall contours of the parameter design space is more important for precise emulation than detailed information at a more limited number of design points.

## Method

The authors developed a heteroskedastic Gaussian process emulator (VarP-GP) that models both the mean observable and the baseline statistical variance as independent Gaussian processes with Matérn covariance kernels. Training data were generated using JETSCAPE simulations of inclusive hadron and jet RAA in lead-lead collisions at 5.02 TeV, with 28 design points in a 6-dimensional parameter space, where each design point was assigned a varying number of simulated events between 30% and 100% of the maximum event count of 1.25 million. The emulator was trained via maximum likelihood estimation using a low-memory BFGS quasi-Newton algorithm, and its performance was compared to a conventional high-fidelity GP emulator (HF-GP) using mean squared error on a separate validation set.

## Key numbers

- 28 design points were used for training after excluding 12 of the original 40 points due to large fluctuations indicating poor simulation data quality
- Maximum event count per design point was 1.25 million events (overline m), with the lower bound set to at least 30% of this maximum
- Hadron RAA data comprised 21 data points with transverse momentum range 4.8 < pT < 400 GeV/c
- Jet RAA data comprised 15 data points with transverse momentum range 100 < pT < 1000 GeV/c
- The parameter space had 6 dimensions (d = 6), with the number of design points n = 28 following the 5-10d rule of thumb
- The full JETSCAPE calculation in prior work required approximately 200 design points and 5 million CPU-core hours

## Context

This work extends the multi-observable Bayesian inference analysis of jet quenching data performed by the JETSCAPE Collaboration (2024), which required approximately 5 million CPU-core hours for a 6-dimensional parameter space with 200 design points. The VarP-GP approach addresses the computational inefficiency of requiring uniform high statistical precision at all design points, which is unnecessarily conservative when incorporating heterogeneous datasets with varying experimental precision. This enables new multi-model and many-observable calibrations of QGP data that would otherwise not be possible with achievable computing resources, and provides a framework for future studies investigating the physical mechanisms underlying parameter sensitivity in jet quenching.
