# Quenching of high-pT hadrons: Energy Loss vs Color Transparency

> High-pT hadrons produced in hard collisions and detected inclusively bear peculiar features: (i) they originate from jets whose initial virtuality and energy are of the same order; (ii) such jets are rare and have a very biased energy sharing among the particles, namely, the detected hadron carries the main fraction of the jet energy. The former feature leads to an extremely intensive gluon radiation and energy dissipation at the early stage of hadronization, either in vacuum or in a medium. As a result, a leading hadron must be produced on a short length scale. Evaluation within a model of perturbative fragmentation confirms the shortness of the production length. This result is at variance with the unjustified assumption of long production length, made within the popular energy loss scenario. Thus we conclude that the main reason of suppression of high-pT hadrons in heavy ion collisions is the controlled by color transparency attenuation of a high-pT dipole propagating through the hot medium. Adjusting a single parameter, the transport coefficient, we describe quite well the data from LHC and RHIC for the suppression factor R_{AA} as function of pT, collision energy and centrality. We observe that the complementary effect of initial state interaction causes a flattening and even fall of R_{AA} at large pT. The azimuthal anisotropy of hadron production, calculated with no further adjustment, also agrees well with data at different energies and centralities.

## Key claim

The main reason for suppression of high-\(p_T\) hadrons in heavy ion collisions is the attenuation of a high-\(p_T\) dipole propagating through the hot medium, controlled by color transparency, rather than the popular energy loss scenario which relies on an unjustified assumption of long production length.

## Method

The authors calculate the production length of a colorless dipole (pre-hadron) within a model of perturbative fragmentation, using the time-dependent radiational energy loss of a highly virtual parton with \(Q^2 = E^2\) and the Sudakov suppression factor from energy conservation. They then evaluate the survival probability of the expanding dipole in the medium using the relation between the dipole cross section and the transport coefficient \(\hat q\), with the medium density modeled as proportional to the number of participants and diluted with time as \(1/t\). The suppression factor \(R_{AA}\) and azimuthal anisotropy \(v_2\) are computed using a path-integral technique and compared to data from RHIC and LHC.

## Key numbers

- Transport coefficient \(\hat q_0\) ranges from \(1.2\ \text{GeV}^2/\text{fm}\) at \(\sqrt{s}=62\ \text{GeV}\) up to \(2\ \text{GeV}^2/\text{fm}\) at \(\sqrt{s}=2.76\ \text{TeV}\) for collisions of heavy nuclei (gold and lead).
- The time to radiate half of the total vacuum energy loss for a high-\(k_T\) parton is \(t(\delta=1/2) = 2/(e\lambda) \approx 0.2\ \text{fm}\), independent of energy, with \(\lambda = 0.7\ \text{GeV}\).
- The equilibration time \(t_0\) is fixed at \(t_0 = l_0 = 0.5\ \text{fm}\).
- The mean fractional momentum \(\langle z_h \rangle\) carried by the detected hadron is above 0.5 for all energies studied, with values shown for \(\sqrt{s} = 200,\ 2760,\ 7000\ \text{GeV}\) as function of \(p_T\).

## Context

This work extends the color transparency approach of Kopeliovich et al. (2003) and the dipole evolution model of Kopeliovich et al. (2005) to high-\(p_T\) hadron production in heavy ion collisions, contradicting the energy loss scenario of Baier et al. (2001) and the analysis of PHENIX (2008) which assumed long production lengths. The authors find that the transport coefficient values are about twice as large as those found in their earlier simplified model (Kopeliovich et al. 2011) but an order of magnitude smaller than those from energy loss analyses. The approach enables extraction of the medium transport coefficient from \(R_{AA}\) data and predicts a flattening or fall of \(R_{AA}\) at large \(p_T\) due to initial state interactions.
