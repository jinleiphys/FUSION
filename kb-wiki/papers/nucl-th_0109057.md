# Double beta decay in deformed nuclei

> The pseudo SU(3) approach has been used to describe many low-lying rotational bands, as well as BE(2) and B(M1) intensities in rare earth and actinide nuclei, both with even-even and odd-mass numbers . The double beta half lives of some of these parent nuclei to the ground and excited states of the daughter ones were evaluated for the two and zero neutrino emitting modes using the pseudo SU(3) scheme. The predictions were in good agreement with the available experimental data for 150Nd and 238U. The double electron capture half-lives of 156Dy, 162Er and 168Yb were also studied using the same formalism. The first nuclei was found to be the best candidate for experimental detection, with a half-life of 10^24 yr to the first excited 0+ state in 156Gd. There are strong selection rules which restrict the two neutrino mode of the double beta decay in some other nuclei, including 160Gd. Experimental limits for the double beta decay of 160Gd have been reported . Recently it was argued that the strong cancellation of the 2nu mode in the double beta decay of 160Gd would suppress the background for the detection of the 0nu mode. In the present contribution we extend the previous research evaluating the double beta half lives of 160Gd using the pseudo SU(3) model. While the 2nu mode is forbidden when the most probable occupations are considered, states with different occupation numbers can be mixed through the pairing interaction. The amount of this mixing is evaluated using perturbation theory. The possibility of observing the double beta decay in 160Gd is discussed for both the 2nu and 0nu modes.

# Double beta decay in deformed nuclei

## Key claim
The pseudo SU(3) model predicts that the two-neutrino double beta decay of 160Gd is forbidden when only the most probable occupation numbers are considered, but mixing through the pairing interaction evaluated via perturbation theory yields a finite half-life that is suppressed by approximately one order of magnitude compared to nuclei with similar Q-values, potentially offering a low-background window for detecting the zero-neutrino mode.

## Method
The authors employed the pseudo SU(3) shell model to construct many-particle wave functions for deformed nuclei in the laboratory frame, using a Hamiltonian that includes single-particle energies, pairing, and quadrupole-quadrupole interactions with parameters fixed by known systematics. For 160Gd, the most probable Nilsson-level occupations were determined, and the pairing-induced mixing between different occupation configurations in the daughter nucleus 160Dy was treated using first-order perturbation theory to compute the nuclear matrix elements for both the two-neutrino and zero-neutrino double beta decay modes.

## Key numbers
- For 160Gd double beta decay to the ground state of 160Dy, the two-neutrino half-life is 1.65 × 10^22 yr.
- For 160Gd double beta decay to the ground state of 160Dy, the zero-neutrino half-life (assuming <m_ν> = 1 eV) is 3.96 × 10^25 yr.
- The mixing coefficients for the 160Dy wave function are a = 0.972 and b = 0.233, with the energy denominator ΔE = 1.50 MeV for promoting a proton pair from normal to unique parity orbitals.
- For 156Dy double electron capture to the first excited 0+ state in 156Gd, the half-life is approximately 10^24 yr (specifically 8.31 × 10^24 yr from Table 2).
- For 150Nd two-neutrino double beta decay, the calculated half-life is 6.0 × 10^18 yr, compared to the experimental value of 9 (17) × 10^18 yr.
- For 238U two-neutrino double beta decay, the calculated half-life is 1.4 × 10^21 yr, compared to the experimental value of 2 × 10^21 yr.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous pseudo SU(3) calculations of double beta decay in deformed nuclei (Cas94, Hir95a, Hir95b, Hir95c, Hir95d) to the specific case of 160Gd, which was predicted to have a forbidden two-neutrino mode. The strong cancellation of the two-neutrino mode in 160Gd was previously argued by Dan00 to suppress background for zero-neutrino mode detection. The results enable experimental searches for neutrinoless double beta decay in 160Gd and confirm that 156Dy is the best candidate among the studied nuclei for detecting double electron capture, with a half-life accessible to near-future experiments with sensitivity near 10^22 yr as estimated by Barabash (1994).
