# Early collective expansion: Relativistic hydrodynamics and the transport properties of QCD matter

> Relativistic hydrodynamics for ideal and viscous fluids is discussed as a tool to describe relativistic heavy-ion collisions and to extract transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma from experimentally measured hadron momentum spectra.

## Key claim
Relativistic hydrodynamics, extended to include viscous corrections, can be used to extract the transport properties (specifically the shear viscosity) of the quark-gluon plasma from experimentally measured hadron momentum spectra in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, provided the system is sufficiently close to local thermal equilibrium and the dissipative corrections remain small.

## Method
The paper reviews the theoretical framework of relativistic ideal and viscous hydrodynamics as applied to the early collective expansion stage of heavy-ion collisions. It discusses the formulation of causal dissipative hydrodynamics (Israel-Stewart theory) to avoid acausal signal propagation, and describes how the hydrodynamic equations are solved with initial conditions constrained by experimental data from the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC). The extracted hadron spectra and anisotropic flow coefficients are then compared to measurements to infer the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio (eta/s) of the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key numbers
- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio (eta/s) of the quark-gluon plasma is extracted from comparisons of viscous hydrodynamic calculations with RHIC data; the paper discusses values near the conjectured lower bound of 1/(4 pi) (approximately 0.08).
- The paper references the QCD transition temperature determined by lattice QCD: Aoki et al. (2006) found a value of about 192 MeV with physical quark masses in the continuum limit.
- The paper discusses the centrality dependence of charged particle multiplicity at mid-rapidity for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 130 GeV, as measured by the PHENIX and PHOBOS collaborations.

## Context
This review extends the long line of hydrodynamic modeling of heavy-ion collisions that began with Landau (1953) and was developed further by Bjorken (1983), and later by Kolb, Heinz, and collaborators (1999-2004) who showed that ideal hydrodynamics successfully described RHIC data. The paper addresses the transition from ideal to viscous hydrodynamics, building on the causal dissipative formalism of Israel (1976) and Israel and Stewart (1979), and on the work of Teaney (2003) who first estimated shear viscosity effects on observables. It enables the systematic extraction of QGP transport coefficients from experimental data, a program pursued by Song and Heinz (2008) and Romatschke and Romatschke (2007).
