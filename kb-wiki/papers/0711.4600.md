# Localization and the interface between quantum mechanics, quantum field theory and quantum gravity

> We show that there are significant conceptual differences between QM and QFT which make it difficult to view the latter as just a relativistic extension of the principles of QM. At the root of this is a fundamental distinction between Born-localization in QM (which in the relativistic context changes its name to Newton-Wigner localization) and modular localization which is the localization underlying QFT, after one liberates it from its standard presentation in terms of field coordinates. The first comes with a probability notion and projection operators, whereas the latter describes causal propagation in QFT and leads to thermal aspects of locally reduced finite energy states. The Born-Newton-Wigner localization in QFT is only applicable asymptotically and the covariant correlation between asymptotic in and out localization projectors is the basis of the existence of an invariant scattering matrix. Taking these significant differences serious has not only repercussions for the philosophy of science, but also leads to a new structural properties as a consequence of vacuum polarization: the area law for localization entropy near the the causal localization horizon and a more realistic cutoff independent setting for the cosmological vacuum energy density which is compatible with local covariance. The article presents some observations about the interface between QFT in CST and QG.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quantum field theory (QFT) cannot be viewed as merely a relativistic extension of quantum mechanics (QM), because the two theories rest on fundamentally different localization concepts: Born-Newton-Wigner localization in QM (which comes with probability notions and projection operators) versus modular localization in QFT (which describes causal propagation and leads to thermal aspects of locally reduced states). The author hedges that these differences are often overlooked because the two localization concepts coalesce in the asymptotic limit needed for scattering theory, making QFT and QM appear compatible where they must agree physically.

## Method

The paper contrasts the conceptual foundations of QM and QFT by examining two localization notions: Born-Newton-Wigner (BNW) localization, which is the standard probabilistic localization of particles in QM, and modular localization, which is the causal localization inherent in QFT when liberated from particular field coordinatizations. The author reviews the Coester-Polyzou theory of direct particle interactions (DPI) as a relativistic particle framework that achieves a Poincaré-invariant S-matrix and cluster factorization without using fields, demonstrating that relativistic particle theories need not be equivalent to QFT. The paper then discusses how modular localization, supported by Tomita-Takesaki modular theory, leads to structural consequences such as the area law for localization entropy and a cutoff-independent setting for the cosmological vacuum energy density.

## Context

This work extends the conceptual analysis of QFT foundations found in Haag's "Local Quantum Physics" and the algebraic approach to QFT (local quantum physics, LQP), while contrasting with the direct particle interaction framework developed by Coester and Polyzou. The paper contradicts the common folklore (attributed to Weinberg) that relativistic quantum theory of particles plus cluster factorization equals QFT, by showing that DPI provides a consistent relativistic particle theory without fields. The results enable a more principled approach to the interface between QFT in curved spacetime and quantum gravity, particularly regarding localization entropy and the cosmological constant problem, building on prior work by Hollands and Wald (2005) on local covariance.
