# Extremely large matter radii in $^{49-51}$Ca isotopes and the $0^{+}$ breathing mode states of $^{48}$Ca

> Through inelastic scattering cross section measurements for $^{42-51}$Ca on a carbon target at 280 MeV/nucleon recently, Tanaka {\it et al.} [ arXiv:1911.05262 [nucl-ex]], in a very significant experiment, have demonstrated large swelling of doubly magic $^{48}$Ca core in calcium isotopes beyond N=28. The matter radii observed in these experiments, are surprisingly much larger than the corresponding, already amazingly large charge radii of the same calcium isotopes, by Garcia {\it et al.} [Nat. Phys. 12 (2016) 594]. Here we propose a novel solution, wherein the breathing mode states $0^{+}$ of $^{48}$Ca, provide a global and consistent solution of this matter radii conundrum.

# Extremely large matter radii in $^{49-51}$Ca isotopes and the $0^{+}$ breathing mode states of $^{48}$Ca

## Key claim

The anomalously large matter radii observed in $^{49-51}$Ca beyond the already-large charge radii can be explained by the valence neutrons polarizing the $^{48}$Ca core into its excited $0^{+}$ breathing mode states, rather than by neutron halos or deformation. The model predicts that this swelling will continue through $^{52-54}$Ca but stop at N=34, avoiding a "calcium-radii-catastrophe."

## Method

The authors propose a phenomenological model inspired by the E2 effective charge concept, where valence neutrons outside the N=28 magic core of $^{48}$Ca polarize the core into successively higher $0^{+}$ breathing mode states. For $^{49}$Ca, the single valence neutron induces the core to breathe into the $0^{+}_{1}$ state at 4.284 MeV; for $^{50}$Ca, two neutrons drive the core to the $0^{+}_{2}$ state at 5.461 MeV; and for $^{51}$Ca, three neutrons drive it to the $0^{+}_{3}$ state at 11.945/11.967 MeV. The model uses experimentally known $0^{+}$ states of $^{48}$Ca from Fujita (1988) and Burrows (1993) to map each isotope's matter radius to a specific core-excited configuration.

## Key numbers

- $^{48}$Ca breathing mode states used: $0^{+}_{1}$ at 4.284 MeV, $0^{+}_{2}$ at 5.461 MeV, $0^{+}_{3}$ at 11.945/11.967 MeV (nearly degenerate doublet)
- Three additional $0^{+}$ states predicted to explain future measurements: $0^{+}_{4}$ at 12.318 MeV, $0^{+}_{5}$ at 12.565 MeV, $0^{+}_{6}$ at 12.869 MeV
- The swelling is predicted to stop at N=34 ($^{54}$Ca), with no further expansion beyond that neutron number
- The matter radii in $^{49-51}$Ca are described as "much larger" than the charge radii measured by Garcia (2016), though no numerical radii values are given in the text

## Context

This work addresses the conundrum created by two recent experiments: Garcia (2016) found surprisingly large charge radii in $^{49-52}$Ca, and Tanaka (2019) found even larger matter radii in $^{49-51}$Ca from interaction cross section measurements at 280 MeV/nucleon. Tanaka (2019) had already ruled out one-, two-, and three-neutron halos as well as deformation as explanations. The present model extends the effective charge concept from Shalit (1959), Abbas (1980), and Zamick (1977) to treat core polarization via breathing mode excitations. It enables experimental tests by predicting continued large radii in $^{52-54}$Ca followed by an abrupt halt, and raises the question of whether a "calcium-radii-catastrophe" analogous to the ultraviolet catastrophe might emerge if the swelling continued unchecked.
