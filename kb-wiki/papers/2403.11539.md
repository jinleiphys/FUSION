# Detecting superfluid transition in the pulsar core

> It is believed that the core of a neutron star can be host to various novel phases of matter, from nucleon superfluid phase to exotic high baryon density quantum chromodynamics (QCD) phases. Different observational signals for such phase transitions have been discussed in the literature. Here, we point out a unique phenomenon associated with phase transition to a superfluid phase, which may be the nucleon superfluid phase or a phase like the CFL phase, allowing for superfluid vortices. In any superfluid phase transition, a random network of vortices forms via the so-called Kibble-Zurek mechanism, which eventually mostly decays away, finally leaving primarily vortices arising from the initial angular momentum of the core. This transient, random vortex network can have a non-zero net angular momentum for the superfluid component, which will generally be oriented in an arbitrary direction. This is in contrast to the final vortices, which arise from initial rotation and hence have the initial angular momentum of the neutron star. The angular momentum of the random vortex network is balanced by an equal and opposite angular momentum in the normal fluid due to the conservation of angular momentum, thereby imparting an arbitrarily oriented angular momentum component to the outer shell of the neutron star. This will affect the pulse timing and pulse profile of a pulsar. These changes in the pulses will decay away in a characteristic manner such that the random vortex network decays, obeying specific scaling laws leading to universal features for the detection of superfluid transitions occurring in a pulsar core.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a superfluid phase transition in a pulsar core (whether nucleon superfluid or color-flavor locked phase) will produce a transient, randomly oriented vortex network via the Kibble-Zurek mechanism, whose net angular momentum is balanced by an opposite angular momentum in the normal fluid that is transferred to the neutron star's outer shell, causing detectable wobbling and pulse timing changes that decay with universal scaling laws.

## Method

The authors apply the Kibble-Zurek mechanism for vortex formation during a superfluid phase transition, incorporating angular momentum conservation constraints from Das (2016) and Dave (2018). They calculate the net vortex number (vortices minus antivortices) ending at a given surface using a random-walk argument around the perimeter of a correlation domain, yielding a scaling relation where the standard deviation of the net vortex number scales as N^(1/4) rather than the naive N^(1/2). They then estimate the resulting angular momentum imbalance and its transfer to the neutron star's outer crust, and discuss observational consequences for pulsar timing and pulse profiles.

## Key numbers

- Coherence length for neutron superfluidity: sample value of ξ = 100 fm, based on estimates ranging from a few tens fm to order 100 fm as neutron Fermi momentum varies from 0.1 fm^(-1) to 0.8 fm^(-1)
- Coherence length for CFL phase: ξ = 0.26 × (100 MeV / T_c) × (1 - T/T_c)^(-1/2) fm, with T_c ≈ (20 - 50) MeV from Δ_0 in the range (40 - 90) MeV, giving ξ in the (0.5 - 1) fm range
- Superfluid critical temperature in the core region: (0.1 - 0.5) MeV for nucleon superfluidity
- Typical interior temperature of neutron star: T ≈ 0.01 MeV
- Saturation density of nuclear matter: ρ_0 = 2.8 × 10^14 g cm^(-3)
- Probability of vortex/antivortex formation per correlation domain in two space dimensions: 1/4
- Universal scaling exponent for net vortex number: ν = 1/4, with constant C of order 1 (e.g., C = 0.57 for triangular domains, 0.71 for square domains)
- Typical inter-vortex separation from initial rotation of neutron star: order 0.1 mm, compared to correlation lengths of 1 fm to 100 fm for CFL or neutron superfluid vortices

## Context

This work extends prior investigations of phase transition effects in neutron star cores by Bagchi (2015) and Bagchi (2021), which considered density fluctuations from topological defects but did not utilize the fluid circulation associated with superfluid vortices. It builds on the Kibble-Zurek mechanism for vortex formation (Kibble 1976, Zurek 1996) and incorporates modifications for rotating systems proposed by Das (2016) and Dave (2018). The mechanism could provide a new observational signature for superfluid transitions in pulsars, complementing existing evidence from pulsar glitches (Anderson 1975, 1976) and cooling observations of Cassiopeia A (Shternin 2011, 2022), though the authors note that Cas A itself is not suitable for testing this mechanism as no pulses have been detected from that neutron star.
