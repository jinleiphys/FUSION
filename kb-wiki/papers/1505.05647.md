# Collective aspects deduced from time-dependent microscopic mean-field with pairing: application to the fission process

> Given a set of collective variables, a method is proposed to obtain the associated conjugated collective momenta and masses starting from a microscopic time-dependent mean-field theory. The construction of pairs of conjugated variables is the first step to bridge microscopic and macroscopic approaches. The method is versatile and can be applied to study a large class of nuclear processes. An illustration is given here with the fission of $^{258}$Fm. Using the quadrupole moment and eventually higher-order multipole moments, the associated collective masses are estimated along the microscopic mean-field evolution. When more than one collective variable are considered, it is shown that the off-diagonal matrix elements of the inertia play a crucial role. Using the information on the quadrupole moment and associated momentum, the collective evolution is studied. It is shown that dynamical effects beyond the adiabatic limit are important. Nuclei formed after fission tend to stick together for longer time leading to a dynamical scission point at larger distance between nuclei compared to the one anticipated from the adiabatic energy landscape. The effective nucleus-nucleus potential felt by the emitted nuclei is finally extracted.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method to extract collective masses and momenta from time-dependent mean-field trajectories without assuming adiabaticity, but the method's application to fission shows that time-dependent energy density functional (TD-EDF) theory cannot spontaneously lead to fission below a certain dynamical threshold (Q₂ᵗʰ ≈ 160 b) due to improper treatment of level crossing, meaning the approach is most reliable for describing the post-barrier evolution rather than the barrier crossing itself.

## Method

The authors derive collective masses from the time-dependent mean-field density using the condition that the collective coordinate and its conjugate momentum satisfy a canonical commutation relation along the trajectory, yielding M_α(t) = m / Tr[ρ(t) ∇Q_α · ∇Q_α]. They apply this to the fission of ²⁵⁸Fm using the Skyrme functional Sly4d with BCS pairing, performing dynamical calculations on a 26.4 × 72.8 × 13.2 fm³ mesh with 0.8 fm spacing and time step 1.5 × 10⁻²⁴ s, starting from constrained mean-field configurations at various quadrupole deformations with and without initial boosts.

## Key numbers

- The dynamical fission threshold for ²⁵⁸Fm is Q₂ᵗʰ ≈ 160 b, well beyond the fission barrier position at Q₂ = 80 b.
- The adiabatic scission point occurs at Q₂ ≈ 270 b, identified by a kink in the potential energy curve.
- The mesh step used in calculations is Δx = 0.8 fm, with time step Δt = 1.5 × 10⁻²⁴ s ≈ 0.45 fm/c.
- The static calculations used a mesh size of 13.2 × 24.4 × 13.2 fm³, while dynamical calculations used 26.4 × 72.8 × 13.2 fm³.
- For initial Q₂ = 160 b without boost, the quadrupole mass along the TD-EDF trajectory remains close to the static mass until near the scission point, where deviation occurs.

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent energy density functional approach to fission dynamics previously studied by Simenel (2014), Goddard et al. (2014, 2015), and Scamps et al. (2015), providing a systematic method to extract macroscopic transport coefficients from microscopic trajectories. The method enables comparison between adiabatic and dynamical collective masses, revealing that dynamical effects beyond the adiabatic limit are important near scission, and allows extraction of the effective nucleus-nucleus potential felt by the emitted fragments. The approach can be generalized to other nuclear processes beyond fission.
