# The Rise and Fall of the Ridge

> Recent data from heavy ion collisions at RHIC show unexpectedly large near-angle correlations that broaden longitudinally with centrality. The amplitude of this ridge-like correlation rises rapidly with centrality, reaches a maximum, and then falls in the most central collisions. In this talk we explain how this behavior can be easily understood in a picture where final momentum-space correlations are driven by initial coordinate space density fluctuations. We propose $v_n^2/ε_{n,part}^{2}$ as a useful way to study these effects and explain what it tells us about the collision dynamics.

## Key claim

The centrality dependence of the ridge amplitude (rising rapidly, reaching a maximum, then falling in the most central collisions) can be explained by initial-state geometry fluctuations, specifically the product of particle density, conversion efficiency, and the third harmonic participant eccentricity, rather than requiring a major revision of the understanding of heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The author calculates the near-side ridge amplitude \(A_1\) from the product of the particle density \(\rho_0\), the conversion efficiency \(c\) (taken from \(v_2/\varepsilon\) data parameterized as a function of \((1/S)dN/dy\)), and the third harmonic participant eccentricity \(\langle\varepsilon_{3,part}^2\rangle\) from a Monte-Carlo Glauber model, scaled by a factor of 0.039 to map from the \(n=3\) component to the Gaussian amplitude. The conversion efficiency for \(n=2\) is applied to \(n=3\) as an approximation, and the centrality dependence is plotted against the parameter \(\nu = 2N_{bin}/N_{part}\).

## Key numbers

* The near-side correlation extends across nearly 2 units of pseudo-rapidity (\(\Delta\eta=2\)) for particles of all \(p_T\), and perhaps as far as \(\Delta\eta=4\) as indicated by PHOBOS data.
* The azimuthal width \(\sigma\) of the near-side Gaussian is weakly dependent on centrality, with a typical value of \(\sigma=0.65\).
* The \(n=3\) component of the near-side ridge is related to the amplitude by \(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\frac{\Delta\rho}{\sqrt{\rho_{ref}}}\cos(3\Delta\phi)d\Delta\phi = 0.039A_1\).
* The ridge amplitude \(A_1\) reaches a maximum near \(\nu=5\) and then falls, matching the shape of preliminary STAR data at both \(\sqrt{s_{_{NN}}}=200\) and 62.4 GeV.
* For intermediate \(p_T\) di-hadron correlations (20-60% central 200 GeV Au+Au), the transfer function \(v_n\{2\}^2/\varepsilon_{n,part}^2\) shows a pronounced peak at \(n=3\) for higher \(p_T^{assoc}\) cuts (e.g., \(c_n\)(2.5 GeV, 2.5 GeV)), but this feature disappears for \(p_T^{trig}>6\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the idea that eccentricity fluctuations can give rise to \(v_n\) fluctuations for multiple harmonics, previously argued by Sorensen (2009), and that these fluctuations could be the source of the near-side ridge, a calculation later borne out by several groups including McLerran (2010), and others. The calculation correctly describes the rise and fall of the ridge amplitude, which has no natural explanation in other proposed scenarios such as the Glauber Linear Superposition model. The proposed transfer function \(v_n^2/\varepsilon_{n,part}^2\) enables future study of length scales in heavy-ion collisions, including effects of mean-free-path, initial-state free-streaming, and de-coherence in the longitudinal direction.
