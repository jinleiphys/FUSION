# The flux-tube model of particle creation in nuclear collisions

> I review some of the history of the flux-tube model, concentrating on work done by the Los Alamos-Tel Aviv collaboration on particle creation and back-reaction in uniform fields and in the central rapidity region. I discuss the incorporation of more realistic geometry and structure of the flux tube via application of the dual superconductor model of confinement.

# The flux-tube model of particle creation in nuclear collisions

## Key claim
The flux-tube model, when treated via quantum field theory in the large-\(N_f\) (many-flavor) limit, provides a first-principles description of particle creation and subsequent plasma oscillations in the central rapidity region of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, but the idealized infinite-capacitor geometry is only a first step; the results are well approximated by kinetic theory for infinite transverse extent, yet agreement becomes only qualitative when finite cylindrical geometry is introduced.

## Method
The author reviews the Los Alamos–Tel Aviv collaboration's work on particle creation in uniform color-electric fields, starting from a kinetic theory approach using the Vlasov equation with a Schwinger-type source term, then moving to a full quantum field theory treatment in the semiclassical (mean-field) approximation justified by taking the number of flavors \(N_f\) to infinity. The field theory solves coupled equations for the Fourier amplitudes of a scalar (or fermion) field and the renormalized Maxwell equation for the electric field, integrated numerically in one and three dimensions. Extensions include boost-invariant initial conditions for longitudinally expanding geometry and a dual superconductor model for the flux tube's dynamical structure, with classical magnetohydrodynamics for the electric charges.

## Key numbers
- The electric field strength in the flux tube is proportional to the square root of the number of gluon exchanges per unit area, scaling as \(E \sim A^{1/3}\) due to a random walk in color space.
- The pair creation rate per unit volume as a function of transverse momentum is given by \(\frac{dN}{dt\,dV\,d^2p_\perp} = eE \log\left[1 + \exp\left(-\frac{\pi(m^2 + p_\perp^2)}{eE}\right)\right]\) (the Casher–Neuberger–Nussinov formula).
- RHIC collisions are at 100 GeV per nucleon in each beam for gold nuclei.
- The dual superconductor model is studied in Type I and Type II regimes, with plasma frequency \(\omega_p\) compared to the gauge boson mass \(m_V\); for Type I, \(\omega_p < m_V\).

## Context
This review extends the flux-tube model originally proposed by Casher, Neuberger, and Nussinov (1979) and the Schwinger mechanism for pair creation, building on work by Kajantie and Bialas on kinetic theory. The Los Alamos–Tel Aviv collaboration (including Cooper, Mottola, Kluger, and Eisenberg) developed the quantum field-theoretic treatment for bosons and fermions in one and three dimensions, and later for boost-invariant geometry. The dual superconductor approach follows 't Hooft and Mandelstam's idea of confinement via a magnetic condensate, with the specific Abelian Higgs model taken from Suzuki and the Osaka group. The paper enables future work incorporating dynamical flux-tube geometry into particle creation models, though the simplest Higgs theory lacks sufficient dynamical confinement for good phenomenology.
