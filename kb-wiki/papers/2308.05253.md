# Fuzzy gauge theory for quantum computers

> Continuous gauge theories, because of their bosonic degrees of freedom, have an infinite-dimensional local Hilbert space. Encoding these degrees of freedom on qubit-based hardware demands some sort of ``qubitization'' scheme, where one approximates the behavior of a theory while using only finitely many degrees of freedom. We propose a novel qubitization strategy for gauge theories, called ``fuzzy gauge theory,'' building on the success of the fuzzy $σ$-model in earlier work. We provide arguments that the fuzzy gauge theory lies in the same universality class as regular gauge theory, in which case its use would obviate the need of any further limit besides the usual spatial continuum limit. Furthermore, we demonstrate that these models are relatively resource-efficient for quantum simulations.

## Key claim

The authors propose that their "fuzzy gauge theory" lies in the same universality class as regular gauge theory, which would mean that no additional limit beyond the usual spatial continuum limit is needed for the theory to reproduce continuum physics, though they provide arguments rather than a rigorous proof.

## Method

The authors replace the infinite-dimensional Hilbert space of functions on the SU(N) group manifold at each lattice link with a finite-dimensional space of matrices, where the matrix elements U_{ij} become D×D matrices that satisfy fuzzy analogs of the unitarity and special determinant conditions. They construct gauge transformations via conjugation by finite-dimensional representations of the SU(N) generators, and build a Hamiltonian analogous to the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian using fuzzy plaquette and kinetic terms, with the physical Hilbert space defined by the Gauss law constraint.

## Key numbers

- The four-dimensional (spinorial) representation of SO(5) yields a 16-dimensional Hilbert space per link for the fuzzy SU(2) gauge theory
- The smallest representation of the "gauge magnet" framework for SU(2) was four-dimensional per link, while the "quantum link" framework used a six-dimensional representation per link
- In the (3+1)-dimensional SU(3) gauge theory with the S(1080) discrete subgroup, the lattice spacing achieved was approximately a≈0.08 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier "fuzzy σ-model" approach (Alexandru 2019, 2021, 2022) to gauge theories, building on finite gauge theory strategies dating back to Horn (1981), Orland and Rohrlich (1989), and the quantum link framework of Chandrasekharan and Wiese (1996). The approach contrasts with truncation schemes based on finite subgroups (Hasenfratz 2001, Caracciolo 2001) or truncation of the Laplacian eigenbasis (Barber 1980, Hamer 1982), which have been shown to fail to reproduce the correct continuum limit. The fuzzy approach aims to enable quantum simulations of real-time evolution and finite-chemical potential problems that are inaccessible to Monte Carlo methods due to the sign problem.
