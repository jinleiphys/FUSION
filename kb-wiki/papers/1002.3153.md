# Astrophysical Measurement of the Equation of State of Neutron Star Matter

> We present the first astrophysical measurement of the pressure of cold matter above nuclear saturation density, based on recently determined masses and radii of three neutron stars. The pressure at higher densities are below the predictions of equations of state that account only for nucleonic degrees of freedom, and thus present a challenge to the microscopic theory of neutron star matter.

## Key claim
The paper presents the first astrophysical measurement of the pressure of cold matter above nuclear saturation density, extracted from mass and radius determinations of three neutron stars. The measured pressures at higher densities are below the predictions of equations of state that account only for nucleonic degrees of freedom, indicating that the data call for a softer equation of state than purely nucleonic models provide.

## Method
The authors combined measurements of the apparent surface area during thermonuclear burst cooling phases and the touchdown flux at the Eddington limit for three neutron stars in the binaries 4U 1608-248, EXO 1745-248, and 4U 1820-30, together with distance measurements, to obtain uncorrelated mass and radius constraints. They then used a piecewise polytropic inversion procedure to convert these three mass-radius measurements into probability densities for the pressure at three fiducial baryon densities: 1.85, 3.7, and 7.4 times nuclear saturation density, incorporating the requirement of causality by rejecting combinations where the sound speed exceeds the speed of light.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear saturation density: ρ_ns = 2.7 × 10^14 g cm^-3 = 0.16 fm^-3
- Pressure at 1.85 ρ_ns (log P_1): [0.6–1.4] MeV fm^-3 (weakly constrained)
- Pressure at 3.7 ρ_ns (log P_2): 1.70 (+0.15, -0.15) MeV fm^-3
- Pressure at 7.4 ρ_ns (log P_3): 2.8 (+0.1, -0.2) MeV fm^-3
- Pressures at ρ_2 and ρ_3 are constrained to within a factor of approximately 10^0.3
- Mass and radius uncertainties are of order 15%

## Context
This work extends the suggestion by Lattimer and Prakash (2001) that neutron star radii indicate the pressure at roughly twice nuclear saturation density, and the inversion method of Lindblom (1992) showing that three mass-radius measurements suffice to infer a piecewise polytropic equation of state. The results challenge purely nucleonic equations of state such as AP4 (Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall 1998), MPA1, and MS1, which are too stiff at higher densities, and instead favor softer equations of state that include additional degrees of freedom such as hyperons, meson condensates, or quarks. The measurements are consistent with the 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙ neutron star mass measured via Shapiro delay by Demorest et al. (2010).
