# Distribution of occupation numbers in finite Fermi-systems and role of interaction in chaos and thermalization

> New method is developed for calculation of single-particle occupation numbers in finite Fermi systems of interacting particles. It is more accurate than the canonical distribution method and gives the Fermi-Dirac distribution in the limit of large number of particles. It is shown that statistical effects of the interaction are absorbed by an increase of the effective temperature. Criteria for quantum chaos and statistical equilibrium are considered. All results are confirmed by numerical experiments in the two-body random interaction model.

# Distribution of occupation numbers in finite Fermi-systems and role of interaction in chaos and thermalization

## Key claim

The paper develops a new "F-distribution" method for calculating single-particle occupation numbers in finite Fermi systems that is more accurate than the canonical (Gibbs) distribution, and shows that the standard Fermi-Dirac distribution can be recovered in the limit of large particle number only if the residual interaction is weak enough; otherwise, statistical effects of the interaction can be absorbed by an increase of the effective temperature, but the F-distribution remains valid even when the Fermi-Dirac distribution fails.

## Method

The authors derive an expression for occupation numbers using a "microcanonical" F-distribution that averages over shell-model basis states weighted by a spreading function F(E_k - E), rather than summing over exact eigenstates as in the canonical distribution. They analyze the spreading width Gamma from the mean-square non-diagonal Hamiltonian matrix elements of the residual interaction, and test the method numerically using the two-body random interaction model with parameters n (number of particles), m (number of orbitals), and V0/d0 (interaction strength relative to single-particle level spacing). They also derive an improved Breit-Wigner form for the spreading function that accounts for energy shifts due to level repulsion and asymmetric density of states.

## Key numbers

- Spreading width relation: (Gamma/2)^2 = V0^2 n(n-1)(m-n)(3+m-n)/4, where V0^2 is the mean square two-body residual interaction matrix element
- For equilibrium F-distribution (region III), the number of principal components Npc ~ Gamma/D >= 100, giving fluctuations ~ Npc^{-1/2} << 1
- For "initial chaotization" (region II), Npc ~ Gamma/D >= 10, with fluctuations Npc^{-1/2} >= 0.1
- The transition from region II to region III in 3D requires only n3* ~ n2* + 2 additional excited particles, making it a very sharp "phase transition"
- For 1D systems, V0 >> d0, meaning strong mixing starts from the ground state; for 3D systems, V0 << d0
- The effective temperature shift from single-particle spreading width gamma is Delta T ~ gamma^2/16T when gamma << mu

## Context

This work extends earlier numerical studies of chaotic eigenstates in the Ce atom (Flambaum et al., 1994), the s-d nuclear shell model (Zelevinsky et al., 1996), and the two-body random interaction model (Flambaum and Izrailev, 1996; Flambaum et al., 1996). The paper provides a theoretical foundation for these numerical observations and introduces a practical "microcanonical" partition function that can be used for further studies of statistical and thermodynamic properties of finite systems of interacting particles, including nuclei, atoms, and mesoscopic systems. The results also clarify the conditions under which quantum chaos and statistical equilibrium emerge, and show that the transition to equilibrium in 3D systems is an extremely sharp function of excitation energy.
