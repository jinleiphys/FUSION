# Suppression of the nuclear rainbow in the inelastic nucleus-nucleus scattering

> The nuclear rainbow observed in the elastic $α$-nucleus and light heavy-ion scattering is proven to be due to the refraction of the scattering wave by a deep, attractive real optical potential. The nuclear rainbow pattern, established as a broad oscillation of the Airy minima in the elastic cross section, originates from an interference of the refracted far-side scattering amplitudes. It is natural to expect a similar rainbow pattern also in the inelastic scattering of a nucleus-nucleus system that exhibits a pronounced rainbow pattern in the elastic channel. Although some feature of the nuclear rainbow in the inelastic nucleus-nucleus scattering was observed in experiment, the measured inelastic cross sections exhibit much weaker rainbow pattern, where the Airy oscillation is suppressed and smeared out. To investigate this effect, a novel method of the near-far decomposition of the inelastic scattering amplitude is proposed to explicitly reveal the coupled partial-wave contributions to the inelastic cross section. Using the new decomposition method, our coupled channel analysis of the elastic and inelastic $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C and $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C scattering at the refractive energies shows unambiguously that the suppression of the nuclear rainbow pattern in the inelastic scattering cross section is caused by a destructive interference of the partial waves of different multipoles. However, the inelastic scattering remains strongly refractive in these cases, where the far-side scattering is dominant at medium and large angles like that observed in the elastic scattering.

# Suppression of the nuclear rainbow in the inelastic nucleus-nucleus scattering

## Key claim

The suppression of the nuclear rainbow pattern (Airy oscillation) in inelastic nucleus-nucleus scattering to excited states with nonzero spin is caused by a destructive interference of the partial waves of different multipoles (K-subamplitudes), not by enhanced absorption in the exit channel as previously assumed. The inelastic scattering remains strongly refractive with far-side dominance at medium and large angles, even though the Airy pattern is smeared out.

## Method

The authors extended Fuller's near-far decomposition method to the inelastic scattering amplitude, allowing explicit separation of near-side and far-side components for each K-subamplitude (where K = L' - L, the change in orbital angular momentum). They performed coupled channel (CC) calculations using the code ECIS97 with double-folding model potentials (CDM3Y3 interaction) and Woods-Saxon imaginary potentials, analyzing elastic and inelastic scattering data for the 12C+12C system at 240 MeV and the 16O+12C system at 200 and 260 MeV, focusing on excitation of the 2+1 state (4.44 MeV) of 12C.

## Key numbers

- 12C+12C at Elab = 240 MeV: first Airy minimum A1 located at θc.m. ≈ 41° in elastic scattering; A1 in partial inelastic cross section with K=0 at θc.m. ≈ 40°, with K=2 at θc.m. ≈ 33°, with K=-2 at θc.m. ≈ 52°
- 16O+12C at Elab = 200 MeV: A1 located at θc.m. ≈ 65° in elastic scattering; A1 in partial inelastic cross section with K=0 at θc.m. ≈ 62°, with K=2 at θc.m. ≈ 49°, with K=-2 at θc.m. ≈ 84°
- 16O+12C at Elab = 260 MeV: A1 located at θc.m. ≈ 50° in elastic scattering; weaker Airy oscillation pattern with no remnant of A1 with K=0 visible in the inelastic cross section
- Best-fit OP volume integrals: 12C+12C at 240 MeV: JR = 336.0 MeV fm3, JW = 117.5 MeV fm3; 16O+12C at 200 MeV: JR = 300.4 MeV fm3, JW = 72.06 MeV fm3; 16O+12C at 260 MeV: JR = 291.8 MeV fm3, JW = 83.92 MeV fm3

## Context

This work extends the nuclear rainbow studies of elastic scattering by Brandan and Satchler (1996, 1997) and Khoa et al. (2007) to inelastic channels, addressing an open question raised by Khoa et al. (2005) about why Airy oscillations are suppressed in inelastic scattering. Previous analyses by Dem'yanova et al. (1992) suggested the suppression was linked to the inelastic form factor shape, while Khoa et al. (2005) attributed it to increased absorption in the exit channel. The present results contradict these earlier interpretations by showing the suppression arises from multipole interference. The generalized near-far decomposition method enables future studies of nuclear rainbow patterns in inelastic scattering to other excited states, such as the 0+2 Hoyle state of 12C (where no suppression occurs due to K=0 only) and the 3-1 state of 12C.
