# Separation of a Slater determinant wave function with a neck structure into spatially localized subsystems

> A method to separate a Slater determinant wave function with a two-center neck structure into spatially localized subsystems is proposed, and its potential applications are presented. An orthonormal set of spatially localized single-particle wave functions is obtained by diagonalizing the coordinate operator for the major axis of a necked system. Using the localized single-particle wave functions, the wave function of each subsystem is defined. Therefore, defined subsystem wave functions are used to obtain density distributions, mass centers, and energies of subsystems. The present method is applied to separations of Margenau--Brink cluster wave functions of $α+ α$, $^{16}$O + $^{16}$O, and $α+ ^{16}$O into their subsystems, and also to separations of antisymmetrized molecular dynamics wave functions of $^{10}$Be into $α$ + $^6$He subsystems. The method is simple and applicable to the separation of general Slater determinant wave functions that have neck structures into subsystem wave functions.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method to separate a Slater determinant wave function with a two-center neck structure into spatially localized subsystem wave functions by diagonalizing the coordinate operator for the major axis, yielding subsystem wave functions with no singularity even when the subsystems overlap.

## Method

The method diagonalizes the norm and coordinate operator \(\hat{z}\) (the major axis) simultaneously on the single-particle wave functions of a Slater determinant, producing an orthonormal set of spatially localized single-particle wave functions with eigenvalues \(z_i\) indicating mean nucleon positions. These single-particle wave functions are then grouped into two subsystems based on the distribution of \(z_i\) values, and subsystem wave functions are constructed as Slater determinants of the grouped orbitals. The method is applied to Margenau-Brink (MB) cluster wave functions for \(\alpha+\alpha\), \(^{16}\mathrm{O}+^{16}\mathrm{O}\), and \(\alpha+^{16}\mathrm{O}\) systems, and to antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) wave functions of \(^{10}\mathrm{Be}\) separated into \(\alpha+^{6}\mathrm{He}\) subsystems.

## Key numbers

- For the \(\alpha+\alpha\) system, the internuclear distance \(R = d / \sqrt{1 - e^{-\nu d^2}}\), where \(d\) is the distance between Gaussian centroids and \(\nu\) is the width parameter; in the \(d \rightarrow 0\) limit, \(R\) tends to \(\nu^{-1/2}\).
- For \(\alpha+\alpha\), the internuclear distance \(R\) is smaller than the sharp-cut distance \(R_\rho\) for \(d \lesssim 2\ \nu^{-1/2}\); at \(d=0\), \(R = R_\mathrm{S}/\sqrt{2}\) compared to the S-method distance \(R_\mathrm{S}\).
- For \(\alpha+^{16}\mathrm{O}\), the internuclear distance \(R\) is smaller than \(R_\rho\) for \(d \lesssim 6\) fm; clusters are in the ground state for \(R \gtrsim 6\) fm.
- For \(^{16}\mathrm{O}+^{16}\mathrm{O}\), the internuclear distance \(R\) is slightly smaller than \(R_\rho\) and has a finite value at \(d=0\); clusters are in the ground state for \(R \gtrsim 7\) fm.
- Width parameter \(\nu\) is set to 0.27 fm\(^{-2}\) for \(\alpha+\alpha\) calculations and 0.16 fm\(^{-2}\) for systems involving \(^{16}\mathrm{O}\).

## Context

This work extends the S method by Saraceno et al. (1983), which defined canonical variables for \(\alpha-\alpha\) systems but did not provide subsystem wave functions, and the YG method by Younes and Gogny (2009), which localizes quasi-particle states but requires determining a neck position with potential ambiguities. The proposed method enables microscopic calculation of subsystem properties (density distributions, mass centers, energies) without singularities, applicable to systems with open-shell clusters or neutron excess, and can be used in Hartree-Fock, AMD, and other Slater-determinant-based approaches for studying cluster structures and nuclear reactions.
