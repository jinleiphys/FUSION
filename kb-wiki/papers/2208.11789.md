# How many quantum gates do gauge theories require?

> We discuss the implementation of lattice gauge theories on digital quantum computers, focusing primarily on the number of quantum gates required to simulate their time evolution. We find that to compile quantum circuits, using available state-of-the-art methods with our own augmentations, the cost of a single time step of an elementary plaquette is beyond what is reasonably practical in the current era of quantum hardware. However, we observe that such costs are highly sensitive to the truncation scheme used to derive different Hamiltonian formulations of non-Abelian gauge theories, emphasizing the need for low-dimensional truncations of such models in the same universality class as the desired theories.

## Key claim

The paper finds that compiling a single time step of an elementary plaquette for SU(2) lattice gauge theory requires a number of quantum gates that is beyond what is reasonably practical on current quantum hardware, but emphasizes that this cost is highly sensitive to the truncation scheme used to derive Hamiltonian formulations of non-Abelian gauge theories.

## Method

The authors develop a quantum circuit compilation method that combines Trotterization, decomposition of the Hamiltonian into Pauli strings, partitioning of Pauli strings into commuting clusters via graph coloring, simultaneous diagonalization of each cluster using the tableau formalism, and a novel binary-tree-based algorithm for exponentiating diagonal Pauli strings that shares CNOT gates across strings within a cluster. They apply this method to two qubitizations of SU(2) gauge theory with four- and five-dimensional Hilbert spaces per link, which preserve exact SU(2) gauge invariance.

## Key numbers

- For the four-dimensional Hilbert space model, one time evolution step involves 180 CNOT gates per plaquette.
- For the five-dimensional Hilbert space model, one time evolution step involves 17,168 CNOT gates per plaquette.
- The Trotterization incurs an error of O(δt) due to nonzero commutators among Hamiltonian terms, and an additional error of O(‖[H_E, H_O]‖δt²) when splitting the Hamiltonian into even and odd commuting sets of plaquette terms.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to implement lattice gauge theories on digital quantum computers, including qubitization proposals by Alexandru (2019), Singh (2019), and Bhattacharya (2020), as well as earlier truncation schemes by Mathur (2004) and Byrnes (2005). The large gate counts for the five-dimensional model demonstrate the critical importance of finding low-dimensional truncations that lie in the same universality class as the target continuum theory, and the results motivate further search for simpler qubitizations of SU(2) gauge theory that could substantially reduce gate requirements.
