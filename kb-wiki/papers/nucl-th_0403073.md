# Nuclear collective processes study with attosecond laser pulses

> In this paper the possibility of the excitation of collective nuclear motion with attosecond laser pulses is investigated. Following our earlier results (Lasers in Engineering 11 (2001) p. 259) the hyperbolic heat transport for nuclear matter is formulated and solved. It is shown that in the vicinity of the 30 MeV excitation energy the recollided electrons can excite the giant collective motions - thermal wave inside the nuclei. Key words: Attosecond laser pulses; Electronuclear reactions; Thermal waves; Giant Resonances.

# Nuclear collective processes study with attosecond laser pulses

## Key claim

The paper claims that attosecond laser pulses can excite giant collective nuclear motions (thermal waves) in the vicinity of 30 MeV excitation energy, but this is conditional on the recollided electrons having sufficient energy (approximately 30 MeV), which occurs only for ions with atomic number Z ≈ 20.

## Method

The authors formulate and solve a relativistic hyperbolic heat transport equation (RQHT) for nuclear matter, extending their earlier damped thermal wave model. They derive the forced Klein-Gordon and telegraph equations as special cases of the transport equation, and solve the initial-boundary value problem for the inhomogeneous thermal wave equation in a semi-infinite interval. The key parameters are the strong coupling constant α_s = 0.15 for the thermal wave velocity and the nucleon mass to determine the relaxation time.

## Key numbers

- Strong coupling constant α_s = 0.15, used to set thermal wave velocity v = α_s c for nuclear collective motion
- Nucleon mass m = 981 MeV/c², used in the calculation of the potential V
- Potential V = ℏ/(8τ) ≈ 30 MeV, derived from the distortionless wave condition q = 0
- 30 MeV energy range identified as the location of giant dipole resonances in nuclear matter
- Attosecond laser pulse duration: 1 as = 10⁻¹⁸ s
- Recollision electron energy of approximately 30 MeV occurs for atomic number Z ≈ 20

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier hyperbolic heat transport formulation for nuclear matter (Marciak-Kozlowska and Kozlowski, Lasers in Engineering, 2001) and builds on a laser configuration proposed by other authors (2004) that enables relativistic electron recollision using counterpropagating circularly polarized beams. The paper connects attosecond laser-induced electron recollision physics to nuclear giant resonance excitation, specifically the giant dipole resonance at 30 MeV, and references experimental observations of mass-proportional temperatures in ultrahigh energy heavy ion reactions at CERN. The work opens the possibility of attosecond nuclear spectroscopy to study decay and damping of giant resonances, as well as fundamental nuclear properties such as dissipation and viscosity.
