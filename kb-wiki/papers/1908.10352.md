# Stringent constraints on neutron-star radii from multimessenger observations and nuclear theory

> The properties of neutron stars are determined by the nature of the matter that they contain. These properties can be constrained by measurements of the star's size. We obtain stringent constraints on neutron-star radii by combining multimessenger observations of the binary neutron-star merger GW170817 with nuclear theory that best accounts for density-dependent uncertainties in the equation of state. We construct equations of state constrained by chiral effective field theory and marginalize over these using the gravitational-wave observations. Combining this with the electromagnetic observations of the merger remnant that imply the presence of a short-lived hyper-massive neutron star, we find that the radius of a $1.4\,\rm{M}_\odot$ neutron star is $R_{1.4\,\mathrm{M}_\odot} = 11.0^{+0.9}_{-0.6}~{\rm km}$ (90% credible interval). Using this constraint, we show that neutron stars are unlikely to be disrupted in neutron-star black-hole mergers; subsequently, such events will not produce observable electromagnetic emission.

## Key claim

By combining gravitational-wave data from GW170817 with chiral effective field theory constraints on the equation of state and electromagnetic observations of the merger remnant, the authors find that the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 11.0^{+0.9}_{-0.6} km (90% credible interval), but this result depends on the assumption that chiral effective field theory remains valid up to twice nuclear saturation density; if it is only valid up to saturation density, larger radii are possible but are excluded by the tidal polarizability constraint from GW170817.

## Method

The authors constructed two families of neutron-star equations of state using chiral effective field theory calculations of neutron matter, constrained either up to nuclear saturation density or up to twice nuclear saturation density, and extended them to higher densities using a general speed-of-sound parameterization that allows for phase transitions. They used Bayesian parameter estimation with Markov-chain Monte Carlo and nested sampling to compute posterior probabilities for the tidal polarizability of GW170817, sampling directly over individual equations of state rather than over tidal parameters. They then applied additional constraints from the electromagnetic counterparts: the requirement that the total binary mass exceeded the threshold for prompt collapse to a black hole, and an upper limit on the maximum neutron-star mass of 2.3 solar masses inferred from the absence of a long-lived remnant.

## Key numbers

- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: R_{1.4 M_sun} = 11.0^{+0.9}_{-0.6} km (90% credible interval)
- Pressure at four times nuclear saturation density: P_{4 n_sat} = 161^{+58}_{-46} MeV/fm^3
- Maximum pressure inside neutron stars: P_max <= 890 MeV/fm^3
- Upper limit on maximum neutron-star mass from electromagnetic constraints: M_max < 2.3 M_sun
- Threshold mass for prompt collapse depends on radius via M_thresh approx M_max (2.380 - 3.606 G M_max / (c^2 R_{1.6 M_sun})) +/- 0.05 M_sun
- For neutron-star-black-hole mergers with a 10 M_sun black hole and 1.4 M_sun neutron star at the upper bound of tidal polarizability (Lambda = 370), the Bayes factor comparing to a binary black hole model is approximately 1, meaning the models are indistinguishable even at signal-to-noise ratio of 190
- Non-spinning black holes must have mass below 3.4 M_sun to disrupt a 1.4 M_sun neutron star

## Context

This work extends previous analyses of GW170817 that either neglected nuclear physics constraints or used parameterizations informed by many nuclear models (De 2018, Abbott 2018), by incorporating chiral effective field theory uncertainties in a systematic way for the first time. The results improve upon earlier radius constraints from Bauswein et al. (2017) who used similar prompt-collapse arguments but required equation-of-state-independent causality assumptions, and from Margalit and Metzger (2017) who placed upper limits on the maximum mass. The constraints imply that neutron-star-black-hole mergers with typical masses will not produce observable electromagnetic emission, which has direct implications for interpreting events like S190814bv reported by LIGO and Virgo.
