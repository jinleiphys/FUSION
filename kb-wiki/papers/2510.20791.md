# A Microphysical Probe of Neutron Star Interiors: Constraining the Equation of State with Glitch Dynamics

> Glitches in neutron stars originate from the sudden transfer of angular momentum between superfluid components and the observable crust. By modeling this glitch dynamics, including vortex motion, mutual friction, and angular momentum exchange, we can probe the dense matter equation of state. We match theoretical predictions of glitch rise times, overshoot patterns, and relaxation timescales to the well-documented observations of the 2016 Vela glitch. Our model incorporates microphysical parameters such as the mutual friction coefficient $\mathcal{B}$, which in the core arises from electron scattering off magnetized vortices, and in the crust from Kelvin wave excitation during vortex-lattice interactions. Our Markov Chain Monte Carlo analysis of the timing residuals reveals detailed glitch dynamics: the crustal superfluid couples on timescales of $\sim100$ seconds, the core exhibits overshoot behavior due to strong central behavior, and the inner crust shows weak entrainment, with $\sim70\%$ of free neutrons remaining superfluid. The modeled rise times are consistent with the observed upper limit of 12.6 seconds, and the observed overshoot requires strong crustal friction but weak core friction, supporting a spatially varying $\mathcal{B}$.These findings highlight the importance of microphysical modeling and demonstrate the potential of future high-cadence timing observations to further constrain the internal dynamics and composition of neutron stars.

# A Microphysical Probe of Neutron Star Interiors: Constraining the Equation of State with Glitch Dynamics

## Key claim

The observed overshoot in the 2016 Vela glitch requires strong crustal mutual friction but weak core mutual friction, supporting a spatially varying mutual friction coefficient $\mathcal{B}$; the modeled rise times are consistent with the observed upper limit of 12.6 seconds, but only under specific combinations of microphysical parameters including the symmetry energy slope, entrainment factor, and neutron star mass.

## Method

The authors constructed unified equations of state using the relativistic mean field (RMF) framework with DD-ME2 and PKDD effective interactions, varying the symmetry energy slope $L_0$ from 30 to 80 MeV. They calculated neutron pairing gaps via the BCS approximation with a scaling factor $\beta = 3.0$ to account for medium polarization, then computed pinning energies in the inner crust using a semiclassical method comparing vortex-on-nucleus and vortex-between-nuclei configurations. The mutual friction coefficients in the crust were derived from Kelvin wave excitation dynamics following Epstein and Baym (1992), while core mutual friction was modeled via electron scattering off magnetized vortices. These microphysical inputs were integrated into an extended three-component glitch model, and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) analysis was performed on the timing residuals of the 2016 Vela glitch to infer key neutron star parameters.

## Key numbers

- The 2016 Vela glitch fractional frequency change: $\Delta\nu/\nu = 1.431 \times 10^{-6}$
- Observed upper limit on glitch rise time: 12.6 seconds
- Crustal superfluid couples on timescales of $\sim 100$ seconds
- Approximately 70% of free neutrons in the inner crust remain superfluid (median entrainment factor $f_e \sim 0.7$)
- Crustal mutual friction coefficient $\mathcal{B}_{\text{crust}}$ ranges from $10^{-4}$ to $10^{-1}$
- Core mutual friction coefficient $\mathcal{B}_{\text{core}}$ ranges from $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$
- Pinning energy $E_p$ ranges from $-1$ to $+10$ MeV (exceeding 100 MeV for $L_0 = 30$ MeV)
- Symmetry energy slope $L_0$ values considered: 30, 40, 60, and 80 MeV
- Maximum neutron star masses: 2.497 $M_\odot$ (DD-ME2) and 2.356 $M_\odot$ (PKDD) for original interactions
- Vela pulsar mass constrained to approximately 1.25–1.45 $M_\odot$ from maximum observed glitch

## Context

This work extends the three-component glitch modeling approach of Graber et al. (2018), who simulated the overshoot and relaxation of the 2016 Vela glitch using density-dependent crustal mutual friction coefficients, and Montoli et al. (2020), who performed Bayesian estimation of superfluid moments of inertia from the same glitch. The authors build on their own previous work (Tu and Li, 2025) which constrained the symmetry energy slope $L_0$ to below 40 MeV using self-consistent calculations of crustal pinning forces applied to the 2000 Vela glitch. The results demonstrate that future high-cadence timing observations can further constrain the internal dynamics and composition of neutron stars, particularly by distinguishing between electron scattering and vortex-fluxtube pinning as the dominant core friction mechanism.
