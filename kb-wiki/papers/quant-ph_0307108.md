# Renormalisation and fixed points in Hilbert Space

> The energies of low-lying bound states of a microscopic quantum many-body system of particles can be worked out in a reduced Hilbert space. We present here and test a specific non-perturbative truncation procedure. We also show that real exceptional points which may be present in the spectrum can be identified as fixed points of coupling constants in the truncation procedure.

# Renormalisation and fixed points in Hilbert Space

## Key claim
The authors present a non-perturbative truncation procedure for reducing the Hilbert space dimension in quantum many-body bound-state problems, and claim that real exceptional points where perturbation expansions diverge can be identified as fixed points of the coupling constant in the renormalisation scheme. The claim is conditional on the existence of level crossings that produce degeneracies in the spectrum, which occur only in specific situations such as systems with special symmetry properties or infinite systems.

## Method
The authors develop a renormalisation algorithm that systematically reduces the Hilbert space dimension by projecting out one state at a time using the Feshbach formalism, imposing the constraint that the lowest eigenvalue remains unchanged at each step. Starting from an initial Hamiltonian \( H = H_0 + g H_1 \) in a space of dimension \( N \), they eliminate the highest-energy basis state to obtain an effective Hamiltonian in dimension \( N-1 \), solving a quadratic equation for the renormalised coupling constant \( g^{(N-1)} \). The process is iterated step by step, generating a sequence of coupling constants \( g^{(k)} \) as the space is reduced. The method is tested on a real symmetric tight-binding Hamiltonian with \( H_0 = 0 \), diagonal elements \( \beta = 1 \), and off-diagonal elements \( \gamma = 0.5 \).

## Key numbers
- For initial dimension \( N = 10 \) and initial coupling \( g = 20 \), the lowest eigenvalue \( \lambda_1 \) changes from 0.81 (full space) to 1.10 (reduced to \( n = 5 \)), with the coupling constant renormalising to \( g = 8.18 \).
- For \( N = 20 \) and \( g = 20 \), \( \lambda_1 \) changes from 0.22 (full space) to 0.30 (reduced to \( n = 5 \)), with \( g \) renormalising to 1.13.
- For \( N = 50 \) and \( g = 20 \), \( \lambda_1 \) changes from 0.04 (full space) to 0.05 (reduced to \( n = 5 \)), with \( g \) renormalising to 0.38.
- For \( N = 20 \) and initial \( g = 1.0 \), \( \lambda_1 \) changes from 0.01 (full space) to 0.015 (reduced to \( n = 5 \)), with \( g \) renormalising to 0.11.
- In the fixed-point example with equal diagonal elements \( H_{ii} = -0.5 \) and equal off-diagonal elements \( H_{ij} = +0.5 \), the lowest eigenstates remain rigorously stable at \(-20\) and the coupling constant stays at \( g = 20 \) for any value of \( N \).

## Context
This work extends earlier renormalisation concepts applied to many-body problems by Glazek and Wilson (1993), Rau (1994), and Mueller (1995), and addresses the breakdown of perturbation methods in strongly coupled systems such as atomic nuclei. The authors build on the Feshbach projection formalism (Feshbach, 1962; Bloch, 1958) and the rigorous analytic structure of eigenvalue functions established by Kato (1966) and Heiss (1990). The approach enables non-perturbative treatment of systems where standard perturbation expansions diverge, particularly at exceptional points associated with level crossings, and can be extended to systems with multiple coupling constants and finite temperature.
