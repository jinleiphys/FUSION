# Squeezing the Efimov effect

> The quantum mechanical three-body problem is a source of continuing interest due to its complexity and not least due to the presence of fascinating solvable cases. The prime example is the Efimov effect where infinitely many bound states of identical bosons can arise at the threshold where the two-body problem has zero binding energy. An important aspect of the Efimov effect is the effect of spatial dimensionality; it has been observed in three dimensional systems, yet it is believed to be impossible in two dimensions. Using modern experimental techniques, it is possible to engineer trap geometry and thus address the intricate nature of quantum few-body physics as function of dimensionality. Here we present a framework for studying the three-body problem as one (continuously) changes the dimensionality of the system all the way from three, through two, and down to a single dimension. This is done by considering the Efimov favorable case of a mass-imbalanced system and with an external confinement provided by a typical experimental case with a (deformed) harmonic trap.

## Key claim
The Efimov effect, which produces infinitely many three-body bound states in three dimensions (3D) and is absent in two dimensions (2D), can be continuously studied across dimensional crossover by applying external harmonic confinement to a mass-imbalanced AAB system. The number of bound states reduces from five in 3D to four in 2D and further to three in 1D for the mass ratio m_B/m_A = 6/133, with the spectral flow showing either abrupt jumps (constant two-body energy) or smoother transitions (varying two-body energy).

## Method
The authors solve the three-body problem using momentum-space integral Faddeev equations modified to include periodic boundary conditions along one or two spatial directions, effectively compactifying those dimensions on a ring of radius R. They model the AB subsystem with a short-range Gaussian potential tuned to reproduce a fixed 3D scattering length a_3D near resonance, and relate the harmonic oscillator length b to the compactification radius R via the two-body energy matching condition b ≈ 2πR. The system is squeezed from 3D to 2D by decreasing b_y, then from 2D to 1D by further decreasing b_x, using the mass ratio m_B/m_A = 6/133 relevant for ⁶Li-¹³³Cs mixtures.

## Key numbers
- Five Efimov trimers are numerically resolved in the 3D limit for a_3D/r_0 ≈ 10⁵, scaling in energy with e^(2π/s) where the Efimov scaling factor e^(π/s) = 4.788 for the mass ratio m_B/m_A = 6/133.
- Four states survive in the strict 2D limit, and three states survive in the 1D limit for the same mass ratio.
- The two-body energy at b_y = r_0 is approximately E₂/E₂^(2D)(b_y=r_0) ≈ 0.05, independent of a_3D for a_3D/r_0 ≫ 1.
- The first spectral jump in the constant-E₂ case occurs around b_y ∼ a_3D, with subsequent jumps following Efimov scaling at b_y ∼ a_3D/e^(Nπ/s).
- In the 2D-to-1D transition, the center of the energy drop occurs around b_x/R_rms⁰ ∼ 10 for all surviving states, where R_rms⁰ = 0.4742 b = 0.2101 ħ/√(m_A E_AB(2D)).

## Context
This work extends the theoretical prediction by Nielsen et al. (2001) that Efimov trimers of identical bosons exist only for dimension d in the interval 2.3 < d < 3.8, providing a practical framework to study continuous dimensional crossover using experimentally realizable harmonic traps. It builds on prior studies of Efimov trimers under confinement by Levinsen et al. (2009) for fermions in quasi-2D and Nishida and Tan (2008) for mixed-dimensional confinement, but is the first to treat continuous squeezing from 3D to 2D to 1D with non-identical particles. The results enable experimental verification through recombination rate studies or radio-frequency association in cold-atom systems such as ⁶Li-¹³³Cs mixtures, and the formalism can be extended to other confinement geometries and mass ratios.
