# Nucleosynthesis Basics and Applications to Supernovae

> This review concentrates on nucleosynthesis processes in general and their applications to massive stars and supernovae. A brief initial introduction is given to the physics in astrophysical plasmas which governs composition changes. We present the basic equations for thermonuclear reaction rates and nuclear reaction networks. The required nuclear physics input for reaction rates is discussed, i.e. cross sections for nuclear reactions, photodisintegrations, electron and positron captures, neutrino captures, inelastic neutrino scattering, and beta-decay half-lives. We examine especially the present state of uncertainties in predicting thermonuclear reaction rates, while the status of experiments is discussed by others in this volume (see M. Wiescher). It follows a brief review of hydrostatic burning stages in stellar evolution before discussing the fate of massive stars, i.e. the nucleosynthesis in type II supernova explosions (SNe II). Except for SNe Ia, which are explained by exploding white dwarfs in binary stellar systems (which will not be discussed here), all other supernova types seem to be linked to the gravitational collapse of massive stars (M$>$8M$_\odot$) at the end of their hydrostatic evolution. SN1987A, the first type II supernova for which the progenitor star was known, is used as an example for nucleosynthesis calculations. Finally, we discuss the production of heavy elements in the r-process up to Th and U and its possible connection to supernovae.

## Key claim

This review paper provides a comprehensive overview of nucleosynthesis processes and their application to massive stars and supernovae, with the caveat that the authors focus on theoretical predictions of thermonuclear reaction rates while noting that experimental status is discussed separately by M. Wiescher in the same volume. The paper specifically excludes Type Ia supernovae (exploding white dwarfs in binary systems) and concentrates on gravitational collapse supernovae from stars with masses greater than 8 solar masses.

## Method

The authors present the fundamental equations for thermonuclear reaction rates in astrophysical plasmas, including Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions for nuclei, photodisintegration cross sections integrated over Planck distributions, electron and positron capture rates integrated over Fermi distributions, and beta-decay half-lives. They describe the construction of nuclear reaction networks using fully implicit time integration with multi-dimensional Newton-Raphson iteration to solve the stiff set of differential equations for abundance changes. For theoretical cross section predictions, the authors employ Hauser-Feshbach statistical model calculations with optical potentials, level density descriptions, and width fluctuation corrections, using global parameter predictions applicable to unstable nuclei where experimental data are unavailable.

## Key numbers

- Stars with masses greater than 8 solar masses (M > 8 M_sun) undergo gravitational collapse at the end of hydrostatic evolution, producing Type II supernovae
- Electron screening factors can enhance reactions by many orders of magnitude at high densities and low temperatures, leading to pycnonuclear ignition
- The electron capture rate depends on electron number density n_e = Y_e * rho * N_A, where Y_e = sum_i Z_i Y_i
- At high densities above rho > 10^12 g cm^-3, neutrino scattering cross sections on nuclei and electrons become sufficient to thermalize a neutrino distribution
- The most recent level density treatment reduces average cross section uncertainty to a factor of about 1.4 (average deviation of about 40% from experiments), compared to earlier uncertainties of a factor of 5 at the neutron separation energy
- Previous cross section uncertainties amounted to a factor of 2-3, while earlier level density predictions gave uncertainties beyond a factor of 10 (Gil & Cameron 1965), about a factor of 8 (Woosley et al. 1978), and a factor of 5 (Thielemann et al. 1987)
- The gamma-transmission coefficient formula includes a neutron-proton exchange contribution chi = 0.2

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on thermonuclear reaction rates by Fowler, Caughlan, and Zimmerman (1967, 1975), Clayton (1983), and Rolfs and Rodney (1988), and builds upon the nucleosynthesis calculations for supernovae by Thielemann, Nomoto, and Hashimoto (1994). The paper enables improved predictions for explosive nucleosynthesis in Type II supernovae and the r-process production of heavy elements up to thorium and uranium, using SN1987A as a test case. The authors identify that the major remaining uncertainty in statistical model calculations stems from nuclear level density predictions, and they present a novel energy-dependent level density treatment that reduces uncertainties to approximately 40% average deviation from experiments.
