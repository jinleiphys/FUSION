# A microscopic model for spontaneous fission: validity of the adiabatic approximation

> We investigate microscopically the tunneling dynamics in spontaneous fission of atomic nuclei. To this end, we employ a schematic solvable model with a pairing-plus-quadrupole interaction. The spontaneous decay of a system is simulated by introducing a small imaginary part to the energy of a fission doorway state. We show that the many-body Hamiltonian can be reduced to an effective 2$\times$2 Hamiltonian, from which one can derive a simple approximate formula for the decay width. We particularly investigate the applicability of the adiabatic approximation, which has often been used in the literature. With typical value of the parameters, we find that the adiabatic approximation works within a factor of around 5 for the decay width.

## Key claim

The adiabatic approximation, which is commonly used in microscopic calculations for nuclear fission, may underestimate the spontaneous fission decay width by orders of magnitude depending on the number of orbital transitions, even though with typical parameter values it works within a factor of around 5 for the specific (6,6) model space.

## Method

The authors constructed a schematic solvable model with a pairing-plus-quadrupole interaction for 6 particles in 6 orbitals, grouped into three orbitals favoring the ground-state shape and three favoring the scission configuration. They introduced a small imaginary part to the energy of a fission doorway state to simulate spontaneous decay, and reduced the many-body Hamiltonian to an effective 2x2 Hamiltonian from which a simple approximate formula for the decay width could be derived. The adiabatic approximation was implemented by first diagonalizing the model Hamiltonian within subspaces of fixed quadrupole moment Q and then taking only the lowest energy state of each Q value, reducing the dimension from 20 to 4 configurations.

## Key numbers

* For the (6,6) model with barrier height V_B = 6 epsilon_0, pairing strength G/epsilon_0 = 0.691, and BCS gap Delta_BCS/epsilon_0 = 1.5, the exact effective coupling strength squared is v_eff^2 = 0.121, while the adiabatic approximation gives (v_ad_eff)^2 = 0.0253, yielding a ratio of 0.209.
* For the (8,8) model with 70 configurations, V_B = 6 epsilon_0, and Delta_BCS/epsilon_0 = 1.5, the ratio of adiabatic to exact coupling strength squared is 2.32 x 10^{-2}.
* For the (10,10) model with 252 configurations, V_B = 6 epsilon_0, and Delta_BCS/epsilon_0 = 1.5, the ratio of adiabatic to exact coupling strength squared is 2.22 x 10^{-3}.
* With the offset Delta = 0.1 epsilon_0 and doorway width Gamma_d = 1.2 epsilon_0, the adiabatic approximation suppresses the decay width by almost a factor of 4, with a suppression factor S = 4.79 extracted from the perturbative estimate.

## Context

This work extends a model previously applied to induced fission by Bertsch (2019) and applies it to spontaneous fission with adapted Hamiltonian parameters. The authors test the adiabatic approximation used in constrained Hartree-Fock calculations for fission potential surfaces, as employed by Goutte (2005), Warda (2012), Staszczak (2013), Schunck (2016), and Nakatsukasa (2016). The findings suggest that for realistic actinide nuclei requiring approximately 20 intermediate states, the adiabatic approximation may underestimate decay rates by many orders of magnitude, motivating the development of alternative approaches such as least-action treatments that artificially increase pairing strength in intermediate-state wave functions.
