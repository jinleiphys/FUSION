# Some nuclear physics aspects of core-collapse supernovae

> Here I review some nuclear physics aspects of core-collapse supernovae concerning neutrinos. Studies of neutrino emission and interactions in supernovae are crucial to our understanding of the explosion mechanism, the heavy element nucleosynthesis, and pulsar proper motions. I discuss the effects of reduced neutrino opacities in dense nuclear matter on supernova neutrino emission and their implications for the delayed supernova explosion mechanism and the synthesis of neutron-rich heavy elements. I also discuss the effects of parity violation in weak interactions on supernova neutrino emission and their implications for pulsar proper motions.

## Key claim

Reduced neutrino opacities in dense nuclear matter, due to frequent nucleon-nucleon interactions, are expected to increase neutrino luminosities and average energies while decreasing the duration of neutrino emission, which may make the delayed supernova explosion mechanism occur more easily; however, the actual impact depends on implementing these new opacities in supernova codes, not on tuning free parameters.

## Method

The paper reviews the effects of reduced neutrino opacities in dense nuclear matter on supernova neutrino emission, using the delayed supernova explosion mechanism as a framework. It analyzes the time evolution of mean neutrino energies for \(\nu_e\) and \(\bar\nu_e\) from Jim Wilson's supernova model, and relates these to the electron fraction \(Y_e\) in supernova ejecta for heavy element nucleosynthesis. It also examines parity violation in weak interactions, specifically neutrino scattering on polarized neutrons in a magnetic field, to estimate the resulting asymmetry in neutrino emission and its implications for pulsar kick velocities.

## Key numbers

- Gravitational binding energy of the final neutron star: \(E_B \sim 3 \times 10^{53}\) erg, with mass \(M \sim 1 M_\odot\) and radius \(R \sim 10\) km.
- Total kinetic energy of supernova debris: \(\sim 10^{51}\) erg, which is \(\sim 1\%\) of the total energy emitted in \(\nu_e\) and \(\bar\nu_e\).
- Average pulsar kick velocity: \(v_{\rm kick} \approx 450\) km/s, corresponding to a kick momentum \(P_{\rm kick} \sim 10^{41}\) g cm/s; the sum of neutrino momentum magnitude is \(E_B/c \sim 10^{43}\) g cm/s.
- Neutron-proton mass difference: \(\Delta \approx 1.3\) MeV; \(\epsilon_{\bar\nu_e}\) must exceed \(\epsilon_{\nu_e}\) by about \(4\Delta \approx 5.2\) MeV to drive ejecta neutron-rich.
- Neutron spin polarization parameter: \(\beta_{\rm out} \sim P_n \approx 6 \times 10^{-6} \left( B / 10^{13}\ {\rm G} \right) \left( 10\ {\rm MeV} / T \right)\).
- Estimated asymmetry in neutrino emission: \(A \sim 0.006 \left( \alpha / 0.1 \right) \left( B / 10^{13}\ {\rm G} \right) \left( 10\ {\rm MeV} / T \right)\), where \(\alpha \sim 0.1\) is a geometric factor.

## Context

This review extends the work of Jim Wilson on the delayed supernova explosion mechanism (Bethe, 1990) and incorporates recent studies by Sawyer, Keil, and Reddy (1990s) on reduced neutrino opacities in dense nuclear matter. It also builds on the analysis of Horowitz and Li (1990s) regarding parity violation in neutrino scattering and its cumulative effect on pulsar kick velocities. The paper enables future tests of nuclear medium effects on neutrino opacities through supernova neutrino detection, such as with Super Kamiokande, and motivates systematic studies of parity violation effects on neutrino transport in neutron star cooling (Lai, 1990s).
