# Frontiers in Nuclear, Heavy Ion and Strong Field Physics

> An introduction describing Walter Greiner's scientific life for the topical volume to be published by EPJA

## Key claim

This paper is an introduction to a topical issue of the *European Physical Journal A* dedicated to Walter Greiner; it does not present a new scientific claim but rather summarizes his scientific legacy, including his role in establishing heavy-ion physics, strong-field QED, and super-heavy element research.

## Method

The paper is a biographical and historical overview of Walter Greiner's career, describing his contributions to nuclear and heavy-ion physics, his educational initiatives (including the "Frankfurt School" of Theoretical Physics and a multi-volume textbook series), and his role in founding research institutions such as GSI (the Helmholtz Center for Heavy-Ion Research) and FIAS (the Frankfurt Institute for Advanced Studies). It also recounts his efforts in fostering international scientific collaborations, particularly between the United States, Germany, and Israel.

## Key numbers

- More than 1000 published articles across a wide variety of fields.
- Eleven honorary doctorates from universities worldwide.
- More than 150 students earned their doctorates under his tutelage; more than 40 became leading scholars and researchers.
- The textbook series on Theoretical Physics consists of more than 15 volumes, translated into several languages.
- The heavy-ion accelerator UNILAC at GSI was completed in 1975; the laboratory has now expanded into the international Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research (FAIR).
- The international race for super-heavy elements has reached element 118.
- The special volume comprises 18 high quality and fully refereed research papers.

## Context

This paper introduces a topical issue of the *European Physical Journal A* (February 2018) dedicated to Walter Greiner, extending the biographical and scientific record of his work. It describes his pioneering role in establishing heavy-ion physics as an independent field, his contributions to strong-field QED (including positron production by vacuum decay), and his predictions of shock waves and collective flow in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The paper enables future work by situating Greiner's legacy within the broader context of nuclear physics, from super-heavy element research (reaching element 118) to the study of the quark-gluon plasma at facilities such as GSI/FAIR, Brookhaven-RHIC, and CERN-LHC.
