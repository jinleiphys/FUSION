# Nuclear transparency and effective kaon-nucleon cross section from the A(e, e'K+) reaction

> We have determined the transparency of the nuclear medium to kaons from $A(e,e^{'} K^{+})$ measurements on $^{12}$C, $^{63}$Cu, and $^{197}$Au targets. The measurements were performed at the Jefferson Laboratory and span a range in four-momentum-transfer squared Q$^2$=1.1 -- 3.0 GeV$^2$. The nuclear transparency was defined as the ratio of measured kaon electroproduction cross sections with respect to deuterium, ($σ^{A}/σ^{D}$). We further extracted the atomic number ($A$) dependence of the transparency as parametrized by $T= (A/2)^{α-1}$ and, within a simple model assumption, the in-medium effective kaon-nucleon cross sections. The effective cross sections extracted from the electroproduction data are found to be smaller than the free cross sections determined from kaon-nucleon scattering experiments, and the parameter $α$ was found to be significantly larger than those obtained from kaon-nucleus scattering. We have included similar comparisons between pion- and proton-nucleon effective cross sections as determined from electron scattering experiments, and pion-nucleus and proton-nucleus scattering data.

## Key claim

The nuclear transparency of kaons extracted from electroproduction data is found to be substantially smaller than the free kaon-nucleon cross sections, and the parameter alpha describing the atomic number dependence is significantly larger than values obtained from kaon-nucleus scattering, contrary to traditional nuclear physics expectations; however, the relatively large experimental uncertainties and the lack of energy dependence in the nuclear transparency do not allow strong conclusions regarding Quantum Chromodynamic effects such as the formation of compact states.

## Method

The experiment measured the A(e,e'K+) reaction on 12C, 63Cu, and 197Au targets at Jefferson Laboratory Hall C using the short orbit spectrometer (SOS) for scattered electrons and the high momentum spectrometer (HMS) for electroproduced kaons, with particle identification via gas Cerenkov counters, aerogel Cerenkov counters, and lead glass calorimeters. Nuclear transparency was defined as the ratio of measured kaon electroproduction cross sections on heavy nuclei relative to deuterium, corrected for Fermi motion, off-shell effects, and spectrometer acceptances using the SIMC Monte Carlo simulation with Independent Particle Shell Model spectral functions. The effective kaon-nucleon cross sections were extracted by fitting the measured transparencies to a classical attenuation model assuming density-independent effective cross sections.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear transparency for 12C relative to deuterium: 0.91 +/- 0.10 (stat) +/- 0.17 (sys) at Q2 = 1.1 GeV2; 1.06 +/- 0.09 +/- 0.11 at Q2 = 2.2 GeV2; 0.91 +/- 0.11 +/- 0.12 at Q2 = 3.0 GeV2
- Nuclear transparency for 63Cu relative to deuterium: 0.62 +/- 0.06 +/- 0.10 at Q2 = 1.1 GeV2; 0.74 +/- 0.05 +/- 0.06 at Q2 = 2.2 GeV2; 0.79 +/- 0.08 +/- 0.11 at Q2 = 3.0 GeV2
- Nuclear transparency for 197Au relative to deuterium: 0.43 +/- 0.04 +/- 0.05 at Q2 = 1.1 GeV2; 0.60 +/- 0.06 +/- 0.05 at Q2 = 2.2 GeV2; 0.63 +/- 0.09 +/- 0.08 at Q2 = 3.0 GeV2
- Scale factor for free kaon-nucleon cross section to match extracted effective cross section: 0.65 +/- 0.14
- Scale factors for comparison: protons 0.68 +/- 0.03, pions 0.81 +/- 0.03
- Q2 range of measurements: 1.1 to 3.0 GeV2

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of hadron propagation in nuclei using quasi-free electron scattering, which previously examined proton propagation (Bates, NE18, JLab experiments) and pion propagation (JLab experiments). The results contradict expectations from traditional nuclear physics models that kaons, introducing a strangeness degree of freedom, would show smaller medium modifications than protons or pions. The findings are compared to kaon-nucleus scattering experiments (Carroll et al.) and heavy-ion collision data (SIS experiments), and the authors suggest that a quark-meson coupling model calculation (Sibirtsev et al.) that successfully described heavy-ion kaon production should be extended to these electroproduction results.
