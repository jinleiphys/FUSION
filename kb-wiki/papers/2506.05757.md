# Pathfinding Quantum Simulations of Neutrinoless Double-Beta Decay

> We present results from co-designed quantum simulations of the neutrinoless double-beta decay of a simple nucleus in 1+1D quantum chromodynamics using IonQ's Forte-generation trapped-ion quantum computers. Electrons, neutrinos, and up and down quarks are distributed across two lattice sites and mapped to 32 qubits, with an additional 4 qubits used for flag-based error mitigation. A four-fermion interaction is used to implement weak interactions, and lepton-number violation is induced by a neutrino Majorana mass. Quantum circuits that prepare the initial nucleus and time evolve with the Hamiltonian containing the strong and weak interactions are executed on IonQ Forte Enterprise. Enabled by tuned model parameters, lepton-number violation is observed in real time, providing a clear signal of neutrinoless double-beta decay. This was made possible by co-designing the simulation to maximally utilize the all-to-all connectivity and native gate-set available on IonQ's quantum computers. Quantum circuit compilation techniques and co-designed error-mitigation methods, informed from executing benchmarking circuits with up to 2,356 two-qubit gates, enabled observables to be extracted with high precision. We discuss the potential of future quantum simulations to provide yocto-second resolution of the reaction pathways in these, and other, nuclear processes.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate a clear signal of lepton-number violation mediated by a Majorana neutrino mass in real-time quantum simulations of neutrinoless double-beta decay, but only in a simplified 1+1D lattice QCD model with deliberately tuned (unphysical) parameters on a two-site lattice, and with the weak interaction truncated to valence fermion operators after finding that the full operator required circuits too deep for practical execution.

## Method

The authors mapped a 1+1D lattice QCD system with two spatial sites, containing up and down quarks (with three colors each), electrons, and neutrinos, onto 32 qubits of IonQ's trapped-ion quantum computers, with 4 additional qubits for flag-based error mitigation. They prepared an initial |Δ⁻Δ⁻⟩ two-baryon state using SC-ADAPT-VQE, then applied two steps of first-order Trotterized time evolution with a Hamiltonian containing free fermion terms, a truncated chromoelectric interaction (λ=1), a valence-only four-Fermi weak interaction, and a neutrino Majorana mass term; circuits were compiled to use 470 native RZZ two-qubit gates for the production runs on IonQ Forte Enterprise, with post-selection on charge conservation and leakage detection applied to the measurement results.

## Key numbers

- 32 qubits used to map the fermionic degrees of freedom across two lattice sites, plus 4 additional qubits for flag-based error mitigation
- 470 two-qubit (RZZ) gates in the production circuits executed on IonQ Forte Enterprise
- 2,356 two-qubit gates in the deeper benchmarking circuits executed on IonQ Forte (full weak operator with sea contributions)
- 10σ difference between lepton numbers obtained with Majorana mass m_M=1.7 and m_M=0 at time t=2.0 on Forte Enterprise
- At t=2.0 with m_M=1.7 on Forte Enterprise: ⟨ℒ⟩ = 1.43 ± 0.12 (QPU) compared to 1.31 (ideal simulation)
- At t=2.0 with m_M=0 on Forte Enterprise: ⟨ℒ⟩ = 0.08 ± 0.07 (QPU) compared to 0.0 (ideal simulation)
- Approximately 10% of shots survived post-selection filtering on leakage detection and conservation of color and total electric charges
- 24,000 shots taken at t=2.0; 14,400 shots at t={0.5, 1.0, 1.5}

## Context

This work extends prior quantum simulations of single beta decay in 1+1D QCD by Farrell (2022) to the neutrinoless double-beta decay channel, and builds on previous trapped-ion simulations of non-abelian gauge theories by Klco (2019), Atas (2022), and others. The results establish a benchmark for future quantum simulations of exotic weak decays that could eventually complement classical lattice QCD approaches to neutrinoless double-beta decay matrix elements being pursued by Tiburzi (2017), Shanahan (2017), Cirigliano (2020), Nicholson (2018), Davoudi (2020), and Detmold (2022). The next steps identified include extending simulations to 2+1D, increasing spatial volumes, working with fermion masses closer to experimental values, and performing simulations with a range of neutrino masses.
