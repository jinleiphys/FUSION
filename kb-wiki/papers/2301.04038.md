# Role of chemical potential at kinetic freeze-out using Tsallis non-extensive statistics in proton-proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider

> The charged-particle transverse momentum spectra ($p_{\rm T}$-spectra) measured by the ALICE collaboration for $pp$ collisions at $\sqrt {s} =$ 7 and 13 TeV have been studied using a thermodynamically consistent form of Tsallis non-extensive statistics. The Tsallis distribution function is fitted to the $p_{\rm T}$-spectra and the results are analyzed as a function of final state charged-particle multiplicity for various light flavor and strange particles, such as $π^{\pm}, K^{\pm}, p+\bar{p}, ϕ, Λ+\barΛ, Ξ+\barΞ, Ω+\barΩ$. At the LHC energies, particles and antiparticles are produced in equal numbers. However, the equality of particle and antiparticle yields at the kinetic freeze-out may imply that they have the same but opposite chemical potential which is not necessarily zero. We use an alternative procedure that makes use of parameter redundancy, by introducing a finite chemical potential at the kinetic freeze-out stage. This article emphasizes the importance of the chemical potential of the system produced in $pp$ collisions at the LHC energies using the Tsallis distribution function which brings the system to a single freeze-out scenario.

## Key claim

The paper argues that at kinetic freeze-out in proton-proton collisions at LHC energies, particles and antiparticles can have a finite, non-zero chemical potential even though they are produced in equal numbers, contradicting the usual assumption that equal yields imply zero chemical potential. The authors use a thermodynamically consistent Tsallis distribution with a parameter redundancy to extract this chemical potential, but they explicitly find that the extracted chemical potential cannot be identified with the volume from femtoscopy measurements, contrary to a prior proposal by Rybczynski (2014).

## Method

The authors fit the transverse momentum spectra of seven particle species (π±, K±, p+p̄, φ, Λ+Λ̄, Ξ+Ξ̄, Ω+Ω̄) measured by ALICE in pp collisions at √s = 7 and 13 TeV, using a thermodynamically consistent Tsallis distribution function. They first fit with zero chemical potential to extract the non-extensive parameter q, temperature T0, and volume V0; then they fix q and refit with a finite chemical potential μ to obtain T, V, and μ, checking consistency via transformation relations between the two parameter sets.

## Key numbers

- For pp collisions at √s = 7 TeV, the non-extensive parameter q for pions ranges from 1.170 ± 0.001 (highest multiplicity class Mul1) to 1.139 ± 0.001 (lowest multiplicity class Mul10).
- For pp collisions at √s = 7 TeV, the temperature T0 for pions ranges from 0.086 ± 0.001 GeV (Mul1) to 0.070 ± 0.001 GeV (Mul10).
- For pp collisions at √s = 7 TeV, the radius R0 for pions ranges from 6.878 ± 0.008 fm (Mul1) to 4.553 ± 0.090 fm (Mul10).
- For pp collisions at √s = 7 TeV, the non-extensive parameter q for protons ranges from 1.097 ± 0.004 (Mul1) to 1.129 ± 0.004 (Mul10).
- For pp collisions at √s = 7 TeV, the temperature T0 for protons ranges from 0.205 ± 0.008 GeV (Mul1) to 0.026 ± 0.006 GeV (Mul10).
- For pp collisions at √s = 7 TeV, the q parameter for Ω+Ω̄ in the merged highest multiplicity bin Mul[1+2] is 1.056 ± 0.009, with T0 = 0.360 ± 0.006 GeV.
- For pp collisions at √s = 13 TeV, the mean charged-particle multiplicity density ⟨dNch/dη⟩ for pions, kaons, and protons ranges from 26.02 ± 0.35 (Mul1) to 2.55 ± 0.04 (Mul10).
- The linear fit of q at μ=0 versus q at μ≠0 for √s = 7 TeV gives slope m = 0.998 ± 0.019 and intercept c = 0.002 ± 0.022; for √s = 13 TeV, m = 1.004 ± 0.018 and c = -0.005 ± 0.021.

## Context

This work extends the use of thermodynamically consistent Tsallis statistics (Cleymans 2011, 2012) to study kinetic freeze-out in pp collisions, addressing the open question of whether chemical potential can be non-zero at kinetic freeze-out even when particle-antiparticle yields are equal. The authors test and reject a prior proposal by Rybczynski (2014) that the Tsallis volume parameter could be identified with femtoscopy volumes, finding instead that the Tsallis volume is specific to the distribution. The results enable future studies of the relationship between chemical freeze-out (where μ is zero at LHC energies, as established by Andronic 2017) and kinetic freeze-out, and they suggest that the non-extensive parameter q is independent of chemical potential, supporting the proposed fitting procedure.
