# Neutrino Processes in Strong Magnetic Fields and Implications for Supernova Dynamics

> The processes of neutrino (antineutrino) absorption and electron (positron) capture on nucleons provide the dominant mechanisms for heating and cooling the material between the protoneutron star and the stalled shock in a core-collapse supernova. Observations suggest that some neutron stars are born with magnetic fields of at least 10^15 G while theoretical considerations give an upper limit of 10^18 G for the protoneutron star magnetic fields. We calculate the rates for the above neutrino processes in strong magnetic fields of 10^16 G. We find that the main effect of such magnetic fields is to change the equations of state through the phase space of electron and positron, which differs from the classical case due to quantization of the motion of electron and positron perpendicular to the magnetic field. As a result, the cooling rate can be greatly reduced by magnetic fields of 10^16 G for typical conditions below the stalled shock and a nonuniform protoneutron star magnetic field (e.g., a dipole field) can introduce a large angular dependence of the cooling rate. In addition, strong magnetic fields always lead to an angle-dependent heating rate by polarizing the spin of nucleons. The implications of our results for the neutrino-driven supernova mechanism are discussed.

## Key claim

Strong magnetic fields of ~10^16 G primarily affect supernova heating and cooling rates not by directly altering neutrino cross sections, but by changing the equations of state through quantization of electron and positron motion perpendicular to the field, which can greatly reduce the cooling rate and introduce a large angular dependence of both heating and cooling rates.

## Method

The authors calculated the rates for neutrino absorption and electron/positron capture on nucleons (ν_e + n ⇌ p + e^- and ν̄_e + p ⇌ n + e^+) in a uniform magnetic field of ~10^16 G, using Landau wavefunctions for electrons and positrons and accounting for nucleon spin polarization. They compared these rates to the zero-field case using typical supernova parameters (luminosity L_ν = 4×10^52 erg s⁻¹, neutrino energies ⟨E_ν_e⟩ = 11 MeV, ⟨E_ν̄_e⟩ = 16 MeV, electron fraction Y_e = 0.5, entropies S = 10 and 20) and determined the gain radius where heating equals cooling.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field strength considered: B ~ 10^16 G, with observational lower bound ~10^15 G for magnetars and theoretical upper limit ~10^18 G for protoneutron stars
- Neutrinosphere radius: R_ν = 50 km; stalled shock radius: R_s = 200 km
- Neutrino luminosities: L_ν_e = L_ν̄_e = 4×10^52 erg s⁻¹
- Average neutrino energies: ⟨E_ν_e⟩ = 11 MeV, ⟨E_ν̄_e⟩ = 16 MeV
- Gain radius without magnetic field: R_g = 137 km for entropy S = 10, R_g = 68 km for S = 20
- Temperature profile: T(r) = 4 MeV × (50 km / r)
- Neutron-proton mass difference: Δ = 1.293 MeV
- Landau level spacing scale: √(eB) = 7.69 (B/10^16 G)^(1/2) MeV
- For B = 10^16 G, electrons/positrons with E_e ≲ 10 MeV occupy only the n = 0 Landau level (n_max = [8.45×10^(-3) (E_e² - m_e²)/MeV² × (10^16 G/B)]_int)
- Nucleon polarization: χ = 3.15×10^(-2) (μ/μ_N) (B/10^16 G) (1 MeV/T) for |μB/T| ≪ 1

## Context

This work extends studies of the neutrino-driven supernova mechanism (Bethe 1985), which fails in spherically symmetric models (Rampp 2000, Liebendorfer 2001) but may work in 3D with convection (Fryer 2002). The authors calculate how magnetic fields up to ~10^16 G affect the microscopic heating and cooling processes, complementing magnetohydrodynamic approaches (Khokhlov 1999) that consider direct dynamical effects like jet production. The results enable future studies of how nonuniform protoneutron star magnetic fields (e.g., dipole fields) introduce angular asymmetries in heating and cooling that could affect shock revival in the neutrino-driven mechanism.
