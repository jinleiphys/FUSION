# Nuclear Glory Phenomenon

> Analytical explanation of the nuclear glory effect, which is similar to the known optical (atmospheric) glory phenomenon, is presented. It is based on the small phase space method for the multiple interaction processes probability estimates and leads to the characteristic angular dependence of the production cross section $dσ\sim 1/ \sqrt {π- θ}$ in the vicinity of the strictly backward direction, for any number of interactions $N\geq 3$, either elastic or inelastic. Rigorous proof of this effect is given for the case of the optimal kinematics, as well as for arbitrary polar scattering angles in the case of the light particle rescattering, but the arguments in favor of the backward azimuthal (axial) focusing are quite general and hold for any kind of the multiple interaction processes. Such behaviour of the cross section near the backward direction agrees qualitatively with available data. In the small interval of final angles including the value $θ=π$ the angular dependence of the cumulative particle production cross section can have the crater-like (or funnel-like) form. Further studies including, probably, certain numerical calculations, are necessary to clear up this point.

# Nuclear Glory Phenomenon

## Key claim

The paper provides an analytical explanation for the nuclear glory effect (backward focusing of cumulative particles) based on the small phase space method for multiple interaction processes, showing that the production cross section follows a characteristic angular dependence of $d\sigma \sim 1/\sqrt{\pi - \theta}$ near the strictly backward direction for any number of interactions $N \geq 3$. The authors hedge that while rigorous proof is given for optimal kinematics and for light particle rescattering at arbitrary polar angles, the angular dependence near $\theta = \pi$ may have a crater-like or funnel-like form in a small interval of final angles, requiring further numerical calculations to clarify.

## Method

The authors apply the small phase space method, originally proposed in Kopeliovich (1985) and developed further in Kopeliovich and collaborators (1985), to calculate probabilities of multiple interaction processes (MIP) leading to cumulative particle production in kinematically forbidden regions. They expand the angular variables (azimuthal deviations $\varphi_k$ and polar deviations $\vartheta_k$) around the optimal kinematics where all scattering angles equal $\theta/N$, integrate the quadratic forms in these variables, and derive the phase space volume as a function of the distance from the kinematical boundary $\Delta_N^{ext}$. The determinant of the matrix defining the quadratic form for azimuthal deviations is shown to equal $s_\theta / s_1$, leading to the $1/\sqrt{\pi - \theta}$ dependence.

## Key numbers

- The characteristic angular dependence near the backward direction is $d\sigma \sim 1/\sqrt{\pi - \theta}$, derived from the factor $\sqrt{s_1/s_\theta}$ where $s_\theta \simeq \pi - \theta$ for $\pi - \theta \ll 1$.
- For light particle rescattering, the maximal final energy follows $\omega_N^{max} \simeq N (2m/\theta^2) + m/(6N)$ at large $N$ and large incident energy.
- For nucleon-nucleon scattering, the maximal final momentum follows $k_N^{max} \simeq N (2m/\theta^2) - m/(3N)$ at large $N$.
- Normal Fermi motion of nucleons inside the nucleus widens the kinematical boundaries by about 40%, with $p_F^{max}/m \simeq 0.27$.
- The constant $C_N$ in the ratio $R_N(\theta) = C_N \sqrt{\Delta_N^{ext}/(\pi - \theta)}$ varies between 0.38 and 0.26 for $N$ between 3 and 7.
- The Jacobian determinants for the quadratic form vanish at specific angles: $J_3(\pi/3)=0$, $J_4(\pi/4)=0$, $J_5(\pi/5)=0$, $J_6(\pi/6)=0$, $J_7(\pi/7)=0$, $J_8(\pi/8)=0$.
- Experimental data shown include secondary protons with kinetic energy between 0.11 and 0.24 GeV emitted from Pb nucleus with incident protons of 4.5 GeV/c.

## Context

This work extends the small phase space method developed in Kopeliovich (1985) and the comprehensive review by Kopeliovich and collaborators (1985) to provide the first explicit analytical derivation of the backward focusing effect for cumulative particle production. The nuclear glory phenomenon was first observed experimentally at JINR (Dubna) by Baldin and collaborators (1977) and at ITEP (Moscow) by Leksin's group, and later confirmed by several experiments including those by Leksin and collaborators (1997, 2001). The paper provides the theoretical foundation for the $1/\sqrt{\pi - \theta}$ angular dependence that had been observed qualitatively in these experiments, and enables future quantitative comparisons with data from experiments at ITEP (Abramov and collaborators) and the NOMAD Collaboration. The authors note that the effect is analogous to the optical (atmospheric) glory phenomenon, though the existing explanation of optical glory remains incomplete.
