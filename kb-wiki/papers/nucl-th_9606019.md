# Quasi-gaussian fixed points and factorial cumulants in nuclear multifragmentation

> We re-analyze the conditions for the phenomenon of intermittency (self-similar fluctuations) to occur in models of multifragmentation. Analyzing two different mechanisms, the bond-percolation and the ERW (Elattari, Richert and Wagner) statistical fragmentation models, we point out a common quasi-gaussian shape of the total multiplicity distribution in the critical range. The fixed-point property is also observed for the multiplicity of the second bin. Fluctuations are studied using scaled factorial cumulants instead of scaled factorial moments. The second-order cumulant displays the intermittency signal while higher order cumulants are equal to zero, revealing a large information redundancy in scaled factorial moments. A practical criterion is proposed to identify the gaussian feature of light-fragment production, distinguishing between a self-similarity mechanism (ERW) and the superposition of independent sources (percolation).

# Quasi-Gaussian Fixed Points and Factorial Cumulants in Nuclear Multifragmentation

## Key claim

The intermittency signal observed in nuclear multifragmentation models arises from a quasi-gaussian fixed point in the multiplicity distribution, but the underlying mechanism differs between models: the ERW model reflects genuine self-similar fragmentation while percolation reflects a superposition of independent sources, and these can be distinguished by the scaling of the variance-to-mean ratio.

## Method

The authors analyzed two multifragmentation models, bond percolation on a 3D cubic lattice and the ERW (Elattari, Richert, Wagner) statistical fragmentation model, by computing the skewness (γ₁) and sharpness (γ₂) coefficients of the total and second-bin multiplicity distributions as functions of the control parameters (bond-breaking probability r for percolation, power-law exponent τ for ERW) and system size A. They then introduced scaled factorial cumulants K_p(δ) as an alternative to scaled factorial moments F_p(δ) for studying fluctuations, and computed the variance-to-mean ratio σ/<n> as a function of mean multiplicity to distinguish self-similar from central-limit behavior.

## Key numbers

- For the ERW model, a fixed point in γ₁ and γ₂ occurs at τ ~ 1.8, which lies within the range 1.8 < τ < 2.0 where intermittency is observed.
- For bond percolation, a fixed point in γ₁ occurs near the critical bond-breaking probability r_c ≈ 0.76 (0.7512 in the continuous limit), with γ₁ near 0 and γ₂ near 3.
- The ERW model yields σ/<n> ∝ <n>^{-0.01} for the total multiplicity distribution, indicating self-similar behavior.
- The percolation model yields σ/<n> ∝ <n>^{-0.44} for the total multiplicity and σ/<n> ∝ <n>^{-0.49} for mass-one fragments, consistent with a central-limit theorem signature.
- System sizes studied ranged from A = 50 to 500 for ERW and from 4³ to 10³ sites for percolation, with statistics of about 10⁵ events per point.
- The minimum of γ₂ for ERW was found near 2.5, indicating maximal fluctuations in the intermittent region.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of intermittency in nuclear multifragmentation previously studied by Ploszajczak and collaborators (1994) using factorial moments, and by Campi (1988) who questioned the phase-transition interpretation. The authors directly compare the bond-percolation model (Stauffer, 1979) with the ERW model (Elattari, Richert, Wagner, 1995), showing that both produce similar factorial moment signals but through different physical mechanisms. The introduction of factorial cumulants, adapted from particle physics (Carruthers, 1991), provides a tool to resolve the information redundancy in factorial moments. The proposed criterion using σ/<n> scaling enables experimental distinction between self-similar fragmentation and independent-source emission, and the authors note that similar gaussian features were observed in a different model by Gross (1990).
