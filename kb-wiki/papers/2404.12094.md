# Strange quark matter as dark matter: 40 years later, a reappraisal

> Forty years ago Witten suggested that dark matter could be composed of macroscopic clusters of strange quark matter. This idea was very popular for several years, but it dropped out of fashion once lattice QCD calculations indicated that the confinement/deconfinement transition, at small baryonic chemical potential, is not first order, which seemed to be a crucial requirement in order to produce large clusters of quarks. Here we revisit the conditions under which strangelets can be produced in the Early Universe. We discuss the impact of an instability in the hadronic phase separating a low density, positive-strange-charge phase from a high-density phase with a negative strange charge. This second phase can rapidly stabilize by forming color-superconducting gaps. The strangelets then undergo partial evaporation. In this way, we obtain distributions of their sizes in agreement with the observational constraints and we discuss the many astrophysical and cosmological implications of these objects. Finally, we examine the most promising techniques to detect this type of strangelets. We also show that strangelets can exist with masses $\lesssim10^{17} \mathrm g$, while primordial black holes are ruled out in that mass range, allowing us to distinguish between these two dark matter candidates.

## Key claim

The authors revisit the possibility that dark matter is composed of macroscopic clusters of strange quark matter (strangelets), showing that even if the confinement/deconfinement transition in the Early Universe is not first order, strangelets can still form through alternative mechanisms involving an instability in the hadronic phase and color-superconducting gaps, provided that the evaporation process is sufficiently suppressed.

## Method

The authors construct a phenomenological model for strangelet production in the Early Universe based on the scheme of Witten (1984), incorporating the evaporation mechanism of Alcock (1985) and Madsen (1986). They assume an initial log-normal distribution of strangelet sizes, then apply an evaporation process operating between approximately 150 MeV and 1 MeV, regulated by parameters including the evaporation suppression factor β and the temperature at which neutrino reheating becomes effective. The resulting final mass distributions are checked against observational constraints from ancient mica, seismic events, femtolensing, microlensing, thermonuclear runaway in compact stars, and proto-neutron star conversion limits.

## Key numbers

- Strangelets can exist with masses less than 10^17 g, while primordial black holes are ruled out in that mass range by Hawking radiation
- The mass ranges not subject to limits for nuclear density monochromatic macros are (55-10^3) g, (5×10^4-10^8) g, and (10^10-10^18) g
- Ancient mica constraints exclude strangelets with mass M < 55 g
- Seismic events on Earth exclude the mass range 10^5 g < M < 3×10^8 g as dominant dark matter
- Lunar seismic data rule out more than 10% of dark matter density in the mass range 5×10^4 g < M < 10^6 g
- Microlensing rules out macros in the range 5×10^22 g < M < 4×10^24 g as dominant dark matter
- Thermonuclear runaway constraints exclude mass ranges 10^8 g < M < 10^10 g and M > 10^18 g for nuclear density macros
- The evaporation limiting temperature T_evap is in the range 50 MeV to 120 MeV depending on strangelet mass
- The final distributions contain approximately 10^29 to 10^34 strangelets in the Milky Way

## Context

This paper revisits the proposal by Witten (1984) that dark matter could be composed of strange quark matter clusters, which fell out of favor after lattice QCD calculations by Aoki (2006) showed the confinement transition at small baryonic chemical potential is a crossover rather than first order. The authors build on the evaporation calculations of Alcock (1985) and Madsen (1986, 1988) and incorporate modern developments including color superconductivity in the Color-Flavor Locked phase (Alford, 2007), the two-family scenario for neutron stars and quark stars (Drago, 2013-2017), and recent evidence for cluster formation in heavy ion collisions from ALICE. The work enables future studies of strangelet capture on main sequence and compact stars, formation of low-mass compact objects, and targeted experimental searches for strangelets in the allowed mass windows.
