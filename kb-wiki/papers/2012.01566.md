# Arbitrarily large neutron amplification in subcritical nuclear reactors

> In a subcritical reactor, each neutron produces only keff < 1 neutron per generation (asymptotically and on average), and thus the neutron population decreases exponentially in the absence of external source. The chain reaction is thus easy to stop, making such reactors inherently stable. Interestingly, and contrary to common wisdom, there is no relation between keff, the external source intensity, and the output power of the reactor. In this paper, I present various possible strategies to exploit this fact, and apply them to the design of a rudimentary multi-layer system that allows to reach an arbitrarily large number of fissions per source neutron, while keeping keff < 1 fixed (I used keff=0.97). The behavior of the amplifier is verified with simple Monte-Carlo transport simulations. The proposal admittedly brings complications and subtleties that need to be studied further, but, if developed, could allow to drive subcritical reactors with faint neutron sources, e.g. radioisotope based.

# Arbitrarily large neutron amplification in subcritical nuclear reactors

## Key claim
Contrary to common intuition, there is no fundamental relation between the asymptotic neutron multiplication factor \(k_{\text{eff}}\) and the total number of fissions produced per source neutron; by designing a multi-layer system with generation-dependent neutron multiplication, one can achieve arbitrarily large neutron amplification while keeping \(k_{\text{eff}} < 1\) fixed (here \(k_{\text{eff}} = 0.97\)), at least in principle and as verified with simple Monte-Carlo simulations, though the proposal brings complications and subtleties that require further study.

## Method
The author designed a rudimentary multi-layer preamplifier consisting of a linear stack of identical layers, each containing four cells: a mixed oxide fissile material (43% \(^{239}\)Pu, 57% \(^{238}\)U), a threshold fissile material (metallic \(^{241}\)Am), a thermal neutron absorber (cadmium), and a moderator (heavy water), surrounded by a lead reflector. The system was simulated using the Monte Carlo transport code openMC to compute the neutron multiplication per generation, the asymptotic \(k_{\text{eff}}\), and the fission rate per layer for stacks of up to 10 layers.

## Key numbers
- The preamplifier achieves a scalable amplification of approximately **1.6 times more neutron flux and fissions per layer** from layer \(n\) to layer \(n+1\), for a fixed \(k_{\text{eff}} = 0.97\).
- Each layer has a cross section of **130 × 130 cm²** and a total width of approximately **38 cm**, with the heavy water moderator occupying **35 cm** of that thickness.
- The mixed oxide fissile section is **1.5 cm** thick, the americium section is **1.3 cm** thick, and the cadmium layer is **3 mm** thick.
- For a 10-layer system, the transient neutron multiplication per generation \(k_i\) reaches a plateau at **\(k \simeq 1.04\)** and remains above 1 for about **100 generations** before dropping below 1, while the asymptotic \(k_{\text{eff}}\) converges to **0.97** regardless of the number of layers.

## Context
This work extends the conceptual argument, noted as early as Borst (1957), that generation-dependent neutron multiplication can decouple the total amplification from the asymptotic multiplication factor. It builds on prior accelerator-driven subcritical reactor (ADSR) designs by Nifenecker et al. (2003) and on two-layer core proposals by Seliverstov (1996) and Kiselev (2002), which achieved only modest amplification. The paper demonstrates that a many-layer preamplifier is physically feasible with existing materials, potentially enabling subcritical reactors to be driven by faint neutron sources such as radioisotopes rather than high-current accelerators, though significant engineering and safety challenges remain, including the need for accurate in-situ \(k_{\text{eff}}\) measurement as studied by Chevret (2014) and Marie (2019).
