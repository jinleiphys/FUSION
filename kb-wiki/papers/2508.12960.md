# Radial oscillations of neutron stars within density-dependent relativistic-mean field model

> The radial oscillations of neutron stars are studied using equations of state derived from density-dependent relativistic mean-field (DDRMF) models, which effectively describe the ground-state properties of finite nuclei. A novel numerical approach, the finite volume method (FVM), is employed to solve the eigenvalue problem associated with oscillation frequencies. Compared to conventional methods such as the finite difference method and shooting method, the FVM avoids the numerical instability encountered at high frequencies with an equation of state that includes a discontinuous adiabatic index and offers greater computational efficiency. The oscillation frequencies of high-order modes exhibit a similar trend of change. The radial displacements and pressure perturbations are largely influenced by the EOSs of crust region. {The frequency of the first excited state shows a strong linear relationship with both the slope and skewness parameters of the symmetry energy.} These findings suggest that the density dependence of the symmetry energy can be constrained through observations of neutron star radial oscillation frequencies.

## Key claim

The frequency of the first excited radial oscillation mode of neutron stars shows a strong linear correlation with both the slope parameter \(L\) and the skewness coefficient \(Q_{\text{sym}}\) of the nuclear symmetry energy, suggesting that these nuclear matter properties can be constrained through observations of neutron star radial oscillation frequencies, provided the finite volume method is used to handle discontinuities in the adiabatic index at the crust-core transition.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain neutron star equilibrium structures using equations of state from 14 density-dependent relativistic mean-field parameter sets that accurately describe ground-state properties of finite nuclei. They then solved the eigenvalue problem for radial oscillation frequencies using the finite volume method, which preserves the conservation form of the differential equation and handles discontinuities in the adiabatic index at the crust-core boundary, unlike the finite difference method or shooting method. The correlations between nuclear saturation properties and oscillation frequencies were analyzed, with additional comparisons to Skyrme-like models (SLy4, SLy9, BSK22, BSK24) and non-linear relativistic mean-field models (TM1, IUFSU).

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star masses predicted by the 14 DDRMF parameterizations range from approximately \(1.9 M_\odot\) to \(2.6 M_\odot\)
- For the first four eigenfrequencies, the error between the finite volume method and the shooting method is less than \(0.1\%\) when using \(N=3000\) grid points; differences for the first 15 eigenfrequencies remain below \(1\%\)
- The correlation coefficient between the slope of symmetry energy \(L\) and the first excited mode frequency \(\nu_1\) is \(R_{1.4} = -0.9681\) at \(1.4 M_\odot\) and \(R_{2.0} = -0.9829\) at \(2.0 M_\odot\)
- The correlation coefficient between the skewness coefficient of symmetry energy \(Q_{\text{sym}}\) and \(\nu_1\) is \(R_{1.4} = 0.8917\) at \(1.4 M_\odot\) and \(R_{2.0} = 0.9335\) at \(2.0 M_\odot\)
- For the smallest \(L\) value (DDT parameter set), the first excited mode frequency is around \(7.0\) kHz; for the largest \(L\) values (DDMEX2 and PKDD sets), frequencies are around \(5.8\) kHz

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutron star radial oscillations (Vaeth and Kokkotas, 1992; Kokkotas and Ruoff, 2001; Glass and Lindblom, 1983) by introducing the finite volume method to address numerical instabilities from discontinuous adiabatic indices at the crust-core transition, an issue not addressed in prior approaches. The finding that the first excited mode frequency correlates strongly with symmetry energy parameters provides a potential observational probe complementary to tidal deformability constraints from events like GW170817, and could be tested with next-generation gravitational wave detectors such as the Cosmic Explorer and Einstein Telescope.
