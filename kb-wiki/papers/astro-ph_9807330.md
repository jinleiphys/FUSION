# Comment on ``Signal of Quark Deconfinement in the Timing Structure of Pulsar Spin-Down''

> This is a comment on a paper by Glendenning, Pei, and Weber (Phys. Rev. Lett., 79, 1603, 1997), where the authors gave an incorrect estimate of the event rate and neglected the important gravitational energy release. Previous work on the same subject is reviewed, and a new suggestion is made to link quark-hadron phase transitions with soft gamma-ray repeaters.

## Key claim
Glendenning, Pei, and Weber (1997) overestimated the event rate of observable quark-hadron phase transitions in pulsars by assuming all pulsars have favorable parameters; in reality, less than 0.1% of neutron stars may undergo such a transition, and the gravitational energy release they neglected makes X-ray observations more promising than radio searches.

## Method
The author recalculates the event rate by noting that the increase in central density during spin-down is only Δρ_c/ρ_c ∼ 10^{-3}, leaving a narrow window of densities that can reach the critical density for phase transition. The gravitational energy released during stellar contraction is estimated from the change in radius (ΔR/R), and the resulting thermal emission is compared to van Riper's (1988) calculations of neutron star thermal response to a steady central energy source. The predicted X-ray luminosity and event rate are then compared to observed properties of soft gamma-ray repeaters.

## Key numbers
- Less than 0.1% of all neutron stars might undergo a phase transition in their lifetime (contrasted with GPW's estimate of 1% of observed pulsars on a 10^5 year timescale).
- The increase in central density due to spin-down is Δρ_c/ρ_c ∼ 10^{-3}.
- Gravitational energy release estimated as E ∼ 10^53 × (ΔR/R) ergs, with an average rate of 10^40 ergs s^{-1} over 10^5 years.
- For a central energy source of 10^40 ergs s^{-1}, the star is heated to a surface temperature of 3×10^6 K, yielding a soft X-ray luminosity of ∼10^35 ergs s^{-1}.
- Among ∼10^8 neutron stars in the Galaxy, ∼10^5 may have phase transitions; if each lasts 10^5 years, a few (order unity) isolated bright X-ray sources (∼20 times solar luminosity) are expected.
- Only 4 soft gamma-ray repeaters are observed, with quiescent X-ray luminosity L_X ∼ 10^35 ergs s^{-1}.

## Context
This comment corrects the event rate estimate of Glendenning, Pei, and Weber (1997) and highlights the neglected gravitational energy release, which makes X-ray searches more promising than radio pulsar surveys. The author extends van Riper's (1988) thermal response calculations to the phase-transition scenario and proposes a new link between quark-hadron phase transitions and soft gamma-ray repeaters, noting that strong magnetic fields (∼10^15 G) cause faster spin-down and thus more likely phase transitions. The work also contrasts with earlier models that predicted a density discontinuity and catastrophic phase transitions on second timescales, which could power gamma-ray bursts (Ma, 1998).
