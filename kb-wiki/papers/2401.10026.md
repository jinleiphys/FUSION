# Jet Quenching: From Theory to Simulation

> With the explosion of data on jet based observables in relativistic heavy-ion collisions at the Large Hadron Collider and the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider, perturbative QCD based simulations of these processes, often interacting with an expanding viscous fluid dynamical background, have taken center stage. This review is meant to bridge the gap between theory, simulation and phenomenology of jet modification in a dense medium. We will demonstrate how the existence of such end-to-end event generators with semi-realistic or even fully realistic final states allows for the most rigorous comparisons between pQCD based jet modification theory and experiment. State-of-the-art calculations of several jet based observables are presented. Extensions of this theory to jets in the small systems of $p$-$A$ and $e$-$A$ collisions is discussed.

# Jet Quenching: From Theory to Simulation

## Key claim
This review demonstrates that end-to-end event generators with semi-realistic or fully realistic final states enable the most rigorous comparisons between pQCD-based jet modification theory and experimental data in heavy-ion collisions, but the extracted values of the jet transport coefficient $\hat{q}$ from different theoretical approaches remain very different, particularly when extending these methods to confined matter in deep-inelastic scattering on nuclei.

## Method
The paper systematically presents the theoretical foundations and simulation modules within the JETSCAPE framework, covering the linear Boltzmann transport (LBT) model for elastic and inelastic parton scatterings, the MATTER module for high-virtuality parton showers, and various hadronization models including fragmentation functions, the Lund string model, the cluster model, and hybrid hadronization. Bayesian statistical analysis using Gaussian process emulators and Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods is applied to calibrate model parameters against experimental data, and the X-SCAPE framework extends this approach to small systems by enforcing energy-momentum conservation between hard scattering and the bulk medium.

## Key numbers
- The string tension in the Lund model is $\kappa \approx 1\;\mathrm{GeV}/\mathrm{fm}$
- The Debye screening mass is $\mu_\mathrm{D}=6\pi\alpha_\mathrm{s}T^2$
- In the information field based Bayesian method, a constant $\mu=1.36$ is chosen as the average value of $\ln(\hat{q}/T^3)$, with correlation strength $\sigma=0.7$ and correlation length $l=\ln(2)$
- The 90% credible region of $\hat{q}/T^3$ is obtained by discarding values at both top and bottom 5 percent from posterior distributions
- The 95% credible regions of the equation of state from the QLBT model are compared with lattice QCD data using transition temperatures of 150 MeV and 154 MeV
- The lower cut-off for gluon emission in LBT is $z_\mathrm{min}=\mu_\mathrm{D}/E$ to avoid divergence at $z\rightarrow 0$

## Context
This review extends the line of work from the JET Collaboration (2013) which used traditional $\chi^2$-fitting to extract $\hat{q}$ values, and from earlier Bayesian analyses of bulk QGP properties by Novak (2013), Bernhard (2019), and the JETSCAPE collaboration (2020). The paper builds on the MATTER+LBT module calibration by JETSCAPE (2021) and the information field based Bayesian method by Xie (2022), while also discussing the Duke-LGV Bayesian analysis by Xu (2017) for heavy quarks. The work enables future multi-stage generator frameworks for jets in cold nuclear matter and small systems such as $p$-$A$ and $e$-$A$ collisions, where the X-SCAPE framework (JETSCAPE, 2023) has already demonstrated good description of hadronic and jet spectra in $p$-$p$ and $p$-$Pb$ collisions.
