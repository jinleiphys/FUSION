# Exotic-Hadron Signature by Constituent-Counting Rule in Perturbative QCD

> We explain a method to find internal quark configurations of exotic hadron candidates by using the constituent counting rule. The counting rule was theoretically predicted in perturbative QCD for hard exclusive hadron reactions, and it has been tested in experiments for stable hadrons including compound systems of hadrons such as the deuteron, $^3$H, and $^3$He. It indicates that the cross section scales as $dσ/dt \sim 1/s^{n-2}$, where $s$ is the center-of-mass energy squared and $n$ is the total number of constituents. We apply this method for finding internal configurations of exotic hadron candidates, especially $Λ(1405)$. There is a possibility that $Λ(1405)$ could be five-quark state or a $\bar K N$ molecule, and scaling properties should be different between the ordinary three-quark state or five-quark one. We predict such a difference in $π^- + p \to K^0 + Λ(1405)$, and it could be experimentally tested, for example, at J-PARC. On the other hand, there are already measurements for $γ+ p \to K^+ + Λ(1405)$ as well as the ground $Λ$ in photoproduction reactions. Analyzing such data, we found an interesting indication that $Λ(1405)$ looks like a five-quark state at medium energies and a three-quark one at high energies. However, accurate higher-energy measurements are necessary for drawing a solid conclusion, and it should be done at JLab by using the updated 12 GeV electron beam. Furthermore, we discuss studies of exotic hadron candidates, such as $f_0 (980)$ and $a_0 (980)$, in electron-positron annihilation by using generalized distribution amplitudes and the counting rule. These studies should be possible as a KEKB experiment.

## Key claim

The constituent counting rule can distinguish ordinary three-quark hadrons from exotic five-quark or tetraquark configurations in hard exclusive reactions, but the analysis of existing photoproduction data for Λ(1405) suggests it may behave as a five-quark state at medium energies and a three-quark state at high energies, though the errors are too large for a solid conclusion.

## Method

The authors apply the perturbative QCD constituent counting rule, which predicts that the differential cross section for hard exclusive scattering scales as dσ/dt ∼ 1/s^(n-2) where n is the total number of elementary constituents in the initial and final hadrons. They analyze existing experimental data for π⁻ + p → K⁰ + Λ(1405) and γ + p → K⁺ + Λ(1405) by fitting the scaling exponent n from the energy dependence of cross sections at fixed center-of-mass scattering angle near 90°. They also extend the method to generalized distribution amplitudes (GDAs) for studying exotic meson candidates in two-photon processes.

## Key numbers

- For the ground-state Λ in π⁻ + p → K⁰ + Λ, the fitted scaling exponent is n = 10.1 ± 0.6, consistent with the three-quark picture (expected n = 10 for 3+3+2+2 constituents).
- For Λ(1405) in γ + p → K⁺ + Λ(1405), the fitted exponent is n = 10.6 in the analysis range √s ≥ 2.5 GeV.
- The minimum-energy scan for Λ(1405) shows n consistent with 11 (five-quark) at √s_min = 2.3-2.5 GeV, and n consistent with 9 (three-quark) at √s_min ≥ 2.6 GeV.
- For the ground Λ in photoproduction, the scaling analysis uses data with √s_min = 2.6 GeV and yields n = 10.0.
- The transition to scaling behavior in γ + p → π⁺ + n occurs at √s ≃ 2.5 GeV according to prior experimental data.

## Context

This work extends the constituent counting rule, previously tested on stable hadrons and light nuclei such as the deuteron, ³H, and ³He in BNL-AGS experiments and photoproduction reactions, to exotic hadron candidates. The method provides a complementary approach to low-energy observables like masses and decay widths for identifying exotic configurations. The results motivate new measurements at J-PARC using the high-momentum beamline for π⁻ + p → K⁰ + Λ(1405) and at JLab with the upgraded 12 GeV electron beam for γ + p → K⁺ + Λ(1405), as well as studies of f₀(980) and a₀(980) in electron-positron annihilation at KEKB using generalized distribution amplitudes.
