# Measuring Neutron Star Radius with second and third generation Gravitational Wave Detector Networks

> The next generation of ground-based interferometric gravitational wave detectors will observe mergers of black holes and neutron stars throughout cosmic time. A large number of the binary neutron star merger events will be observed with extreme high fidelity, and will provide stringent constraints on the equation of state of nuclear matter. In this paper, we investigate the systematic improvement in the measurability of the equation of state with increase in detector sensitivity by combining constraints obtained on the radius of a $1.4 \, \mathrm{M}_{\odot}$ neutron star from a simulated source population. Since the measurability of the equation of state depends on its stiffness, we consider a range of realistic equations of state that span the current observational constraints. We show that a single 40km Cosmic Explorer detector can pin down the neutron star radius for a soft, medium and stiff equation of state to an accuracy of 10m within a decade, whereas the current generation of ground-based detectors like the Advanced LIGO-Virgo network would take $\mathcal{O}(10^5)$ years to do so for a soft equation of state.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a single 40km Cosmic Explorer detector can measure the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star to an accuracy of 10 meters within a decade for soft, medium, and stiff equations of state, whereas the current Advanced LIGO-Virgo network would require on the order of 10^5 years to achieve the same precision for a soft equation of state. This improvement depends critically on the stiffness of the true equation of state, with softer equations of state requiring longer observation times even for next-generation detectors.

## Method

The authors performed a full Bayesian parameter estimation on a simulated astrophysical population of binary neutron star mergers, drawing component masses from a Gaussian centered at 1.4 solar masses with standard deviation 0.05 solar masses and spins aligned with the orbital angular momentum. They used the IMRPhenomD_NRTidal waveform approximant and considered six different detector networks: the Advanced LIGO-Virgo network at design sensitivity, a post-O5 network with three detectors at A-sharp sensitivity, single 20km and 40km Cosmic Explorer detectors, and two multi-detector next-generation networks. For each network, they combined posterior distributions on the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star from multiple events hierarchically, drawing 500 random realizations of the universe to account for the stochastic ordering of loud events.

## Key numbers

- Advanced LIGO-Virgo network would require 200,000 (+40,000/-40,000) years for a soft equation of state, 50,000 (+10,000/-10,000) years for a medium equation of state, and 7,000 (+900/-900) years for a stiff equation of state to measure the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star to 10 meter accuracy
- A single 40km Cosmic Explorer detector would require 12 (+3/-3) years for a soft equation of state, 8 (+1/-1) years for a medium equation of state, and 1 (+0.6/-0.6) year for a stiff equation of state to achieve the same 10 meter accuracy
- A single 20km Cosmic Explorer detector would require 21 (+10/-10) years for a soft equation of state, 15 (+7/-7) years for a medium equation of state, and 3 (+1/-1) years for a stiff equation of state
- The three-detector A-sharp network would require 300 (+50/-50) years for a soft equation of state, 100 (+40/-40) years for a medium equation of state, and 20 (+4/-4) years for a stiff equation of state
- The network of 40km Cosmic Explorer plus two A-sharp detectors would require 9 (+4/-4) years for a soft equation of state, 6 (+2/-2) years for a medium equation of state, and 0.4 (+0.2/-0.2) years for a stiff equation of state
- The network of 40km Cosmic Explorer, 20km Cosmic Explorer, and one A-sharp detector would require 5 (+2/-2) years for a soft equation of state, 3 (+1/-1) years for a medium equation of state, and 0.2 (+0.07/-0.07) years for a stiff equation of state

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Finstad et al. (2023), who combined approximately 300 signals in an Advanced LIGO-Virgo network and compared constraints from one year of a single Cosmic Explorer detector, by systematically studying multiple detector network configurations and using a full Bayesian parameter estimation framework. The authors use the same set of 2000 equations of state calibrated with chiral effective field theory up to nuclear saturation density as in Finstad et al. (2023) and Capano et al. (2019), and adopt the median binary neutron star merger rate of 320 Gpc^-3 yr^-1 from LIGO Scientific Collaboration (2020). The results demonstrate that building Cosmic Explorer is essential for constraining the neutron star radius to 10 meter accuracy within a human lifetime, a precision unattainable with current-generation detectors.
