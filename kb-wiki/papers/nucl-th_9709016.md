# Stability and Production of Superheavy Nuclei

> Beyond uranium heavy elements rapidly become increasingly unstable with respect to spontaneous fission as the proton number Z increases, because of the disruptive effect of the long-range Coulomb force. However, in the region just beyond Z = 100 magic proton and neutron numbers and the associated shell structure enhances nuclear stability sufficiently to allow observation of additional nuclei. Some thirty years ago it was speculated that an island of spherical, relatively stable superheavy nuclei would exist near the next doubly magic proton-neutron combination beyond 208:Pb, that is, at proton number Z = 114 and neutron number N = 184. Theory and experiment now show that there also exists a rock of stability in the vicinity of Z = 110 and N = 162 between the actinide region, which previously was the end of the peninsula of known elements, and the predicted island of spherical superheavy nuclei slightly southwest of the magic numbers Z = 114 and N = 184. We review here the stability properties of the heavy region of nuclei. Just as the decay properties of nuclei in the heavy region depend strongly on shell structure, this structure also dramatically affects the fusion entrance channel. The six most recently discovered new elements were all formed in cold-fusion reactions. We discuss here the effect of the doubly magic structure of the target in cold-fusion reactions on the fusion barrier and on dissipation.

## Key claim

Theory and experiment now show that there exists a "rock" of stability in the vicinity of proton number Z=110 and neutron number N=162, consisting of deformed shell-stabilized nuclei, located between the actinide region and the predicted island of spherical superheavy nuclei near Z=114 and N=184; the paper reviews stability properties and the cold-fusion reaction mechanism that produced the six most recently discovered new elements.

## Method

The authors use a macroscopic-microscopic model combining a diffuse-surface folded-Yukawa single-particle potential for the microscopic term with a generalized droplet model and Yukawa-plus-exponential potential for the macroscopic term, applying the Strutinsky method to obtain microscopic corrections. They calculate potential-energy surfaces for fusion and fission, single-particle level diagrams for merging nuclei, and fusion barriers for both cold-fusion reactions (using spherical doubly magic targets) and hot-fusion reactions (using deformed actinide targets). The calculations are compared to experimental decay chains of the heaviest known nucleus 277112 and its alpha-decay products.

## Key numbers

- The heaviest known element at the time is 277112, with its alpha-decay chain providing the first measured half-life of 271Hs at the center of the predicted rock of stability, measured at about 10 s, compared to the FRDM (1992) prediction of 630 s.
- For the cold-fusion reaction 70Zn + 209Bi → 279113, the calculated polar barrier height is 193.3 MeV and the equatorial barrier height is 208.0 MeV.
- For the hot-fusion reaction 124Sn + 176Yb → 300120, the equatorial barrier is 378.2 MeV, corresponding to emission of only two neutrons for collisions at the barrier.
- For the hugging configuration reaction 114Cd + 180Hf → 294120, the barrier is 381.8 MeV with four evaporated neutrons and a touching distance of 11.08 fm.
- For the reaction 116Cd + 180Hf → 296120 with oblate projectiles, the polar-parallel barrier is 365.6 MeV (2n evaporation) with touching distance 11.91 fm, while the equatorial-transverse barrier is 389.3 MeV (five neutrons) with touching distance 10.49 fm.
- The spontaneous-fission half-life of 258Fm is 0.38 ms compared to 2.86 h for 256Fm, with symmetric fission of 258Fm showing fragment kinetic energy about 35 MeV higher than in asymmetric fission of 256Fm.
- Shell effects lower the fusion valley at touching by about 20 MeV in the most favorable case relative to the macroscopic model for division into fragments near 132Sn.

## Context

This review extends the long-standing prediction of an island of spherical superheavy nuclei near Z=114, N=184 (first proposed by Meldner in 1965 and by Myers and Swiatecki in 1967) by documenting the experimentally confirmed "rock" of deformed shell-stabilized nuclei near Z=110, N=162. The authors connect their macroscopic-microscopic calculations (FRDM 1992) to the experimental discovery of elements Z=107-112 via cold-fusion reactions using doubly magic 208Pb and near-doubly magic 209Bi targets (Hofmann et al., 1996). The work enables future exploration of superheavy elements through both cold-fusion and hot-fusion reactions, including proposed reactions to reach Z=114 and Z=120, and identifies the need to understand how evaporation-residue cross sections depend on projectile and target species, deformation, and relative orientation.
