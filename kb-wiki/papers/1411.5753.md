# Study of isospin nonconservation in the framework of spectral distribution theory

> The observed isospin-symmetry breaking in light nuclei are caused not only by the Coulomb interaction but by the isovector one and two body plus isotensor two body nuclear interactions as well. Spectral distribution theory which treats nuclear spectroscopy and other structural properties in a statistical framework was earlier applied to isospin conserving Hamiltonians only. In this paper we extend that to include the nuclear interactions non-scalar in isospin and work out examples in sd shell to calculate the linear term in the isobaric mass-multiplet equation originating from these non-scalar parts.

## Key claim

Spectral distribution theory, previously applied only to isospin-conserving Hamiltonians, can be extended to include isovector and isotensor nuclear interactions; the resulting calculations of the linear term `b` in the isobaric mass-multiplet equation (IMME) for sd-shell nuclei agree reasonably well with experimental values, though the authors note that the changes in centroids come almost entirely from the one-body isovector part, with the two-body nonisoscalar contributions being negligible.

## Method

The authors constructed a total Hamiltonian consisting of isoscalar, isovector, and isotensor parts, using Wildenthal's mass-dependent interaction for the isoscalar part and the Ormand-Brown (1989) parameters for the nonisoscalar one- and two-body matrix elements in the 1s-0d shell. They first evaluated the centroids and widths (first two moments) in proton-neutron configuration spaces, then projected out the traces in fixed-isospin configuration spaces using a Clebsch-Gordan coefficient inversion procedure. Ground state energies were obtained via the Ratcliff prescription, integrating the configuration-isospin level densities from the low-energy side until half the ground-state degeneracy was reached, and the coefficient `b` was extracted from the energy difference between calculations with only the isoscalar Hamiltonian and those including the isovector part.

## Key numbers

* For the (4,1) system (m=4 valence particles, T=1) in the sd shell: the ground state energy with only the isoscalar Hamiltonian was -33.83 MeV; with the total Hamiltonian it was -37.26 MeV; the nuclear contribution to `b` was -3.48 MeV; adding the Coulomb contribution of -0.7 MeV gave a total |b| of 4.18 MeV, compared to the observed |b| of 4.21 MeV.
* For the (5,3/2) system: the isoscalar-only ground state energy was -42.16 MeV; the total Hamiltonian ground state energy was -47.30 MeV; the nuclear `b` was -3.47 MeV; the Coulomb contribution was -0.9 MeV; the total |b| was 4.37 MeV, compared to the observed |b| of 4.44 MeV.
* For the (6,1) system: the isoscalar-only ground state energy was -62.88 MeV; the total Hamiltonian ground state energy was -66.22 MeV; the nuclear `b` was -3.50 MeV; the Coulomb contribution was -1.1 MeV; the total |b| was 4.60 MeV, matching the observed |b| of 4.60 MeV.
* For the m=4, T=1 configuration (400) (all four particles in 0d5/2): the isoscalar centroid was -23.91 MeV with width 4.62 MeV; the total Hamiltonian centroid was -27.31 MeV with width 4.64 MeV.
* For the m=5, T=3/2 configuration (500): the isoscalar centroid was -31.22 MeV with width 5.30 MeV; the total Hamiltonian centroid was -36.33 MeV with width 5.32 MeV.

## Context

This work extends spectral distribution theory, previously developed by French (1966, 1967), Brody (1981), and Kota (1989) for isospin-conserving Hamiltonians, to include isospin-nonconserving nuclear interactions. It follows earlier studies by Hecht and Draayer (1974) on nonisoscalar interactions in spectral distributions and uses the interaction parameters fitted by Ormand and Brown (1989) to reproduce experimental IMME coefficients. The results enable future applications of spectral distribution methods to heavier shells such as 0f-1p and suggest that the nonisoscalar two-body parts may be approximated by a simple one-body isovector term, simplifying further statistical studies of isospin symmetry breaking.
