# What Invariant One-Particle Multiplicity Distributions and Two-Particle Correlations Are Telling Us about Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We use a nine-parameter expanding source model that includes special relativity, quantum statistics, resonance decays, and freeze-out on a realistic hypersurface in spacetime to analyze invariant pi+, K+, and K- one-particle multiplicity distributions and pi+ and pi- two-particle correlations in nearly central collisions of Pb + Pb at a laboratory bombarding momentum per nucleon of 158 GeV/c. The adjustable parameters of the model are determined by minimizing chi-square with a total of 2137 data points for the five types of data considered, which leads to an acceptable value of 1.017 for chi-square per degree of freedom. Compared to earlier results obtained with this model for nearly central collisions of Si + Au at a laboratory bombarding momentum per nucleon of 14.6 GeV/c, the present results indicate that in Pb + Pb collisions the freeze-out density is somewhat lower, the freeze-out temperature is slightly higher, the source at freeze-out is somewhat larger, and the longitudinal collective velocity is very poorly determined (because of the limited experimental coverage in rapidity). For both reactions, the freeze-out temperature is less than 100 MeV and the longitudinal and transverse collective velocities-which are anti-correlated with the temperature-are substantial. We reconcile these results with those in several previous analyses that yielded a much higher freeze-out temperature of approximately 140 MeV, and finally perform two additional studies with our expanding source model.

## Key claim

The freeze-out temperature in Pb+Pb collisions at 158 GeV/c per nucleon is less than 100 MeV, contradicting earlier analyses that reported approximately 140 MeV; this discrepancy is resolved by showing that the earlier work used a non-relativistic extrapolation formula or a thermal model with several approximations that neglected resonance decays, quantum statistics, and absolute normalization information.

## Method

The authors applied a nine-parameter expanding source model that includes special relativity, quantum statistics, resonance decays, and freeze-out on a realistic spacetime hypersurface to analyze invariant one-particle multiplicity distributions for pi+, K+, and K- and two-particle correlations for pi+ and pi- from the NA44 experiment at CERN's Super Proton Synchrotron. The eight adjustable parameters (the source velocity was fixed for symmetric collisions) were determined by minimizing chi-square against 2137 data points for the central 20% most central Pb+Pb collisions at a laboratory momentum of 158 GeV/c per nucleon. The model uses a Wigner distribution function on the freeze-out hypersurface, with the Cooper-Frye formula for direct particle production and contributions from 10 resonance decays, assuming boost invariance and a linear transverse velocity profile.

## Key numbers

- Chi-square per degree of freedom: 1.017 for 2129 degrees of freedom (chi-square = 2165.5), with a 28.6% probability that a perfect model would yield at least this value
- Freeze-out temperature for Pb+Pb: 95.8 +/- 3.5 MeV (99% confidence)
- Central baryon density for Pb+Pb: 0.062 (+0.019/-0.015) times normal nuclear density n0 = 0.153 fm^-3
- Transverse collective velocity for Pb+Pb: 0.664 +/- 0.035 c (99% confidence)
- Longitudinal collective velocity for Pb+Pb: 0.9985 (+0.0015/-0.94) c (99% confidence)
- Transverse radius at freeze-out for Pb+Pb: 11.4 +/- 1.5 fm (99% confidence)
- Longitudinal proper time at freeze-out for Pb+Pb: 12.2 +/- 2.1 fm/c (99% confidence)
- Pion incoherence fraction for Pb+Pb: 0.690 +/- 0.074 (99% confidence)
- For Si+Au at 14.6 GeV/c per nucleon: freeze-out temperature 92.9 +/- 4.4 MeV, transverse collective velocity 0.683 +/- 0.048 c, central baryon density 0.145 (+0.063/-0.045) n0

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier expanding source model analysis of Si+Au collisions at 14.6 GeV/c per nucleon (Chapman and Nix, 1996) to the higher-energy Pb+Pb system, finding a lower freeze-out density, slightly higher temperature, and larger source size. The results contradict analyses by the NA44 Collaboration (Bearden et al., 1997) and others (Braun-Munzinger et al., 1995, 1996) that reported freeze-out temperatures near 140 MeV; the paper reconciles this by showing those analyses used a non-relativistic extrapolation formula (Eq. 4) or a thermal model by Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz (1993) that neglected resonance decays, quantum statistics, and absolute normalization. The findings agree with the low freeze-out temperature conclusions of Csorgo and Lorstad (1995, 1996). The model enables future searches for a quark-gluon plasma signal through sharp discontinuities in freeze-out properties as functions of bombarding energy or nuclear size.
