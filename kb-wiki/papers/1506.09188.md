# Electromagnetic multipoles - theory issues

> Some predictions of the Hypercentral Constituent Quark Model for the helicity amplitudes are discussed and compared with data and with the recent analysis of the Mainz group; the role of the pion cloud contribution in explaining the major part of the missing strength at low $Q^2$ is emphasized.

## Key claim

The Hypercentral Constituent Quark Model (hCQM) reproduces the overall Q² behavior of helicity amplitudes for nucleon resonances, but systematically underestimates the strength at low Q²; the missing strength can be largely attributed to pion cloud contributions rather than to relativistic boosting effects.

## Method

The authors use the hCQM with a confining hypercentral potential V(x) = -τ/x + αx plus a standard hyperfine interaction treated as a perturbation, with parameters fixed from the non-strange baryon spectrum. They calculate the transverse helicity amplitudes A₁/₂ and A₃/₂ for electromagnetic transitions from the nucleon to various resonances in the Breit frame, then compare these predictions to experimental data and to the dynamical model calculations of the Mainz group that include explicit pion cloud contributions.

## Key numbers

- The confining potential parameters are τ = 4.59 and α = 1.61 fm⁻², with the hyperfine strength fixed by the N-Δ mass difference.
- The hCQM gives a proton r.m.s. radius of 0.48 fm, matching the value fitted to the D13 photocoupling in earlier work.
- The pion cloud contribution to the N-Δ A₃/₂ amplitude is significant at low Q² and diminishes strongly up to 3 GeV².

## Context

This work extends the hCQM predictions for helicity amplitudes previously published by the same group (Giannini, 2001; Aiello et al., 1998) and compares them with data from TJNAF (Dytman, 2003) and the Mainz group's dynamical model (DMT). The analysis shows that the low-Q² discrepancies, which are common to all constituent quark models, can be largely explained by pion cloud effects, as calculated by the Mainz group (L. Tiator et al., 2003), rather than by relativistic corrections (which were ruled out in earlier work by M. De Sanctis et al., 2002). This enables a clearer separation between quark-core and meson-cloud contributions to nucleon structure.
