# Second-order spin hydrodynamics from Zubarev's nonequilibrium statistical operator formalism

> Using the Zubarev's nonequilibrium statistical operator formalism, we derive the second-order expression for the dissipative tensors in relativistic spin hydrodynamics, {\em viz.} rotational stress tensor ($τ_{μν}$), boost heat vector ($q_μ$), shear stress tensor ($π_{μν}$), and bulk viscous pressure ($Π$). The first two ($τ_{μν}$ and $q_μ$) emerge due to the inclusion of the antisymmetric part in the energy-momentum tensor, which, in turn, governs the conservation of spin angular momentum ($Σ^{αμν}$). As a result, new thermodynamic forces, generated due to the antisymmetric part of $T_{μν}$, contain the spin chemical potential. In this work, we have also taken the spin density ($S^{μν}$) as an independent thermodynamic variable, in addition to the energy density and particle density, thereby resulting in two novel transport coefficients given by the correlation between spin density tensor and rotational stress tensor and vice versa. Additionally, the newly found terms in $π_{μν}$ and $Π$ are the artifacts of the new thermodynamic forces that arise due to the antisymmetric part of $T^{μν}$. Finally, we have derived the evolution equations for the aforesaid tensors: $τ_{μν}$, $q_μ$, $π_{μν}$, and $Π$.

## Key claim

Using Zubarev's nonequilibrium statistical operator formalism, the authors derive second-order expressions for dissipative tensors in relativistic spin hydrodynamics, with the key condition that the spin chemical potential ($\Omega_{\alpha\beta}$) is treated as first-order in gradient expansion ($\mathcal{O}(\partial^1)$) while spin density ($S^{\alpha\beta}$) is treated as zeroth-order ($\mathcal{O}(\partial^0)$), a physically motivated ordering scheme that differs from alternative conventions in the literature.

## Method

The authors employ Zubarev's nonequilibrium statistical operator (NESO) formalism, which extends the Gibbs canonical ensemble by incorporating an infinitesimal source term into the Liouville equation to introduce irreversibility. They decompose the nonequilibrium statistical operator into equilibrium and nonequilibrium parts, then expand the statistical average of dissipative tensor operators around local equilibrium up to second order in gradients, expressing transport coefficients in Kubo-type formulas involving two-point and three-point correlation functions. The antisymmetric part of the energy-momentum tensor is included alongside the symmetric part, with spin density treated as an independent thermodynamic variable in addition to energy density and particle density.

## Key numbers

- The rotational stress tensor ($\tau_{\mu\nu}$) and boost heat vector ($q_\mu$) emerge at first order with transport coefficients $\gamma$ (rotational viscosity) and $\lambda$ (boost heat conductivity), respectively, as derived in prior work by Hattori (2019)
- The shear stress tensor ($\pi_{\mu\nu}$) and bulk viscous pressure ($\Pi$) at first order have transport coefficients $\eta$ (shear viscosity) and $\zeta$ (bulk viscosity), respectively, with $\pi^{\mu\nu} = 2\eta\sigma^{\mu\nu}$ and $\Pi = -\zeta\theta$
- Two novel transport coefficients arise from the correlation between spin density tensor and rotational stress tensor, and vice versa, due to treating spin density as an independent thermodynamic variable

## Context

This work extends the first-order spin hydrodynamics framework developed by Hattori (2019) and Fukushima (2020), where boost heat conductivity and rotational viscosity were first identified, to second order in gradients. The second-order theory is necessary to restore causality (first-order theory is acausal) and may enable numerical implementation of causal spin hydrodynamic theory to address the "spin sign problem" in heavy-ion collisions, where experimental data on differential spin polarization of $\Lambda$ hyperons from STAR (2017, 2018, 2019) cannot be explained by standard relativistic hydrodynamics. The approach contrasts with alternative methods such as quantum kinetic theory (Florkowski 2017, Weickgenannt 2020), entropy current analysis (Becattini 2023, Biswas 2023), and the equilibrium partition function approach (Jensen 2012, Gallegos 2021).
