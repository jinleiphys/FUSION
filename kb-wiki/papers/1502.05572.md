# Anisotropic parton escape is the dominant source of azimuthal anisotropy in transport models

> We trace the development of elliptic anisotropy ($v_2$) via parton-parton collision history in two transport models. The parton $v_2$ is studied as a function of the number of collisions of each parton in Au+Au and $d$+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{_{\rm NN}}}=200$ GeV. It is found that the majority of $v_2$ comes from the anisotropic escape probability of partons, with no fundamental difference at low and high transverse momenta. The contribution to $v_2$ from hydrodynamic-type collective flow is found to be small. Only when the parton-parton cross-section is set unrealistically large does this contribution start to take over. Our findings challenge the current paradigm emerged from hydrodynamic comparisons to anisotropy data.

## Key claim

The majority of azimuthal anisotropy ($v_n$) in transport models arises from the anisotropic escape probability of partons, not from hydrodynamic-type collective flow, and this holds for both low and high transverse momenta; the contribution from collective flow only becomes dominant when the parton-parton cross-section is set to unrealistically large values.

## Method

The authors used two transport models, AMPT (with string melting) and MPC, to simulate Au+Au (impact parameter $b=7.3$ fm) and $d$+Au ($b=0$ fm) collisions at $\sqrt{s_{_{\rm NN}}}=200$ GeV. They traced the full collision history of each parton by recording the number of collisions ($N_{\rm coll}$) it suffered before freezeout, and computed the parton $v_2$ and $v_3$ as functions of $N_{\rm coll}$. To isolate the escape contribution, they performed a control calculation in AMPT where outgoing parton azimuthal directions were randomized after each scattering, eliminating collective flow while preserving the escape mechanism.

## Key numbers

- In AMPT with Debye cross-section $\sigma=3$ mb, the average number of collisions per parton is $\langle N_{\rm coll}\rangle=4.6$ for Au+Au and 1.2 for $d$+Au.
- The final-state parton $v_2$ in AMPT Au+Au with $\sigma=3$ mb is 3.9%; in the azimuth-randomized control (escape-only) it is 2.7%, giving an escape contribution ratio $\langle v_2\rangle_{\rm Rndm}/\langle v_2\rangle = 69\%$.
- In MPC Au+Au with isotropic $\sigma=5.5$ mb, $\langle N_{\rm coll}\rangle=4.7$ and $\langle v_2\rangle=5.5\%$; the escape ratio is 64%.
- At the largest cross-section studied (MPC isotropic $\sigma=60$ mb), $\langle N_{\rm coll}\rangle=53$ and $\langle v_2\rangle=10\%$, yet the escape ratio is still 26%.
- For $d$+Au in AMPT with $\sigma=3$ mb, $\langle v_2\rangle=2.7\%$ and the escape ratio is 93%.

## Context

This work challenges the prevailing paradigm, established by hydrodynamic comparisons to data (Heinz 2013; Gale 2013), that large elliptic flow in heavy-ion collisions arises from nearly perfect fluid behavior with a small shear viscosity to entropy density ratio. The authors show that transport models like AMPT (Lin 2004) and MPC (Molnar 2000) generate most of their $v_2$ through anisotropic escape of partons from the collision zone, a mechanism distinct from hydrodynamic collective flow. This finding implies that the small $\eta/s$ values extracted from hydrodynamic fits may be severely underestimated, and it calls for re-examination of the escape mechanism within the hydrodynamic framework, similar to how continuous particle emission was shown to affect pion interferometry (Grassi 1994).
