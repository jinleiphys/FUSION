# Substantially enhanced deuteron-triton fusion probabilities in intense low-frequency laser fields

> Deuteron-triton (DT) fusion is the primary fusion reaction used in controlled fusion research, mainly for its relatively high reaction cross sections compared to other fusion options. Even so, to attain appreciable reaction probabilities very high temperatures (on the order of 10-100 million kelvins) are required, which are extremely challenging to achieve and maintain. We show that intense low-frequency laser fields, such as those in the near-infrared regime for the majority of intense laser facilities around the world, are highly effective in transferring energy to the DT system and enhancing the DT fusion probabilities. The fusion probabilities are shown to be enhanced by at least an order of magnitude in 800-nm laser fields with intensities on the order of 10$^{21}$ W/cm$^2$. The demanding temperature requirement of controlled nuclear fusion may be relaxed if intense low-frequency lasers are exploited.

# Substantially enhanced deuteron-triton fusion probabilities in intense low-frequency laser fields

## Key claim

Intense low-frequency laser fields (e.g., 800 nm) can enhance DT fusion probabilities by at least an order of magnitude, but this enhancement is achieved by transferring energy to the relative-motion particle via the Volkov state mechanism, not by altering the nuclear cross section itself; the effect is strongest at low bare energies (e.g., 1 keV) and drops as energy increases.

## Method

The author models the deuteron-triton system in the center-of-mass frame as a single particle with effective charge q = 0.2e, described by a Volkov state in a linearly polarized laser field. The Volkov wavefunction is expanded in photon-number components, yielding probabilities P_n for each energy E_n = E + U_p + nω, where U_p is the ponderomotive energy. An angle-averaged effective fusion cross section σ_L is computed by summing the product of P_n and the standard DT fusion cross section (using the Bosch-Hale parametrization) over all photon numbers n and averaging over the angle θ between particle momentum and laser polarization.

## Key numbers

- The effective charge for relative motion is q = 0.2e.
- For 800 nm laser wavelength (photon energy 1.55 eV) at intensity 1×10^20 W/cm^2 and bare energy E = 5 keV, the populated energy range is 3.0 keV < E_n < 7.3 keV, with photon numbers −1400 < n < 1400.
- For 400 nm at the same intensity and bare energy, the populated range is 4.0 keV < E_n < 6.2 keV, with −350 < n < 350.
- For 100 nm at the same intensity and bare energy, the populated range is 4.7 keV < E_n < 5.3 keV, with −25 < n < 25.
- At E = 1 keV with 800 nm and intensity 5×10^21 W/cm^2, the enhancement is over 9 orders of magnitude relative to the laser-free cross section of 1.37×10^−11 barn.
- At E = 5 keV with the same laser parameters, the enhancement is over 2 orders of magnitude.
- At E = 10 keV with the same laser parameters, the enhancement is over 1 order of magnitude.
- The angle-averaged σ_L at 800 nm and 5×10^21 W/cm^2 is 0.0272 barn for E = 1 keV and 0.423 barn for E = 10 keV.
- The laser-free DT fusion cross section at 10 keV is 0.027 barn.

## Context

This work extends qualitative estimates by Queisser and Schützhold (2019) using Floquet scattering and by Lv et al. (2019) using the Kramers-Henneberger approximation, which were limited to high-frequency (X-ray) lasers where photon numbers are small. The present analysis uses the Volkov state formalism, widely successful in strong-field atomic physics (Keldysh 1965, Faisal 1973, Reiss 1980, Lewenstein 1994), to handle the large photon numbers (exceeding 10,000) that arise when intense low-frequency lasers interact with the DT system. The results suggest that the demanding temperature requirement of controlled fusion (10-100 million kelvins) may be relaxed by exploiting intense low-frequency laser fields, and that longer wavelengths are more efficient than shorter ones for this purpose.
