# Improved microscopic-macroscopic approach incorporating the effects of continuum states

> The Woods-Saxon-Strutinsky method (the microscopic-macroscopic method) combined with Kruppa's prescription for positive energy levels, which is necessary to treat neutron rich nuclei, is studied to clarify the reason for its success and to propose improvements for its shortcomings. The reason why the plateau condition is met for the Nilsson model but not for the Woods-Saxon model is understood in a new interpretation of the Strutinsky smoothing procedure as a low-pass filter. Essential features of Kruppa's level density is extracted in terms of the Thomas-Fermi approximation modified to describe spectra obtained from diagonalization in truncated oscillator bases. A method is proposed which weakens the dependence on the smoothing width by applying the Strutinsky smoothing only to the deviations from a reference level density. The BCS equations are modified for the Kruppa's spectrum, which is necessary to treat the pairing correlation properly in the presence of continuum. The potential depth is adjusted for the consistency between the microscopic and macroscopic Fermi energies. It is shown, with these improvements, that the microscopic-macroscopic method is now capable to reliably calculate binding energies of nuclei far from stability.

## Key claim

The Woods-Saxon-Strutinsky method can be made reliable for calculating binding energies of nuclei far from stability by combining Kruppa's prescription for continuum states with three specific improvements: a reference level density to weaken smoothing-width dependence, modified BCS equations for Kruppa's spectrum, and automatic adjustment of the potential depth to match macroscopic Fermi energies.

## Method

The authors diagonalize the Woods-Saxon single-particle Hamiltonian (with central, spin-orbit, and Coulomb terms) in an anisotropic harmonic oscillator basis, then apply the Strutinsky shell correction method using Kruppa's prescription to replace the divergent continuum level density with the continuum level density derived from scattering phase shifts. They reinterpret the Strutinsky smoothing as a low-pass filter in Fourier space, propose applying the smoothing only to deviations from a reference level density obtained via a modified Thomas-Fermi approximation for oscillator-basis spectra, and modify the BCS pairing equations to handle Kruppa's discretized continuum spectrum. The potential depth is adjusted iteratively to enforce consistency between the microscopic and macroscopic Fermi energies.

## Key numbers

- The cutoff wavenumber for the Strutinsky filter is approximately \(k_p^{\rm cut} = 2\sqrt{p}\), where \(p\) is half the order of the polynomial part of the smoothing function.
- The half-maximum point of the Fourier-transformed smoothing function occurs at \(k \approx 2\sqrt{p}\) (except for \(p=0\)), with the drop from 90% to 10% of maximum spanning approximately 2.5 in \(k\) independent of \(p\).
- The standard harmonic oscillator energy used is \(\hbar\omega = 41/A^{1/3}\) MeV, and the Woods-Saxon potential is diagonalized with oscillator frequency \(\omega_0 = 1.2\,\omega\).
- The recommended basis truncation for avoiding divergence in the conventional approach is \(\noscmax \approx 12\) oscillator shells.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic-macroscopic (Woods-Saxon-Strutinsky) method of Cwiok et al. (1987) and the Kruppa prescription (1998) for continuum level densities, addressing difficulties identified by Nazarewicz et al. (1994) regarding shell correction ambiguity, pairing treatment, and Fermi energy inconsistency in weakly bound nuclei. The improvements enable reliable binding energy calculations for neutron-rich nuclei near the drip line, where selfconsistent HFB calculations (e.g., Dobaczewski and Nazarewicz, 2007) and the shell correction approach often give divergent predictions. The method provides a computationally efficient alternative to full coordinate-space HFB for studying shell effects and pairing in unstable nuclei.
