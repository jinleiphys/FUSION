# Bose-Einstein correlations in thermal field theory

> Two-particle correlation functions are calculated for bosons emitted from a localized thermal source (the ``glow'' of a ``hot spot''). In contrast to existing work, non-equilibrium effects up to first order in gradients of the particle distribution function are taken into account. The spectral width of the bosons is shown to be an important quantity: If it is too small, they do not equilibrate locally and therefore strongly increase the measured correlation radius. In memoriam of Eugene Wigner and Hiroomi Umezawa.

## Key claim

Non-equilibrium effects up to first order in gradients of the particle distribution function can significantly increase the measured Bose-Einstein correlation radius for bosons emitted from a localized thermal source, but only when the spectral width of the bosons is sufficiently small; the abstract's claim that a small spectral width "strongly increase[s] the measured correlation radius" is conditional on the product of the spectral width and the source rms radius being much less than one.

## Method

The authors formulate the two-particle correlation function for bosons using thermo field dynamics (TFD) for a static, spatially inhomogeneous temperature distribution. They expand the quantum fields in terms of generalized free fields with a continuous mass spectrum, characterized by a spectral function that includes a nonzero spectral width gamma. The correlation function is calculated to first order in gradients of the local Bose-Einstein distribution, and the results are compared to the local equilibrium approximation where gradient terms are neglected.

## Key numbers

- For a simple parameterization with constant spectral width gamma = 50 MeV, the non-equilibrium correlation function agrees well with the local equilibrium result and closely resembles a gaussian.
- For gamma = 5 MeV, the non-equilibrium correlation function is much narrower in momentum space than the local equilibrium result and deviates from a gaussian form.
- The boundary between two regimes occurs at 2 gamma R_rms[gamma] = 1, corresponding to gamma approximately 30.2 MeV for the simple spectral function.
- For 2 gamma R_rms much less than 1, the measured correlation radius R approximately equals R_loc + 1/gamma, which is much greater than R_rms.
- For 2 gamma R_rms much greater than 1, R approximately equals R_loc, which is slightly greater than R_rms.
- With a semi-realistic pion spectral function including coupling to Delta_33 resonances, at central temperature T_0 = 100 MeV and pair momentum 100 MeV, the measured correlation radius R = 3.99 fm compared to the local equilibrium radius R_loc = 2.89 fm and the rms radius R_rms = 3.14 fm, giving R/R_rms = 1.27.
- At T_0 = 160 MeV and pair momentum 100 MeV, R = 4.86 fm, R_loc = 3.23 fm, R_rms = 3.51 fm, giving R/R_rms = 1.38.
- At T_0 = 100 MeV and pair momentum 350 MeV, R = 3.23 fm, R_loc = 3.16 fm, R_rms = 3.34 fm, giving R/R_rms = 0.97.
- At T_0 = 160 MeV and pair momentum 350 MeV, R = 3.57 fm, R_loc = 3.49 fm, R_rms = 3.73 fm, giving R/R_rms = 0.96.

## Context

This work extends the standard treatment of Bose-Einstein correlations, which approximates a non-equilibrium thermal source as a superposition of classical currents with a gaussian space-time distribution (GKW79, P84, DS92). The authors use thermo field dynamics (TFD) rather than the Schwinger-Keldysh method to incorporate non-equilibrium effects beyond the local equilibrium approximation. The results enable a reinterpretation of experimental correlation radii from heavy-ion collisions: the larger correlation radius measured by NA49 in central Pb+Pb collisions (6-7 fm) compared to S+Au collisions (4.5 fm) might indicate a higher temperature of the reaction zone rather than a bigger source size, depending on the momentum range of the measured pions.
