# Charged Particle Multiplicities in A+A and p+p Collisions in the Constituent Quarks Framework

> Charged particle multiplicities in A+A and p(pbar)+p collisions as a function of pseudorapidity, centrality and energy are studied in both the nucleon and the constituent quark frameworks. In the present work, the calculation of the number of nucleon and constituent quark participants using the nuclear overlap model takes into account the fact that for the peripheral A+A and p+p collisions can not be smaller than two. A striking agreement is seen between the particle density in A+A and p(pbar)+p collisions, both at mid-rapidity and in the fragmentation regions, when normalized to the number of participating constituent quarks. The observations presented in this paper imply that the number of constituent quark pairs participating in the collision controls the particle production. One may therefore conjecture that the initial states in A+A and p(pbar)+p collisions are similar when the partonic considerations are used in normalization.

# Charged Particle Multiplicities in A+A and p+p Collisions in the Constituent Quarks Framework

## Key claim

Charged particle multiplicities in A+A and p(pbar)+p collisions exhibit a striking agreement at both mid-rapidity and in fragmentation regions when normalized to the number of participating constituent quarks, but only after correcting a previous calculation error that allowed the number of participants to fall below two in peripheral collisions. This agreement implies that the initial states in A+A and p(pbar)+p collisions are similar when partonic considerations are used in normalization, though the author presents this as a conjecture rather than a definitive conclusion.

## Method

The author calculates the number of nucleon participants and constituent quark participants using a nuclear overlap model with a Woods-Saxon density profile for nuclei and a sharp sphere profile for protons, introducing a modification that prevents the number of participants from being smaller than two in peripheral A+A and p+p collisions. For constituent quarks, the calculation uses three times the nucleon density (0.51 fm^-3), cross sections equal to sigma_NN/9, and mass numbers three times their values while keeping nuclear sizes unchanged. The charged particle densities from PHOBOS Au+Au and Cu+Cu data at RHIC energies (19.6, 62.4, and 200 GeV) and from p(pbar)+p data at multiple energies are then compared under both nucleon and constituent quark normalizations.

## Key numbers

- For Au+Au at 200 GeV, the number of constituent quark participants (N_q_part) ranges from 952.5 for 0-3% centrality to 123.1 for 45-50% centrality
- For Au+Au at 62.4 GeV, N_q_part ranges from 907.2 (0-3%) to 112.8 (45-50%)
- For Au+Au at 19.6 GeV, N_q_part ranges from 869.4 (0-3%) to 105.1 (45-50%)
- For p(pbar)+p at 200 GeV, the minimum-bias N_q_part is 3.29, and for 0-6% central collisions it is 4.94
- For p(pbar)+p at 900 GeV, the minimum-bias N_q_part is 3.57, and for 0-6% central collisions it is 5.72
- The constituent quark cross section at 200 GeV is 4.66 mb (sigma_NN/9)
- The nuclear density profile uses R_n = 1.16 A^1/3 - (1.16)^2 A^-1/3 fm and d = 0.54 fm
- The constituent quark density n_0^q = 3 n_0 = 0.51 fm^-3

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Bhasin (2005), Nouicer (2006), and Eremin (2003) that used an older version of the participant calculation which allowed the number of nucleon and constituent quark participants to fall below two in peripheral collisions, a problem also noted by Kharzeev (2004) for d+Au collisions using an optical approach. The paper extends the constituent quark scaling analysis from Au+Au at 200 GeV (previously studied by Eremin (2003)) to 19.6 and 62.4 GeV, and adds Cu+Cu data and p(pbar)+p comparisons. The results enable a unified description of particle production across different collision systems and energies, suggesting that the initial states in A+A and p+p collisions are similar when viewed at the partonic level, which could inform future theoretical work on the initial conditions of heavy-ion collisions.
