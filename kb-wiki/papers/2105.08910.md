# Ab initio nuclear structure via quantum adiabatic algorithm

> Background: Solving nuclear many-body problems with an ab initio approach is widely recognized as a computationally challenging problem. Quantum computers offer a promising path to address this challenge. There are urgent needs to develop quantum algorithms for this purpose. Objective: In this work, we explore the application of the quantum algorithm of adiabatic state preparation with quantum phase estimation in ab initio nuclear structure theory. We focus on solving the low-lying spectra (including both the ground and excited states) of simple nuclear systems. Ideas: The efficiency of this algorithm is hindered by the emergence of small energy gaps (level crossings) during the adiabatic evolution. In order to improve the efficiency, we introduce techniques to avoid level crossings: 1) by suitable design of the reference Hamiltonian; 2) by insertions of perturbation terms to modify the adiabatic path. Results: We illustrate this algorithm by solving the deuteron ground state energy and the spectrum of the deuteron bounded in a harmonic oscillator trap implementing the IBM Qiskit quantum simulator. The quantum results agree well the classical results obtained by matrix diagonalization. Outlook: With our improvements to the efficiency, this algorithm provides a promising tool for investigating the low-lying spectra of complex nuclei on future quantum computers.

## Key claim

The quantum adiabatic state preparation with quantum phase estimation (ASP-QPE) algorithm can solve low-lying spectra of simple nuclear systems, but its efficiency is hindered by small energy gaps (level crossings) during the adiabatic evolution; the authors introduce two techniques to avoid these crossings: suitable design of the reference Hamiltonian and insertion of perturbation terms to modify the adiabatic path.

## Method

The authors apply the adiabatic state preparation (ASP) algorithm to evolve eigenstates of a diagonal reference Hamiltonian into eigenstates of the target nuclear Hamiltonian along a time-dependent path, then use quantum phase estimation (QPE) to extract eigenenergies. For the natural deuteron, the reference Hamiltonian is constructed as a harmonic oscillator plus the diagonal part of the nuclear interaction and a shift term; for the trapped deuteron, a perturbation term is added to the adiabatic path during the period of level crossing to strengthen the interplay between crossing levels. The algorithm is implemented on the IBM Qiskit quantum simulator using a compact mapping scheme that encodes the 4-dimensional model space onto 2 qubits.

## Key numbers

* Deuteron ground state energy: recovered at evolution time T = 1 MeV^{-1} using the ASP-QPE algorithm on the IBM Qiskit simulator, with quantum results agreeing well with classical matrix diagonalization.
* Squared overlap between ASP-prepared trial ground state and exact ground state: above 93% throughout the adiabatic evolution at T = 1 MeV^{-1}, and beyond 99.5% at T = 10 MeV^{-1}.
* Level crossing in trapped deuteron spectrum: occurs at t approximately 2.9 MeV^{-1} between the 2nd and 3rd excited states, with minimal gap size Delta E approximately 0.02 MeV.
* Harmonic oscillator energy: hbar omega = 5 MeV for the 3DHO basis.
* Trap strength parameter alpha = 0.5, with trap depth alpha V_depth set to -12 MeV.
* Adiabatic step length: delta t = 0.01 MeV^{-1}.
* Shift Hamiltonian entries: +3 MeV on first diagonal entry, -3 MeV on second diagonal entry, alternating for third and fourth entries.

## Context

This work extends the application of adiabatic state preparation with quantum phase estimation from quantum chemistry (Aspuru-Guzik 2005, Whitfield 2011) to ab initio nuclear structure, building on prior no-core shell model approaches (Barrett 2013, Navratil 2000). The variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) method had previously been applied to the deuteron (Dumitrescu 2018, DiMatteo 2020), but the ASP-QPE approach avoids the need for designing trial wavefunctions and performing high-dimensional classical optimization. The techniques for avoiding level crossings enable more efficient computation of low-lying spectra for complex nuclei on future quantum computers, though the deep circuits required make near-term implementation challenging.
