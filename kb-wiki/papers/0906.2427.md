# Impact of Strange Quark Matter Nuggets on Pycnonuclear Reaction Rates in the Crusts of Neutron Stars

> This paper presents an investigation into the pycnonuclear reaction rates in dense crustal matter of neutron stars contaminated with strange quark matter nuggets. The presence of such nuggets in the crustal matter of neutron stars would be a natural consequence if Witten's strange quark matter hypothesis is correct. The methodology presented in this paper is a recreation of a recent representation of nuclear force interactions embedded within pycnonuclear reaction processes. The study then extends the methodology to incorporate distinctive theoretical characteristics of strange quark matter nuggets, like their low charge-per-baryon ratio, and then assesses their effects on the pycnonuclear reaction rates. Particular emphasis is put on the impact of color superconductivity on the reaction rates. Depending on whether or not quark nuggets are in this novel state of matter, their electric charge properties vary drastically which turns out to have a dramatic effect on the pycnonuclear reaction rates. Future nuclear fusion network calculations may thus have the potential to shed light on the existence of strange quark matter nuggets and on whether or not they are in a color superconducting state, as suggested by QCD.

## Key claim

If strange quark matter nuggets (strangelets) exist in neutron star crusts, their presence could dramatically alter pycnonuclear reaction rates, with the effect depending critically on whether the strangelets are in a color-flavor-locked (CFL) superconducting state or a non-CFL state, because their electric charge properties differ drastically between these two phases.

## Method

The authors recreated a recent pycnonuclear reaction rate methodology based on the Sao Paulo model for nuclear interactions, which uses a double-folding procedure with a velocity-dependent nucleon-nucleon interaction to account for Pauli nonlocality, combined with a WKB barrier penetration calculation for the astrophysical S-factor. They then extended this framework by replacing one of the two reacting nuclei with a strangelet, modifying only the strangelet's mass, radius, density, and charge according to theoretical models of strange quark matter, while leaving the nuclear interaction methodology unchanged. The pycnonuclear reaction rates were computed for a body-centered cubic lattice using the static lattice approximation, with calculations limited to a single primitive lattice cell containing one strangelet surrounded by normal matter ions.

## Key numbers

- For ordinary (non-CFL) strangelets with baryon number A much less than 10^3, the charge is approximately Z ≈ 0.1 × m₁₅₀² × A, where m₁₅₀ ≡ m_s / 150 MeV and m_s is the strange quark mass
- For ordinary strangelets with A much greater than 10^3, the charge is approximately Z ≈ 8 × m₁₅₀² × A^{1/3}
- For CFL strangelets, the charge-to-mass ratio gives Z ≈ 0.3 × m₁₅₀ × A^{2/3}
- Tidally-released strange quark matter from binary neutron star mergers may produce strangelets with baryon numbers A ≈ 10^2 to 10^3
- The nucleon-nucleon interaction strength parameter V₀ = -456 MeV fm³ was used in the folding potential
- The matter diffuseness parameter for a nucleon a_m = 0.3 fm was used in the nucleon-nucleon interaction
- The pycnonuclear reaction rate formula for a bcc lattice includes a factor of 3.90 × 10⁴⁶ s⁻¹ multiplied by density, mass numbers, charges, S-factor, and λ^{7/4} exp(-2.638/λ^{1/2})

## Context

This work extends the pycnonuclear reaction rate methodology developed by the Sao Paulo group and the Joint Institute of Nuclear Astrophysics (JINA) at the University of Notre Dame, which previously calculated rates for ordinary nuclear matter in neutron star crusts. The study is motivated by Witten's strange quark matter hypothesis and the possibility that neutron stars may contain strange quark matter, as explored by Alcock (1986), Alcock (1988), and Madsen (1998). The results enable future nuclear fusion network calculations that could potentially test for the existence of strange quark matter nuggets and determine whether they are in a color superconducting state, as suggested by quantum chromodynamics.
