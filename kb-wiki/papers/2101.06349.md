# g-mode Oscillations in Hybrid Stars: A Tale of Two Sounds

> We study the principal core g-mode oscillation in hybrid stars containing quark matter and find that they have an unusually large frequency range ($\approx$ 200 - 600 Hz) compared to ordinary neutron stars or self-bound quark stars of the same mass. Theoretical arguments and numerical calculations that trace this effect to the difference in the behaviour of the equilibrium and adiabatic sound speeds in the mixed phase of quarks and nucleons are provided. We propose that the sensitivity of core g-mode oscillations to non-nucleonic matter in neutron stars could be due to the presence of a mixed quark-nucleon phase. Based on our analysis, we conclude that for binary mergers where one or both components may be a hybrid star, the fraction of tidal energy pumped into resonant g-modes in hybrid stars can exceed that of a normal neutron star by a factor of 2-3, although resonance occurs during the last stages of inspiral. A self-bound star, on the other hand, has a much weaker tidal overlap with the g-mode. The cumulative tidal phase error in hybrid stars, $Δϕ\cong$ 0.5 rad, is comparable to that from tides in ordinary neutron stars, presenting a challenge in distinguishing between the two cases. However, should the principal g-mode be excited to sufficient amplitude for detection in a post-merger remnant with quark matter in its interior, its frequency would be a possible indication for the existence of non-nucleonic matter in neutron stars.

## Key claim

The principal core g-mode in hybrid stars containing a mixed quark-nucleon phase has an unusually large frequency range (approximately 200-600 Hz) compared to ordinary neutron stars or self-bound quark stars of the same mass, driven by the difference between equilibrium and adiabatic sound speeds in the mixed phase rather than by a sharp phase transition.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic fluid oscillation equations within the Cowling approximation for non-rotating neutron stars, treating the g-mode as an eigenvalue problem for the fundamental (n=1, l=2) mode. They employed the Zhao-Lattimer (ZL) equation of state for nucleonic matter and the vMIT bag model for quark matter, using the Gibbs construction to model a smooth hadron-to-quark phase transition with a mixed phase of nucleons and quarks. Analytical expressions for the equilibrium and adiabatic sound speeds in the mixed phase were derived and used to compute the Brunt-Vaisala frequency and resulting g-mode frequencies across a range of stellar masses.

## Key numbers

- Principal core g-mode frequency range in hybrid stars: approximately 200-600 Hz, exceeding typical core g-mode frequencies of nucleonic stars by a factor of two or more
- The fraction of tidal energy pumped into resonant g-modes in hybrid stars can exceed that of a normal neutron star by a factor of 2-3 during binary mergers
- Cumulative tidal phase error in hybrid stars: Delta-phi approximately 0.5 rad, comparable to that from tides in ordinary neutron stars
- Central densities of observed neutron stars lie in the range 3-8 times nuclear saturation density (n_s approximately 0.16 fm^-3)
- vMIT model parameters explored: B^(1/4) = 155-180 MeV and a = (G_v/m_V)^2 = 0.1-0.3 fm^2

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of g-modes in neutron stars by Finn (1987), Strohmayer (1993), and McDermott (1990), and more recent work on superfluid hyperonic stars by Dommes and Gusakov (2015) which was restricted to a fixed mass of about 1.64 solar masses. The paper also updates results from Wei et al. (2020) by incorporating general relativistic effects in the Cowling approximation and using nuclear equations of state consistent with radius constraints from NICER data (Riley et al., Miller et al.) and tidal polarizability from GW170817 (Abbott et al.). The findings enable the possibility of using g-mode frequencies as a diagnostic for non-nucleonic matter in neutron stars, particularly in post-merger remnants, though distinguishing hybrid stars from ordinary neutron stars via tidal phase errors alone remains challenging.
