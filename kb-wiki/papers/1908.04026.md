# Puzzling Radii of Calcium Isotopes: $^{40}{\rm Ca} \rightarrow ^{44}{\rm Ca} \rightarrow ^{48}{\rm Ca} \rightarrow ^{52}{\rm Ca}$, and Duality in the Structure of $^{42}_{14}{\rm Si}_{28}$ and $^{48}_{20}{\rm Ca}_{28}$

> In this paper we study the issue of the puzzle of the radii of calcium isotopes. Despite an excess of eight neutrons, strangely $^{48}{\rm Ca}$ exhibits essentially the same charge radius as $^{40}{\rm Ca}$ does. A fundamental microscopic description of this is still lacking. Also strange is a peak in charge radius of calcium at N = 24. The $^{52}{\rm Ca}$ (N = 32) nucleus, well known to be doubly magical, amazingly has recently been found to have a very large charge radius. Also amazing is the property of $^{42}_{14}{\rm Si}_{28}$ which simultaneously appears to be both magical/spherical and strongly deformed as well. We use a Quantum Chromodynamics based model, which treats triton as elementary entity to make up $^{42}_{14}{\rm Si}_{28}$. We show here how this QCD based model is able to provide a consistent physical understanding of simultaneity of magicity/sphericity and strong deformation of a single nucleus. This brings in an essential duality in the structure of $^{42}_{14}{\rm Si}_{28}$ and subsequently also that of $^{48}_{20}{\rm Ca}_{28}$ We also provide consistent understanding of the puzzling radii of calcium isotopes. We predict that the radius of $^{54}{\rm Ca}$ should be even bigger than that of $^{52}{\rm Ca}$; and also that the radius of $^{60}{\rm Ca}$ should be the same as that of $^{40}{\rm Ca}$. In addition we also show wherefrom arises the neutron E2 effective charge of $\frac{1}{2}$.

# Wiki Page: Puzzling Radii of Calcium Isotopes and Duality in Nuclear Structure

## Key claim

The authors propose that a Quantum Chromodynamics-based model treating tritons as elementary building blocks can resolve the apparent contradiction between experiments showing $^{42}_{14}\mathrm{Si}_{28}$ as both magically spherical (Fridmann et al., 2005) and strongly deformed (Bastin et al., 2007; Takeuchi et al., 2005), by invoking an essential duality in nuclear structure where the standard proton-neutron degree of freedom and a tritonic degree of freedom coexist at a fundamental level.

## Method

The authors apply a QCD-based model in which neutron-rich nuclei of the form ${^{3Z}_{\;\;Z}\mathrm{X}_{2Z}}$ are composed of Z tritons (${^{3}_{1}\mathrm{H}_{2}}$) as elementary entities. They analyze the shell structure of triton-rich nuclei by mapping proton number to triton number, and alternatively by mapping neutron number to triton number via isospin symmetry, generating dual descriptions. The model is applied to interpret experimental data on charge radii of calcium isotopes from Garcia Ruiz et al. (2016), $E(2^+)$ and $B(E2)$ values for silicon and calcium isotopes, and the three-point mass indicator $\Delta_3$ from Jurado et al. (2007).

## Key numbers

- $^{48}\mathrm{Ca}$ exhibits essentially the same charge radius as $^{40}\mathrm{Ca}$, despite an excess of eight neutrons (Garcia Ruiz et al., 2016)
- $^{52}\mathrm{Ca}$ (N=32) has a very large charge radius, challenging its doubly magic nature (Garcia Ruiz et al., 2016)
- The three-point indicator $\Delta_3$ for Z=14 (silicon) behaves similarly to Z=20 (calcium), showing constant single-particle energy between N=20 and N=27 (Jurado et al., 2007)
- The neutron E2 effective charge is predicted to be $\frac{1}{2}$, matching experimental values for $^{41}\mathrm{Ca}$ (de-Shalit, 1966)
- The isoscalar effective charge is predicted to be of magnitude unity ($\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1$)
- The model predicts the radius of $^{54}\mathrm{Ca}$ should be even bigger than that of $^{52}\mathrm{Ca}$
- The model predicts the radius of $^{60}\mathrm{Ca}$ should be the same as that of $^{40}\mathrm{Ca}$

## Context

This work extends the QCD-based triton model previously developed by Abbas (2001, 2018) which successfully identified halo nuclei and predicted tennis-ball (bubble) like nuclear structures. The paper addresses the long-standing puzzle of calcium isotope charge radii noted by Garcia Ruiz et al. (2016), where $^{48}\mathrm{Ca}$ has the same radius as $^{40}\mathrm{Ca}$ despite eight extra neutrons, and $^{52}\mathrm{Ca}$ shows an unexpectedly large radius. The duality concept resolves conflicting experimental claims about $^{42}\mathrm{Si}$ from Fridmann et al. (2005) (sphericity/magicity) and Bastin et al. (2007) plus Takeuchi et al. (2005) (strong deformation). The model also connects to the well-established concept of effective charge from de-Shalit (1966), providing a microscopic origin for the neutron E2 effective charge of $\frac{1}{2}$. The work enables predictions for radii of $^{54}\mathrm{Ca}$ and $^{60}\mathrm{Ca}$, and suggests that $^{24}\mathrm{O}$, $^{42}\mathrm{Si}$, and $^{60}\mathrm{Ca}$ form a triton-magic chain analogous to the $^{16}\mathrm{O}$, $^{28}\mathrm{Si}$, $^{40}\mathrm{Ca}$ chain in the standard shell model.
