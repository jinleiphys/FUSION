# Cluster mean field description of alpha emission

> We show that the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method is able to describe experimental values of alpha decay widths by including a residual nucleon-nucleon Surface Gaussian Interaction (SGI) within the standard procedure used to calculate the nuclear mean field. We call this method the Cluster HFB (CHFB) approach. In this way we correct the deficient asymptotic behaviour of the corresponding single-particle (sp) wave functions generated by the standard mean field. The corrected mean field becomes a sum between the standard mean Woods-Saxon-like field and a cluster Gaussian component centered at the same radius as the SGI. Thus, we give a confirmation of the mean field plus cluster potential structure, which was assumed in our previous work on alpha-decay widths. Systematic calculations evidence the linear correlation between the SGI strength and fragmentation potential, allowing for reliable predictions concerning the half lives of superheavy emitters.

## Key claim

The Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method can describe experimental alpha decay widths when a residual nucleon-nucleon Surface Gaussian Interaction (SGI) is included, correcting the deficient asymptotic behavior of single-particle wave functions from the standard mean field. The resulting mean field becomes a sum of a standard Woods-Saxon-like field and a cluster Gaussian component, confirming the mean field plus cluster potential structure assumed in the authors' previous work.

## Method

The authors added a Surface Gaussian Interaction (SGI) residual nucleon-nucleon potential, peaked on the nuclear surface, to the standard HFB mean field procedure, calling this the Cluster HFB (CHFB) approach. They solved the HFB equations and pairing gap equations simultaneously using this combined potential, then computed alpha decay formation amplitudes via a harmonic oscillator expansion with Talmi-Moshinsky recoupling coefficients. The decay widths were calculated using R-matrix theory with a factorization that includes a deformation correction via the Froman propagator matrix for quadrupole deformation.

## Key numbers

- The residual interaction strength \(x_c\) = 19 MeV reproduces the observed alpha decay width for \(^{242}\)Pu
- The geometrical touching radius for \(^{242}\)Pu is \(R_c\) = 9.38 fm
- The cluster Gaussian parameters satisfy \(R_{cl} = R_0\), \(b_{cl} = \sqrt{2}b_{cm} = b_{rel}/\sqrt{2}\) with \(b_{rel}\) = 2 fm and \(R_0 = 1.275(A_D^{1/3} + A_{\alpha}^{1/3})\)
- The mean pairing strength for alpha emitters with A > 150 is approximately 9 MeV for both pp and nn pairing, rising to roughly 10 MeV when the residual interaction is turned on
- The maximal formation amplitude component for \(^{242}\)Pu corresponds to cm radial quantum number \(N_{\alpha}\) = 12
- The plateau condition for the decay width is established at a little over 10 fm, about 1 fm beyond the geometrical contact radius
- Deformation-corrected calculations improve the decay width estimate by a factor of roughly 5 over spherical calculations for a given residual strength
- For superheavy emitters, calculated decay widths agree with experiment usually within a factor of 3
- The experimental uncertainty for \(^{266}_{106}\)Sg is 95% of the recommended half-life value

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous phenomenological approach (Delion 2013) that used a Woods-Saxon mean field plus a Gaussian clustering potential, now providing a microscopic justification via the HFB method with a surface-enhanced residual interaction. The linear correlation found between SGI strength and fragmentation potential confirms the phenomenological systematics of Dumitrescu (2022), and the method enables reliable predictions for superheavy alpha emitters where experimental data are scarce or uncertain. The approach contrasts with earlier shell-model calculations (Fliessbach 1976, Tonozuka 1979) that still deviated from experiment by more than an order of magnitude even with many configurations, and with the method of Varga (1992) that used a shell-model wave function plus a cluster component expanded in shifted Gaussians.
