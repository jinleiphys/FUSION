# Review of the k-Body Embedded Ensembles of Gaussian Random Matrices

> The embedded ensembles were introduced by Mon and French as physically more plausible stochastic models of many--body systems governed by one--and two--body interactions than provided by standard random--matrix theory. We review several approaches aimed at determining the spectral density, the spectral fluctuation properties, and the ergodic properties of these ensembles: moments methods, numerical simulations, the replica trick, the eigenvector decomposition of the matrix of second moments and supersymmetry, the binary correlation approximation, and the study of correlations between matrix elements.

## Key claim

This review argues that the k-body embedded ensembles (EGE(k)) are more physically plausible stochastic models for many-body systems than canonical random-matrix theory (RMT), but the paper's central hedge is that the spectral fluctuation properties of EGE(k) do not always coincide with canonical RMT predictions, particularly for Bosonic systems in the dense limit and for the low-lying part of spectra.

## Method

The authors review multiple analytical and numerical approaches to determining the spectral density, spectral fluctuations, and ergodic properties of EGE(k): moments methods (Mon and French, 1975), numerical simulations for both Fermionic and Bosonic systems, the replica trick (Verbaarschot and Zirnbauer, 1984), eigenvector decomposition of the matrix of second moments combined with supersymmetry, the binary correlation approximation, and studies of correlations between matrix elements. The ensembles are defined for m Fermions or Bosons in l degenerate single-particle states interacting via a random k-body interaction, with the limit l → ∞ taken to achieve infinite matrix dimension.

## Key numbers

* For Fermions in the dilute limit (l → ∞, m → ∞, m/l → 0), the spectral density is Gaussian, with the even moment M_{2p}(m) given by (2p - 1)!! (M_2(m))^p.
* For k = 1 and m = 9, the spectral shape is already very close to Gaussian.
* Early TBRE numerical simulations used matrices of dimension ≤ 61.
* For the TBRE, the Brody parameter for the nearest-neighbour spacing distribution was ω ≈ 0.80 ± 0.05 (Cota et al., 1974).
* For Bosonic EGE(k), numerical simulations used matrices of dimension 364 (l=4, m=11) and l=5, m=10, finding a Brody parameter ω = 0.95 for the nearest-neighbour spacing distribution (Patel et al., 2000).
* For Bosons with l=2 and m=3000, the Hilbert space dimension is N_B = (2 + 3000 - 1 choose 3000) = 3001.
* For Bosons in the dense limit (m → ∞ for fixed l and k), the fluctuations of centroids and widths of the spectrum do not vanish but attain constant values.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Mon and French (1975) who introduced EGE(k), and the numerical simulations of French and Wong (1970, 1971) and Bohigas and Flores (1971, 1971a) on the two-body random ensemble (TBRE). It contrasts with canonical RMT introduced by Wigner (1951) and classified by Dyson (1962), which predicts a semicircular spectral density and universal fluctuations. The review shows that while EGE(k) with k=2 often reproduces canonical RMT fluctuations at the center of the spectrum, recent work by Asaga et al. (2001, 2002) demonstrates non-ergodic behavior for Bosons in the dense limit, and work by Johnson et al. (1998) and Bijker and Frank (1999, 2000) shows that random two-body interactions can produce ordered low-lying spectra with J^π=0^+ ground states, contradicting canonical RMT expectations. The review enables future work on the transition from integrable to chaotic behavior in many-body systems as the number of single-particle states l increases.
