# Neutrino absortion cross sections in supernova environment

> We study charged-current neutrino cross sections on neutronrich nuclei in the mass $A\sim60$ region. Special attention is paid to environmental effects, i.e. finite temperature and density, on the cross sections. As these effects are largest for small neutrino energies, it is sufficient to study only the Gamow-Teller (GT) contributions to the cross sections. The relevant GT strength distributions are derived from large-scale shell model calculations. We find that the low-energy cross sections are enhanced at finite temperatures. However, for $(ν_e,e^-)$ reactions Pauli blocking of the electrons in the final state makes the cross sections for low-energy neutrinos much smaller than for the competing inelastic scattering on electrons at moderate and large densities. Absorption cross sections for low-energy antineutrinos are strongly enhanced at finite temperatures.

# Neutrino absorption cross sections in supernova environment

## Key claim

Finite temperature enhances low-energy neutrino and antineutrino absorption cross sections on neutron-rich nuclei in the A~60 mass region, primarily through thermal population of "backresonances" (GT strength built on low-lying daughter states). However, for electron neutrino absorption under supernova core-collapse conditions, Pauli blocking of the outgoing electron by the degenerate electron gas makes the stellar absorption rates much smaller than competing inelastic scattering on electrons at moderate and large densities, limiting the practical importance of the temperature enhancement.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale shell model calculations to derive Gamow-Teller (GT) strength distributions for neutron-rich nuclei in the A~60 region (56Fe, 60,62Ni, 59,61Fe, 60,62Co). They computed charged-current neutrino and antineutrino absorption cross sections using a formalism that separates the nuclear response into a component obeying Brink's hypothesis (same as ground-state response) and a backresonance component built on low-lying daughter states, with the latter included via explicit shell-model B(GT) values. The stellar environment was modeled by a Boltzmann distribution of initial nuclear states, and electron Pauli blocking was incorporated via a Fermi-Dirac blocking factor with chemical potentials calculated for three representative density-temperature conditions.

## Key numbers

- For 56Fe at T=10^10 K (0.86 MeV), the backresonances are located at excitation energies E_i ~ 7-9 MeV, making them hardly populated at these temperatures.
- Electron chemical potentials for the three stellar conditions studied: 8.3 MeV (T=0.86 MeV, rho=10^10 g/cm^3), 18.1 MeV (T=1.29 MeV, rho=10^11 g/cm^3), and 36.2 MeV (T=1.72 MeV, rho=10^12 g/cm^3).
- For odd-odd nuclei like 60Co, backresonances reside at excitation energies E_i = 1-2 MeV, making them easily thermally accessible.
- For 60Co, the cross section at finite temperature increases by several orders of magnitude at low neutrino energies compared to the ground-state calculation.
- Antineutrino absorption cross sections are significantly affected by finite temperature for antineutrino energies E_nu_bar < 10 MeV, yielding finite and sizable results even below the reaction Q-value.

## Context

This work extends the earlier treatment of neutrino-nucleus reactions in supernova simulations by Bruenn and Haxton (1991), who had assumed that Brink's hypothesis made finite-temperature effects negligible. The authors show that "backresonances" (first noted by Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (1982) for stellar beta-decay) become important at the higher temperatures (T > 10^10 K) relevant for core contraction. The results indicate that for electron neutrino absorption, the ground-state cross section is adequate for the preheating scenario proposed by Haxton (1988), while for antineutrino absorption the temperature enhancement is strong enough that a representative nucleus may suffice for core-collapse simulations. The work also motivates future calculations including higher multipole operators and momentum-transfer dependence, which are in progress using the random phase approximation.
