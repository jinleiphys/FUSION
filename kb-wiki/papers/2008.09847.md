# Nanoplasmonic Laser Fusion. Response to Földes and Pokol -- Letter to the Editor

> Földes and Pokol in their letter "Inertial fusion without compression does not work either with or without nanoplasmonics" criticized our works \cite{CS2015,CKP2018}. Here we refute their argumentation. Our proposed improvement is the combination of two basic research discoveries: (i) The possibility of detonations on space-time hyper-surfaces with time-like normal (i.e. simultaneous detonation in a whole volume) and (ii) to increase the ignition volume to the whole target, by regulating the laser light absorption using nanoantennas. These principles can be realized in an in-line, one dimensional configuration, in the simplest way with two opposing laser beams as in particle colliders \cite{CsEA2020,Bonasera2019}.

# Nanoplasmonic Laser Fusion. Response to Földes and Pokol – Letter to the Editor

## Key claim

The authors argue that their proposed nanoplasmonic laser fusion scheme, which combines time-like detonations (simultaneous volume ignition) with nanoantenna-regulated laser absorption, can achieve ignition conditions in approximately 10-100 fs over most of the target volume, thereby avoiding the Rayleigh-Taylor instabilities and flame-front propagation that plague conventional inertial confinement fusion. The claim is hedged by the admission that the scheme requires limited pre-compression (not zero compression) and that the quantitative ignition rate advantage depends on achieving pre-acceleration to several GeV/nucleon and pre-compression by a factor of 8.

## Method

The authors propose a Laser Wake Field Collider (LWFC) configuration using two opposing laser beams to create counter-propagating Laser Wake Field (LWF) waves in a pre-compressed and pre-accelerated target. The target contains embedded nanoantennas (nano-shells or nano-rods) to regulate laser light absorption and enable simultaneous volume ignition via time-like detonations, where the entire target ignites on a space-time hypersurface with time-like normal within 10-100 fs, rather than relying on a central hot-spot with subsequent flame propagation.

## Key numbers

- Pre-compression factor of 8 is proposed, compared to 3-Dim compression reaching 800 g/cm³ at NIF.
- For ions accelerated to 5 GeV/nucleon, the Lorentz factor γ ≈ 6.
- The estimated burning rate in the LWFC scheme is approximately 270 times larger than at NIF, calculated as 2 · γ² · (8² n²)/(800 n) · (c/v_th) with v_th ≈ c/2.
- A typical laser pulse of 20 mJ intensity, 7 fs length, and λ wavelength creates a LWF dense plasma wave of about 10 λ wavelength.
- The target plasma density from LWFA is approximately 4 × 10¹⁹ / cm³.
- Preliminary calculations indicate constructive interaction of two LWF waves at around 125-150 fs after target and projectile touch.
- Ignition is targeted in approximately 10-100 fs, in contrast to the conventional ICF hot-spot formation time of ≈ 10-20 ns.

## Context

This letter responds to criticism by Földes and Pokol (2020) regarding the authors' earlier works (Csernai et al. 2015, Csernai et al. 2018). The proposed scheme extends the concept of time-like detonations, previously found theoretically and experimentally in high-energy heavy-ion collisions (Csernai 1987, Csernai et al. 2015), to laser-driven fusion. It builds on experimental work by Bonasera et al. (2019) demonstrating colliding laser beams achieving high target density, and on validation experiments at Wigner RCP, Budapest (Kroó et al. 2016) showing nanoantenna-mediated phase transitions at threshold energies. The approach contrasts with the NIF ablator-driven compression scheme and aims to enable fusion without the extreme compression (800 g/cm³) and associated Rayleigh-Taylor instabilities of conventional inertial confinement fusion.
