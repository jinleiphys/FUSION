# Transport coefficients from the lattice?

> The prospects of extracting transport coefficients from euclidean lattice simulations are discussed. Some general comments on the reconstruction of spectral functions using the Maximal Entropy Method are given as well.

# Transport coefficients from the lattice?

## Key claim
Extracting transport coefficients from Euclidean lattice simulations is extremely difficult in weakly-coupled field theories because the low-frequency part of the spectral function, which determines the transport coefficient, contributes only a constant term to the Euclidean correlator that cannot be easily disentangled from the high-frequency contribution.

## Method
The authors calculated the spectral function for shear viscosity at high temperature in weakly-coupled scalar λφ⁴ theory and nonabelian gauge theories, using the Kubo relation that connects transport coefficients to the slope of spectral functions at zero frequency and momentum. They then computed the Euclidean two-point function exactly at leading order in the coupling constant via the dispersion relation G(τ) = ∫ dω/(2π) K(τ,ω) ρ(ω), and analyzed how the low-frequency part of the spectral function manifests in the Euclidean correlator.

## Key numbers
- Shear viscosity η = (1/20) ∂/∂ω ρ_{ππ}(ω,0) evaluated at ω=0, where ρ_{ππ} is the spectral function of the traceless spatial energy-momentum tensor.
- Electrical conductivity σ = (1/6) ∂/∂ω ρ_{em}(ω,0) evaluated at ω=0, where ρ_{em} is the spectral function of the electromagnetic current.
- At large frequencies, the spectral function for shear viscosity in scalar theory increases as ρ_{ππ}(ω) = (ω⁴/96π) [1 + 2n_B(ω/2)].
- The low-frequency spectral function for shear viscosity has the approximate form ρ_{ππ}(ω)/T⁴ ∼ ωγ/(ω² + 4γ²) for ω ≪ T, where γ is the thermal damping rate.
- The Euclidean correlator G_{ππ}(τ) at leading order contains a τ-independent term proportional to (4a₂π²/45)T⁵, where a₂ = 1 for scalar theory and a₂ = 2(N_c²-1) for SU(N_c) theory.
- For the electromagnetic current correlator, the spectral function at small frequencies expands as ρ_{em}(ω)/T² = d₁(ω/T) + d₃(ω/T)³ + d₅(ω/T)⁵ + ..., where d₁ is proportional to the electrical conductivity.

## Context
This work extends the earlier lattice approach of Karsch and Wyld (1986) and Nakamura (1996) for extracting transport coefficients by fitting ansätze for spectral functions, and comments on the Maximal Entropy Method used by Asakawa (2000). The authors identify a fundamental difficulty: the low-frequency part of spectral functions, which contains transport coefficients, contributes a constant term to Euclidean correlators that is insensitive to the detailed shape of the spectral function. This result is generic for correlators of composite operators bilinear in the fields at finite temperature, due to the presence of pinching poles in the complex energy plane. The work enables future lattice studies to recognize that extracting transport coefficients requires either very precise data or alternative approaches beyond standard spectral function reconstruction methods.
