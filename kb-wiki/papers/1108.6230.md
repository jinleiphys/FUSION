# Determination of the Shear Viscosity Relaxation Time at Weak and Strong Coupling

> We investigate the microscopic origin of the relaxation time coefficient in relativistic fluid dynamics. We show that the extraction of the shear viscosity relaxation time via the gradient expansion is ambiguous and in general fails to give the correct result. The correct value for the shear viscosity relaxation time is extracted from the slowest non-hydrodynamic pole of the corresponding retarded Green's function, if such a pole is purely imaginary. According to the AdS/CFT correspondence, in strongly-coupled $\mathcal{N}=4$ SYM the non-hydrodynamic poles of the shear stress tensor nearest to the origin have a nonzero real part, which implies that the transient fluid-dynamical equations for this gauge theory are not equivalent to the well-known Israel-Stewart equations.

# Determination of the Shear Viscosity Relaxation Time at Weak and Strong Coupling

## Key claim

The extraction of the shear viscosity relaxation time via the gradient expansion is ambiguous and in general fails to give the correct result; the correct value must instead be extracted from the slowest non-hydrodynamic pole of the corresponding retarded Green's function, but only if that pole is purely imaginary. In strongly-coupled N=4 SYM theory, the non-hydrodynamic poles nearest to the origin have a nonzero real part, which implies that the transient fluid-dynamical equations for this gauge theory are not equivalent to the Israel-Stewart equations.

## Method

The authors analyze two distinct approaches to deriving relativistic fluid dynamics: the gradient-expansion method of Baier et al. (2008) for conformal fluids, which introduces the relaxation time as a regulator, and the pole-based method of Denicol et al. (2011), which derives fluid dynamics from the underlying microscopic theory by taking into account the slowest non-hydrodynamic mode for each dissipative current. They compare the relaxation times obtained from each method for dilute gases (using the Boltzmann equation) and for strongly-coupled N=4 SYM theory (using the AdS/CFT correspondence), showing that the gradient-expansion approach yields infinitely many possible values for the relaxation time while the pole method gives a unique result.

## Key numbers

- In strongly-coupled N=4 SYM theory, the relaxation time computed via the gradient-expansion prescription is tau_pi = lambda_1/(2 eta) = (2 - ln 2)/(2 pi T).
- For strongly-coupled N=4 SYM theory, the coefficients of the second-order relaxation equation derived from the retarded Green's function are: chi_1 = 1.27/(4 pi T), chi_2 = 0.93/(4 pi T)^2, and eta/s = 1/(4 pi).
- In the pole-based approach applied to dilute gases via the Boltzmann equation, the formulas for eta and tau_pi are consistent with those previously derived by Israel and Stewart (1979) via the 14-moment approximation.

## Context

This work extends the derivation of relativistic fluid dynamics by Denicol et al. (2011) and clarifies a fundamental ambiguity in the gradient-expansion approach of Baier et al. (2008) for conformal fluids. It shows that the relaxation time is of microscopic origin, not of fluid-dynamic origin, and that a truncated Taylor expansion around the origin cannot correctly obtain the relaxation time. The results enable a rigorous foundation for relativistic viscous hydrodynamics, particularly for strongly-coupled plasmas described by the AdS/CFT correspondence, and open the question of how to formulate transient fluid-dynamical equations when the non-hydrodynamic poles have nonzero real parts, as in N=4 SYM theory.
