# Confinement in QCD

> The guiding lines of lattice investigations on colour confinement are reviewed, together with recent results.

# Confinement in QCD

## Key claim

Colour confinement in QCD is produced by dual superconductivity of the vacuum, meaning that magnetic monopoles condense in the confined phase and produce a dual Meissner effect that constrains chromoelectric fields into flux tubes. The author states that while there is "definite evidence" for this mechanism, the choice of abelian projection field $\vec\Phi(x)$ remains unspecified, and there is no rigorous proof that all monopole species condense, though lattice observations support their equivalence.

## Method

The paper reviews lattice QCD simulations that construct a disorder parameter $\langle\mu\rangle$ describing monopole condensation, following a strategy adapted from known dual systems. The operator $\mu$ is built from the original gluon fields, and its derivative $\rho = d/d\beta \ln\langle\mu\rangle$ is computed across the deconfinement transition for SU(2) and SU(3) gauge theories on asymmetric lattices $N_s^3 \times N_t$ with $N_t = 4$. A finite-size scaling analysis of $\rho$ versus the lattice coupling $\beta$ extracts the critical coupling $\beta_c$ and critical index $\nu$ at the deconfinement temperature.

## Key numbers

- Experimental upper limit on quark abundance relative to protons: $n_q/n_p \leq 10^{-27}$, from Millikan-like analysis of approximately 1 g of matter.
- Cosmological standard model expectation without confinement: $n_q/n_p \simeq 10^{-12}$, giving a factor $10^{-15}$ as evidence for confinement.
- For SU(2) gauge theory with $N_t = 4$: $\nu = 0.63(5)$, $\beta_c = 2.30(2)$, $\delta = 0.20(8)$.
- For SU(3) gauge theory with $N_t = 4$: $\nu = 0.33(2)$, $\beta_c = 5.70(3)$, $\delta = 0.54(4)$.
- The topological susceptibility prediction from the $U(1)$ problem: $\chi \sim (180\ \mathrm{MeV})^4$, confirmed by lattice simulations.

## Context

This review extends the dual superconductor picture of confinement originally proposed by 't Hooft (1981) and Mandelstam, and builds on lattice techniques developed by the author's group in collaboration with Del Debbio, Paffuti, Pieri, Lucini, and Martelli. The work contrasts with approaches using maximal abelian projection, which the author notes may be more convenient for effective lagrangians but does not limit the symmetry pattern of confinement. The results enable further investigation into why all abelian projections appear equivalent and whether a more synthetic understanding of the dual symmetry exists.
