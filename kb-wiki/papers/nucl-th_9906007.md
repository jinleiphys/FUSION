# Semi-Microscopical Description of the Scission Configuration in the Cold Fission of $^{252}$Cf

> The cold(neutronless) fission of $^{252}$Cf is studied in the frame of a molecular model in which the scission configuration is described by two aligned fragments interacting by means of Coulomb (+ nuclear) forces. The study is carried out for different distances between the fragments tips and excitation energies. For a given deformation, the fragment's total energy is computed via the constrained Hartree-Fock + BCS formalism. The total excitation energy present in the fragments is supposed to contribute only to the fragments deformation and the asymptotic value of the kinetic energy is equated to the inter-fragment potential at scission. These two constraints yield not more than one or two fission channels for a fixed tip distance and excitation energy. Discarding those fission channels corresponding to a disequilibrated sharing of the excitation energy between the two fragments, we were able to establish the most likely scission configurations for a specified excitation energy.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for the cold (neutronless) fission of $^{252}$Cf, the scission configuration can be described by two aligned fragments interacting via Coulomb and nuclear forces, and that for a fixed tip distance and excitation energy, the constraints of equating the asymptotic kinetic energy to the inter-fragment potential and assigning all excitation energy to deformation yield not more than one or two fission channels, with the most likely configurations selected by discarding those with a disequilibrated sharing of excitation energy between the fragments.

## Method

The authors used a molecular model where the scission configuration consists of two coaxial fragments with a variable distance between their tips. For the specific binary splitting $^{252}$Cf $\rightarrow$ $^{104}$Mo + $^{148}$Ba, they computed the total energy of each fragment for various deformations using the constrained Hartree-Fock + BCS formalism with the Skyrme SIII interaction. They then solved two non-linear equations: one equating the interaction energy at scission to the asymptotic kinetic energy (the Q-value minus the excitation energy), and the other equating the total excitation energy to the sum of the deformation energies of both fragments, to find the allowed pairs of fragment deformations for given tip distances and excitation energies.

## Key numbers

*   For the cold fission case ($E^* = 0$ MeV), the scission configuration for the pair ($^{104}$Mo, $^{148}$Ba) has deformations $\beta(^{104}\text{Mo}) = 0.370$ and $\beta(^{148}\text{Ba}) = 0.270$, occurring at a tip distance $d = 2.95$ fm.
*   The tip distance of 2.95 fm is very close to the border of 3 fm, below which the Tübingen group alleges cold fission occurs.
*   For excitation energies $E^* = 0, 2, 4,$ and 6 MeV, the authors list the possible scission configurations (fragment deformations and tip distances) in Table I of the paper.
*   The first region of valid scission configurations has tip distances starting at $d = 2.95$ fm and going up to 3.8 fm, while a second, narrower region is centered around $d = 5$ fm.

## Context

This work extends the static scission-point model of nuclear fission, which previously described fragments as two deformed liquid drops with shell corrections (Wilkins 1976), by incorporating the fragments' shell structure through a self-consistent Hartree-Fock method. It also builds on the cluster-like model of Gönnenwein (1990), which determined the distance of closest approach between fragments in cold fission. The study enables a more microscopic determination of scission configurations for low-energy fission, and the authors note that a limitation is the absence of higher multipole deformations (e.g., hexadecupole), which were shown by Sandulescu (1998) to explain a whole region of cold fission for $^{252}$Cf.
