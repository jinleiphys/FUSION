# At the extremes of nuclear charge and spin

> Using scaling rules valid in the liquid drop model of nuclei, as well as universal rules associated with exchanges of stability in families of equilibrium configurations, we constructed closed formulae in terms of the atomic and mass numbers Z and A and the angular momentum L, which represent the properties of nuclei rotating synchronously (with `rigid' moments of inertia), as calculated numerically using the Thomas-Fermi model of [5,6]. The formulae are accurate in the range of mass numbers where the transition to rapidly elongating triaxial `Jacobi' shapes takes place. An improved set of formulae is also provided, which takes account of the decreased moments of inertia at low angular momenta. The formulae should be useful in guiding experimental searches for the Jacobi transition. In the second part of the paper we discuss qualitatively some aspects of the dynamics of nucleus-nucleus fusion, and outline a possible way of estimating cross-sections for the synthesis of superheavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper provides closed-form analytical formulae, derived from Thomas-Fermi model calculations and liquid-drop scaling rules, that accurately represent the energies and fission barriers of synchronously rotating nuclei in the mass-number range where the oblate-to-triaxial Jacobi shape transition occurs; the formulae are accurate for mass numbers between about 70 and 170 (fissilities between about 15.8 and 30.7) and should not be used outside that range.

## Method

The authors generated self-consistent stable and saddle-point rotating configurations for six nuclei (74Se, 94Mo, 108Cd, 126Xe, 140Nd, 168Yb) using the Thomas-Fermi model, with angular momenta ranging from zero through the Jacobi transition point L1 to the point L2 where the fission barrier vanishes. Using scaling rules from the liquid drop model and universal rules for bifurcations in families of equilibrium shapes, they constructed closed formulae in terms of Z, A, and angular momentum L that reproduce the numerical results. They then modified these formulae by interpolating between a scaled version of the low-L energy expression (with a factor k=1.5 to account for reduced moments of inertia at low angular momenta) and the Jacobi-regime formula, imposing continuity of value, slope, and curvature at L=L1.

## Key numbers

- The critical angular momentum for the Jacobi transition is given by L1 = 0.06029 A^(7/6) sqrt(40.83 - zeta), where zeta is the fissility parameter.
- The angular momentum at which the fission barrier vanishes is L2 = 0.09108 A^(7/6) sqrt(36.34 - zeta).
- The formulae are accurate for mass numbers A greater than about 70 and less than about 170, or for fissilities zeta greater than about 15.8 and less than about 30.7.
- For a sample of 17 out of 37 nuclei studied by Chasman (2000) using cranked-Strutinsky calculations, the deviations between microscopic and modified Thomas-Fermi fission barriers were -0.67 +/- 1.18 MeV at L=60 and 0.41 +/- 1.53 MeV at L=70; for all 34 values at both L=60 and L=70 the deviation was -0.13 +/- 1.45 MeV.
- The interpolation parameter k was taken to be 1.5 to give rough correspondence with preliminary experimental results from reactions studied by Ward, Diamond, Stephens, and Fallon (2000).

## Context

This work extends the liquid-drop model predictions of the Jacobi transition in nuclei (Cohen, Plasil, and Swiatecki 1974; Mustafa, Baisden, and Chandra 1986) to the more realistic Thomas-Fermi model, providing closed-form formulae that avoid separate numerical calculations for each nucleus. The modified formulae incorporating reduced moments of inertia at low angular momenta are designed to guide experimental searches for the Jacobi transition, and the paper also qualitatively discusses a three-stage model of nucleus-nucleus fusion that could enable semi-empirical estimates of cross-sections for superheavy element synthesis.
