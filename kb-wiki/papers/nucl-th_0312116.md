# Formation of hot heavy nuclei in supernova explosions

> We point out that during the supernova II type explosion the thermodynamical condition of stellar matter between the protoneutron star and the shock front corresponds to the nuclear liquid-gas phase coexistence region, which can be investigated in nuclear multifragmentation reactions. We have demonstrated, that neutron-rich hot heavy nuclei can be produced in this region. The production of these nuclei may influence dynamics of the explosion and contribute to the synthesis of heavy elements.

## Key claim

During supernova type II explosions, the thermodynamic conditions in the stellar matter between the protoneutron star and the shock front correspond to the nuclear liquid-gas phase coexistence region, where neutron-rich hot heavy nuclei can be produced; this production may influence explosion dynamics and contribute to heavy element synthesis, but the authors note that the mass distributions presented correspond to hot primary nuclei and that after de-excitation the resulting cold nuclei distributions are shifted to lower masses by several units.

## Method

The authors applied a grandcanonical version of the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) to calculate mass and charge distributions of nuclear species under supernova conditions, including electrons, positrons, and neutrinos with electric neutrality. They considered three physically distinctive scenarios: fixed lepton fraction with trapped neutrinos inside the neutrinosphere, fixed electron fraction without beta-equilibrium in a hot bubble, and full beta-equilibrium without neutrinos. The internal free energy of nuclear species was parameterized in the spirit of the liquid drop model with bulk, surface, symmetry, and Coulomb terms, including screening effects of electrons via the Wigner-Seitz approximation.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range for the transient state of nuclear matter in multifragmentation: T ~ 3-6 MeV, with density 3-5 times smaller than nuclear saturation density (rho_0 ≈ 0.15 fm^{-3})
- For lepton fraction Y_L=0.4 at T=3 MeV and rho_B=0.1 rho_0, heavy nuclei with 100 < A < 500 appear with Z/A ≈ 0.38; for Y_L=0.2, Z/A ≈ 0.27
- For fixed electron fraction Y_e=0.4 at T=1 MeV, most probable nuclei have Z/A ratios of 0.400, 0.406, and 0.439 at densities 0.1 rho_0, 10^{-3} rho_0, and 10^{-5} rho_0 respectively
- For full beta-equilibrium with standard symmetry energy (gamma=25 MeV) at T=1 MeV, Z/A ratios of most probable nuclei are 0.311, 0.359, and 0.435 at densities 0.01 rho_0, 10^{-3} rho_0, and 10^{-5} rho_0 respectively
- For Y_e=0.4 at T=1 MeV and rho_B=10^{-3} rho_0, the combination mu_e+mu_p-mu_n driving electron capture is 14 MeV, giving an enhancement factor of about 20 for electron capture rate compared to equilibrium
- At densities rho_B > 10^{-3} rho_0, more than half of the neutrons are free; at lower density Y_n ≈ 0.2 meaning 80% of neutrons are trapped in nuclei
- For lepton conservation at Y_L=0.4 and T=5 MeV with rho_B=0.1 rho_0, a reasonable overall fit to solar element abundances for A > 70 is achieved

## Context

This work extends the understanding of nuclear multifragmentation reactions, which have been studied experimentally in hadron and heavy-ion reactions, to the astrophysical context of supernova explosions. The authors apply the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), previously developed for describing multifragmentation data (SMM; Botvina 1995; D'Agostino; Scharenberg; GANIL), to supernova conditions. The work contrasts with earlier calculations by Lattimer and collaborators (Lat; Latt) which considered only one "average" nucleus characterizing the liquid phase, whereas this approach considers all fragments in the transition region. The results enable future hydrodynamical simulations of supernova explosions to incorporate realistic nuclear composition, and suggest that the r-process may be responsible for the final redistribution of element abundances leading to the pronounced peaks around A ≈ 80, 130, and 200.
