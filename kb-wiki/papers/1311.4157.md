# Fermionic Functional Renormalization Group Approach to Bose-Einstein Condensation of Dimers

> Fermionic functional renormalization group (f-FRG) is applied to describe Bose-Einstein condensation (BEC) of dimers for a two-component fermionic system with attractive contact interaction. In order to describe the system of dimers without introducing auxiliary bosonic fields (bosonization), we propose a new exact evolution-equation of the effective action in f-FRG with an infrared regulator for the fermion vertex. Then we analyze its basic properties in details. We show explicitly that the critical temperature of the free Bose gas is obtained naturally by this method without bosonization. Methods to make systematic improvement from the deep BEC limit are briefly discussed.

# Fermionic Functional Renormalization Group Approach to Bose-Einstein Condensation of Dimers

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new fermionic functional renormalization group (f-FRG) formalism that can describe Bose-Einstein condensation of composite bosons (dimers) without introducing auxiliary bosonic fields, by instead adding an infrared regulator to the four-fermion vertex. In the deep BEC limit at low density, the method reproduces the critical temperature of the free Bose gas, but the authors note that systematic improvement beyond this limit requires numerical computation and UV renormalization techniques that are not yet fully developed.

## Method

The authors introduce a vertex infrared regulator into the four-fermion interaction term of a two-component non-relativistic fermionic system with attractive contact interaction, rather than the usual regulator in the fermion propagator. They derive the exact evolution equation for the effective action with this vertex regulator, then solve the coupled flow equations for the four-point vertex function (in the vacuum limit) and the fermion self-energy (within a low-density approximation that neglects self-energy corrections in the flow). The number density is computed from the self-energy at the end of the flow, and the critical temperature is determined via the Thouless criterion (divergence of the four-point vertex at zero center-of-mass momentum).

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature ratio: \( T_c / \varepsilon_F = 0.218\ldots \), obtained from the formula \( T_c/\varepsilon_F = \left( 2 / (9\pi \zeta(3/2)^2) \right)^{1/3} \), where \( \varepsilon_F = (3\pi^2 n)^{2/3} / (2m) \) and \( n \) is the particle number density.
- The chemical potential in the deep BEC limit is \( \mu = -1/(2m a_s^2) \), which is exactly half the binding energy of the composite boson in vacuum.
- The self-energy magnitude is estimated as \( |\Sigma_k(p)| \lesssim (1/(2m a_s^2)) \times (\sqrt{2m T} a_s)^3 \times n_B(k^2/4m) \), which is much smaller than \( |\mu| = 1/(2m a_s^2) \) when \( a_s \to 0^+ \) as long as \( n_B(k^2/4m) \sim 1 \).
- The critical scale for the approximation is estimated as \( (k_c^2/2m)/T \sim (\sqrt{2m T} a_s)^3 \sim n a_s^3 \ll 1 \).

## Context

This work extends the author's previous f-FRG studies (Tanizaki 2013) that reproduced BCS theory and its Gorkov-Melik-Barkhudarov correction on the BCS side of the BCS-BEC crossover. It contrasts with prior approaches that used functional renormalization group with auxiliary bosonic fields (Birse 2005, PhysRevB 2008, PhysRevA 2009) or the self-consistent t-matrix method (Haussmann 1993, PhysRevB 1994). The method enables unbiased treatment of the BEC side without prior knowledge of ground state properties, and the authors suggest that combining this vertex regulator technique with the standard fermion-propagator regulator could describe the entire BCS-BEC crossover, a work reported as in preparation.
