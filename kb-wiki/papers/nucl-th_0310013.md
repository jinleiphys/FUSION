# Simplified approach to the application of the geometric collective model

> The predictions of the geometric collective model (GCM) for different sets of Hamiltonian parameter values are related by analytic scaling relations. For the quartic truncated form of the GCM -- which describes harmonic oscillator, rotor, deformed gamma-soft, and intermediate transitional structures -- these relations are applied to reduce the effective number of model parameters from four to two. Analytic estimates of the dependence of the model predictions upon these parameters are derived. Numerical predictions over the entire parameter space are compactly summarized in two-dimensional contour plots. The results considerably simplify the application of the GCM, allowing the parameters relevant to a given nucleus to be deduced essentially by inspection. A precomputed mesh of calculations covering this parameter space and an associated computer code for extracting observable values are made available through the Electronic Physics Auxiliary Publication Service. For illustration, the nucleus 102Pd is considered.

## Key claim

The geometric collective model (GCM) with a quartic-truncated Hamiltonian can be reduced from four effective parameters to two (the shape parameter \(d\) and the structure parameter \(S\)) using analytic scaling relations, allowing the entire parameter space to be surveyed via two-dimensional contour plots and enabling parameter values for a given nucleus to be deduced essentially by inspection.

## Method

The author applies analytic scaling relations to the quartic-truncated GCM Hamiltonian, which contains only the leading-order kinetic term and the three lowest-order potential terms (quadratic, cubic, and quartic in \(\beta\)). By exploiting the invariance of the Schrödinger equation under overall multiplication and under simultaneous deepening and dilation of the potential, the four original parameters (\(B_2, C_2, C_3, C_4\)) are reduced to two invariant parameters: \(d\) (which uniquely defines the potential shape) and \(S\) (which determines level energies relative to potential features). Numerical diagonalizations are then performed over a mesh covering the \((d,S)\) plane, and the resulting energy ratios and \(B(E2)\) ratios (using a linear quadrupole operator) are presented as contour plots.

## Key numbers

- The ratio \(R_{4/2} \equiv E(4^+_1)/E(2^+_1)\) is plotted over the range \(-5000 \leq d \leq 5\) and \(10^{-2} \leq S/S_> \leq 10^{+1}\).
- The normalized \(\beta\)-vibrational energy \(E(0^+_2)_\mathrm{norm} \equiv E(0^+_2)/E(2^+_1)\) is plotted over the same parameter range.
- The normalized \(\gamma\)-vibrational energy \(E(2^+_2)_\mathrm{norm} \equiv E(2^+_2)/E(2^+_1)\) is plotted over the same parameter range.
- The ratio \(B(E2;4^+_1\rightarrow2^+_1)_\mathrm{norm} \equiv B(E2;4^+_1\rightarrow2^+_1)/B(E2;2^+_1\rightarrow0^+_1)\) is plotted over the same parameter range.
- The ratio \(B(E2;2^+_2\rightarrow0^+_1)_\mathrm{norm} \equiv B(E2;2^+_2\rightarrow0^+_1)/B(E2;2^+_1\rightarrow0^+_1)\) is plotted over the same parameter range.
- The analytic estimate for the ratio of \(\beta\) to \(\gamma\) vibrational energies is \(E_\beta/E_\gamma \approx \sqrt{r/3}\), where \(r \equiv \sqrt{1-d}\), predicting \(\beta\)-stiff rotors (\(E_\beta > E_\gamma\)) for \(d < -8\) and \(\gamma\)-stiff rotors (\(E_\gamma > E_\beta\)) for \(d > -8\).

## Context

This work extends the geometric collective model (GCM) of Gneuss, Mosel, and Greiner (1969-1971) by providing a simplified two-parameter formulation that avoids the technical difficulties of automated fitting in the full eight-parameter space. The scaling relations used here are equivalent to those applied in earlier GCM fitting procedures by von Bernus (1971), Habs (1974), and Troltenier (1991), but the present work systematically exploits them to reduce the parameter count and produce comprehensive contour plots. The results enable rapid identification of GCM parameters for transitional nuclei such as \(^{102}\)Pd, and the precomputed mesh and extraction code made available through EPAPS allow other researchers to obtain observable values without performing new diagonalizations.
