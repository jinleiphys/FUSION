# Skyrmions and clustering in light nuclei

> One of the outstanding problems in modern nuclear physics is to determine the properties of nuclei from the fundamental theory of the strong force, quantum chromodynamics (QCD). Skyrmions offer a novel approach to this problem by considering nuclei as solitons of a low energy effective field theory obtained from QCD. Unfortunately, the standard theory of Skyrmions has been plagued by two significant problems, in that it yields nuclear binding energies that are an order of magnitude larger than experimental nuclear data, and it predicts intrinsic shapes for nuclei that fail to match the clustering structure of light nuclei. Here we show that extending the standard theory of Skyrmions, by including the next lightest subatomic meson particles traditionally neglected, dramatically improves both these aspects. We find Skyrmion clustering that now agrees with the expected structure of light nuclei, with binding energies that are much closer to nuclear data.

## Key claim
Including rho mesons (the next lightest mesons after pions) in the Skyrme model dramatically improves both the clustering structure and binding energies of light-nuclei Skyrmions, reducing binding energies from over 10% of the Skyrmion mass to no more than 3%, while producing cluster configurations that match experimental expectations for nuclei with mass numbers A=5 through A=8.

## Method
The authors performed extensive parallel computations on a high-performance computing cluster to minimize the static energy of the extended Skyrme model that includes both massive pions and rho meson fields, using a dimensional deconstruction formulation that introduces no additional free parameters. Skyrmion solutions were computed by evolving dynamical second-order-in-time field equations with fourth-order accurate finite difference approximations on a cubic lattice of 128^3 points with lattice spacing Δx=0.08, using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method with timestep Δt=0.02, and flow to minimal energy states was achieved by instantaneously freezing the motion whenever the energy was increasing. For each baryon number A>1, a wide variety of initial conditions were applied, including a product ansatz of separate single Skyrmions and the rational map approximation, to avoid trapping in local minima.

## Key numbers
- Binding energies of Skyrmions in the standard model are greater than 10% of the Skyrmion mass, compared to experimental nuclear binding energies no greater than 1% of the nucleus mass (for A=1 to 8).
- In the extended model including rho mesons, binding energies are reduced to no more than 3% of the Skyrmion mass.
- The pion mass in dimensionless Skyrme units is fixed at m=0.526, corresponding to the physical pion mass.
- The constants used in the extended model are: c1=0.141, c2=0.198, c3=0.153, c4=0.050, c5=0.038, c6=0.078, c7=0.049.
- The A=8 Skyrmion in the extended model displays a 2α cluster structure (a pair of alpha-particles) without artificially increasing the pion mass, unlike in the standard model where an artificially large pion mass of around twice the physical value was required.

## Context
This work extends the standard Skyrme model (Skyrme, 1961) by including rho mesons, which were previously neglected to make computations tractable, and directly addresses two major problems identified in prior work: binding energies an order of magnitude too large (as shown in the red circles of Fig. 2) and failure to reproduce clustering structure for A>4 (as seen in Fig. 1a). The results improve upon recent attempts to address clustering by including vibrations (Halcrow, 2017) or using artificially large pion masses (Bates, Manton, and Sutcliffe, 2018), and maintain the successful description of the Hoyle state of 12C found in the standard model with large pion mass (Lau and Manton, 2014). The work enables a systematic program of including successively heavier mesons to converge Skyrmion predictions toward nuclear data.
