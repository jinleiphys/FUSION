# Calculating the initial energy density in heavy ion collisions by including the finite nuclear thickness

> The initial energy density produced in heavy ion collisions can be estimated with the Bjorken energy density formula after choosing a proper formation time $τ{_{\rm F}}$. However, the Bjorken formula breaks down at low energies because it neglects the finite nuclear thickness. Here we include both the finite time duration and finite longitudinal extension of the initial energy production. When $τ{_{\rm F}}$ is not too much smaller than the crossing time of the two nuclei, our results are similar to those from a previous study that only considers the finite time duration. In particular, we find that at low energies the initial energy density has a much lower maximum value but evolves much longer than the Bjorken formula, while at large-enough $τ{_{\rm F}}$ and/or high-enough energies our result approaches the Bjorken formula. We also find a qualitative difference in that our maximum energy density $ε^{\rm max}$ at $τ{_{\rm F}}=0$ is finite, while the Bjorken formula diverges as $1/τ{_{\rm F}}$ and the previous result diverges as $\ln (1/τ{_{\rm F}})$ at low energies but as $1/τ{_{\rm F}}$ at high energies. Furthermore, our solution of the energy density approximately satisfies a scaling relation. As a result, the $τ{_{\rm F}}$-dependence of $ε^{\rm max}$ determines the $A$-dependence, and the weaker $τ{_{\rm F}}$-dependence of $ε^{\rm max}$ in our results at low energies means a slower increase of $ε^{\rm max}$ with $A$.

## Key claim

The authors extend the Bjorken energy density formula to include both the finite time duration and finite longitudinal extension of initial energy production in heavy ion collisions, finding that at low collision energies the maximum energy density is much lower and evolves much longer than the Bjorken formula predicts, while at high energies or large formation times the result approaches the Bjorken formula. The key qualifier is that the finite nuclear thickness becomes comparable to or larger than the formation time at low energies, causing the breakdown of the Bjorken formula.

## Method

The authors model the crossing of two identical relativistic nuclei in the center-of-mass frame, where partons can be produced anywhere inside a rhombus-shaped production area in the z-t plane. They calculate the initial energy density averaged over the transverse area at central spacetime-rapidity by integrating over production points (z0, x) within the production area, subject to light cone constraints and a formation time condition t_F = τ_F cosh y, where τ_F is the proper formation time. The initial transverse mass rapidity density of produced partons is parametrized as a Gaussian in rapidity, with the peak value taken from a parametrization of AMPT model results and the width determined by energy conservation.

## Key numbers

- For central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 50 GeV, the crossing time d_t ≈ 0.5 fm/c, comparable to the usual parton formation time
- The parametrized peak value of dm_T/dy(0) = 168 (√s_NN/GeV - 0.930)^0.348 GeV for produced partons
- The Bjorken formula breaks down (differs by 20% or more from the finite-thickness result) when τ_F/d_t ≲ 1
- The finite-thickness result differs significantly (by 20% or more) from the previous triangular solution when τ_F/d_t ≲ 0.2
- At τ_F = 0, the maximum energy density ε^max is finite at any energy, unlike the Bjorken formula which diverges as 1/τ_F and the previous result which diverges as ln(1/τ_F) at low energies
- The ε^max(τ_F=0) value is within 20% of the ε^max at τ_F = 0.1 fm/c for central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN ≲ 7 GeV

## Context

This work extends a previous study by Lin (2017) that only considered finite time duration of initial energy production, finding that including the finite longitudinal width gives qualitatively different behavior at τ_F = 0 (finite maximum energy density versus logarithmic divergence). The results enable more accurate estimates of initial energy density for hydrodynamic models at low collision energies such as those in the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program, and show that the A-dependence of maximum energy density is determined by the τ_F-dependence, with slower increase with A at low energies compared to the Bjorken formula's ε^max ∝ A^(1/3).
