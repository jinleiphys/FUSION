# On the change of old neutron star masses with galactocentric distance

> We show that the pulsar mass depends on the environment, and that it decreases going towards the center of the Milky Way. This is due to two combined effects, the capture and accumulation of self-interacting, non-annihilating dark matter by pulsars, and the increase of the dark matter density going towards the galactic center. We show that mass decrease depends both on the density profile of dark matter, steeper profiles producing a faster and larger decrease of the pulsar mass, and on the strength of self-interaction. Once future observations will provide the pulsar mass in a dark matter rich environment, close to the galactic center, the present result will be able to put constraints on the characteristics of our Galaxy halo dark matter profile, on the nature of dark matter, namely on its annihilating or non-annihilating nature, on its strength of self-interaction, and on the particle mass.

## Key claim

The paper claims that old neutron star (pulsar) masses decrease as they are located closer to the Galactic center, due to the capture and accumulation of self-interacting, non-annihilating dark matter. This mass decrease depends on both the dark matter density profile (steeper profiles produce faster and larger mass decreases) and the strength of dark matter self-interaction, and the result is presented as a testable prediction contingent on future observations of pulsar masses in dark-matter-rich environments near the Galactic center.

## Method

The authors model the accretion of asymmetric dark matter (ADM) onto neutron stars using the capture rate formula from Kouvaris (2013), which depends on local dark matter density, accretion time, and scattering cross section. They adopt an Einasto dark matter density profile for the Milky Way, with fiducial parameters (alpha = 0.11, r_{-2} = 15 kpc) chosen to account for baryonic effects such as adiabatic contraction, and they compute the accreted dark matter mass as a function of galactocentric distance. The resulting accreted mass is then used to determine the corresponding decrease in neutron star mass, assuming a typical neutron star of 2 solar masses and an accretion time of 10 Gyr.

## Key numbers

* A neutron star at the Sun's location accretes approximately 10^{-11} solar masses of dark matter.
* A neutron star at 10^{-5} pc from the Galactic center accretes 6.5 x 10^{-4} solar masses for the Einasto profile with alpha = 0.09.
* A neutron star at 0.1 pc from the Galactic center accretes 3.4 x 10^{-6} solar masses for the Einasto profile with alpha = 0.09.
* The total accreted dark matter mass from the TOV equation is approximately 10^{-5} solar masses for a dark matter particle mass of 200 GeV.
* The fiducial local dark matter density at the Sun's position is taken as 0.420 GeV/cm^3, with uncertainties of +0.019 and -0.021 GeV/cm^3.
* The dark matter-nucleon scattering cross section from XENON1T for a particle mass of several hundred GeV is approximately 10^{-45} cm^2, leading to a scattering fraction f approximately equal to 1.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of dark matter admixed with neutron star matter, such as those by Sandin (2009), Tolos et al. (2015), and Deliyergiyev et al. (2019), which showed that increasing the dark matter fraction in neutron stars leads to smaller masses and radii. The paper proposes a novel observational test: measuring pulsar masses as a function of galactocentric distance could constrain the Milky Way's dark matter density profile, the nature of dark matter (annihilating vs. non-annihilating), the strength of dark matter self-interaction, and the dark matter particle mass. This approach is presented as more feasible than previous probes such as neutron star temperature evolution from dark matter annihilation, which has proven difficult to detect.
