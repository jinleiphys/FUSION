# Testing viscous and anisotropic hydrodynamics in an exactly solvable case

> We exactly solve the one-dimensional boost-invariant Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation for arbitrary shear viscosity. The results are compared with the predictions of viscous and anisotropic hydrodynamics. Studying different non-equilibrium cases and comparing the exact kinetic-theory results to the second-order viscous hydrodynamics results we find that recent formulations of second-order viscous hydrodynamics agree better with the exact solution than the standard Israel-Stewart approach. Additionally, we find that, given the appropriate connection between the kinetic and anisotropic hydrodynamics relaxation times, anisotropic hydrodynamics provides a very good approximation to the exact relaxation time approximation solution.

## Key claim

The paper claims that, for a one-dimensional boost-invariant system, recent formulations of second-order viscous hydrodynamics (e.g., Denicol (2012) and Jaiswal (2013)) agree better with the exact solution of the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation than the standard Israel-Stewart approach, and that anisotropic hydrodynamics provides a very good approximation to the exact solution provided the relaxation times in the two approaches are properly matched (with a factor-of-two difference).

## Method

The authors exactly solved the 0+1 dimensional boost-invariant Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation (RTA) for massless partons, using an iterative numerical method to obtain the effective temperature as a function of proper time. They then compared the exact results for the energy-momentum tensor components (energy density, longitudinal pressure, transverse pressure) to predictions from second-order viscous hydrodynamics (Israel-Stewart and more recent formulations) and from anisotropic hydrodynamics (using the Romatschke-Strickland form), for various initial temperatures, initial momentum-space anisotropies, and shear viscosity to entropy density ratios.

## Key numbers

* Shear viscosity to entropy density ratios used: \( \bar{\eta} \in \{ 1/(4\pi), 3/(4\pi), 10/(4\pi) \} \).
* Initial proper time: \( \tau_0 = 0.25 \) fm/c.
* Initial effective temperatures used: \( T_0 = 300 \) MeV and \( T_0 = 600 \) MeV.
* Initial anisotropy parameters used: \( \xi_0 = 0 \) (isotropic) and \( \xi_0 = 10 \) (highly oblate).
* The relaxation time in the exact solution is related to the shear viscosity by \( \tau_{\rm eq}(\tau) = 5\bar{\eta} / T(\tau) \).
* The paper analytically proves a factor of two difference between the relaxation time in the anisotropic hydrodynamics approximation and the exact case when the system is close to equilibrium.

## Context

This work extends the method of Baym (1984) for solving the 0+1d RTA Boltzmann equation to the case of a proper-time-dependent relaxation time, and provides an exact benchmark for testing hydrodynamic approximations used in heavy-ion collisions. It directly compares the exact solution to the standard Israel-Stewart approach (Israel 1976, Israel 1979) and to more recent second-order formulations by Denicol (2012) and Jaiswal (2013), as well as to anisotropic hydrodynamics as developed by Martinez and Strickland (2010). The results enable precise calibration of the relationship between the shear viscosity coefficient and the relaxation time in RTA, and demonstrate that leading-order anisotropic hydrodynamics can achieve agreement with the exact solution at a level comparable to second-order viscous hydrodynamics.
