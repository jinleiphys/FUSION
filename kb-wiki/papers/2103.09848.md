# Exploring theoretical uncertainties in the hydrodynamic description of relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We explore theoretical uncertainties in the hydrodynamic description of relativistic heavy-ion collisions by examining the full non-linear causality conditions and quantifying the second-order transport coefficients' role on flow observables. The causality conditions impose physical constraints on the maximum allowed values of inverse Reynolds numbers during the hydrodynamic evolution. Including additional second-order gradient terms in the Denicol-Niemi-Molnár-Rischke (DNMR) theory significantly shrinks the casual regions compared to those in the Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics. For Au+Au collisions, we find the variations of flow observables are small with and without imposing the necessary causality conditions, suggesting a robust extraction of the Quark-Gluon Plasma's transport coefficients in previous model-to-data comparisons. However, sizable sensitivity is present in small p+Au collisions, which poses challenges to study the small systems' collectivity.

# Exploring theoretical uncertainties in the hydrodynamic description of relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
The full non-linear causality conditions impose constraints on the maximum allowed inverse Reynolds numbers during hydrodynamic evolution, but for Au+Au collisions the variations in flow observables are small with or without imposing these conditions, suggesting robust extraction of Quark-Gluon Plasma transport coefficients in previous model-to-data comparisons. However, sizable sensitivity appears in small p+Au collisions, where 17% of fluid cells violate necessary causality conditions, posing challenges for studying collectivity in small systems.

## Method
The authors performed event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations using the IP-Glasma + MUSIC + UrQMD framework for Au+Au and p+Au collisions at top RHIC energy (200 GeV), implementing both the restricted (Israel-Stewart-like) and full Denicol-Niemi-Molnár-Rischke (DNMR) hydrodynamic theories. They evaluated the six necessary causality conditions derived by Bemfica et al. (2020) across all fluid cells with temperature above 145 MeV, using two different parameterizations for the bulk relaxation time: one from kinetic theory (τΠ,1) and one from strongly-coupled theory (τΠ,2). The authors then imposed restrictions on inverse Reynolds numbers to enforce causality and quantified the resulting changes to flow observables.

## Key numbers
- For 30-40% Au+Au collisions with DNMR theory and τΠ,1: 3.8% of fluid cells violate necessary causality conditions; for 0-5% p+Au collisions this fraction increases to 17%
- For restricted DNMR (Israel-Stewart) with τΠ,1: 1.8% of fluid cells violate necessary conditions in 30-40% Au+Au, and 9% in 0-5% p+Au
- For restricted DNMR with τΠ,2: only 0.1% of fluid cells violate necessary conditions in 30-40% Au+Au, and 0.2% in 0-5% p+Au
- The sufficient causality conditions require extremely small viscous corrections: Rπ ≤ 0.1 when bulk viscous pressure is negative
- Imposing Rπ ≤ √2 P/(ε+P) and |RΠ| ≤ P/(ε+P) ensures all fluid cells satisfy necessary causality conditions for restricted DNMR with τΠ,1
- With τΠ,2, requiring Rπ and |RΠ| ≤ 0.6 ensures all fluid cells remain within the causal region
- Pions' mean pT increases by 5-10% in peripheral centrality bins when causality restrictions are imposed
- The elliptic flow coefficient v2{2}(pT) is approximately 10% smaller with τΠ,2 compared to τΠ,1

## Context
This work extends the derivation of full non-linear causality conditions by Bemfica et al. (2020) for Israel-Stewart and DNMR hydrodynamics into realistic event-by-event simulations of heavy-ion collisions. It quantifies theoretical uncertainties that affect Bayesian inference analyses such as those by Bernhard et al. (2019) and Everett et al. (2020), which have been used to extract QGP transport coefficients. The results show that previous extractions for large systems (Au+Au) are robust, but the findings pose challenges for studying collectivity in small systems (p+Au), where the hydrodynamic description is pushed further from equilibrium. The work also provides practical guidance on numerical regulators for hydrodynamic simulations, building on stability conditions used by Schenke et al. (2020).
