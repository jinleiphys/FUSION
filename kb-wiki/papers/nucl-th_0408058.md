# Systematic Study of Shell Effect near Drip-lines

> Variation of nuclear shell effects with nucleon numbers are evaluated using the modified Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula (BWM) and the measured atomic masses. The shell effects at magic neutron numbers N = 8, 20, 28, 50, 82 and 126 and magic proton numbers Z = 8, 20, 28,50 and 82 are found to vary rapidly approaching the drip lines. The shell effect due to one magic number increases when it approaches another magic number. Thus, shell effects are not always negligible near the drip lines.

## Key claim

Shell effects at magic numbers do not always vanish near the drip lines; instead, they vary rapidly with nucleon number and can actually increase when one magic number approaches another magic number, contrary to the common expectation of universal quenching.

## Method

The authors compared experimental atomic masses with predictions from a modified Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula (BWM) that contains no shell correction or Wigner term. They computed one- and two-nucleon separation energies, shell gaps (G₂ₙ, G₂ₚ), and the binding-energy difference ΔB = BE(EXP) − BE(BWM) for nuclei near the magic numbers N = 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126 and Z = 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, using experimental mass data from Audi (2000), Nosek (2002), and Scheidenberger (2001).

## Key numbers

- For N=8 at Z=4 (Be), the ΔB peak at N=8 disappears, indicating loss of magicity; this is experimentally confirmed.
- For N=8 at Z=8 (O), a large break in Sₙ at N=8 is clearly seen in experimental data and not reproduced by BWM.
- For Z=82, at N=106 the difference between experimental G₂ₚ and BWM prediction almost vanishes at approximately 140 keV.
- For N=28, three peaks in G₂ₙ are seen at Z=16, 20, and 28, of which Z=20 and Z=28 are known magic numbers.
- For Z=20, the ΔB values at N=29, 31, and 33 are higher than at N=28, suggesting a possible shift of neutron magicity to higher N values.

## Context

This work extends the study of shell quenching initiated by Chen et al. (1995), who showed that quenching of the N=82 shell gap for Z<50 improves reproduction of heavy-element abundances. It also builds on the modified Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula (BWM) previously developed by the same authors (Samanta 2002). The results contradict the simple expectation that shell effects always diminish near drip lines and instead show that shell effects can persist or even increase when approaching another magic number. The paper calls for further experimental mass measurements, particularly for neutron-rich sulfur isotopes and for cadmium isotopes near N=82, to confirm predicted shifts in magicity and the extent of N=82 shell quenching.
