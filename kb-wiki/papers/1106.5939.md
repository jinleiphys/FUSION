# Can We Rigorously Define Phases in a Finite System?

> Here we propose the generalized statistical multifragmentation model which includes the liquid phase pressure of the most general form. This allows us to get rid of the absolute incompressibility of the nuclear liquid. Also the present model employes a very general form the surface tension coefficient of nuclear fragments. Such a model is solved analytically for finite volumes by the Laplace-Fourier transform method to isobaric ensemble. A complete analysis of the isobaric partition singularities of this model is also done for finite volumes. It is shown that the real part of any simple pole of the isobaric partition defines the free energy of the corresponding state, whereas its imaginary part, depending on the sign, defines the inverse decay/formation time of this state. The developed formalism allows us to exactly define the finite volume analogs of gaseous, liquid and mixed phases of the class of similar models from the first principles of statistical mechanics and demonstrate the pitfalls of earlier works. The finite width effects for large nuclear fragments and quark gluon bags are also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that for finite systems, the analogs of gaseous, liquid, and mixed phases can be rigorously defined from first principles of statistical mechanics using the singularities of the isobaric partition, but only if the size of the largest fragment grows with the system volume; otherwise, no phase transition exists even in the thermodynamic limit.

## Method

The authors generalize the statistical multifragmentation model (GSMM) by introducing a general form for the liquid phase pressure, replacing the absolute incompressibility of nuclear matter, and a general parameterization of the surface tension coefficient that can become negative above the critical temperature. They solve the model analytically for finite volumes using the Laplace-Fourier transform method applied to the isobaric ensemble, then analyze the singularities (simple poles) of the isobaric partition to identify the finite-volume analogs of gaseous, liquid, and mixed phases.

## Key numbers

- The bulk binding energy per nucleon is \(W_{\rm o}=16\) MeV, with the excited state contribution parameter \(\epsilon_{\rm o}=16\) MeV.
- The surface tension coefficient uses \(\sigma_{\rm o}=18\) MeV and a critical temperature \(T_c=18\) MeV, with exponent \(\zeta \ge 1\).
- The normal nuclear density is \(\rho_{\rm o}=0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\), which is the maximal baryonic density in the simplified SMM but is exceeded in the GSMM.
- For the case of fixed largest fragment size \(K(V)=const\), the decay/formation time of metastable states is \(\tau_n \approx K [\pi n T]^{-1}\), which is volume independent.
- For the case where \(K(V)\) grows with volume, the decay/formation time of the closest poles grows as \(\tau_n \approx K(V) [\pi n T]^{-1}\), becoming large with system size.

## Context

This work extends the earlier simplified statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) studied by Gupta (1998, 1999) and the Laplace-Fourier transform method developed by Bugaev (2005a, 2007a). It directly addresses the definition of phases in finite systems, contradicting the approach of Chomaz (2003) which claimed a one-to-one correspondence between bimodal distributions and Lee-Yang zeros; the authors argue that such a definition cannot be established experimentally. The formalism enables rigorous study of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition and deconfinement phase transition in finite systems, with potential application to quark gluon bags and heavy-ion collisions.
