# Impact of Geometric Inflation on Nucleon Size Sensitivity in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The intrinsic transverse size of nucleons, parameterized by a Gaussian width $w$, is a critical yet uncertain input in the initial-state modeling of relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Using a finite $w$ in standard initial geometry models introduces an unintentional ``geometric inflation'' that alters the initial nuclear density profile. In this study, we implement a self-consistent density correction to eliminate this artifact and investigate its impact on final-state observables. Through hybrid (viscous hydrodynamics + hadronic transport) simulations of $^{208}$Pb+$^{208}$Pb collisions at the LHC, we demonstrate that removing geometric inflation significantly modifies the sensitivity of observables to the nucleon width $w$. While elliptic flow and mean transverse momentum ($\langle [p_{\rm T}]\rangle$) become less sensitive to variations in $w$, the Pearson correlation coefficient $ρ(v_{n}^{2}, δp_{\rm T})$, $[p_{\rm T}]$ fluctuations, and triangular flow exhibit enhanced sensitivity to fluctuations in nucleon positions. Our results indicate that uncorrected geometric inflation can bias the extraction of nucleon structure and quark-gluon plasma properties. This underscores the necessity of a self-consistent initial-state geometry for reliable Bayesian inference in heavy-ion collisions.

# Impact of Geometric Inflation on Nucleon Size Sensitivity in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim
The sensitivity of heavy-ion collision observables to the intrinsic nucleon width parameter $w$ is significantly altered when a self-consistent density correction removes the unintentional "geometric inflation" artifact; the Pearson correlation coefficient $\rho(v_2^2, \delta p_{\rm T})$ and triangular flow become more sensitive to fluctuations in nucleon positions, while elliptic flow and mean transverse momentum become less sensitive, implying that uncorrected geometric inflation can bias Bayesian extraction of nucleon structure and quark-gluon plasma properties.

## Method
The authors implemented a self-consistent density correction framework using an inverse Weierstrass transform to modify the sampling distribution of nucleon centers, ensuring that the global nuclear density profile remains invariant under Gaussian smearing with width $w$. They performed hybrid simulations using the iEBE-VISHNU model (TRENTO initial conditions + (2+1)D viscous hydrodynamics + UrQMD hadronic transport) for $^{208}$Pb+$^{208}$Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02$ TeV, comparing observables calculated with standard (uncorrected) and corrected initial-state geometries for a representative nucleon width of $w = 0.92$ fm.

## Key numbers
- Corrected Woods-Saxon sampling parameters for $^{208}$Pb at $w = 0.92$ fm: $\tilde{R} = 6.72$ fm, $\tilde{a} = 0.24$ fm (compared to standard $R = 6.62$ fm, $a = 0.546$ fm)
- For $^{208}$Pb at $w = 0.4$ fm: $\tilde{R} = 6.64$ fm, $\tilde{a} = 0.504$ fm
- For $^{208}$Pb at $w = 1.0$ fm: $\tilde{R} = 6.74$ fm, $\tilde{a} = 0.109$ fm
- For $^{48}$Ca at $w = 0.92$ fm: $\tilde{R} = 3.80$ fm, $\tilde{a} = 0.271$ fm (standard $R = 3.723$ fm, $a = 0.523$ fm)
- The inflated mass radius squared for $w = 0.92$ fm satisfies $R_m^2 \approx R_{\rm ch}^2 + 1.8$ fm$^2$
- Charged hadron observables calculated within $0.2 < p_{\rm T} < 3.0$ GeV

## Context
This work extends the self-consistent density correction framework proposed by Xu (2026) to full hydrodynamic simulations, addressing the geometric inflation artifact identified in standard Monte Carlo Glauber approaches. It directly challenges the large nucleon width values ($w \approx 0.9$–$1.1$ fm) inferred by Bayesian analyses such as Moreland (2018), Bernhard (2019), Nijs (2020), and JETSCAPE (2020), which contradict the smaller widths ($w \approx 0.4$–$0.5$ fm) favored by comparisons of $\rho(v_n^2, \delta p_{\rm T})$ with ALICE (2021) and ATLAS (2019) data. The results enable a physically motivated revision of Bayesian inference frameworks for extracting both nucleon structure and QGP transport coefficients, and call for re-evaluation of calibrations such as JETSCAPE and Trajectum with inflation-corrected TRENTO profiles.
