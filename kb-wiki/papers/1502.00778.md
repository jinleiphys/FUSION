# Revisit of the neutron/proton ratio puzzle in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions

> Incorporating a newly improved isospin- and momentum-dependent interaction in the isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck transport model IBUU11, we have investigated relative effects of the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy $E_{sym}(ρ)$ and the neutron-proton effective mass splitting $m^*_n-m^*_p$ on the neutron/proton ratio of free nucleons and those in light clusters. It is found that the $m^*_n-m^*_p$ has a relatively stronger effect than the $E_{sym}(ρ)$ and the assumption of $m^*_n\leq m^*_p$ leads to a higher neutron/proton ratio. Moreover, this finding is independent of the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections used. However, results of our calculations using the $E_{sym}(ρ)$ and $m^*_n-m^*_p$ both within their current uncertainty ranges are all too low compared to the recent NSCL/MSU double neutron/proton ratio data from central $^{124}$Sn+$^{124}$Sn and $^{112}$Sn+$^{112}$Sn collisions at 50 and 120 MeV/u, thus calling for new mechanisms to explain the puzzlingly high neutron/proton ratio observed in the experiments.

# Revisit of the neutron/proton ratio puzzle in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
The neutron-proton effective mass splitting ($m^*_n - m^*_p$) has a relatively stronger effect than the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy $E_{sym}(\rho)$ on the neutron/proton ratio, and the assumption $m^*_n \leq m^*_p$ leads to a higher neutron/proton ratio. However, even when both $E_{sym}(\rho)$ and $m^*_n - m^*_p$ are varied within their current uncertainty ranges, all calculated double neutron/proton ratios remain too low compared to the NSCL/MSU experimental data, indicating that new mechanisms beyond these two ingredients are needed.

## Method
The authors implemented a newly improved isospin- and momentum-dependent interaction (ImMDI), based on the Gogny force, in the isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck transport model IBUU11. They generated events for central ($b=2$ fm) $^{124}$Sn+$^{124}$Sn and $^{112}$Sn+$^{112}$Sn collisions at beam energies of 50 and 120 MeV/u, using 200 test particles per nucleon and about 20,000 events per beam energy and impact parameter. The coalescence invariant yield was constructed by including both free nucleons and those bound in light clusters (deuterons, tritons, $^3$He, $^4$He) with an angular gate of $70^\circ < \theta_{cm} < 110^\circ$, and the evolution was stopped at $t=150$ fm/c.

## Key numbers
- The double neutron/proton ratio DR(n/p) at 50 MeV/u is slightly larger for a softer symmetry energy with slope parameter $L=10$ MeV compared to a stiffer one with $L=60$ MeV, for high-energy nucleons.
- At 120 MeV/u, the DR(n/p) is rather insensitive to the stiffness of the symmetry energy.
- The ImMDI interaction parameters are fixed to saturation density $\rho_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$, binding energy $E_0(\rho_0) = -16$ MeV, incompressibility $K_0 = 230$ MeV, isoscalar effective mass $m^*_{s,0} = 0.7m$, symmetry energy $E_{sym}(\rho_0) = 32.5$ MeV, and isoscalar potential at infinite momentum $U_{0,\infty} = 75$ MeV.
- The current uncertainty range of the slope parameter $L$ of $E_{sym}$ is about $50 \pm 20$ MeV, and the chosen range in this study is (10, 60) MeV.
- The coalescence parameters used are spatial distance $\Delta r = 3$ fm and momentum distance $\Delta p = 300$ MeV/c, with results not sensitive to variations within about 30% of these values.

## Context
This work extends previous studies using the IBUU model that under-predicted the old NSCL double neutron/proton data (NSCL 2006) regardless of adjustments to symmetry energy and in-medium cross sections. It directly addresses the recent analysis by Coupland (2014) using the ImQMD model with Skyrme interactions, which suggested protons have a slightly larger effective mass than neutrons based on the same NSCL/MSU data. The failure of the IBUU11 model with ImMDI to reproduce the data, despite the success of the ImQMD model with the SLy4 interaction, calls for new mechanisms such as isospin-dependent short-range nucleon-nucleon correlations dominated by the tensor force, which have been predicted by several microscopic many-body theories (Xu and Li, 2011; Vidana and others, 2011; Lovato and others, 2012; Carbone and others, 2012; Rios and others, 2014). The authors suggest multi-observables versus multi-inputs covariance analyses as a promising way forward.
