# Multi-nucleon transfer in ${}^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}$ and ${}^{60} \text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}$ in stochastic mean-field approach

> The multi-nucleon exchange mechanism in ${}^{58} \text{Ni}+{}^{60} \text{Ni}$ and ${}^{60} \text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}$ collisions is analyzed in the framework of the stochastic mean-field approach. The results of calculations are compared with the TDRPA calculations and the recent data of ${}^{58} \text{Ni}+{}^{60} \text{Ni}$. A good description of the data and a relatively good agreement with the TDRPA calculations are found.

# Multi-nucleon transfer in \({}^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) and \({}^{60}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) in stochastic mean-field approach

## Key claim
The stochastic mean-field (SMF) approach provides a good description of the fragment mass distribution observed in \({}^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) collisions at \(E_\mathrm{c.m.}=135.6\) MeV without adjustable parameters, but the SMF calculations give up to 30% larger values than TDRPA results for the mass dispersion in the impact parameter interval \(b=(5.2-5.6)\) fm, and the agreement with experimental data depends on the orientation (side vs. tip) of the deformed \({}^{58}\text{Ni}\) nucleus.

## Method
The authors employed the stochastic mean-field (SMF) approach, which incorporates mean-field fluctuations via an ensemble of mean-field events with initial quantal and thermal fluctuations, to compute neutron and proton diffusion coefficients and drift coefficients from TDHF wave functions using the SLy4d Skyrme interaction. From these transport coefficients, they solved coupled differential equations for the covariances \(\sigma_{NN}^2\), \(\sigma_{ZZ}^2\), and \(\sigma_{NZ}^2\) of the projectile-like fragment neutron and proton numbers, and then constructed the mass number variance \(\sigma_{AA}^2 = \sigma_{NN}^2 + \sigma_{ZZ}^2 + 2\sigma_{NZ}^2\). For the \({}^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) system, calculations were performed for both side and tip configurations of the deformed \({}^{58}\text{Ni}\) nucleus, and the final mass distribution was obtained by averaging Gaussian fragment distributions over a range of initial orbital angular momenta from \(\ell_0 = 73\hbar\) to \(\ell_m = 96\hbar\).

## Key numbers
- Center-of-mass energy: \(E_\mathrm{c.m.} = 135.6\) MeV for both \({}^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) and \({}^{60}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) collisions.
- Impact parameter for detailed covariance calculations: \(b = 5.2\) fm, corresponding to initial orbital angular momentum \(\ell = 73\hbar\).
- Contact time in \({}^{60}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) collisions: about \(600\) fm/\(c\); contact time in \({}^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) side configuration: about \(800\) fm/\(c\).
- SMF calculations give up to 30% larger mass dispersion than TDRPA results in the impact parameter interval \(b = (5.2-5.6)\) fm for \({}^{60}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\).
- Equilibrium neutron and proton numbers: \((N_0, Z_0) = (32, 28)\) for \({}^{60}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) and \((31, 28)\) for \({}^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\).
- Maximum angular momentum for detector resolution: \(\ell_m = 96\hbar\); lowest non-fusion angular momentum: \(\ell_0 = 73\hbar\).
- Box size for TDHF calculations: \(70 \times 30 \times 50\) fm\(^3\); initial separation: 30 fm.

## Context
This work extends the experimental investigation of Williams et al. (2018) on nucleon transfer in \({}^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{60}\text{Ni}\) collisions, which used TDHF and TDRPA calculations, by applying the SMF approach that incorporates mean-field fluctuations beyond the standard TDHF description. The SMF approach, developed by Ayik (2008) and further elaborated in Ayik et al. (2017, 2018), provides a microscopic framework for computing quantal transport coefficients without adjustable parameters. The results enable a more detailed understanding of how deformation and orientation affect multi-nucleon transfer in deep-inelastic collisions, and suggest that partitioning integrated experimental data into small impact parameter bins may introduce large errors that could explain discrepancies between theory and experiment.
