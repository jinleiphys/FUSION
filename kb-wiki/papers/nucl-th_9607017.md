# Links Between Different Models for Multifragmentation

> We establish links between three different models for multifragmentation: the percolation model, the lattice gas model and the statistical multifragmentation model. There are remarkable similarities between the lattice gas model and the statistical multifragmentation model. For completeness, we also compare with a model based upon classical molecular dynamics which gives rather different results.

# Links Between Different Models for Multifragmentation

## Key claim

The lattice gas model and the statistical multifragmentation model (SMFM), despite starting from seemingly very different premises, share many common features including the ability to produce a liquid-gas type phase transition with a critical point; however, the classical molecular dynamics model gives rather different results and its critical behavior appears at a temperature just below 1 MeV, far lower than the other models.

## Method

The authors compare four models for nuclear multifragmentation: the percolation model, the lattice gas model, the statistical multifragmentation model (SMFM), and classical molecular dynamics. For the lattice gas model, nucleons are placed on lattice sites with nearest-neighbor attraction epsilon and momenta sampled from a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution; clusters are defined by the condition that relative kinetic energy between adjacent nucleons is insufficient to overcome the attraction. For the SMFM, the most probable cluster distribution is obtained by minimizing free energy in a freeze-out volume with excluded volume effects, and pressure is calculated from the grand canonical ensemble. Classical molecular dynamics simulations use a Yukawa-type nucleon-nucleon potential with neutron-proton attraction and like-particle repulsion, with particles propagated via a leap-frog algorithm in a canonical ensemble with a friction coefficient to maintain constant temperature.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density: rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- Lattice gas model critical temperature (infinite system, mean field): T_c = 1.1275 |epsilon|; critical density: rho_c = rho_0/2
- SMFM critical point for mass 137: T_c = 6.8 MeV, rho_c = 0.4 rho_0
- SMFM critical point for mass 85: T_c = 6.2 MeV, rho_c = 0.14 rho_0
- Lattice gas model critical temperature (infinite system) with binding energy 16 MeV: T_c = 6.0 MeV, rho_c = rho_0/2
- Lattice gas model epsilon value used for P-V diagrams: -5 MeV
- Classical molecular dynamics critical temperature: just below 1 MeV; critical density: approximately rho_0/3
- Nuclear compressibility coefficient for molecular dynamics potential: K = 250 MeV
- Volume energy term in SMFM: W = 16 MeV
- Lattice gas model freeze-out density ratio providing good fit to data: N/n = 2.54

## Context

This work establishes theoretical connections between the percolation model (Bauer 1988, Campi 1988), the lattice gas model (Pan and Das Gupta 1995), and the statistical multifragmentation model (Bondorf et al. 1995, Fai and Randrup 1982, Gross 1985). The lattice gas model is shown to contain the percolation model as a subset while also incorporating thermal phase transitions and an equation of state. The SMFM is shown to produce P-rho diagrams and specific heat maxima similar to the lattice gas model, suggesting a unified theoretical framework for multifragmentation. The classical molecular dynamics approach (Lenk et al. 1990) yields qualitatively different results with no plateau regions in pressure isotherms and no pronounced specific heat peaks at the temperatures relevant to the other models. This comparison enables future work to identify which model features are essential for reproducing experimental data from the ALADIN and EOS TPC experiments.
