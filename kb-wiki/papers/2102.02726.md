# Normalizing flows for microscopic many-body calculations: an application to the nuclear equation of state

> Normalizing flows are a class of machine learning models used to construct a complex distribution through a bijective mapping of a simple base distribution. We demonstrate that normalizing flows are particularly well suited as a Monte Carlo integration framework for quantum many-body calculations that require the repeated evaluation of high-dimensional integrals across smoothly varying integrands and integration regions. As an example, we consider the finite-temperature nuclear equation of state. An important advantage of normalizing flows is the ability to build highly expressive models of the target integrand, which we demonstrate enables precise evaluations of the nuclear free energy and its derivatives. Furthermore, we show that a normalizing flow model trained on one target integrand can be used to efficiently calculate related integrals when the temperature, density, or nuclear force is varied. This work will support future efforts to build microscopic equations of state for numerical simulations of supernovae and neutron star mergers that employ state-of-the-art nuclear forces and many-body methods.

## Key claim

Normalizing flow-based importance sampling enables precise evaluations of high-dimensional integrals for the nuclear equation of state, and a flow trained on one integrand can be efficiently transferred to related integrals when temperature, density, or the nuclear force is varied, with the caveat that the initial training requires high sample complexity (36,000 iterations) to reach a total relative uncertainty of 10^-5.

## Method

The authors computed the second-order perturbation theory contribution to the grand canonical potential of isospin-symmetric nuclear matter from an antisymmetrized two-body force, which involves a seven-dimensional momentum-space integral. They implemented a normalizing flow using a composition of six rational-quadratic spline coupling transforms with 16 bins each, trained by minimizing the Pearson chi-squared divergence between the model distribution and the target integrand using gradient descent with the Adam optimizer. The flow was trained at a fixed density of n0 = 0.16 fm^-3 and temperature T = 25 MeV, then transferred to nearby phase space points using only 100 additional training iterations (500,000 samples).

## Key numbers

- The normalizing flow achieved a total relative uncertainty of 10^-5 for the integral Omega^(2)(n0, T=25 MeV) after 36,000 training iterations, with a batch relative uncertainty of approximately 1.5 x 10^-3, an order of magnitude improvement over VEGAS.
- The flow used batches of N=5000 samples drawn randomly at each training iteration.
- The high-momentum cutoff scale Lambda was set to 450 MeV, with integration limits replaced by k_max = 2Lambda.
- The scalar-isoscalar boson exchange potential used m_phi = 600 MeV and g = 1.
- The normalizing flow achieved speedup factors on the order of 100 compared to VEGAS when precise integral evaluations must be repeated across a multi-dimensional phase space.

## Context

This work extends adaptive Monte Carlo methods previously employed by Drischler et al. (2017) for high-order perturbation theory corrections to the cold dense matter equation of state, which used VEGAS and other Cuba library integrators. The paper addresses the computational challenge of tabulating the free energy and its derivatives across more than 1,000,000 phase space points for astrophysical simulations of supernovae and neutron star mergers, which previously relied on simplified mean field models (Lattimer 1991, Shen 1991, 2011, Steiner 2013) or microscopic calculations with limited three-body forces (Togashi 2017, Lu 2019). The approach enables future inclusion of high-order many-body perturbation theory corrections and state-of-the-art chiral effective field theory nuclear forces in finite-temperature calculations, and could be applied to nucleon single-particle energies and nuclear matter response functions.
