# An anomalous propulsion mechanism

> We consider a gas of free chiral fermions trapped inside a uniform rotating spherical shell. Once the shell becomes transparent the fermions are emitted along the axis of rotation due to the chiral and mixed anomaly. In return, owing to momentum conservation, the shell is propelled forward. We study the dependence of the magnitude of this effect on the shell parameters in a controlled setting and find that it is sensitive to the formation of an ergosphere around the rotating shell. A brief discussion on a possible relation to pulsar kicks is provided.

## Key claim

A rotating spherical shell containing a gas of free chiral fermions will be propelled forward when the shell becomes transparent and the fermions are emitted along the rotation axis, due to the chiral and mixed anomaly; the magnitude of this effect becomes large (logarithmically divergent) when the shell's rotation approaches the critical frequency at which an ergosphere forms, but for typical neutron star parameters the predicted recoil velocity is too small to be observable unless the star is extremely compact.

## Method

The authors solve the hydrodynamic conservation equations for a gas of free chiral fermions in thermal equilibrium inside a uniformly rotating spherical shell, using the thermodynamic frame and the constitutive relations that include anomalous contributions from the chiral and mixed anomalies. They compute the four-momentum of the fermion gas by integrating the stress tensor over the interior volume, then assume instantaneous emission of the gas when the shell becomes transparent and use momentum conservation to obtain the recoil velocity of the shell. The metric of the rotating shell is constructed perturbatively in the angular velocity using the Israel junction conditions, matching an interior flat metric to an exterior asymptotically flat metric.

## Key numbers

- Anomaly coefficients for a left-handed fermion: \(c_A = -1/(24\pi^2)\) and \(c_m = -1/(192\pi^2)\).
- Critical angular velocity for ergosphere formation: \(\omega_c R = (R-r_s)(R+r_s)^3/(R\lambda_0)\), where \(r_s = M/2\) is the Schwarzschild radius.
- For typical proto-neutron star parameters (\(R_* \sim 10\) km, \(M \sim 1.5 M_\odot\), rotation period \(T \sim 1\) ms), the recoil velocity is \(v \sim 10^{-23} c\).
- For extreme parameters (\(R_* \sim 6.9\) km, \(M \sim 2.2 M_\odot\), \(T \sim 0.5\) ms), an ergosphere forms (\(\delta \sim 0\)) and the recoil velocity diverges logarithmically as \(v/c \propto \ln(\delta)\).
- Neutrino luminosity from proto-neutron stars: \(E_\nu \sim 10^{52}\) erg s\(^{-1}\) over about half a second.

## Context

This work extends the study of anomalous transport phenomena in chiral fermion systems, specifically the chiral vorticity effect, to a dynamical setting where the rotating gas is suddenly released. It builds on the hydrodynamic framework for anomalous fluids developed by Jensen (2012), Banerjee (2012), and Landsteiner (2012), and on the metric for a rotating spherical shell first obtained by Brill (1966). The authors suggest a possible connection to the pulsar kick problem (Lai, 1999), where neutron stars acquire high velocities, and note that earlier proposals (Kusenko, 1998; Charbonneau, 2009; Kaminski, 2014) attributed such kicks to the chiral magnetic effect rather than the vorticity-driven mechanism studied here. The result enables future work on the dynamical evolution of rotating compact objects with anomalous fermion gases, particularly when an ergosphere is present.
