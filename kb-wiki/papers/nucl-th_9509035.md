# Modern meson--exchange potential and superfluid neutron star crust matter

> In this work we study properties of neutron star crusts, where matter is expected to consist of nuclei surrounded by superfluid neutrons and a homogeneous background of relativistic electrons. The nuclei are disposed in a Coulomb lattice, and it is believed that the structure of the lattice influences considerably the specific heat of the neutronic matter inside the crust of a neutron star. Using a modern meson--exchange potential in the framework of a local--density approximation we calculate the neutronic specific heat accounting for various shapes of the Coulomb lattice, from spherical to non--spherical nuclear shapes. We find that a realistic nucleon--nucleon potential leads to a significant increase in the neutronic specific heat with respect to that obtained assuming a uniform neutron distribution. The increase is largest for the non--spherical phase of the crust. These results may have consequences for the thermal history of young neutron stars.

# Modern meson-exchange potential and superfluid neutron star crust matter

## Key claim

Using a realistic meson-exchange nucleon-nucleon potential (Bonn A) instead of an effective interaction leads to a significant increase in the neutronic specific heat of neutron star crust matter compared to uniform neutron matter, with the largest increase occurring for non-spherical nuclear shapes; however, the pairing gaps obtained are smaller (about 2.5 MeV maximum) than those found by Broglia et al. (1994) using the Gogny interaction (about 3.5 MeV), which may have important consequences for thermal evolution models.

## Method

The authors used the Bonn A meson-exchange potential within a Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) self-consistent scheme to obtain single-particle energies and effective masses, then solved the Anderson-Morel gap equation to compute the neutron pairing gap as a function of position inside Wigner-Seitz cells. The neutron and proton density profiles were taken from Oyamatsu (1993) parametrizations for spherical, cylindrical, and slab-shaped nuclear phases at different densities in the crust, and the total fermionic specific heat was calculated including contributions from superfluid neutrons, normal neutrons, and relativistic electrons.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of nuclear matter: $\rho_0 = 2.8 \times 10^{14}$ g cm$^{-3}$
- Spherical nuclear shapes expected for densities $\rho < 0.35\rho_0$ (about 80% of the crust)
- Cylindrical region expected for densities between $0.35\rho_0$ and $0.46\rho_0$
- Slab-like form expected for $0.46\rho_0 < \rho < 0.5\rho_0$
- Neutron drip point occurs at $\rho \sim 4.3 \times 10^{11}$ g cm$^{-3}$
- Maximum pairing gap obtained in this work: about 2.5 MeV (spherical phase)
- Maximum pairing gap obtained by Broglia et al. (1994): about 3.5 MeV
- For the slab phase at $\rho/\rho_0 = 0.48$, Fermi momentum $k_F \approx 1.3$ fm$^{-1}$
- The ratio $C_T(n.u.)/C_T(u.)$ increases dramatically for non-spherical phases at temperatures $T > 0.008$ MeV, typical of the inner crust in the first $\sim 10^2$ years after formation

## Context

This work extends the approach of Broglia et al. (1994) by replacing the effective Gogny interaction with a realistic meson-exchange potential (Bonn A) from Machleidt (1989), yielding smaller pairing gaps that are consistent with those of Baldo et al. (1990) who also employed realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials. The results enable more accurate modeling of the thermal history of young neutron stars, as the enhanced specific heat from non-spherical nuclear shapes may affect the heat diffusion time through the crust, as studied by De Blasio et al. (1995).
