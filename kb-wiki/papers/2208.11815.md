# A tensor decomposition model for evaluating isotopic yield in neutron-induced fission

> After constructing yield tensors with three dimensions for 851 fission products and filling the tensors with the independent yield data from the ENDF/B-VIII.0 database, the tensor decomposition algorithm is applied to predict the independent isotopic yield in fission, which resulting in the Fission Yield Tensor Decomposition (FYTD) model. The fission yields of 235U and 239Pu are set as missing values and then predicted. The predictions for235U fissions by the FYTD model agree with the ENDF/B-VIII.0 data and are better than those by the Talys and BNN+Talys models. Furthermore, we predict not only the mass distribution but also the isotopic yields in the fissions. For fast neutron-induced fission of 239Pu, 98% predictions of the isotopic yields by the FYTD model agree with the ENDF/B-VIII.0 data within 1 order of magnitude. The fission yields of 238Np, 243Am, and 236Np that do not exist in the ENDF/B-VIII.0 database are predicted and compared with those in the JEFF-3.3 database, as well as the experimental data. Good agreement demonstrates the predictive ability of the FYTD model for the target nucleus dependence. The scalability of the yield tensor decomposition model over the incident neutron energy degrees of freedom is examined. After adding a set of 2 MeV neutron-induced 239Pu fission yield data into the yield tensor, the 2 MeV neutron-induced fission yields of 235U and 239Pu are predicted. The comparison with the experimental data shows that the predictions are similar to those by the GEF model in the peak area but more accurate in the valley area. Finally, the yields of the ratio of isomeric states and neutron excess of the products as a function of product charge number are also studied. The FYTD model can capture the multi-dimensional dependence of the fission yield data and make reasonable predictions.

# A tensor decomposition model for evaluating isotopic yield in neutron-induced fission

## Key claim

The Fission Yield Tensor Decomposition (FYTD) model can predict independent fission yields by capturing multi-dimensional dependencies from sparse data, but its reliability depends on the availability of data sharing the same dimensions as the target prediction; predictions for systems with no data in a given dimension (e.g., $N_t-Z_t$ = 47 for $^{227}$Th) are unreliable and produce extremely large root mean square error (RMSE up to 2.034).

## Method

The authors constructed 851 three-dimensional yield tensors with dimensions $Z_t$ (target proton number), $N_t-Z_t$ (target neutron excess), and $E_n$ (incident neutron energy discretized to 0.0253 eV, 0.5 MeV, and 14 MeV), filled with independent yield data from the ENDF/B-VIII.0 database for 45 fission systems across 24 target nuclei. They applied the Bayesian Gaussian CANDECOMP/PARAFAC (BGCP) tensor decomposition algorithm to iteratively update factor matrices via Bayesian inference, then normalized the reconstructed yields so that light and heavy fragment sums each equal 1.

## Key numbers

- The yield tensor covered $Z_t$ = 90–96, $N_t-Z_t$ = 47–54, and three neutron energies, reducing the missing rate to 73% (from 98% for a 6D tensor).
- For fast neutron-induced fission of $^{239}$Pu, 98% of isotopic yield predictions agreed with ENDF/B-VIII.0 data within 1 order of magnitude, and 88% within 0.5 orders of magnitude.
- The FYTD model achieved a validation $\chi_N^2$ of $0.701 \times 10^{-5}$ for $^{235}$U mass distribution, compared to $1.134 \times 10^{-5}$ for BNN-40+TALYS and $8.334 \times 10^{-5}$ for TALYS alone (Wang 2019).
- The $^{239}$Pu fast neutron-induced fission yield data spanned 16 orders of magnitude from $10^{-18}$ to $10^{-3}$.
- Prediction RMSE values for individual fission systems ranged from 0.244 (for $^{234}$U at 14 MeV) to 2.034 (for $^{227}$Th at 0.0253 eV).

## Context

This work extends machine learning approaches to fission yield prediction, specifically improving upon the BNN+TALYS model of Wang (2019) which only predicted mass distributions and overestimated valley yields. The FYTD model enables prediction of full isotopic distributions (not just mass chains) and demonstrates scalability to new neutron energies (2 MeV), where it performed similarly to the GEF model (Schmidt 2016) in peak areas but more accurately in valley areas. The method also predicts yields for nuclei missing from ENDF/B-VIII.0 (e.g., $^{236}$Np, $^{243}$Am, $^{238}$Np) and can capture isomeric state ratios and neutron excess trends.
