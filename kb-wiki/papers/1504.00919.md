# Fission dynamics within time-dependent Hartree-Fock: deformation-induced fission

> Background: Nuclear fission is a complex large-amplitude collective decay mode in heavy nuclei. Microscopic density functional studies of fission have previously concentrated on adiabatic approaches based on constrained static calculations ignoring dynamical excitations of the fissioning nucleus, and the daughter products. Purpose: To explore the ability of dynamic mean-field methods to describe fast fission processes beyond the fission barrier, using the nuclide $^{240}$Pu as an example. Methods: Time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculations based on the Skyrme interaction are used to calculate non-adiabatic fission paths, beginning from static constrained Hartree-Fock calculations. The properties of the dynamic states are interpreted in terms of the nature of their collective motion. Fission product properties are compared to data. Results: Parent nuclei constrained to begin dynamic evolution with a deformation less than the fission barrier exhibit giant-resonance-type behaviour. Those beginning just beyond the barrier explore large amplitude motion but do not fission, whereas those beginning beyond the two-fragment pathway crossing fission to final states which differ according to the exact initial deformation. Conclusions: Time-dependent Hartree-Fock is able to give a good qualitative and quantitative description of fast fission, provided one begins from a sufficiently deformed state.

## Key claim
Time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) can provide a good qualitative and quantitative description of fast fission in ²⁴⁰Pu, but only if the initial state is sufficiently deformed, specifically, starting from a deformation beyond the intersection point of the one-fragment and two-fragment fission pathways on the potential energy surface.

## Method
Static constrained Hartree-Fock (CHF) calculations using the Skyrme SkM* interaction were performed on a 3D Cartesian grid to generate initial states of ²⁴⁰Pu with varying quadrupole deformation β₂₀. These states were then evolved in time using TDHF in a larger grid, with BCS pairing correlations frozen during the dynamics, to study the resulting fission dynamics and fragment properties.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state binding energy of ²⁴⁰Pu: -1781.95 MeV, with rms radius 5.941 fm and β₂₀ = 0.280.
- Fission isomer binding energy: -1778.91 MeV, with rms radius 6.418 fm and β₂₀ = 0.682.
- First fission barrier height (E_A): 8.25 MeV; second fission barrier height (E_B): 7.68 MeV; isomer energy (E_II): 3.04 MeV (HF+BCS, SkM*).
- Static one-fragment pathway jumps to two-fragment pathway beyond β₂₀ = 1.25; the two-fragment pathway jumps back to one-fragment below β₂₀ = 1.01.
- For the two-fragment pathway at β₂₀ = 1.19, fragment masses are A₁ = 107.14 (Z₁ = 43.14) and A₂ = 132.85 (Z₂ = 50.85), corresponding to ¹⁰⁷Tc and ¹³³Sb.
- Fission occurs for initial states with β₂₀ > 1.085 (beyond the pathway intersection); scission takes between 775 and 800 fm/c for the β₂₀ = 1.19 initial state.

## Context
This work extends adiabatic constrained mean-field studies of fission (e.g., Flocard 1974, Möller 2009) by introducing non-adiabatic dynamics via TDHF, which includes time-odd fields and internal excitations absent in static calculations. It builds on earlier TDHF fission attempts (Negele 1978) and modern 3D TDHF solvers (Maruhn 2013). The results enable future studies of pre- and post-fission dynamics, including fragment mass distributions and the role of dynamical pairing, though the authors note that time-dependent pairing effects (as in Scamps 2015) are likely needed for quantitative predictions.
