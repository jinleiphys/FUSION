# When a DNA Triple helix melts: An analog of the Efimov state

> The base sequences of DNA contain the genetic code and to decode it a double helical DNA has to open its base pairs. Recent studies have shown that one can use a third strand to identify the base sequences without opening the double helix but by forming a triple helix. It is predicted here that such a three chain system exhibits the unusual behaviour of the existence of a three chain bound state in the absence of any two being bound. This phenomenon is analogous to the Efimov state in three particle quantum mechanics. A scaling theory is used to justify the Efimov connection. Real space renormalization group (RG), and exact numerical calculations are used to validate the prediction of a biological Efimov effect.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that a three-stranded DNA system can form a bound state (a triple helix) at or above the duplex melting temperature, where no two individual strands would be bound to each other. This prediction is conditional on the pairwise interactions being near their critical threshold for binding, and the effect is described as an analog of the quantum Efimov state in three-particle physics.

## Method

The authors use a scaling theory to derive an effective attractive 1/R² interaction between two noninteracting chains mediated by a third chain near the duplex melting point. They then perform real-space renormalization group calculations on hierarchical lattices with tunable dimensionality (d≥2) and exact numerical transfer matrix calculations for directed polymer models in 1+1 dimensions, using finite-size scaling to locate melting transitions.

## Key numbers

- For the hierarchical lattice with branching factor b=4, the three-chain melting occurs at y₀=2.32402..., which is less than the duplex melting point y_c=3 (b-1=3).
- For the 1+1 dimensional directed polymer model with bubble fugacity σ=0.5, the duplex melting transition occurs at y_m=4/3.
- For model A in 1+1 dimensions, the extrapolated melting temperature from finite-size scaling is y_t=1.3326±0.0001, compared to the duplex value of 1.3333±0.0001, indicating a higher melting temperature for the triplex.
- For model B in 1+1 dimensions, the extrapolated melting temperature is 1.3333±0.0001, identical to the duplex value within uncertainty.

## Context

This work extends the concept of the Efimov effect, originally discovered in nuclear physics by Efimov (1970) and later observed in cold atom systems, to a classical biological system of DNA triple helices. The paper builds on prior studies of DNA melting using directed polymer models (Fisher, 1984; Gotoh, 1983) and the Y-model for duplex unzipping (ymodel, ymodel-2, ymodel-3). The results enable experimental searches for triple-helix DNA states at temperatures above the duplex melting point, with potential implications for understanding biological processes involving DNA opening and for therapeutic applications of triplex-forming oligonucleotides.
