# Non Nucleonic Components in Short Nuclear Distances

> One of the important features of nuclear forces is their strong repulsive nature at short ($\le 0.5-0.6$~Fm) distances which prevents atomic nuclei from collapsing, thus guarantying the stability for the visible matter. However the dynamical nature of this repulsion (referred to as a nuclear core) is as elusive as ever. We present the study of nuclear dynamics at extremely large internal momenta in the deuteron dominated by the nuclear core. It is demonstrated that the paradigm shift in the description of the deuteron consisting of proton and neutron to the description of the deuteron as a pseudo-vector composite system in which proton and neutron is observed in high energy electro-disintegration processes results in the emergence of a new structure. We demonstrate that this new structure can exist only if it emerges from pre-existing non-nucleonic component in the deuteron. The study of the dynamics of the predicted new structure is presented focusing on the question if it allows to understand the anomaly observed in the recent experiment at Jefferson Lab that probed deuteron structure at internal momenta above 800 MeV/c.

# Non Nucleonic Components in Short Nuclear Distances

## Key claim
The paper argues that a new P-wave-like structure in the light-front deuteron wave function, which produces an explicit dependence on the transverse component of internal momentum, can only arise from pre-existing non-nucleonic components (such as ΔΔ, N*N, or hidden-color NcNc states) in the deuteron. This new structure may explain the anomaly observed in a recent Jefferson Lab experiment that probed deuteron structure at internal momenta above 800 MeV/c.

## Method
The author develops a light-front (LF) framework for the deuteron wave function in high-energy electrodisintegration (e + d → e′ + p + n), constructing a general vertex function Γμd that satisfies time reversal, parity, and charge conjugate symmetries. By keeping leading-order terms in the high-energy approximation and expanding the wave function through two-component spinors, the analysis reveals that the Γ5 term in the vertex produces an additional P-wave-like contribution that is purely relativistic in origin and contributes only for deuteron polarizations λd = ±1. The author then examines whether such a term could arise from purely nucleonic degrees of freedom by analyzing Weinberg-type equations on the light front for NN scattering, concluding that inelastic transitions involving non-nucleonic components are required to produce the predicted k⊥ dependence.

## Key numbers
- The nuclear repulsive core operates at short distances ≤ 0.5–0.6 Fm
- The new P-wave radial function P(k) is proportional to k³/mN³, with an extra k²/mN² factor beyond the standard l=1 term
- Measurable angular dependence in the LF momentum distribution is predicted at k ≳ 1 GeV/c
- The tensor asymmetry shows qualitative differences from S- and D-wave-only predictions already at k ≈ 800 MeV/c
- The Jefferson Lab experiment (HallC:2020kdm) reached k ∼ 1 GeV/c and qualitatively disagrees with conventional deuteron wave functions for k ≳ 800 MeV/c
- The author estimates the Γ5 vertex by assuming 1% contribution to total normalization from the P wave, parameterized in dipole form A/(1 + k²/0.71)²

## Context
This work extends the author's previous light-front approach (Sargsian 2022) to deuteron structure at extreme internal momenta, building on QCD-based predictions by Harvey (1980) and Brodsky (1985) that non-nucleonic components (ΔΔ and hidden-color states) contribute almost 90% of the strength at distances dominated by the repulsive core. The predictions directly address the anomaly observed in a Jefferson Lab experiment (HallC 2020) that measured high-Q² deuteron electro-disintegration at k ∼ 1 GeV/c. If confirmed, the results would motivate theoretical modeling of non-nucleonic components (ΔΔ, N*N, or hidden-color NcNc) and advance understanding of high-density nuclear matter and quark-hadron transitions relevant to neutron star cores. The analysis also notes that final state interactions must be carefully accounted for, building on eikonal regime treatments by Frankfurt (1996) and Sargsian (2001, 2009).
