# On the production mechanism of Sigma-hypernuclear systems in A(K-,pi+-) reactions

> It is shown that the new data on the excitation energy Eex spectrum of the residual nuclear system in the Sigma-hypernuclear region in the reactions (K-,pi+-) on Be-9 and in the reaction (K-,pi+) on He-4 and C-12 can be described without the supposition on the existence of excited Sigma-hypernuclear states. The basis is formed by a simultaneous consideration of the quasi-free Sigma production and Sigma-nuclear rescattering (elastic and with Sigma -> Lambda conversion) with account of interference of the respective amplitudes. For final decision of the question about the nature of the irregularities in Eex spectrum, it is proposed to study the picture corresponding to the so-called moving complex singularity of the triangle graph with Sigma rescattering: the position and the width of the peak in Eex distribution must appreciably change with momentum transferred from the initial kaon to the final pion.

## Key claim

The bumps observed in the excitation energy spectra of residual nuclear systems in (K⁻,π⁺⁻) reactions on ⁹Be, ⁴He, and ¹²C in the Σ-hypernuclear region can be described without invoking excited Σ-hypernuclear states, using only quasi-free Σ production, Σ-nuclear rescattering (elastic and with Σ→Λ conversion), and their interference; however, the authors acknowledge that this good description does not completely exclude the existence of Σ-hypernuclei, and a decisive test requires studying the momentum-transfer dependence of the cross section.

## Method

The authors calculated differential cross sections using Feynman diagrams: a pole graph for quasi-free Σ production (Fig. 2a) and triangle graphs for elastic Σ rescattering (Fig. 2b) and Σ→Λ conversion (Fig. 2c). They treated the relative phase between the pole and triangle amplitudes and their normalization as free parameters, using oscillator wave functions for the nuclear vertices (p₀ = 130 MeV/c for ⁹Be core protons, p₀ = 90 MeV/c for ⁴He, p₀ = 80 MeV/c for ¹²C) and a cluster-model form factor for the outer neutron in ⁹Be. For the ⁹Be(K⁻,π⁺) channel, they also considered three-particle intermediate states in the triangle graph to account for continuum states of the residual ⁸Li nucleus.

## Key numbers

- The outer neutron in ⁹Be has a binding energy of 1.67 MeV.
- For the ⁹Be(K⁻,π⁻) reaction on the outer neutron, the peak in the excitation energy spectrum has a maximum at 8–10 MeV and a width of 15–20 MeV.
- For ⁹Be(K⁻,π⁺), the maximum of the excitation energy distribution is shifted to 25–30 MeV.
- The elementary cross sections σ(Σ⁻p→Σ⁻p) and σ(Σ⁻p→Λn) are each about 150 mb in the Σ momentum region 100–200 MeV/c.
- For the ⁹Be(K⁻,π⁻) reaction, the triangle graph amplitude squared (Fig. 3b) shows a sharp peak near E_ex = 0 with a width of about 15 MeV.
- The oscillator parameter used for ⁹Be(K⁻,π⁺) was p₀ = 115 MeV/c, close to the value 110 MeV/c suggested by (e,ep) data for large proton removal energies.
- For the ¹²C(K⁻,π⁺) reaction, the authors present predictions for |M|² of the conversion graph at momentum transfers q = 200, 250, 300, and 350 MeV/c, showing a moving and broadening peak.
- For the hypernuclear resonance case (Fig. 2d), a Breit-Wigner pole with width 10 MeV and mass 15 MeV above the Σ + ground-state threshold was used; the peak position remains practically constant with q.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of BNL data on ⁹Be(K⁻,π⁺⁻) reactions at 600 MeV/c (Nagae et al., 1999), which showed structures with widths of about 20 MeV for (K⁻,π⁻) and 30–40 MeV for (K⁻,π⁺), replacing earlier indications of narrow peaks (Γ < 10 MeV) reviewed in the literature. The authors argue against the interpretation of these structures as excited Σ-hypernuclear states, which had been problematic due to the expected large widths (20–40 MeV) from ΣN→ΛN conversion (Dover and Gal, 1984; Batty et al., 1994). The proposed test using moving triangle singularities as a function of momentum transfer could distinguish reaction-mechanism peaks from genuine hypernuclear states, and the same approach could be applied to other reactions such as A(e,e'K⁻)X.
