# A new approach to solve the Boltzmann-Langevin equation for fermionic systems

> We present a new method to introduce phase-space fluctuations in transport theories, corresponding to a full implementation of the Boltzmann-Langevin equation for fermionic systems. It is based on the procedure originally developed by Bauer et al. for transport codes employing the test particle method. In the new procedure, the Pauli principle is carefully checked, leading to a good reproduction of the correct fluctuations in the ``continuum limit'' ($h \to 0$). Accurate tests are carried out in one and two dimensional idealized systems, and finally results for a full 3D application are shown. We stress the reliability of this method, which can be easily plugged into existing tranport codes using test particles, and its general applicability to systems characterized by instabilities, like for instance multifragmentation processes.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new method for implementing the Boltzmann-Langevin equation in fermionic systems that improves upon the earlier approach of Bauer et al. by carefully checking the Pauli principle for the entire cloud of test particles involved in a correlated collision, rather than only for the two original test particles, thereby achieving a good reproduction of the correct fluctuation variance in the continuum limit (h → 0).

## Method

The method is implemented within the test particle framework used in standard Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport codes. Two test particles are selected as colliding partners; if the collision is accepted, the scattering actually involves two entire "clouds" of neighboring test particles (corresponding to two full nucleons), where the contiguity is defined by a phase-space distance metric involving both coordinate and momentum separations scaled by the system's Fermi momentum and radius. The final momenta of all particles in each cloud are adjusted to conserve energy and momentum using the average momenta of the two clouds as initial states, and crucially, the Pauli blocking is checked for every test particle in the clouds, not just the original two.

## Key numbers

- The phase-space distance metric used to define neighboring test particles is \(d_{ij}^2 = (\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j)^2 / p_F^2 + (\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j)^2 / R^2\), where \(p_F\) is the Fermi momentum and \(R\) is the radius of the system.
- The equilibrium one-body density variance for a phase-space cell of volume \(\Delta s\) is given by \(\sigma^2_f(s) = f_{eq}(s) \bar{f}_{eq}(s) h^D / (g \Delta s)\), where \(D\) is the spatial dimension and \(g\) is the degeneracy factor.
- The average number of transitions between phase-space cells in a time step \(\Delta t\) is \(\bar{\nu}(12;34) = (\Delta s_1 / h^D)(\Delta s_2 / h^D)(\Delta s_3 / h^D)(\Delta s_4 / h^D) f_1 f_2 \bar{f}_3 \bar{f}_4 W(12;34) \delta(\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2) \delta(\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_3) \delta(\mathbf{r}_2 - \mathbf{r}_4) \Delta t\), and its variance equals the mean (\(\sigma^2_\nu = \bar{\nu}\)).
- The method was tested for a \(^{20}\text{Ne} + ^{20}\text{Ne}\) reaction at 100 AMeV in the original work by Bauer et al.

## Context

This work extends the stochastic mean-field approach to nuclear dynamics pioneered by Ayik and Randrup, who derived the Boltzmann-Langevin equation as a stochastic extension of the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck transport equation. The method directly improves upon the earlier pseudo-particle correlation method of Bauer et al. (1992), which introduced phase-space fluctuations but suffered from Pauli principle violations that led to inaccurate fluctuation variances. The new procedure enables full 3D simulations of the Boltzmann-Langevin equation, which was previously only feasible in 2D using the lattice method of Chomaz et al. (1998), and is applicable to systems characterized by instabilities such as multifragmentation processes in heavy-ion collisions.
