# The anomalous $^{14}$C-dating $β$ decay problem revisited

> The anomalous inhibition of $^{14}$C-dating $β$ decay rate is restudied in terms of shell-model calculations in the $jj$ coupling scheme with both realistic and empirical Hamiltonians. It is seen that the accidental cancellation of the decay strength is dominated by the mixing effect of two configurations of the final state wave function, $|0p^{-2}_{1/2}>$ and $|0p_{3/2}^{-1}0p_{1/2}^{-1}>$. By decomposing the effective interactions into different tensor components, it is clearly seen that the mixing is largely induced by the tensor force. The failure of realistic calculations in reproducing the inhibition may be related to its ill description of the monopole component rather than the tensor force.

# The anomalous $^{14}$C-dating $\beta$ decay problem revisited

## Key claim

The accidental cancellation that suppresses the $^{14}$C beta decay rate is dominated by the mixing of two configurations in the final state wave function, $|0p^{-2}_{1/2}\rangle$ and $|0p_{3/2}^{-1}0p_{1/2}^{-1}\rangle$, with the mixing largely induced by the tensor force; however, the failure of realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials to reproduce the inhibition is related to an ill description of the monopole component of the effective interaction rather than the tensor force itself.

## Method

Shell-model calculations in the $jj$ coupling scheme were performed for the $^{14}$C ($J^\pi=0^+$, $T=1$) and $^{14}$N ($J^\pi=1^+$, $T=0$) ground states, treating the nuclei as two holes in the $0p_{1/2}$ and $0p_{3/2}$ orbits relative to a $^{16}$O inert core. The effective interactions were decomposed into central, spin-orbit, and tensor components via the spin-tensor decomposition procedure, and the wave function amplitudes and Gamow-Teller transition matrix elements were computed for both empirical interactions (CK, HWM, WBT, WBP) and realistic interactions derived from chiral and Bonn potentials.

## Key numbers

- The experimental half-life of $^{14}$C corresponds to a transition amplitude thousands of times smaller than typical allowed Gamow-Teller transitions.
- The energy splitting between $0p_{1/2}^{-1}$ and $0p_{3/2}^{-1}$ orbits in $^{15}$C and $^{15}$N is $\varepsilon = 6.3$ MeV (CK), 7.3 MeV (HWM), and 6.5 MeV (WBT).
- For the chiral interaction, the diagonal matrix element $\langle 0p_{1/2}^2|V|0p_{1/2}^2\rangle^{J=0,T=1}$ changes from $-0.61$ MeV to $+1.91$ MeV under in-medium modification; the corresponding $J=1,T=0$ term changes from $-1.67$ MeV to $+0.72$ MeV.
- The wave function amplitude $a$ for $^{14}$N is 0.95 (CK), 0.96 (HWM), 0.90 (WBT), 0.72 (chiral), and 0.80 (medium chiral); the amplitude $b$ is $-0.31$ (CK), $-0.27$ (HWM), $-0.43$ (WBT), $-0.68$ (chiral), and $-0.59$ (medium chiral).
- With the tensor force removed, the $^{14}$N ground state is overwhelmingly dominated by $|0p^{-2}_{1/2}\rangle$, with $b$ values of $-0.085$ (CK), $-0.086$ (HWM), and $-0.033$ (WBT) for central force only.

## Context

This work revisits the long-standing problem of the anomalously long $^{14}$C beta decay half-life, first identified by Inglis (1953) and Jancovici and Talmi (1954), who introduced an unreasonably large tensor force to induce the cancellation. Later studies by Zamick (1966), Fayache (1999), and Zheng (1991) showed that realistic Hamiltonians derived from the Hamada-Johnston and Bonn potentials could not reproduce the cancellation, a failure also seen in recent calculations by Holt et al. (2008, 2009, 2010) with modern one-boson-exchange and chiral potentials. The present work systematically compares empirical and realistic interactions, demonstrating that the problem lies in the monopole components of realistic interactions rather than the tensor force, and suggests that rigorous in-medium modification or three-nucleon corrections to the monopole interaction would be needed to resolve the discrepancy.
