# Exact solution of the pairing problem for spherical and deformed systems

> There has been increasing interest in studying the Richardson model from which one can derive the exact solution for certain pairing Hamiltonians. However, it is still a numerical challenge to solve the nonlinear equations involved. In this paper we tackle this problem by employing a simple hybrid polynomial approach. The method is found to be robust and is valid for both deformed and nearly spherical nuclei. It also provides important and convenient initial guesses for spherical systems with large degeneracy. As an example, we apply the method to study the shape coexistence in neutron-rich Ni isotopes.

## Key claim

The paper presents a hybrid polynomial approach that can solve the nonlinear Richardson equations for pairing Hamiltonians in both deformed and spherical nuclei, overcoming numerical instabilities that arise from singularities and complex-valued Richardson variables. The method is robust for systems with doubly-degenerate orbitals and can be extended to spherical systems with large degeneracy by slightly breaking the degeneracy to provide initial guesses.

## Method

The authors reformulate the nonlinear Richardson equations into polynomial equations for the expansion coefficients of a polynomial whose roots are the Richardson variables. They develop three variants: Eq. (poly3) and Eq. (poly4) for doubly-degenerate orbitals, and Eq. (poly5) for systems with higher degeneracy. For spherical systems where Eq. (poly3) and Eq. (poly4) become ill-defined, the degeneracy is artificially broken by a small deformation (1% splitting) to generate initial guesses, after which the full polynomial approach is applied.

## Key numbers

- Largest problem solved: half-filled system with 25 pairs and 50 orbitals, corresponding to a Hilbert space dimension of 1.26 × 10¹⁴
- Computation time on a desktop with Intel Core i7-2600k 3.40 GHz processor (single thread): approximately 0.3 seconds for 10 orbitals, 5 seconds for 20 orbitals, and 170 seconds for 50 orbitals (half-filled systems)
- For the two-level half-filled system with N=6 pairs in two Ω=6 orbitals separated by one unit, the approximate solution from the degeneracy-breaking method shows nearly negligible difference from the exact solution for G < 0.15
- The deformation splitting parameter used to break degeneracy is d = 0.01 (1% of the orbital separation)

## Context

This work extends the polynomial approach of Faribault (2011) and Guan (2012) for solving Richardson-Gaudin equations, which are known to be numerically challenging due to singularities and complex solutions. The method enables exact pairing calculations for both deformed and spherical nuclei, overcoming limitations of the BCS approximation such as particle number fluctuations and collapsed condensate around shell closures. The authors apply their method to study shape coexistence in neutron-rich Ni isotopes, specifically ⁷⁰Ni, building on prior potential energy surface calculations by Wu (2015) and Xu (2013).
