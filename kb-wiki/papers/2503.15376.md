# Study of hadron interactions and compositeness

> Plenty of hadrons have been established experimentally, yet the nonperturbative nature of the strong interaction complicates a comprehensive understanding of their internal structure, particularly for exotic hadrons that extend beyond conventional mesons and baryons. One prominent candidate for the internal structure of exotic hadrons is the hadronic molecule, a loosely bound system of hadrons analogous to atomic nuclei. Understanding such systems requires precise knowledge of hadron interactions, which traditional scattering experiments struggle to provide, especially in the low-energy region. Recent advances, including precise baryon-baryon interaction measurements, femtoscopy techniques that probe momentum correlations between particles, and first-principles lattice QCD calculations, have significantly improved our understanding of hadron interactions. Here, we review these recent developments, demonstrate the successful application of femtoscopy to antikaon-nucleon interactions, utilize the concept of compositeness to quantify the hadronic molecular component of the Lambda(1405), and discuss both experimental and theoretical prospects, including future studies at J-PARC.

# Study of hadron interactions and compositeness

## Key claim

The paper argues that the $\Lambda(1405)$ resonance is predominantly a $\bar{K}N$ hadronic molecular state, with the compositeness (fraction of molecular component) exceeding 0.5 even when accounting for theoretical uncertainties, but this conclusion depends on the weak-binding relation applied to a quasibound state and requires careful handling of complex compositeness and correction terms.

## Method

The author reviews three complementary approaches to hadron interactions: high-precision baryon-baryon scattering experiments (J-PARC E40), femtoscopy of two-particle momentum correlation functions (ALICE collaboration), and lattice QCD calculations (HAL QCD and BaSc collaborations). For the $\Lambda(1405)$ specifically, the chiral coupled-channel framework at next-to-leading order and next-to-next-to-leading order is used to extract the $\bar{K}N$ scattering length and eigenenergy. The weak-binding relation generalized to unstable quasibound states is then applied to these observables to determine the complex $\bar{K}N$ compositeness, with a prescription to convert it into a physically interpretable fraction.

## Key numbers

- The $\Lambda(1405)$ has a binding energy of approximately 15 MeV relative to the $\bar{K}N$ threshold (ParticleDataGroup 2024).
- The ALICE $K^{-}p$ correlation function data resolve the cusp at the $\bar{K}^{0}n$ threshold at $q\sim 58$ MeV/$c$.
- The best fit to ALICE data uses a Gaussian source size $R\sim 0.9$ fm and $\pi\Sigma$ weight $\omega_{\pi\Sigma}\sim 2$ (Kamiya 2019).
- ALICE Pb-Pb collisions correspond to a larger source size of $R\sim 5.2$ fm, confirming suppression of low-momentum correlation.
- The $\bar{K}N$ interaction range is estimated from $\rho$ meson exchange as $R_{\rm typ} \sim 0.25$ fm.
- The length scale associated with the $\pi\Sigma$ threshold energy difference is $\ell \sim 1.08$ fm.
- The $\bar{K}N$ compositeness $\tilde{X}_{\bar{K}N}$ of the $\Lambda(1405)$ remains greater than 0.5 when including the uncertainty band (Kamiya 2015, Kamiya 2016).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing study of the $\Lambda(1405)$ as a candidate exotic hadron, originally predicted by Dalitz (1959) from $\bar{K}N$ scattering data. It builds on chiral coupled-channel analyses by Kaiser (1995), Oset (1997), and Oller (2000), and the next-to-leading order determinations by Ikeda (2011, 2012) and Mai (2012, 2014). The femtoscopy technique applied here was pioneered by the ALICE collaboration (2019) and theoretically formulated by Kamiya (2019). The compositeness concept originates from Weinberg (1965) and was generalized to unstable states by Kamiya (2015, 2016). The results enable future studies at J-PARC, including femtoscopy of correlation functions involving light nuclei such as $\Lambda\alpha$ and $\Xi\alpha$ (Jinno 2024, Kamiya 2024).
