# Dispersive evaluation of the Lamb shift in muonic deuterium from chiral effective field theory

> We merge the dispersive relation approach and the ab initio method to compute nuclear structure corrections to the Lamb shift in muonic deuterium. We calculate the deuteron response functions and corresponding uncertainties up to next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order in chiral effective field theory and compare our results to selected electromagnetic data to test the validity of the theory. We then feed response functions calculated over a wide range of kinematics to the dispersion-theory formalism and show that an improved accuracy is obtained compared to that with the use of available experimental data in the dispersive analysis. This opens up the possibility of applying this hybrid method to other light muonic atoms and supplementing experimental data with ab initio theory for kinematics where data are scarce or difficult to measure with the goal of reducing uncertainties in estimates of nuclear structure effects in atomic spectroscopy.

## Key claim

By merging chiral effective field theory (χEFT) response functions with a dispersion-relations formalism, the authors achieve improved accuracy for the nuclear structure correction to the Lamb shift in muonic deuterium compared to using only available experimental data in the dispersive analysis, though the improvement is conditional on the χEFT calculations being validated against electromagnetic data in accessible kinematic regions.

## Method

The authors computed deuteron response functions \(R_L\) and \(R_T\) using χEFT at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N3LO) in the nuclear interactions and up to order \(k^3\) in the electromagnetic currents, solving the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for the nucleon-nucleon system. They validated these response functions by comparing calculated photodissociation and electrodissociation cross sections against experimental data, then fed the response functions over a wide kinematic range into the dispersion-theory formalism to evaluate the two-photon exchange contribution to the Lamb shift. The subtraction function was handled via a nuclear sum rule that exploits the separation between nuclear and hadronic energy scales.

## Key numbers

- Baldin sum rule prediction: \(\alpha_{E1}+\beta_{M1} = 0.698(18)\ \text{fm}^3\), coinciding with the empirical result \(0.69(0.04)\ \text{fm}^3\) from Friar (1983)
- Electric polarizability: \(\alpha_{E1} = 0.626(18)\ \text{fm}^3\), compared to the empirical extraction \(0.61(0.04)\ \text{fm}^3\) from Friar (1983)
- Magnetic polarizability: \(\beta_{M1} = 0.0715(15)\ \text{fm}^3\), compared to the model result \(0.071(6)\ \text{fm}^3\) from Friar (1983)
- Dipole contribution to electric polarizability: \(\alpha_{E1}^\mathrm{dipole} = 0.6340(10)\ \text{fm}^3\), in agreement with the average over phenomenological potentials \(0.6328(17)\ \text{fm}^3\) from Friar (1997)

## Context

This work merges two recent advances: the χEFT approach of Hernandez et al. (2014, 2017, 2019) for rigorous uncertainty estimates, and the fully relativistic dispersive treatment of Carlson et al. (2013) which gave a 35% uncertainty due to scarce experimental data in the low-energy quasielastic regime. By using χEFT to generate the electron-deuteron scattering data that inform the dispersion analysis, the authors obtain model-independent results with controlled approximations, opening the possibility of applying this hybrid method to other light muonic atoms where experimental data are scarce or difficult to measure.
