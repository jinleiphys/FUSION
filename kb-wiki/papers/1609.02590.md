# Collectivity and electromagnetic radiation in small systems

> Collective behaviour has been observed in hadronic measurements of high multiplicity proton+lead collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), as well as in (proton, deuteron, helium-3)+gold collisions at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC). To better understand the evolution dynamics and the properties of the matter created in these small systems, a systematic study of the soft hadronic observables together with electromagnetic radiation from these collisions is performed, using a hydrodynamic framework. Quantitative agreement is found between theoretical calculations and existing experimental hadronic observables. The validity of the fluid dynamical description is estimated by calculating Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers. Sizeable thermal yields are predicted for low $p_T$ photons. Further predictions of higher order charged hadron anisotropic flow coefficients and of thermal photon enhancement are proposed.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a hydrodynamic framework can quantitatively describe both hadronic and electromagnetic observables in small collision systems (p+Pb, p+Au, d+Au, \(^3\)He+Au), but the validity of this fluid-dynamical description is explicitly conditional on the system being sufficiently close to local equilibrium, as estimated by Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers.

## Method
The authors simulate small collision systems using a (2+1)D and (3+1)D viscous hydrodynamics framework (VISH2+1 and MUSIC) with Monte-Carlo-Glauber initial conditions, a lattice-QCD based equation of state (s95p-v1.2), and a hadronic cascade afterburner (UrQMD v3.4) for the dilute phase. Photon production is computed from thermal emission in the QGP and hadronic phases, including shear viscous corrections, plus prompt photons from perturbative QCD at next-to-leading order with nCTEQ15 nuclear parton distribution functions.

## Key numbers
- Effective specific shear viscosity \(\eta/s = 0.08\) for collisions at top RHIC energy (200 GeV) and \(\eta/s = 0.10\) for p+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV
- Switching temperature \(T_\mathrm{sw} = 155\) MeV for transition from hydrodynamics to hadronic cascade
- Hydrodynamic starting time \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm/\(c\)
- In 0-5% p+Au collisions at 200 GeV: charged hadron multiplicity \(dN^\mathrm{ch}/d\eta = 11.8(1)\), \(\langle p_T \rangle(\pi^+) = 0.52(1)\) GeV, \(v_2^\mathrm{ch}\{2\} = 0.037(1)\)
- In 0-5% d+Au collisions at 200 GeV: charged hadron multiplicity \(dN^\mathrm{ch}/d\eta = 17.7(1)\), \(\langle p_T \rangle(\pi^+) = 0.50(1)\) GeV, \(v_2^\mathrm{ch}\{2\} = 0.054(1)\)
- In 0-5% \(^3\)He+Au collisions at 200 GeV: charged hadron multiplicity \(dN^\mathrm{ch}/d\eta = 22.9(1)\), \(\langle p_T \rangle(\pi^+) = 0.49(1)\) GeV, \(v_2^\mathrm{ch}\{2\} = 0.059(1)\)
- Identified particle mean \(\langle p_T \rangle\) values at RHIC are about 20% smaller than in p+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV
- Anisotropic flow coefficients at RHIC are about 50% smaller in p+Au than in p+Pb at 5.02 TeV
- Averaged number of binary collisions \(N_\mathrm{coll} = 15.92(4)\) for 0-1% p+Pb at 5.02 TeV; \(N_\mathrm{coll} = 10.28(1)\) for 0-5% p+Au at 200 GeV; \(N_\mathrm{coll} = 18.48(2)\) for 0-5% d+Au at 200 GeV; \(N_\mathrm{coll} = 26.48(2)\) for 0-5% \(^3\)He+Au at 200 GeV

## Context
This work extends previous hydrodynamic studies of small systems by Shen et al. (2015) to include detailed comparisons with hadronic measurements and systematic predictions of direct photon observables across multiple small collision systems. It builds on the observation of collective behavior in high-multiplicity p+Pb collisions at the LHC (CMS, ATLAS) and (p, d, \(^3\)He)+Au collisions at RHIC (PHENIX). The results enable future experimental searches for thermal photon enhancement at low \(p_T\) in small systems, which would provide a penetrating probe of the quark-gluon plasma droplet dynamics. The paper also quantifies the validity of the hydrodynamic approach by computing Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers, addressing the fundamental question of whether fluid dynamics can apply in systems only a few fm in size.
