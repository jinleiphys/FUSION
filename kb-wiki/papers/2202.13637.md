# Time-dependent generator coordinate method study of fission: dissipation effects

> Starting from a quantum theory of dissipation for nuclear collective motion, the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) is extended to allow for dissipation effects in the description of induced fission dynamics. The extension is based on a generalization of the GCM generating functions that includes excited states, and the resulting equation of motion in the collective coordinates and excitation energy. With the assumption of a narrow hamiltonian kernel, an expansion in a power series in collective momenta leads to a Schrödinger-like equation that explicitly includes a dissipation term, proportional to the momentum of the statistical wave function. An illustrative calculation is performed for induced fission of $^{228}$Th. The three-dimensional model space includes the axially-symmetric quadrupole and octupole shape variables, and the nuclear temperature. When compared to data for photo-induced fission of $^{228}$Th, the calculated fission yields demonstrate the important role of the additional term in the hamiltonian that explicitly takes into account the dissipation of energy of collective motion into intrinsic degrees of freedom.

## Key claim

The authors extend the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) to include dissipation effects in induced fission dynamics by generalizing the GCM generating functions to include excited states, but the dissipation function itself is approximated with a phenomenological Gaussian random ansatz rather than determined fully microscopically, and the dissipative term is neglected in the actual flux calculation through the scission hyper-surface.

## Method

Starting from a quantum theory of dissipation for nuclear collective motion (Kerman and Koonin, 1974), the authors derive a Schrodinger-like equation that includes a dissipation term proportional to the momentum of the statistical wave function. They perform a three-dimensional TDGCM calculation for induced fission of 228Th in the space of axially-symmetric quadrupole and octupole shape variables and nuclear temperature, using the finite-temperature relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov model with the DD-PC1 energy density functional and a finite-range separable pairing force, with the collective potential given by the Helmholtz free energy and the mass tensor calculated in the finite-temperature perturbative cranking approximation.

## Key numbers

* For 228Th, the collective space intervals are: -1 ≤ β₂ ≤ 7 with step Δβ₂ = 0.04; 0 ≤ β₃ ≤ 3.5 with step Δβ₃ = 0.05; temperature range 0 ≤ T ≤ 2.0 MeV with step ΔT = 0.1 MeV.
* The time step is δt = 5×10⁻⁴ zs (1 zs = 10⁻²¹ s), and charge and mass distributions are calculated after 4×10⁴ time steps, corresponding to 20 zs.
* The absorption rate parameter is r = 20×10²² s⁻¹ and the width of the absorption band is w = 1.0.
* The cut-off value for the dissipation function is β₂⁰ = 1.5, set slightly beyond the second fission barrier for 228Th.
* The average excitation energy of the initial state E_coll* is chosen 1 MeV above the fission barrier for fixed-temperature calculations, and 11 MeV for the full 3D calculation.
* The pairing phase transition occurs at critical temperature T_c ∼ 0.7 MeV, and a shape transition at T ∼ 1.2 MeV.
* For T = 0 MeV, the scission contour starts from an elongated symmetric point at β₂ ∼ 6 and evolves to minimal elongation β₂ ∼ 3 as reflection asymmetry increases.

## Context

This work extends the adiabatic TDGCM approach, which has been successfully employed in fission studies by Regnier (2016, 2018), Tao (2017), and Zhao (2019, 2020, 2021), by explicitly including dissipation effects that are present in time-dependent density functional theory approaches (Simenel, 2012; Bulgac, 2016) but absent in the standard TDGCM. The method revives a microscopic theory of dissipation introduced by Kerman and Koonin (1974) and provides a framework for including energy dissipation from collective motion into intrinsic degrees of freedom during the saddle-to-scission phase of fission, though the phenomenological treatment of the dissipation function and the neglect of the dissipative term in the flux calculation limit the fully microscopic character of the approach.
