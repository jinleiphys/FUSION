# Neutron drip transition in accreting and nonaccreting neutron star crusts

> The neutron-drip transition in the dense matter constituting the interior of neutron stars generally refers to the appearance of unbound neutrons as the matter density reaches some threshold density $ρ_\textrm{drip}$. This transition has been mainly studied under the cold catalyzed matter hypothesis. However, this assumption is unrealistic for accreting neutron stars. After examining the physical processes that are thought to be allowed in both accreting and nonaccreting neutron stars, suitable conditions for the onset of neutron drip are derived and general analytical expressions for the neutron drip density and pressure are obtained. Moreover, we show that the neutron-drip transition occurs at lower density and pressure than those predicted within the mean-nucleus approximation. This transition is studied numerically for various initial composition of the ashes from X-ray bursts and superbursts using microscopic nuclear mass models.

## Key claim

The neutron-drip transition in accreting neutron stars occurs at lower density and pressure than predicted by the mean-nucleus approximation, and the equilibrium nuclei at the onset of neutron drip are stable against neutron emission, contrary to earlier assumptions based on semi-empirical mass formulae.

## Method

The authors derived general analytical expressions for the neutron drip density and pressure by examining the stability of dense matter against electron captures and neutron emissions, going beyond the mean-nucleus approximation to allow for continuous variation of the neutron fraction. They applied these conditions to both nonaccreting (cold catalyzed) and accreting neutron stars, using microscopic nuclear mass models (HFB-19, HFB-20, HFB-21) to compute the transition numerically for various initial compositions of X-ray burst and superburst ashes.

## Key numbers

- For nonaccreting neutron stars, the neutron drip density is approximately \(4.3 \times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) (e.g., \(4.40 \times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) for HFB-19, \(4.39 \times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) for HFB-20, \(4.30 \times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) for HFB-21).
- The corresponding drip pressures for nonaccreting stars are \(7.91 \times 10^{29}\) dyn cm\(^{-2}\) (HFB-19), \(7.89 \times 10^{29}\) dyn cm\(^{-2}\) (HFB-20), and \(7.84 \times 10^{29}\) dyn cm\(^{-2}\) (HFB-21).
- For accreting neutron stars, the neutron drip density was previously found to be \(6.1 \times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) for pure \(^{56}\)Fe ashes and \(7.8 \times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) for pure \(^{106}\)Pd ashes (Haensel and Zdunik 1990, 2003).
- The equilibrium nucleus at the bottom of the outer crust in nonaccreting stars is \(^{126}\)Sr (HFB-19, HFB-20) or \(^{124}\)Sr (HFB-21), with positive neutron separation energies (0.73, 0.48, and 0.83 MeV respectively), while the corresponding drip-line isotopes (\(^{121}\)Sr) have negative separation energies (-0.62, -0.71, -0.33 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the study of the neutron-drip transition beyond the cold catalyzed matter hypothesis of Harrison and Wheeler (1958) and Harrison et al. (1965), which is unrealistic for accreting neutron stars. The authors correct misconceptions from the mean-nucleus approximation used in earlier work (e.g., Baym et al. 1971, Shapiro and Teukolsky 1983) by showing that the transition involves an infinitesimally small fraction of nuclei emitting neutrons, leading to a continuous variation of the neutron chemical potential. The results enable more accurate modeling of neutron star crust properties relevant to phenomena such as pulsar glitches and thermal relaxation of transiently accreting neutron stars, and provide a framework for future calculations using updated nuclear mass models.
