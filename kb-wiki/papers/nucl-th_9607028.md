# Exact method for locating potential resonances and Regge trajectories

> We propose an exact method for locating the zeros of the Jost function for analytic potentials in the complex momentum--plane. We further extend the method to the complex angular--momentum plane to provide the Regge trajectories. It is shown, by using several examples, that highly accurate results for extremely wide as well as for extremely narrow resonances with or without the presence of the Coulomb interaction can be obtained.

## Key claim
The paper presents an exact method for locating Jost function zeros in the complex momentum plane and complex angular momentum plane, claiming that it achieves highly accurate results for both extremely wide and extremely narrow resonances, including cases with Coulomb interaction, without requiring expansion or variational procedures.

## Method
The method combines the variable-constant approach with complex coordinate rotation to solve the radial Schrödinger equation. The Jost function is obtained directly by integrating first-order coupled differential equations for functions that become constant at large distances, using two different representations of the wave function: one using Hankel functions for large distances and another using regular and irregular Coulomb functions for short distances. The zeros of the Jost function are located by searching for minima of its modulus in the complex momentum or angular momentum plane, exploiting the maximum modulus principle for holomorphic functions.

## Context
This work extends earlier complex-energy methods for locating resonances, which typically required expansion in square-integrable functions or variational procedures as described in reviews by Kukulin (1989) and Rescigno (1985). The authors compare their results against the direct dilatation method of Isaacson (1986) which achieved 5-digit accuracy using over 40 basis functions, the real-energy box methods of Maier (1980) and Mandelzweig (1982), and the semi-complex method of Yamani (1986). The method enables uniform treatment of bound, scattering, and resonant states regardless of width, and can be generalized to coupled-channel problems and nonlocal potentials.
