# Understanding the approach to thermalization from the eigenspectrum of non-Abelian gauge theories

> We study some interesting aspects of the spectral properties of SU(3) gauge theory, both with and without dynamical quarks (QCD) at thermal equilibrium using lattice gauge theory techniques. By calculating the eigenstates of a massless overlap Dirac operator on the gauge configurations, we implement a gauge-invariant method to study spectral properties of non-Abelian gauge theories. We have unambiguously categorized Dirac eigenvalues into different regimes based on a quantity defined in terms of the ratios of nearest neighbor spacings. While majority of these eigenstates below the magnetic scale are similar to those of random matrices belonging to the Gaussian Unitary ensemble at temperatures much higher than the chiral crossover transition in QCD, a few among them start to become prominent only near the crossover. These form fractal-like clusters with the median value for their fractal dimensions hinting at the universality class of the chiral transition in QCD. We further demonstrate that momentum modes below the magnetic scale in a particular non-equilibrium state of QCD are classically chaotic and estimate an upper bound on the thermalization time $\sim 1.44$ fm/c by matching this magnetic scale with that of a thermal state at $\sim 600$ MeV.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Dirac eigenspectrum of SU(3) gauge theory and QCD can be categorized into distinct regimes based on nearest-neighbor spacing ratios, and that matching the magnetic scale between a classical non-thermal state and a thermal state at approximately 624 MeV provides an upper bound on the thermalization time, with the caveat that this bound is estimated by assuming thermalization is achieved when the magnetic scale reaches its thermal value.

## Method

The authors used lattice gauge theory with Möbius domain wall fermions for 2+1 flavor QCD on 32³×8 lattices at temperatures between 149-195 MeV, and pure SU(3) gauge configurations at T≈624 MeV. They computed eigenvalues of the massless overlap Dirac operator and classified them into "bulk" and "intermediate" eigenmodes using the ratio of consecutive level spacings ⟨r̃⟩. For the non-equilibrium study, they evolved classical SU(3) gauge fields on a 64³ lattice from an over-occupied initial condition and measured the Lyapunov exponent from the exponential growth of a gauge-invariant distance between initially close trajectories.

## Key numbers

- The thermalization time upper bound for QCD with dynamical quarks is τ_th ≈ 1.44 fm/c, estimated by matching the magnetic scale of a non-thermal state to that of a thermal state at T ≈ 624 MeV
- For pure SU(3) gauge theory without fermions, the thermalization time estimate is τ_th ≈ 5.2 fm/c
- The Lyapunov exponent γ scales with energy density as γ/Q = 0.0400(7) × (ε/Q⁴)^(0.24(3)), with γ⁻¹ ≈ 4.9(1) fm/c at ε = 0.209 Q⁴
- The pseudo-critical temperature for the chiral crossover is T_c = 156.5(1.5) MeV
- The median fractal dimension D_f of intermediate eigenmodes near T_c ranges between 2.49-2.55, consistent with the O(4) universality class value D_f ≈ 2.485
- Bulk eigenmodes have ⟨r̃⟩ = 0.60266, consistent with the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble prediction
- The magnetic scale in QCD with dynamical quarks at T ≈ 624 MeV is g²T/π = 1.19T, approximately 1.5 times larger than in pure SU(3) gauge theory (0.81T)

## Context

This work extends the Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit conjecture to non-Abelian gauge theories in three spatial dimensions, which had previously only been verified in spin systems. The study builds on lattice QCD investigations of the Dirac spectrum by the HotQCD collaboration (2014) and earlier work on spectral properties by Kovacs (2017) and Giordano (2021). The thermalization time estimate connects to non-equilibrium studies of over-occupied gluon systems by Berges (2013) and provides a non-perturbative upper bound that is smaller than the perturbative QCD estimate of approximately 3 fm/c by Heinz (2004). The fractal dimension analysis near the chiral crossover enables future studies of the U_A(1) restoration and the nature of the QCD phase transition.
