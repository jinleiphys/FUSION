# Radiation of Neutron Stars Produced by Superfluid Core

> We find that neutron star interior is transparent for collisionless electron sound, the same way as it is transparent for neutrinos. In the presence of magnetic field the electron sound is coupled with electromagnetic radiation and form the fast magnetosonic wave. We find that electron sound is generated by superfluid vortices in the stellar core. Thermally excited helical vortex waves produce fast magnetosonic waves in the stellar crust which propagate toward the surface and transform into outgoing electromagnetic radiation. The vortex radiation has the spectral index -0.45 and can explain nonthermal radiation of middle-aged pulsars observed in the infrared, optical and hard X-ray bands. The radiation is produced in the stellar interior which allows direct determination of the core temperature. Comparing the theory with available spectra observations we find that the core temperature of the Vela pulsar is T=8*10^8K, while the core temperature of PSR B0656+14 and Geminga exceeds 2*10^8K. This is the first measurement of the temperature of a neutron star core. The temperature estimate rules out equation of states incorporating Bose condensations of pions or kaons and quark matter in these objects. Based on the temperature estimate and cooling models we determine the critical temperature of triplet neutron superfluidity in the Vela core Tc=(7.5\pm 1.5)*10^9K which agrees well with recent data on behavior of nucleon interactions at high energies. Another finding is that in the middle aged neutron stars the vortex radiation, rather then thermal conductivity, is the main mechanism of heat transfer from the stellar core to the surface. Electron sound opens a perspective of direct spectroscopic study of superdense matter in the neutron star interiors.

## Key claim

The paper claims that neutron star interiors are transparent to collisionless electron sound (zero sound), and that thermally excited helical waves on superfluid vortices in the core generate fast magnetosonic waves that propagate through the crust and transform into observable electromagnetic radiation. The key hedge is that this mechanism applies specifically to middle-aged pulsars and that the radiation spectrum depends on the core temperature, which the author claims to measure for the first time.

## Method

The author develops a theoretical model in which superfluid neutron vortices in the stellar core support thermally excited helical waves; these waves, via the drag effect on superconducting protons, generate density oscillations in electrons at the core-crust interface. The resulting zero sound waves couple with the magnetic field to form fast magnetosonic waves that propagate through the crust in a collisionless regime (frequencies above about 10^12 Hz) and transform into outgoing electromagnetic radiation near the stellar surface. The predicted spectrum is compared with observed nonthermal emission from middle-aged pulsars in infrared, optical, and hard X-ray bands to infer core temperatures.

## Key numbers

- Spectral index of vortex radiation: α ≈ -0.45.
- Core temperature of the Vela pulsar: T ≈ 8 × 10^8 K.
- Core temperature of PSR B0656+14 and Geminga: exceeds 2 × 10^8 K.
- Critical temperature of triplet neutron superfluidity in the Vela core: T_c = (7.5 ± 1.5) × 10^9 K.
- Density at the outer crust boundary: ≈ 4.3 × 10^11 g/cm^3.
- Saturation nuclear matter density ψ_0: 2.8 × 10^14 g/cm^3.
- Vortex lattice period for neutron superfluid at Ω/2π = 10 s^{-1}: b = 6 × 10^{-3} cm.
- Vortex core size: ξ = 10^{-12} cm.
- Total number of vortices in the superfluid: N ≈ 10^17.
- Electron-phonon collision frequency at T = 10^8 K, ρ = 10^14 g/cm^3: ν_{e,ph} = 8 × 10^11 s^{-1}.
- Electron-electron collision frequency at same conditions: ν_{e,e} = 10^10 s^{-1}.
- Alfven velocity for H = 10^12 Gs and ρ_e = 10^11 g/cm^3: u_A = 9 × 10^5 cm/s.

## Context

This work extends the theory of zero sound in Fermi liquids (Landau, 1957) to the electron fluid in neutron stars and introduces a new radiation mechanism distinct from magnetospheric emission models. It contradicts equations of state that incorporate Bose condensations of pions or kaons and quark matter for the Vela, PSR B0656+14, and Geminga pulsars, based on the inferred core temperatures. The results enable direct spectroscopic study of superdense matter in neutron star interiors and allow determination of the superfluid transition temperature of neutrons, which the author compares with predictions from nucleon-nucleon interaction potentials such as Nijmegen I and II (Bald98, Arnd97).
