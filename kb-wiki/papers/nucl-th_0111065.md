# Isospin effects in Nuclear Fragmentation

> We investigate properties of the symmetry term in the equation-of-state (EOS) of nuclear matter (NM) from the analysis of simulations of fragmentation events in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions. For charge asymmetric systems a qualitative new feature in the liquid-gas phase transition is predicted: the onset of chemical instabilities with a mixture of isoscalar and isovector components. This leads to a separation into a higher density (``liquid'') symmetric and a low density (``gas'') neutron-rich phase, the so-called neutron distillation effect. We analyse the simulations with respect to the time evolution of the isospin dynamics, as well as with respect to the distribution and asymmetry of the final primary fragments. Qualitatively different effects arise in central collisions, with bulk fragmentation, and peripheral collisions with neck-fragmentation. The neck fragments produced in this type of process appear systematically more neutron-rich from a dynamical nucleon migration effect which is very sensitive to the symmetry term in regions just below normal density. In general the isospin dynamics plays an important role in all the steps of the reaction, from prompt nucleon emission to the sequential decay of the primary fragments. A fully microscopic description of the reaction dynamics including stochastic elements to treat fluctuations realistically is absolutely necessary in order to extract precise information on the fragmentation and the nuclear equation of state. We have performed simulations for fragment production events in $n$-rich ($^{124}Sn$) and $n$-poor ($^{112}Sn$) symmetric colliding systems.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that in charge-asymmetric nuclear systems, the liquid-gas phase transition exhibits a mixture of isoscalar and isovector instabilities, leading to "neutron distillation" where the high-density liquid phase becomes more symmetric and the low-density gas phase becomes neutron-rich; however, the authors note that the signal for distinguishing between different symmetry term stiffnesses is "rather weak due to the low initial asymmetry and to secondary evaporation."

## Method

The authors performed stochastic transport simulations (Boltzmann-Nordheim-Vlasov type with fluctuations) for collisions of neutron-rich (124Sn) and neutron-poor (112Sn) symmetric systems at 50 AMeV, using three different density-dependent symmetry terms (asy-soft, asy-stiff, asy-superstiff) while keeping the symmetric part of the equation of state identical. They analyzed semi-central (b=2 fm) and semi-peripheral (b=6 fm) collisions, tracking the time evolution of mass, asymmetry, and fragment multiplicity, as well as freeze-out distributions of charge, asymmetry, and multiplicity.

## Key numbers

- Collision energy: 50 AMeV for both 124Sn+124Sn and 112Sn+112Sn systems
- Impact parameters studied: b=2 fm (semi-central) and b=6 fm (semi-peripheral)
- Compressibility of symmetric nuclear matter: K=201 MeV (soft EOS used for all simulations)
- Freeze-out time for semi-central collisions: around 260-280 fm/c
- Freeze-out time for semi-peripheral collisions: around 140 fm/c
- Asymmetry parameter I=0.2 used for mean field calculations (N=1.5 Z)
- Density threshold for fragment definition: Z ≥ 3 for liquid phase, IMF defined as 3 < Z < 15

## Context

This work extends theoretical predictions on chemical instabilities in asymmetric nuclear matter from Mueller and Serot (1995) and Baran et al. (2001) to realistic heavy-ion collision simulations, and is motivated by new exclusive data from MSU detectors (Xu et al., 2000; various 2001 references). The results enable experimental extraction of the symmetry term of the nuclear equation of state below saturation density, which is relevant for neutron skin thickness in nuclei (Brown, 2000; Horowitz, 2001) and for astrophysical contexts such as supernovae and neutron stars (Lattimer, 1991; Prakash, 1997).
