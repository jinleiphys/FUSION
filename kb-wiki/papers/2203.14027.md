# Improved phenomenological nuclear charge radius formulae with kernel ridge regression

> The kernel ridge regression (KRR) method with Gaussian kernel is used to improve the description of the nuclear charge radius by several phenomenological formulae. The widely used $A^{1/3}$, $N^{1/3}$ and $Z^{1/3}$ formulae, and their improved versions by considering the isospin dependence are adopted as examples. The parameters in these six formulae are refitted using the Levenberg-Marquardt method, which give better results than the previous ones. The radius for each nucleus is predicted with the KRR network, which is trained with the deviations between experimental and calculated nuclear charge radii. For each formula, the resultant root-mean-square deviations of 884 nuclei with proton number $Z \geq 8$ and neutron number $N \geq 8$ can be reduced to about 0.017~fm after considering the modification of the KRR method. The extrapolation ability of the KRR method for the neutron-rich region is examined carefully and compared with the radial basis function method. It is found that the improved nuclear charge radius formulae by KRR method can avoid the risk of overfitting and have a good extrapolation ability. The influence of the ridge penalty term on the extrapolation ability of the KRR method is also discussed. At last, the nuclear charge radii of several recently observed K and Ca isotopes have been analyzed.

# Improved phenomenological nuclear charge radius formulae with kernel ridge regression

## Key claim

The kernel ridge regression (KRR) method with Gaussian kernel can reduce the root-mean-square deviations of six phenomenological nuclear charge radius formulae to about 0.017 fm for 884 nuclei with Z ≥ 8 and N ≥ 8, but the extrapolation quality depends on the choice of the ridge penalty term λ and the specific formula used, with the Z¹/³ formula including isospin dependence showing the best extrapolation power among the six.

## Method

The authors refitted parameters in six phenomenological formulae (A¹/³, N¹/³, Z¹/³, and their isospin-dependent versions) using the Levenberg-Marquardt least-squares method on 884 experimental charge radii from Angeli (2013). They then trained a KRR network with Gaussian kernel on the residuals between experimental and calculated radii, using leave-one-out cross-validation to determine the two hyperparameters (σ, λ). The extrapolation ability was tested by removing the six most neutron-rich nuclei from each isotopic chain and comparing KRR predictions against the radial basis function (RBF) method.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square deviation of the Z¹/³ formula with isospin dependence without KRR: 0.049 fm (best among the six bare formulae)
- Root-mean-square deviation after KRR correction for five of the six formulae: about 0.017 fm (specifically 0.0166, 0.0165, 0.0168, 0.0165, 0.0166 fm for the first five formulae in Table 2)
- Root-mean-square deviation after KRR correction for the Z¹/³ formula with isospin dependence: 0.0197 fm
- Original root-mean-square deviation of the bare N¹/³ formula: 0.151 fm (largest among the six)
- Test set root-mean-square deviations for the 102 "new" nuclei (CR13-04) ranged from 0.0268 fm (Z¹/³ formula) to 0.0369 fm (N¹/³ formula)
- Optimal hyperparameter σ values ranged from 2.46 to 3.86 across the six formulae; optimal λ values were 0.001, 0.01, or 0.06

## Context

This work extends the use of kernel ridge regression, previously applied to nuclear mass predictions by Wu (2020, 2021), to the problem of nuclear charge radii. It improves upon earlier phenomenological formulae from Bohr (1969), Zeng (1957), Nerlo-Pomorska (1993), Bayram (2013), and Zhang (2002) by refitting parameters and adding machine-learning corrections. The KRR method is compared directly with the radial basis function (RBF) method used by Wang (2011) and Niu (2013, 2016) for nuclear masses and beta-decay half-lives; the authors find KRR avoids overfitting better than RBF due to the ridge penalty term. The results enable more accurate predictions of charge radii for neutron-rich nuclei, including recently measured K and Ca isotopes from Ruiz (2016), Miller (2019), and Koszorus (2021), though the authors note that a microscopic model providing better baseline descriptions is still needed.
