# Sensitivity of Neutron Star Observations to Three-nucleon Forces

> Astrophysical observations of neutron stars have been widely used to infer the properties of the nuclear matter equation of state. Beside being a source of information on average properties of dense matter, however, the data provided by electromagnetic and gravitational wave (GW) facilities are reaching the accuracy needed to constrain, for the first time, nuclear dynamics in dense matter. In this work we assess the sensitivity of current and future neutron star observations to directly infer the strength of repulsive three-nucleon forces, which are key to determine the stiffness of the equation of state. Using a Bayesian approach we focus on the constraints that can be derived on three-body interactions from binary neutron star mergers observed by second and third-generation of gravitational wave interferometers. We consider both single and multiple observations. For current detectors at design sensitivity the analysis suggests that only low mass systems, with large signal-to-noise ratios (SNR), allow to reliably constrain the three-body forces. However, our results show that a single observation with a third-generation interferometer, such as the Einstein Telescope or Cosmic Explorer, will constrain the strength of the repulsive three-body potential with exquisite accuracy, turning third-generation GW detectors into new laboratories to study the nucleon dynamics.

## Key claim

Current and near-future gravitational wave observations can constrain the strength of repulsive three-nucleon forces in dense matter, but for second-generation detectors at design sensitivity only low-mass binary neutron star systems with high signal-to-noise ratios allow reliable constraints; a single observation with a third-generation interferometer will constrain the three-body potential with exquisite accuracy.

## Method

The authors constructed a family of nuclear equations of state based on the Argonne v18 nucleon-nucleon potential with boost corrections and the Urbana IX* three-nucleon potential, varying the strength of the repulsive three-body term by a multiplicative factor α in the range [0.7, 2.0]. They performed a hierarchical Bayesian analysis using real observations (GW170817, NICER data for PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620) and simulated 30 binary neutron star merger events for both a second-generation detector network (LIGO Hanford, LIGO Livingston, Virgo at design sensitivity) and the third-generation Einstein Telescope, injecting waveforms with α=1 and α=1.3 and recovering parameters with the IMRPhenomPv2_NRTidal template.

## Key numbers

- The baseline APR model corresponds to α = 1; the allowed range of α is [0.7, 2.0], within which the departure from the empirical saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter is at most ~15% and the change in energy per particle never exceeds 3%.
- For PSR J0030+0451, the NICER collaboration reported M = 1.34^{+0.15}_{-0.16} M⊙ and R = 12.71^{+1.14}_{-1.19} km (68% credibility) from one analysis, and M = 1.44^{+0.15}_{-0.14} M⊙ and R = 13.02^{+1.24}_{-1.06} km from the other.
- For PSR J0740+6620, the mass is M = 2.08^{+0.072}_{-0.069} M⊙ (68.3% credibility), with radius measurements of R = 12.39^{+1.30}_{-0.98} km and R = 13.7^{+2.62}_{-1.50} km from two independent analyses.
- GW170817 provided an effective tidal parameter measurement of Λ̃ = 300^{+420}_{-230} within 90% highest posterior density interval.
- For second-generation detectors, observations with SNR smaller than ~25 leave α almost unconstrained; only events with SNR > 30 and chirp mass M < 1.4 M⊙ provide useful constraints.
- Changing α from 1 to 1.3 results in a 4% change in the ground state energy of 4He and a 6% change for 12C.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering study of Maselli (2020), which first proposed using multimessenger astrophysical data to constrain three-nucleon interactions, by adding the NICER radius measurement of PSR J0740+6620 and simulating future gravitational wave observations. The analysis uses the same phenomenological Hamiltonian framework (AV18 + boost correction + UIX*) employed in the APR equation of state by Akmal, Pandharipande and Ravenhall (1998), and demonstrates that third-generation detectors like the Einstein Telescope will turn gravitational wave astronomy into a new laboratory for studying nucleon dynamics at supranuclear densities, complementing terrestrial constraints from heavy-ion collisions and the PREX-II neutron skin measurement.
