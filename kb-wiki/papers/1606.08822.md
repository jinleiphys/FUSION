# Novel applications of the dispersive optical model

> A review of recent developments of the dispersive optical model (DOM) is presented. Starting from the original work of Mahaux and Sartor, several necessary steps are developed and illustrated which increase the scope of the DOM allowing its interpretation as generating an experimentally constrained functional form of the nucleon self-energy. The method could therefore be renamed as the dispersive self-energy method. The aforementioned steps include the introduction of simultaneous fits of data for chains of isotopes or isotones allowing a data-driven extrapolation for the prediction of scattering cross sections and level properties in the direction of the respective drip lines. In addition, the energy domain for data was enlarged to include results up to 200 MeV where available. An important application of this work was implemented by employing these DOM potentials to the analysis of the (\textit{d,p}) transfer reaction using the adiabatic distorted wave approximation (ADWA). We review the fully non-local DOM potential fitted to ${}^{40}$Ca where elastic-scattering data, level information, particle number, charge density and high-momentum-removal $(e,e'p)$ cross sections obtained at Jefferson Lab were included in the analysis. An important consequence of this new analysis is the finding that the spectroscopic factor for the removal of valence protons in this nucleus comes out larger by about 0.15 than the results obtained from the NIKHEF analysis of their $(e,e'p)$ data. Another important consequence of this analysis is that it can shed light on the relative importance of two-body and three-body interactions as far as their contribution to the energy of the ground state is concerned through application of the energy sum rule.

## Key claim

The dispersive optical model (DOM) can be reinterpreted as an experimentally constrained functional form of the nucleon self-energy, but only when non-local absorptive potentials are introduced to properly reproduce ground-state properties like particle number and charge density, which was not possible in the original local-potential formulation of Mahaux and Sartor.

## Method

The authors review the extension of the DOM to include simultaneous fits of elastic-scattering data, level information, particle number, charge density, and high-momentum-removal (e,e'p) cross sections for chains of isotopes or isotones, using dispersion relations to link positive-energy scattering and negative-energy bound-state information. They implement fully non-local Hartree-Fock and absorptive potentials in the solution of the Dyson equation, and apply the resulting DOM potentials to the analysis of (d,p) transfer reactions using the adiabatic distorted wave approximation (ADWA).

## Key numbers

- The spectroscopic factor for removal of valence protons in 40Ca from the DOM analysis is larger by about 0.15 than results obtained from the NIKHEF analysis of their (e,e'p) data.
- The energy domain for data was enlarged to include results up to 200 MeV where available.
- The DOM analysis of 40Ca included elastic-scattering data, level information, particle number, charge density, and high-momentum-removal (e,e'p) cross sections obtained at Jefferson Lab.

## Context

This work extends the original dispersive optical model approach of Mahaux and Sartor (1986, 1991) by introducing non-local absorptive potentials motivated by ab initio Green's function calculations (Waldecker 2011, Dussan 2011), which remediate the overestimate of particle number that occurred with local absorptive potentials. The method enables data-driven extrapolations toward the drip lines for predicting scattering cross sections and level properties, with direct relevance for experiments at FRIB, and provides a framework to study the relative contributions of two-body and three-body interactions to ground-state energies through the energy sum rule.
