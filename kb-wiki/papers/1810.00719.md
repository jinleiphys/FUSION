# General predictions for the neutron star crustal moment of inertia

> The neutron star crustal EoS and transition point properties are computed within a unified meta-modeling approach. A Bayesian approach is employed including two types of filters: bulk nuclear properties are controlled from low density effective field theory (EFT) predictions as well as the present knowledge from nuclear experiments, while the surface energy is adjusted on experimental nuclear masses. Considering these constraints, a quantitative prediction of crustal properties can be reached with controlled confidence intervals and increased precision with respect to previous calculations: {$\approx 11\%$} dispersion on the crustal width and {$\approx 27\%$} dispersion on the fractional moment of inertia. The crust moment of inertia is also evaluated as a function of the neutron star mass, and predictions for mass and radii are given for different pulsars. The possible crustal origin of Vela pulsar glitches is discussed within the present estimations of crustal entrainment, disfavoring a large entrainment phenomenon if the Vela mass is above $1.4M_\odot$. Further refinement of the present predictions requires a better estimation of the high order isovector empirical parameters, e.g. $K_{sym}$ and $Q_{sym}$, and a better control of the surface properties of extremely neutron rich nuclei.

## Key claim
The paper provides quantitative predictions for neutron star crustal properties with controlled confidence intervals, finding that the crustal origin of Vela pulsar glitches is disfavored if the Vela mass is above 1.4 solar masses and if large crustal entrainment is assumed, but this conclusion depends on the still-poorly-constrained high-order isovector empirical parameters K_sym and Q_sym and the isovector surface energy parameter p.

## Method
The authors employ a unified meta-modeling approach generating 100 million nuclear equation-of-state models with fully independent parameters, using a Bayesian framework with two types of filters: low-density constraints from many-body perturbation theory based on chiral effective field theory at N3LO, and high-density constraints including causality, stability, and compatibility with 2-solar-mass neutron stars. The surface energy is adjusted to reproduce experimental masses of spherical magic and semi-magic nuclei, and the crust composition is determined variationally within the compressible liquid drop model approximation.

## Key numbers
- Dispersion on crustal width: approximately 11%
- Dispersion on fractional moment of inertia: approximately 27%
- For a 1.4 solar mass neutron star with the combined LD+HD filter and p=3: average transition density 0.077 fm^-3 (sigma 0.010), average transition pressure 0.389 MeV/fm^3 (sigma 0.111), average crust thickness 1.03 km (sigma 0.10), average crustal moment of inertia fraction 4.50% (sigma 1.25%)
- For a 1.4 solar mass neutron star with the LD filter alone and p=3: average crust thickness 0.95 km (sigma 0.11), average crustal moment of inertia fraction 3.54% (sigma 1.33%)
- The 2-sigma surface of the complete LD+HD prediction corresponds to an 84% confidence level
- Minimal crustal moment of inertia fraction needed to explain Vela glitches ranges from 1.6% up to 15% depending on entrainment assumptions

## Context
This work extends the meta-modeling approach of Margueron (2018a, 2018b) and the Bayesian uncertainty quantification of Steiner (2013), using ab-initio chiral EFT constraints from Drischler (2016) to reduce uncertainties compared to previous calculations. The results are compared to dynamical spinodal calculations by Ducoin (2011) and to the unified EoS approach of Douchin and Haensel (2001). The paper enables improved tests of the crustal glitch theory for pulsars such as Vela, and provides predictions for mass and radius of different pulsars that can be compared with the method of Pizzochero (2017). Further refinement requires better experimental determination of K_sym and Q_sym and better microscopic modeling of surface energy at extreme isospin ratios.
