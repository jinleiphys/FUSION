# Why we need an Electron-Ion Collider

> small typos corrected, 6 pages, contribution to appear in a special issue of Annalen Der Physik titled "Particle Physics after the Higgs", Guest Editors: H. Abramowicz, A. Caldwell, B. Foster

# Why we need an Electron-Ion Collider

## Key claim
An Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) is necessary to resolve fundamental puzzles in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) regarding hadron structure, particularly the origin of proton mass and spin, the transition between constituent quark and parton model descriptions, and the dynamics of gluon saturation at high energies. The author argues that while HERA provided crucial data, its unpolarized beams and limited luminosity leave these questions unanswered, and that the US EIC's unique capabilities, polarized beams, nuclear targets, and 100-1000 times higher luminosity than HERA, will enable measurements that are otherwise impossible.

## Method
The paper reviews the scientific case for an EIC by examining four key areas: (1) extraction of polarized parton distribution functions (PDFs) through inclusive and semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (DIS) with polarized electron and hadron beams; (2) measurement of transverse momentum dependent distributions (TMDs) in semi-inclusive DIS and generalized parton distributions (GPDs) in exclusive DIS to construct three-dimensional tomographic images of hadrons; (3) nuclear DIS across a wide range of atomic number A, Bjorken x, and momentum transfer Q² to study the transition from hadronic to quark-gluon degrees of freedom in nuclei; and (4) diffractive and saturation measurements to test the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) effective theory of high-energy QCD.

## Key numbers
- The Spin Muon Collaboration found that only ~30% of the proton's spin comes from the spin carried by quarks and anti-quarks.
- RHIC measured the gluon spin contribution ΔG = ∫Δg(x)dx = 0.2 ± 0.06 ± 0.07 at Q² = 10 GeV² for x_min = 0.05.
- The maximum electron-proton energy in present US EIC designs is √s ≈ 166 GeV, approximately half of HERA's energy.
- The maximum electron-nucleus energy is √s ≈ 90 GeV per nucleon.
- The EIC will have a luminosity 10² to 10³ times that of HERA.
- The LHeC proposal at CERN has electron-proton √s ≈ 1.3–2 TeV, approximately four to six times that of HERA.
- The Bjorken sum rule is currently known to ~10% accuracy.
- Some models predict the diffractive cross-section at EIC will be about a quarter of the total cross-section for electron-nucleus collisions.

## Context
This paper extends the scientific case for an EIC as documented in the EIC whitepaper (Accardi et al., 2012) and other reports (Boer et al., 2011; Brodsky et al., 2015), building on discoveries from HERA, RHIC, JLab, and the LHC. It directly addresses puzzles raised by the Spin Muon Collaboration's finding of the proton spin crisis, the EMC effect in nuclei, and the need to test the Color Glass Condensate effective theory (Gelis et al., 2010) against alternative descriptions of high-energy QCD dynamics. The EIC would enable measurements that complement and extend the p+A programs at RHIC and LHC, particularly for diffractive processes and nuclear PDF extraction, and would provide unique access to gluon TMDs and GPDs that are extremely challenging to obtain from hadron-hadron collisions.
