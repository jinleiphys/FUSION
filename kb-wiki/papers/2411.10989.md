# Fragmentation of Nuclear Remnants in Electron-Nucleus Collisions at High Energy as a Nonextensive Process

> Utilizing a partitioning method based on equal (or unequal) probabilities -- without incorporating the alpha-cluster ($α$-cluster) model -- allows for the derivation of diverse topological configurations of nuclear fragments resulting from fragmentation. Subsequently, we predict the multiplicity distribution of nuclear fragments for specific excited nuclei, such as $^9$Be$^*$, $^{12}$C$^*$, and $^{16}$O$^*$, which can be formed as nuclear remnants in electron-nucleus ($eA$) collisions at high energy. Based on the $α$-cluster model, an $α$-cluster structure may result in deviations in the multiplicity distributions of nuclear fragments with charge $Z=2$, compared to those predicted by the partitioning methods. Furthermore, in the framework of Tsallis statistics, the nonextensive generalized temperature, entropy index, and $q$-entropy are obtained from the multiplicity distribution of nuclear fragments with given charge number. Our work shows that fragmentation of nuclear remnants in electron-nucleus collisions at high energy is a nonextensive process.

## Key claim

The paper claims that fragmentation of nuclear remnants formed in high-energy electron-nucleus collisions at the Electron-Ion Collider is a nonextensive process that can be described by Tsallis statistics, but this conclusion is conditional on the specific excited nuclei studied (^9Be^*, ^12C^*, and ^16O^*) and on the partitioning methods used (equal or unequal probability) which do not incorporate the alpha-cluster model.

## Method

The authors employed equal and unequal probability partitioning methods to enumerate all possible topological configurations of nuclear fragments from excited ^9Be^*, ^12C^*, and ^16O^* nuclei, which would be formed as nuclear remnants in electron-nucleus collisions at the EIC with center-of-mass energies between 20 and 100 GeV. They then computed multiplicity distributions for all fragments and for fragments with specific charge numbers Z, and extracted nonextensive parameters (generalized temperature, entropy index, and q-entropy) from these distributions using Tsallis statistics. The results were compared with experimental data from nuclear emulsion experiments on ^10B, ^9C, ^10C, ^11C, and ^16O fragmentation at beam energies of 1-200 GeV/nucleon.

## Key numbers

- For ^9Be fragmentation: 2He channel fraction from equal probability partitioning is 30% (1.5/5), from unequal probability partitioning is 20.8% (5/24)
- For ^12C fragmentation: 3He channel fraction from equal probability partitioning is 13.64% (1.5/11), from unequal probability partitioning is 6.25% (45/720)
- For ^16O fragmentation: 4He channel fraction from equal probability partitioning is 7.95% (1.75/22), from unequal probability partitioning is 1.93% (777/40320)
- Experimental 2He channel fraction in ^10B fragmentation at E_beam = 1 GeV/nucleon is 78%, leading to an estimated 2.6-3.8 times enhancement over partitioning methods for ^9Be
- Experimental 3He channel fractions for ^9C, ^10C, and ^11C at E_beam = 1.2 GeV/nucleon are 15.2%, 5.3%, and 17.5% respectively
- Experimental 4He channel fractions for ^16O at E_beam = 3.65 and 200 GeV/nucleon are 12.5% and 2.3% respectively
- The number of configurations (exchanges) in equal (unequal) probability partitioning: 5 (24) for ^9Be, 11 (720) for ^12C, and 22 (40320) for ^16O

## Context

This work extends previous studies by the same group (Liu et al.) that used partitioning methods to investigate excited nuclear fragmentation in nucleus-nucleus collisions at intermediate and high energies, where conditional moments and their normalized forms were introduced for examining critical behavior. The paper provides baseline predictions for multiplicity distributions of nuclear fragments from excited nuclei that could be produced at the future Electron-Ion Collider, enabling experimental tests of alpha-cluster structure in nuclei. The authors compare their predictions with experimental data from nuclear emulsion experiments by various groups (reported in references 30-34) on ^10B, ^9C, ^10C, ^11C, and ^16O fragmentation at beam energies of 1-200 GeV/nucleon, and propose a statistical criterion (twice the baseline percentage from partitioning methods) for judging the presence of alpha clustering with over 95% confidence level.
