# Symmetries Created by Random Interactions- Ultimacy of "More Is Different"

> The dominance (preponderance) of the 0+ ground state for random interactions is shown to be a consequence of certain random interactions with chaotic features. These random interactions, called chaotic random interactions, impart a symmetry property to the ground-state wave function: an isotropy under an appropriate transformation, such as zero angular momentum for rotation. Under this mechanism, the ground-state parity and isospin can also be predicted in such a manner that positive parity is favored over negative parity and the isospin T = 0 is favored over higher isospins. As chaotic random interaction is a limit with no particular dynamics at the level of two interacting particles, this realization of isotropic symmetry in the ground state can be considered as the ultimate case of many-body correlations. A possible relation to the isotropy of the early universe is mentioned.

## Key claim

The dominance of 0⁺ ground states under random interactions is explained not by any specific dynamical mechanism but by a class of "chaotic random interactions" that produce an isotropic (invariant) ground-state wave function; this isotropy arises only when the random interaction lacks particular dynamics at the two-body level, and the preponderance probability depends on the fraction of such chaotic interactions in the random sampling.

## Method

The authors perform configuration-interaction (shell-model) calculations for systems of neutrons (and protons) in model spaces such as the sd shell (1d₅/₂, 2s₁/₂, 1d₃/₂) and a space comprising 1f₅/₂, 2p₃/₂, 2p₁/₂, and 1g₉/₂ orbits, using two-body matrix elements drawn from a Gaussian-distributed two-body random matrix ensemble (TBRE). They identify "quantum mutants", two-body eigenvalues lying far below the rest, and test the effect of shifting those eigenvalues to remove specific dynamics, or alternatively replace all two-body eigenvalues with equally spaced values while preserving the random wave functions, to isolate the behavior of chaotic random interactions.

## Key numbers

- For six neutrons in the sd shell, the original TBRE yields 76% probability of a J=0 ground state (citing Johnson, Bertsch, and Dean (1998)).
- After removing the first quantum mutant from cases with J≠0 ground states, approximately half of those cases change to J=0; after removing a second quantum mutant, a total of 91% of all cases show J=0 ground states.
- For the same six-neutron sd-shell system, replacing two-body eigenvalues with equally spaced values increases the J=0 probability by approximately 20% relative to the original TBRE.
- For ten neutrons in the 1f₅/₂, 2p₃/₂, 2p₁/₂, 1g₉/₂ space, the ground state is predominantly 0⁺, while the Hilbert-space dimension fraction peaks around J≈8 with no practical parity difference.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing puzzle of J=0 ground-state preponderance first reported by Johnson, Bertsch, and Dean (1998) for random interactions, which had resisted a general mechanistic explanation beyond special algebraic cases (e.g., Fiete and Iachello (2002), Zhao and collaborators (2002)). The authors propose that chaotic random interactions represent the ultimate limit of "More Is Different" (Anderson (1972)), where no two-body dynamics survives yet many-body correlations produce symmetry. This mechanism enables predictions for parity and isospin preponderance, and the authors suggest it may relate to the isotropy of the early universe as seen in the Cosmic Microwave Background (Smoot (1992)).
