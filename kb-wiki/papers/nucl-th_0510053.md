# The fully self-consistent quasiparticle random phase approximation and its application to the isobaric analog resonances

> A microscopic model aimed at the description of charge-exchange nuclear excitations along isotopic chains which include open-shell systems, is developed. It consists of quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) made on top of Hartree-Fock-Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (HF-BCS). The calculations are performed by using the Skyrme interaction in the particle-hole channel and a zero-range, density-dependent pairing force in the particle-particle channel. At variance with the (many) versions of QRPA which are available in literature, in our work special emphasis is put on the full self-consistency. Its importance, as well as the role played by the charge-breaking terms of the nuclear Hamiltonian, like the Coulomb interaction, the charge symmetry and charge independence breaking (CSB-CIB) forces and the electromagnetic spin-orbit, are elucidated by means of numerical calculations of the isobaric analog resonances (IAR). The theoretical energies of these states along the chain of the Sn isotopes agree well with the experimental data in the stable isotopes. Predictions for unstable systems are presented.

# The fully self-consistent quasiparticle random phase approximation and its application to the isobaric analog resonances

## Key claim
A fully self-consistent charge-exchange quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) built on Hartree-Fock-Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (HF-BCS) with Skyrme interactions can reproduce isobaric analog resonance (IAR) energies in stable Sn isotopes to within approximately 200 keV, provided that the T=1 component of the residual particle-particle force is fixed by exploiting isospin invariance and that charge-breaking terms (Coulomb, CSB-CIB forces, electromagnetic spin-orbit) are included.

## Method
The authors solve HF-BCS equations in coordinate space using the Skyrme SLy4 interaction for the particle-hole channel and a zero-range, density-dependent pairing force for the particle-particle channel. The QRPA equations are then solved in a discretized continuum using spherical box boundary conditions, with the residual particle-hole and particle-particle matrix elements derived consistently from the same energy functional used for the ground state. Charge-symmetry breaking (CSB) and charge-independence breaking (CIB) forces are included in the HF-BCS iteration, while the electromagnetic spin-orbit contribution is added via first-order perturbation theory.

## Key numbers
- IAR energies in stable Sn isotopes (112–124Sn) are reproduced with a typical difference of approximately 200 keV between theory and experiment, except at the extremes 112Sn and 124Sn where the difference is larger.
- The residual proton-neutron pairing force induces a downward shift of the IAR energy of about 500 keV in 114Sn.
- The IAR peak exhausts between 95% and 98% of the N−Z sum rule across the isotopic chain.
- The pairing strength V0 is set to 680 MeV fm³, with variations in the range 650–710 MeV fm³ producing sizeable (≈20%) variations in the pairing gap Δ but not seriously affecting the IAR energy.
- The two-body spin-orbit contributes approximately 100 keV to the IAR energy in 120Sn.
- The IAR energy in 120Sn varies by less than 100 keV when using Skyrme forces SLy4, SIII, or SGII.

## Context
This work extends the few existing fully self-consistent QRPA calculations for charge-exchange excitations, following the initial attempt by Engel et al. (1999) for Gamow-Teller beta-decay half-lives and the analysis by Bender et al. (2002) of Skyrme parametrizations for GT resonances. The authors contrast their non-relativistic approach with relativistic mean-field (RMF) calculations by Paar et al. (2003-2004) for IAR and GTR, noting that the isovector channel and symmetry energy differ between the two frameworks. The results enable predictions for IAR energies in unstable Sn isotopes (104–132Sn) and provide a benchmark for future experiments, while the method can be extended to other multipolarities and spin-flip sectors to constrain the isovector part of the effective nucleon-nucleon interaction.
