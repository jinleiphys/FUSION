# Bulk effects in the coherent inelastic scattering of ultracold neutrons

> With the use of theory developed earlier, bulk effects in ultracold neutron coherent inelastic scattering are considered both for solid and liquid target samples related to energy and momentum exchange with phonon and diffusion-like modes. For the neutron in a material trap, differential and integral probabilities for the energy transfer per bounce are presented in a simple analytic form which exhibits the parameter dependence. As an example, the theoretical values for the ultracold neutron loss rate from a storage bottle with Fomblin coated walls and stainless steel walls are evaluated. Possible contribution from incoherent inelastic scattering on hydrogen contamination is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper presents a theory of coherent inelastic scattering of ultracold neutrons (UCN) that accounts for bulk effects in both solid and liquid targets, providing analytic expressions for energy-transfer probabilities per bounce; the authors emphasize that their approach avoids the common but invalid extrapolation of Van Hove's Born-approximation formula to the ultracold regime, and they restrict their analysis to hydrogen-free materials where coherent scattering dominates.

## Method

The authors apply their previously developed general theory of neutron scattering (Barabanov & Belyaev, 2000), which solves the N+1-body Schroedinger equation for a neutron plus target nuclei system using a short-range, deep neutron-nucleus potential. They expand the neutron amplitudes in the small parameter Q·u (momentum transfer times nuclear displacement), compute the inelastic cross section from the first-order term, and evaluate the probability per bounce as the ratio of inelastic to elastic cross sections for a macroscopic plane sample. The correlation functions for the target material are modeled separately for phonon modes and slow diffusion-like modes (thermo-diffusion and low-frequency transverse modes in liquids), and the resulting expressions are integrated over the geometry of a uniform thick layer with optical potential U.

## Key numbers

* For Fomblin oil coated walls and stainless steel walls, the theoretical values for the ultracold neutron loss rate from a storage bottle are evaluated (no explicit numerical loss rates given in the truncated text).
* The intrusion length of the neutron wave function into the target is approximately 10 nm, below the barrier energy U.
* For phonon processes in solids, the damping factor for longitudinal modes is given by Γ²_l = γ|ω|³/c⁴_l with γ = ζ/ρ + αD_S, and for transverse modes Γ²_t = ν|ω|³/c⁴_t with ν = η/ρ.
* For the thermo-diffusion mode, the damping factor is Γ²_d = |ω|/D_S.
* For liquids, the transverse correlation function uses a relaxation time τ = η/G = ν/c²_t, and the damping factor is Γ²_t = |ω|/(c²_tτ) = |ω|/ν.
* The probability per bounce for small heating and small cooling observed in experiments is in the range 10⁻⁸–10⁻⁶ (cited from prior work, not computed in this paper).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier general theory of slow neutron scattering (Barabanov & Belyaev, 2000) to treat bulk inelastic scattering of UCN in material traps, providing the first calculations of non-lattice (thermo-diffusion and transverse) contributions to small energy transfers in solids, and the first estimates of bulk-mode contributions for liquids and amorphous samples. It contrasts with the common approach that extrapolates the Van Hove formula (Van Hove, 1954) from cold to ultracold energies using a 1/v law and an imaginary optical potential (Ignatovich, 1990; Golub, 1991), which the authors argue is invalid because rescattering dominates for UCN. The results enable quantitative comparison with storage experiments on Fomblin oil and stainless steel, and may support spectroscopic studies of low-frequency modes in surface layers and thin films (Kornilov, 2004).
