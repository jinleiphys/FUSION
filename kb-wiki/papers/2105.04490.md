# Halo effective field theory analysis of one-neutron knockout reactions of $^{11}\rm Be$ and $^{15}\rm C$

> Background: One-nucleon knockout reactions provide insightful information on the single-particle structure of nuclei. When applied to one-neutron halo nuclei, they are purely peripheral, suggesting that they could be properly modeled by describing the projectile within a Halo Effective Field Theory (Halo-EFT). Purpose: We reanalyze the one-neutron knockout measurements of $^{11}$Be and $^{15}$C-both one-neutron halo nuclei-on beryllium at about 60MeV/nucleon. We consider Halo-EFT descriptions of these nuclei which already provide excellent agreement with breakup and transfer data. Method: We include a Halo-EFT description of the projectile within an eikonal-based model of the reaction and compare its outcome to existing data. Results: Excellent agreement with experiment is found for both nuclei. The asymptotic normalization coefficients inferred from this comparison confirm predictions from \emph{ab initio} nuclear-structure calculations and values deduced from transfer data. Conclusions: Halo-EFT can be reliably used to analyze one-neutron knockout reactions measured for halo nuclei and test predictions from state-of-the-art nuclear structure models on these experimental data.

## Key claim

Halo-EFT descriptions of one-neutron halo nuclei, when combined with eikonal-based reaction models, reproduce the measured knockout cross sections and parallel-momentum distributions for both $^{11}$Be and $^{15}$C without any parameter fitting, but the agreement depends on the choice of optical potentials and the stripping contribution misses some dynamical effects in the low-momentum tail.

## Method

The projectile is modeled as a two-body system (core plus halo neutron) using Halo-EFT at next-to-leading order, with effective interactions in the $s_{1/2}$ and $p_{1/2}$ (for $^{11}$Be) or $s_{1/2}$ and $d_{5/2}$ (for $^{15}$C) partial waves. The knockout cross section is computed within an eikonal-based framework: diffractive breakup is treated with the dynamical eikonal approximation (DEA), while stripping is computed at the usual eikonal approximation (which includes the adiabatic approximation). Four combinations of optical potentials for the core-target and neutron-target interactions are used to estimate the reaction-model uncertainty.

## Key numbers

* For $^{11}$Be knockout on $^9$Be at 60 MeV/nucleon, the experimental integrated knockout cross section is 203 $\pm$ 31 mb; theoretical calculations with four optical-potential combinations range from 185 to 207 mb.
* For $^{15}$C knockout on $^9$Be at 54 MeV/nucleon, the experimental integrated knockout cross section is 109 $\pm$ 13 mb; theoretical calculations range from 95 to 124 mb.
* The inferred asymptotic normalization coefficient squared for $^{11}$Be is ANC$^2 = 0.62 \pm 0.06 \pm 0.09$ fm$^{-1}$, where the first uncertainty is from optical-potential choice and the second from experimental error.
* The inferred ANC$^2$ for $^{15}$C is $1.57 \pm 0.30 \pm 0.18$ fm$^{-1}$.
* The uncertainty from the Halo-EFT description of the projectile is less than 1.5% for integrated knockout cross sections.
* The optical-potential choice leads to about 11% difference in the $^{11}$Be cross section and up to 26% in the $^{15}$C calculations.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis (Hebborn and Capel, 2019) which showed that one-neutron knockout of halo nuclei is peripheral and sensitive only to the asymptotic normalization coefficient, not the spectroscopic factor. It uses Halo-EFT descriptions of $^{11}$Be (Calci et al., 2016; Capel et al., 2018) and $^{15}$C (Moschini et al., 2019) that already successfully reproduced breakup and transfer data. The results complete a series of studies demonstrating that a single Halo-EFT description can reproduce breakup, transfer, radiative-capture, and now knockout measurements, enabling reliable tests of ab initio nuclear-structure predictions. The work also suggests that the quenching factor $R_S \sim 1$ observed for halo nuclei in knockout analyses (Gade, 2008; Tostevin and Gade, 2014, 2021) arises from using realistic ANCs rather than accurate spectroscopic factors.
