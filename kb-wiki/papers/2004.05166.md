# Correlation between global polarization, angular momentum and flow in heavy-ion collisions

> Possible correlations of the global polarization of $Λ$ hyperons with the angular momentum and transverse flow in the central region of colliding nuclei are studied based on refined estimate of the global polarization. Simulations of Au+Au collisions at collision energies $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=$ 6-40 GeV are performed within the model of the three-fluid dynamics. Within the crossover and first-order-phase-transition scenarios this refined estimate quite satisfactorily reproduces the experimental STAR data. Hadronic scenario fails at high collision energies, $\sqrt{s_{NN}}>$ 10 GeV, and even predicts opposite sign of the global polarization. It is found that the global polarization correlates with neither the angular momentum accumulated in the central region nor with directed and elliptic flow. At the same time we observed correlation between the angular momentum and directed flow in both their time and collision-energy dependence. These results suggest that, although initially the angular momentum is the driving force for the vortex generation, later the angular momentum and vortex motion become decorrelated in the midrapidity region. Then the midrapidity angular momentum is determined by the pattern of the directed flow and even becomes negative when the antiflow occurs. At the freeze-out stage, the dominant part of the participant angular momentum is accumulated in the fragmentation regions.

## Key claim

The global polarization of Λ hyperons in the midrapidity region does not correlate with the angular momentum accumulated in that same central region, nor with directed or elliptic flow, despite the fact that initially the angular momentum drives vortex generation; the midrapidity angular momentum instead correlates with the directed flow slope and can even become negative at high collision energies.

## Method

Simulations of Au+Au collisions at fixed impact parameter b = 8 fm and collision energies √s_NN = 6–40 GeV were performed using the three-fluid dynamics (3FD) model, which treats projectile, target, and fireball fluids coupled by friction terms. The global Λ polarization was computed from the thermal zx vorticity averaged over a central rapidity slab (|y_h| < 0.5 or |y_h| < 0.7) weighted by energy density, using a refined freeze-out procedure where the freeze-out energy density depends on collision energy, equation of state, and impact parameter. Three equations of state were tested: a purely hadronic EoS, a crossover deconfinement transition EoS, and a first-order phase transition (1PT) EoS.

## Key numbers

- The global polarization from the crossover and 1PT EoS reproduces STAR data for Au+Au at √s_NN = 6–40 GeV; the hadronic EoS fails at √s_NN > 10 GeV and predicts negative polarization.
- At √s_NN = 19.6 GeV, the angular momentum in the central slab (|y_h| < 0.5) is |J| ≈ 20 ħ, while the total participant angular momentum is J_participants ≈ 2 × 10^4 ħ, three orders of magnitude larger.
- The ratio of midrapidity angular momentum to total participant angular momentum at freeze-out does not exceed a few percent across the entire energy range studied (√s_NN = 6–40 GeV).
- The freeze-out energy density ε_frz varies with collision energy and EoS, with values shown in Fig. 1 for b = 2 and 8 fm.
- The rapidity width Δy_h of the central slab at freeze-out is approximately 1.4 for the |y_h| < 0.7 condition and approximately 1.0 for the |y_h| < 0.5 condition, with some dependence on √s_NN.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous 3FD studies of global polarization (Ivanov 2019) by using a refined polarization estimate with an expanding central slab and energy-density-dependent freeze-out, and by directly comparing polarization with angular momentum and flow in the same spatial region. It contradicts the naive expectation from the Barnett effect that polarization should correlate with angular momentum, and it confirms earlier findings by Karpenko (2016) that global polarization does not correlate with total participant angular momentum, but goes further by showing the same lack of correlation holds for the angular momentum in the midrapidity region itself. The observed correlation between midrapidity angular momentum and directed flow slope, including simultaneous sign changes, enables future studies of how the fireball tilting (antiflow) mechanism affects angular momentum transport, and suggests that polarization measurements in fragmentation regions may be needed to connect to the total angular momentum.
