# Uncertainties in $ν$p-process nucleosynthesis from Monte Carlo variation of reaction rates

> It has been suggested that a $ν$p process can occur when hot, dense, and proton-rich matter is expanding within a strong flux of anti-neutrinos. In such an environment, proton-rich nuclides can be produced in sequences of proton captures and (n,p) reactions, where the free neutrons are created in situ by $\overlineν_\mathrm{e}+\mathrm{p} \rightarrow \mathrm{n}+\mathrm{e}^+$ reactions. The detailed hydrodynamic evolution determines where the nucleosynthesis path turns off from N = Z line and how far up the nuclear chart it runs. In this work, the uncertainties on the final isotopic abundances stemming from uncertainties in the nuclear reaction rates were investigated in a large-scale Monte Carlo approach, simultaneously varying ten thousand reactions. A large range of model conditions was investigated because a definitive astrophysical site for the $ν$p process has not yet been identified. The present parameter study provides, for each model, identification of the key nuclear reactions dominating the uncertainty for a given nuclide abundance. As all rates appearing in the $ν$p process involve unstable nuclei, and thus only theoretical rates are available, the final abundance uncertainties are larger than those for nucleosynthesis processes closer to stability. Nevertheless, most uncertainties remain below a factor of three in trajectories with robust nucleosynthesis. More extreme conditions allow production of heavier nuclides but show larger uncertainties because of the accumulation of the uncertainties in many rates and because the termination of nucleosynthesis is not at equilibrium conditions. It is also found that the solar ratio of the abundances of ${}^{92}$Mo and ${}^{94}$Mo could be reproduced within uncertainties.

## Key claim
The paper finds that, for trajectories with robust nucleosynthesis, most abundance uncertainties remain below a factor of three, but this conclusion is conditional on fixing the rates of the triple-alpha reaction and the Ni56(n,p)Co56 reaction, which dominate all other uncertainties and are therefore excluded from the Monte Carlo variation.

## Method
The authors used a Monte Carlo approach to simultaneously vary over ten thousand nuclear reaction rates within their assigned uncertainty ranges, post-processing 23 parameterised hydrodynamic trajectories covering electron fractions from 0.55 to 0.725 and entropies from 11.4 to 184 kB per baryon. Each trajectory was run 10,000 times with a reaction network containing 2,216 nuclides, and the Pearson correlation coefficient (threshold |r| >= 0.65) was used to identify key reactions dominating the uncertainty of each final isotopic abundance.

## Key numbers
- Most abundance uncertainties remain below a factor of three in trajectories with robust nucleosynthesis.
- The explored parameter space covers electron fractions from 0.55 to 0.725 and entropies from 11.4 to 184 kB per baryon.
- The anti-electron neutrino luminosity used was L = 2.06 x 10^51 erg with mean energy 15.2 MeV at the start of nucleosynthesis calculations.
- The triple-alpha rate of Fynbo (2005) was assigned an uncertainty factor of 5 upward and 0.5 downward.
- The reaction network included 2,216 nuclides.
- Each trajectory was run 10,000 times in the Monte Carlo procedure.
- The threshold for identifying key rates was a Pearson correlation coefficient |r_cor| >= 0.65.
- Only nuclides produced with mass fractions larger than 2 x 10^-5 were included in the uncertainty analysis.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous Monte Carlo studies of nucleosynthesis uncertainties applied to p nuclides in massive stars (Rauscher 2016), thermonuclear supernovae (Nishimura 2018), the weak s process in massive stars (Nishimura 2017), and the main s process in AGB stars (Cristallo 2018). The study uses the same methodology and input as those earlier works, enabling direct comparison of abundance uncertainties across different nucleosynthesis processes. The results enable identification of which nuclear reaction rate measurements would most reduce uncertainties in nu p-process abundance predictions, and show that the solar ratio of Mo92 to Mo94 can be reproduced within the computed uncertainties.
