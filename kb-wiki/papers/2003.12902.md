# A Novel Feature of Valence Quark Distributions in Hadrons

> Examining the evolution of the maximum of valence quark distribution weighted by Bjorken x, $h(x,t)\equiv xq_V(x,t)$, we observe that $h(x,t)$ at the peak should become a one parameter function; $h(x_p,t)=Φ(x_p(t))$, where $x_p$ is the position of the peak and $t= \log{Q^2}$. This observation is used to derive a new model independent relation which connects the partial derivative of the valence parton distribution functions (PDFs) in $x_p$ to the QCD evolution equation through the $x_p$-derivative of the logarithm of the function $Φ(x_p(t))$. A numerical analysis of this relation using empirical PDFs results in a observation of the exponential form of the $Φ(x_p(t)) = h(x_p,t) = Ce^{D x_p(t)}$ for leading to next-to-next leading order approximations of PDFs for the all $Q^2$ range covering four orders in magnitude. The exponent, $D$, of the observed "height-position" correlation function converges with the increase of the order of approximation. This result holds for all PDF sets considered. A similar relation is observed also for pion valence quark distribution, indicating that the obtained relation may be universal for any non-singlet partonic distribution. The observed "height - position" correlation is used also to indicate that no finite number exchanges can describe the analytic behavior of the valence quark distribution at the position of the peak at fixed $Q^2$.

# A Novel Feature of Valence Quark Distributions in Hadrons

## Key claim

The paper claims that the height of the peak of the valence quark structure function $h(x,t) \equiv x q_V(x,t)$ and its position $x_p$ are correlated by an exponential function $h(x_p,t) = C e^{D x_p(t)}$ that holds across four orders of magnitude in $Q^2$, but this result is purely empirical and derived from existing PDF parameterizations rather than from first-principles QCD. The authors explicitly state that the exponential form is "purely empirical" and that understanding its origin requires further numerical study of the evolution equations.

## Method

The authors examined the evolution of the peak of $x q_V(x,t)$ for valence quarks in the nucleon using existing PDF parameterizations (CT14, NNPDF30, ABM12, CJ15, JR14) at LO, NLO, and NNLO approximations accessed via the LHAPDF library. They derived a model-independent relation connecting the partial derivative of valence PDFs in $x_p$ to the QCD evolution equation, then numerically evaluated the left-hand side of this relation to extract the correlation function $\Phi(x_p)$. The QCD evolution equation at leading order was used to calculate $\partial q_V(x_p,t)/\partial t$, while $dx_p/dt$ and $\partial \log q_V(x_p,t)/\partial x_p$ were computed numerically from the PDF grids. The analysis was extended to pion valence quark distributions using the parameterization of Barry et al. (2018).

## Key numbers

- The exponential correlation $h(x_p,t) = C e^{D x_p}$ was observed for $Q^2$ values covering four orders of magnitude, from $1.8$ GeV$^2$ to $3.3 \times 10^4$ GeV$^2$.
- For CT14 at LO: $d$-valence quark parameters $C = 0.16$, $D = 2.8$; $u$-valence quark parameters $C = 0.36$, $D = 2.02$.
- For CT14 at NNLO: $d$-valence quark parameters $C = 0.193(6)$, $D = 3.1(2)$; $u$-valence quark parameters $C = 0.37(1)$, $D = 2.7(1)$.
- For CT14 at NLO: $d$-valence quark parameters $C = 0.19(1)$, $D = 3.1(1)$; $u$-valence quark parameters $C = 0.37(1)$, $D = 2.71(9)$.
- For the pion valence quark distribution using the parameterization of Barry et al. (2018): $C = 0.202(3)$, $D = 1.50(2)$.

## Context

This work extends the study of universal properties of fermionic systems with conserved number of constituents, analogous to the approach used for ultra-cold two-component Fermi atomic systems and atomic nuclei by Hen et al. (2014). The observed exponential correlation suggests that valence quark PDFs at the peak position are governed by mean-field type interactions rather than finite-number exchanges, contrasting with the fixed-exponent predictions of Lepage and Brodsky (1980) and Gunion and Soper (1983) for hard gluon exchange. The result enables new constraints on lattice QCD calculations of valence PDFs for the proton and other hadrons, and may extend to fragmentation functions due to similar evolution equations.
