# Properties of Lithium-11 and Carbon-22 at leading order in halo effective field theory

> We study the $^{11}\mathrm{Li}$ and $^{22}\mathrm{C}$ nuclei at leading order (LO) in halo effective field theory (Halo EFT). Using the value of the $^{22}\mathrm{C}$ rms matter radius deduced in Ref. [1] as an input in a LO calculation, we simultaneously constrained the values of the two-neutron (2$n$) separation energy of $^{22}\mathrm{C}$ and the virtual-state energy of the $^{20}\mathrm{C}-$neutron system (hereafter denoted $^{21}$C). The 1$-σ$ uncertainty of the input rms matter radius datum, along with the theory error estimated from the anticipated size of the higher-order terms in the Halo EFT expansion, gave an upper bound of about 100 keV for the 2$n$ separation energy. We also study the electric dipole excitation of 2$n$ halo nuclei to a continuum state of two neutrons and the core at LO in Halo EFT. We first compare our results with the $^{11}\mathrm{Li}$ data from a Coulomb dissociation experiment and obtain good agreement within the theoretical uncertainty of a LO calculation. We then obtain the low-energy spectrum of $B(E1)$ of this transition at several different values of the 2$n$ separation energy of $^{22}\mathrm{C}$ and the virtual-state energy of $^{21}\mathrm{C}$. Our predictions can be compared to the outcome of an ongoing experiment on the Coulomb dissociation of $^{22}\mathrm{C}$ to obtain tighter constraints on the two- and three-body energies in the $^{22}\mathrm{C}$ system.

## Key claim

Using the measured rms matter radius of Carbon-22 as input at leading order in halo effective field theory, the authors obtain an upper bound of about 100 keV for the two-neutron separation energy of Carbon-22, but this bound carries both experimental and theoretical uncertainties that prevent a precise determination of the separation energy or the virtual-state energy of Carbon-21 individually.

## Method

The authors constructed leading-order halo effective field theory (Halo EFT) wavefunctions for two-neutron halo nuclei using zero-range two-body interactions characterized by neutron-neutron and neutron-core scattering lengths, with a three-body force fixed by the two-neutron separation energy. They calculated the rms matter radius of Carbon-22 as a function of the two-neutron separation energy and the neutron-core virtual-state energy, then used the experimentally extracted radius to constrain these parameters. They also computed the electric dipole response (B(E1) spectrum) for breakup into the core plus two neutrons, including all S-wave rescatterings in both initial and final states.

## Key numbers

- The rms matter radius of Carbon-22 deduced by Tanaka et al. (2010) is 5.4 ± 0.9 fm, used as input to constrain the two-neutron separation energy and the Carbon-21 virtual-state energy.
- The leading-order calculation gives an upper bound of about 100 keV for the two-neutron separation energy of Carbon-22, which is stricter than the 120 keV bound from Yamashita et al. (2011) and the 220 keV bound from Fortune (2012).
- For the Lithium-11 B(E1) calculation, the two-neutron separation energy was taken as 369.15 ± 0.65 keV and the neutron-core virtual-state energy as 26 keV.
- The Carbon-22 dipole response spectrum was computed for three parameter combinations: (E_B = 50 keV, E_nc = 10 keV), (E_B = 50 keV, E_nc = 100 keV), and (E_B = 70 keV, E_nc = 10 keV).

## Context

This work extends the Halo EFT framework previously applied to two-neutron halo nuclei by Bedaque et al. (1998) and Kaplan et al. (1998), and applies it specifically to Carbon-22 and Lithium-11. The matter radius constraint on Carbon-22 improves upon earlier three-body model analyses by Yamashita et al. (2011) and potential model work by Fortune (2012). The predicted B(E1) spectra for Carbon-22 can be compared to an ongoing Coulomb dissociation experiment by Nakamura et al. to further constrain the two-neutron separation energy and the Carbon-21 virtual-state energy.
