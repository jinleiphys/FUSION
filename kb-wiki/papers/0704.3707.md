# Softening of the equation of state of matter at large densities and temperatures: chiral symmetry restoration vs. quark deconfinement

> We discuss two models for describing the behavior of matter at large densities and intermediate temperatures. In both models a softening of the equation of state takes place due to the appearance of new degrees of freedom. The first is a hadronic model in which the softening is due to chiral symmetry restoration. In the second model the softening is associated with the formation of clusters of quarks in the mixed phase. We show that both models allow a significant softening but, in the first case the bulk modulus is mainly dependent on the density, while in the mixed phase model it also strongly depends on the temperature. We also show that the bulk modulus is not vanishing in the mixed phase due to the presence of two conserved charges, the baryon and the isospin one. Only in a small region of densities and temperatures the incompressibility becomes extremely small. Finally we compare our results with recent analysis of heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies.

# Softening of the equation of state of matter at large densities and temperatures: chiral symmetry restoration vs. quark deconfinement

## Key claim

Both a hadronic model with chiral symmetry restoration and a mixed-phase model with quark cluster formation produce a significant softening of the equation of state, but the bulk modulus in the chiral model depends mainly on density while in the mixed-phase model it also depends strongly on temperature; the incompressibility does not vanish in the mixed phase because two conserved charges (baryon and isospin) are present, becoming extremely small only in a limited region of densities and temperatures.

## Method

The authors compare two models: a chiral-dilaton model (Minnesota group formalism) that includes chiral fields, a dilaton field for scale symmetry breaking, and quantum fluctuations beyond mean field approximation; and a mixed hadron-quark phase model using the NLρδ hadronic model with an MIT bag model for quarks, where the bag constant is made density-dependent to mimic diquark condensate effects. The mixed phase is described using the Gibbs formalism with two conserved charges (baryon and isospin), and the surface tension at the hadron-quark interface is set to 10 MeV/fm². The bulk modulus and an effective incompressibility parameter are computed as functions of density and temperature and compared with experimental constraints from heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies.

## Key numbers

- In the chiral-dilaton model, the incompressibility at saturation density is K⁻¹ = 322 MeV.
- For the mixed-phase model at T=50 MeV and ρ=3 ρ₀, the effective incompressibility parameter is approximately 220 MeV; at T=90 MeV and ρ=3.4 ρ₀ it is approximately 170 MeV; at T=100 MeV and ρ=3.6 ρ₀ it is approximately 120 MeV.
- The mixed-phase model uses a density-dependent bag constant with B₀¹/⁴ = 215 MeV, Δ̄ = 100 MeV, μ₀ = 300 MeV, a = 300 MeV, and αₛ = 0.35.
- The critical temperature at μ=0 in the mixed-phase model is approximately 170 MeV, as suggested by lattice QCD.
- The mixed phase starts forming at a density slightly exceeding 3 ρ₀ for a temperature of approximately 90 MeV.
- The surface tension used in the mixed-phase model is σ = 10 MeV/fm².
- The chiral-dilaton model shows incompressibility almost temperature independent up to T approximately 150 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of equation-of-state softening mechanisms studied in the chiral-dilaton model by Heide (1993), Carter (1995, 1997) and in the mixed-phase Gibbs formalism by Glendenning (1992) and Muller (1995, 1997). It directly compares with the experimental constraints from heavy ion collisions analyzed by Danielewicz (2002) and the 3-fluid dynamics simulations by Russkikh (2006) that suggest progressive softening at energies from 2A GeV to 8A GeV. The results enable future tests at facilities like FAIR at GSI, particularly regarding whether the oscillatory behavior of directed flow observed at 40A GeV gradually appears at energies exceeding approximately 10A GeV.
