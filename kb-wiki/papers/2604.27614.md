# Hyperfine-resolved laser excitation and detection of nuclear isomer in trapped $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$ ions

> We present a comprehensive theoretical investigation of hyperfine-resolved excitation and detection of the low-energy isomeric state of $^{229}$Th in trapped $^{229}\mathrm{Th}^{3+}$ ions. Using a quantum master equation approach, we analyze the dependence of the isomeric population on laser linewidth, detuning, and irradiation time, showing that their proper matching is essential for efficient excitation. We further propose two nuclear-state detection schemes based on three hyperfine-resolved electronic fluorescence channels at 690, 984, and 1088 nm. Our analysis shows that the 690-nm and 984-nm scheme yields detectable photon rates on the order of $10^4~\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ per ion for each wavelength, whereas the 1088-nm scheme achieves a higher rate on the order of $10^5~\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ per ion. By quantifying the trade-off between irradiation time and scan-step size, we show that the nuclear transition can be located within one month for a 100-MHz uncertainty using currently available vacuum-ultraviolet laser technology. These results provide practical guidance for trapped-ion $^{229}\mathrm{Th}$ spectroscopy and the development of nuclear clocks.

## Key claim
The paper shows that with proper matching of laser linewidth, detuning, and irradiation time, the nuclear isomer in trapped $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$ ions can be excited to a quasi-steady-state population of 1/2, and that the nuclear transition can be located within one month for a 100-MHz uncertainty using currently available vacuum-ultraviolet laser technology, provided the laser linewidth is larger than the nuclear Rabi frequency.

## Method
The authors developed a quantum master equation approach to model the hyperfine-resolved laser excitation dynamics of the $^{229}$Th nuclear isomer in trapped $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$ ions, using a four-level model for the 148-nm VUV laser excitation and three-level or two-level models for electronic fluorescence detection schemes. They calculated hyperfine constants for both nuclear ground and isomeric states using experimental data and scaling relations, then solved the master equation numerically and analytically under the adiabatic approximation to obtain time-dependent isomeric populations and fluorescence rates.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear Rabi frequency: $\Omega_n/2\pi = -3.4$ Hz for a 148-nm laser intensity of $0.5$ W/cm$^2$ (100 nW power focused onto $20$ $\mu$m$^2$)
- Nuclear decay rates: $\gamma_{n,21}/2\pi = 3.0\times10^{-5}$ Hz, $\gamma_{n,23}/2\pi = 1.4\times10^{-5}$ Hz, $\gamma_{n,24}/2\pi = 2.0\times10^{-5}$ Hz
- Electronic decay rates: $\gamma_{e,ba}/2\pi = 2.8\times10^4$ Hz for $6d_{5/2}\rightarrow5f_{5/2}$ (lifetime 5.8 $\mu$s), $\gamma_{e,bc}/2\pi = 2.1\times10^5$ Hz for $6d_{5/2}\rightarrow5f_{7/2}$ (lifetime 0.8 $\mu$s)
- Detectable photon rates: $10^4$ s$^{-1}$ per ion for the 690-nm and 984-nm scheme; $10^5$ s$^{-1}$ per ion for the 1088-nm scheme
- Nuclear transition search time: within one month for a 100-MHz uncertainty using currently available VUV laser technology
- Isomeric excitation energy: approximately 8 eV (148 nm wavelength)
- Vacuum radiative lifetime of the isomer: about 2500 s
- Hyperfine splittings range: several hundred MHz to several GHz for the electronic levels considered

## Context
This work extends theoretical studies of $^{229}$Th nuclear clock candidates by Peik (2003), Campbell (2012), and others, providing a comprehensive quantum-optical treatment that accounts for hyperfine structure in trapped $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$ ions. It builds on the first resonant excitation of the $^{229}$Th isomer in solid-state hosts by Tiedau (2024) and Elwell (2024), and on kilohertz-level frequency measurements by Zhang (2024), while addressing the specific challenges of trapped-ion platforms where direct laser excitation has not yet been achieved. The results enable experimental searches for the nuclear transition in ion traps using the recently developed continuous-wave VUV laser sources based on four-wave mixing in cadmium vapor by Xiao (2026) and second-harmonic generation in SrB$_4$O$_7$ crystal by Lal (2025).
