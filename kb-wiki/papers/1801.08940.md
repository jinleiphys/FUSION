# Precision mass measurements on neutron-rich rare-earth isotopes at JYFLTRAP - reduced neutron pairing and implications for the $r$-process calculations

> The rare-earth peak in the $r$-process abundance pattern depends sensitively on both the astrophysical conditions and subtle changes in nuclear structure in the region. This work takes an important step elucidating the nuclear structure and reducing the uncertainties in $r$-process calculations via precise atomic mass measurements at the JYFLTRAP double Penning trap. $^{158}$Nd, $^{160}$Pm, $^{162}$Sm, and $^{164-166}$Gd have been measured for the first time and the precisions for $^{156}$Nd, $^{158}$Pm, $^{162,163}$Eu, $^{163}$Gd, and $^{164}$Tb have been improved considerably. Nuclear structure has been probed via two-neutron separation energies $S_{2n}$ and neutron pairing energy metrics $D_n$. The data do not support the existence of a subshell closure at $N=100$. Neutron pairing has been found to be weaker than predicted by theoretical mass models. The impact on the calculated $r$-process abundances has been studied. Substantial changes resulting in a smoother abundance distribution and a better agreement with the solar $r$-process abundances are observed.

## Key claim

Precision mass measurements of neutron-rich rare-earth isotopes at JYFLTRAP reveal that neutron pairing is weaker than predicted by theoretical mass models commonly used in r-process calculations, and the data do not support the existence of a subshell closure at N=100. The resulting changes in calculated r-process abundances produce a smoother abundance distribution and better agreement with solar r-process abundances, but the authors note that the neutron-capture rate calculations using TALYS should be taken as a representative example of the effect.

## Method

The neutron-rich rare-earth isotopes were produced via 25 MeV proton-induced fission of a natural uranium target at the IGISOL facility, followed by mass separation with a dipole magnet and cooling in an RFQ cooler-buncher. Mass measurements were performed at the JYFLTRAP double Penning trap using the time-of-flight ion-cyclotron resonance method, with quadrupolar excitation (400 ms) for some isotopes and separated oscillatory fields (Ramsey-type excitations of 25-350-25 ms or 25-750-25 ms) for others to determine cyclotron frequencies. The magnetic field was calibrated by interleaving measurements of well-known reference ions (136Xe, 158Gd, 163Dy, and 171Yb), and the resulting frequency ratios were converted to mass-excess values.

## Key numbers

- First-time mass measurements were performed for 158Nd, 160Pm, 162Sm, and 164-166Gd; precision was improved for 156Nd, 158Pm, 162,163Eu, 163Gd, and 164Tb.
- The new mass-excess value for 156Nd is -60210(2) keV, compared to the AME16 value of -60470(200) keV, a difference of 260(200) keV.
- For 163Gd, the measured mass-excess is -61200(4) keV assuming the measured state is the isomer at 137.8 keV, giving a ground-state mass of -61338(4) keV; this differs from the CPT measurement by 24(9) keV.
- The two-neutron shell-gap energies for N=100 are less than 1 MeV down to Gd, not supporting a subshell closure.
- The r-process abundance calculations using the new masses yield chi-squared = 10.7 over mass range A=154-168, compared to chi-squared = 18.9 for the baseline AME16+FRDM12 values.
- Changes of up to 24% in calculated abundances were observed, with neutron-capture rates changing by 10-25%.

## Context

This work extends previous mass measurements in the rare-earth region performed by the Canadian Penning Trap (van Schelt, 2012) and Q-beta measurements using a total absorption Clover detector (Hayashi, 2014). The results contradict the proposed N=100 subshell closure suggested by Hartree-Fock calculations (Ghorui, 2012) and gamma-ray spectroscopy (Jones, 2005), and instead agree with recent half-life measurements (Wu, 2017) and K-isomer studies (Yokoyama, 2017) that found no supporting evidence for such a closure. The new masses enable improved r-process abundance calculations for neutron star merger scenarios, which is increasingly important in the era of multi-messenger observations from events like GW170817.
