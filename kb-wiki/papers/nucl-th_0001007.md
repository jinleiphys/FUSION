# Two-Body Correlations in Nuclear Systems

> Correlations in the nuclear wave-function beyond the mean-field or Hartree-Fock approximation are very important to describe basic properties of nuclear structure. Various approaches to account for such correlations are described and compared to each other. This includes the hole-line expansion, the coupled cluster or ``exponential S'' approach, the self-consistent evaluation of Greens functions, variational approaches using correlated basis functions and recent developments employing quantum Monte-Carlo techniques. Details of these correlations are explored and their sensitivity to the underlying nucleon-nucleon interaction. Special attention is paid to the attempts to investigate these correlations in exclusive nucleon knock-out experiments induced by electron scattering. Another important issue of nuclear structure physics is the role of relativistic effects as contained in phenomenological mean field models. The sensitivity of various nuclear structure observables on these relativistic features are investigated. The report includes the discussion of nuclear matter as well as finite nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that beyond-mean-field correlations are essential for describing nuclear structure, but the Green's function approach specifically shows that going beyond the Hartree-Fock approximation leads to a redistribution of spectroscopic strength from a single quasi-particle peak into multiple poles, with the quasi-particle strength Z being smaller than one and particle number conservation not automatically guaranteed unless self-consistency is enforced.

## Method

The authors present the Green's function formalism for nuclear many-body systems, deriving the single-particle Green's function in the Lehmann representation and showing how it can be evaluated via the Dyson equation with a self-energy constructed from irreducible diagrams. They then extend this to the two-particle Green's function and discuss the ladder approximation, where the two-particle propagator is obtained by solving an integral equation that includes the bare nucleon-nucleon interaction V and the noninteracting product of dressed propagators.

## Key numbers

- The single-particle Green's function g(k, ω) exhibits poles at energies corresponding to (E^(A+1)_γ - E^A_0) for particle addition and (E^A_0 - E^(A-1)_δ) for particle removal, with the residua given by spectroscopic factors |⟨Ψ^(A+1)_γ|a†_k|Ψ_0⟩|² and |⟨Ψ^(A-1)_δ|a_k|Ψ_0⟩|²
- The momentum distribution n(k) is obtained by integrating the hole spectral function over ω from -∞ to the Fermi energy ε_F: n(k) = ∫_{-∞}^{ε_F} dω S_h(k, ω)
- The ground-state energy can be calculated via the Koltun sum rule: E^A_0 = (1/2) Σ_{α,β} ∫_{-∞}^{ε_F} dω S_h(αβ, ω) (⟨α|T_kin|β⟩ + ωδ_{α,β})
- The self-energy in second order contains contributions from 2-particle-1-hole (2p1h) and 2-hole-1-particle (2h1p) intermediate states, with denominators ω - (ε_{ρ1} + ε_{ρ2} - ε_ν) + iη and ω - (ε_{ν1} + ε_{ν2} - ε_ρ) - iη respectively

## Context

This review extends the discussion of nuclear correlations beyond the hole-line expansion and coupled cluster method (CCM) by presenting the Green's function approach, which also yields dynamic properties like the single-particle spectral function relevant to electron-induced nucleon knock-out experiments. The work builds on the textbooks of Fetter and Walecka, Negele and Orland, and Mattuck, as well as the comprehensive description by Mahaux and Sartor, and the self-consistent Green's function techniques developed by Baym and Kadanov. The paper enables future calculations of spectroscopic factors and momentum distributions in both nuclear matter and finite nuclei, with the BAGEL scheme of Skouras et al. and the numerical work of van Neck et al. providing practical paths toward self-consistent solutions.
