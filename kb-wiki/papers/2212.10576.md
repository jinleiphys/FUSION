# Constraints on strong phase transitions in neutron stars

> We study current bounds on strong first-order phase transitions (PTs) along the equation of state (EOS) of dense strongly interacting matter in neutron stars, under the simplifying assumption that on either side of the PT the EOS can be approximated by a simple polytropic form. We construct a large ensemble of possible EOSs of this form, anchor them to chiral effective field theory calculations at nuclear density and perturbative QCD at high densities, and subject them to astrophysical constraints from high-mass pulsars and gravitational-wave observations. Within this setup, we find that a PT permits neutron-star solutions with larger radii, but only if the transition begins below twice nuclear saturation density. We also identify a large parameter space of allowed PTs currently unexplored by numerical-relativity studies. Additionally, we locate a small region of parameter space allowing twin-star solutions, though we find them to only marginally pass the current astrophysical constraints. Finally, we find that sizeable cores of high-density matter beyond the PT may be located in the centers of some stable neutron stars, primarily those with larger masses.

# Constraints on strong phase transitions in neutron stars

## Key claim
Under the simplifying assumption that the equation of state on either side of a first-order phase transition can be approximated by a simple polytropic form, a phase transition permits neutron-star solutions with larger radii, but only if the transition begins below twice nuclear saturation density; the authors also identify a large parameter space of allowed phase transitions currently unexplored by numerical-relativity studies.

## Method
The authors constructed a large ensemble of equations of state using a two-polytrope form with a first-order phase transition inserted between them, anchored to chiral effective field theory calculations at nuclear density (up to 1.1 times nuclear saturation density) and to perturbative QCD constraints at ten times nuclear saturation density. They sampled five parameters uniformly (interpolation between soft and stiff chiral EFT equations of state, transition onset density, density jump at the transition, and energy density and pressure at ten times nuclear saturation density) and applied astrophysical constraints requiring support of a 2 solar mass neutron star and consistency with the GW170817 tidal deformability measurement, yielding approximately 280,000 viable candidate equations of state from about 1,500,000 sampled.

## Key numbers
- Approximately 280,000 equations of state remained viable after applying astrophysical constraints from a total sample of about 1,500,000 causal equations of state
- Phase transitions extend the range of allowed neutron-star radii beyond 13.5 km found in Annala (2021) for stars at masses around or above 1.4 solar masses
- A minimal transition onset energy density of approximately 275 MeV/fm^3 was found for phase transitions that destabilize the neutron-star sequence, set by the 2 solar mass constraint
- Twin-star solutions were found to only marginally pass the current astrophysical constraints, with high-mass twins all possessing masses of approximately 2 solar masses and binary tidal deformabilities near the GW170817 limit of 720
- Cores of high-density matter with masses exceeding 0.5 solar masses are possible for transition onsets up to 5 times nuclear saturation density (with small density jumps)
- The chirp mass from GW170817 is known to very good accuracy as 1.186 solar masses, with mass ratio constraint q > 0.73 and binary tidal deformability constraint less than 720

## Context
This work extends model-agnostic equation-of-state inference studies such as Annala (2021) and Komoltsev (2021) by explicitly including first-order phase transitions without a priori limitations on their strength or onset density. The results map out the space of possible phase transitions for merger simulations, showing that previous numerical-relativity studies such as Bauswein (2018), Most (2018), and Fujimoto (2022) have focused on lower transition densities, leaving a large range of allowed phase transition parameters at higher densities unexplored. The work also enables systematic comparison with twin-star solutions first discussed by Gerlach (1968) and Schertler (2000).
