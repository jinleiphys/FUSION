# Hadronization revisited: the dynamics behind hadro-chemical equilibrium

> The multiplicity of hadronic species created in elementary, and in nucleus-nucleus collisions, are known to be well reproduced by the statistical hadronization model, in its canonical and grand-canonical versions.To understand the origin of the implied equilibrium we revisit the hadronization models developed for e+e- annihilation to hadrons which imply spatial color pre-confinement clusters forming at the end of the pQCD evolution, which decays into on-shell hadrons/resonances. The classical ensemble description arises as a consequence of decoherence and phase space dominance during cluster formation, and decay.For A+A collisions we assume that hadronization occurs from similar singlet clusters which will overlap spatially owing to the extreme density. This is imaged in the transition to the grand-canonical ensemble.This transition sets in with increasing A and collision centrality. It can be described by a percolation model.

# Hadronization revisited: the dynamics behind hadro-chemical equilibrium

## Key claim

The apparent hadro-chemical equilibrium observed in both elementary and nucleus-nucleus collisions arises not from thermalization but from stochastic elements and phase space dominance during the non-perturbative decay of color-singlet clusters into hadrons and resonances, with the transition from canonical to grand-canonical description in A+A collisions resulting from spatial overlap of these clusters at high density rather than from inelastic hadronic rescattering after hadronization.

## Method

The paper revisits the 1980s QCD jet hadronization models developed for e+e- annihilation, specifically the DGLAP perturbative evolution leading to color pre-confinement and singlet cluster formation as implemented in the HERWIG Monte Carlo code by Webber and collaborators. For A+A collisions, the author assumes that hadronization occurs from similar color-singlet clusters that overlap spatially due to extreme energy density, with the transition from isolated clusters to overlapping super-clusters described by a percolation model, and the resulting yield distribution captured by the grand-canonical statistical ensemble.

## Key numbers

- Hadronization temperature T ≈ 160 MeV derived from canonical fit to e+e- annihilation at √s = 91.2 GeV (LEP Z0 energy), corresponding to energy density of about 0.7 GeV/fm³
- Hadronic freeze-out temperature T = 158 MeV from grand-canonical fit to NA49 central Pb+Pb collisions at √s = 17.3 GeV, with baryochemical potential μB ≈ 250 MeV
- Fireball volume of 27 fm³ for e+e- annihilation at √s = 91.2 GeV, consistent with about 10 clusters of dimension 1 fm decaying simultaneously
- Strangeness suppression factor γs = 0.83 in the grand-canonical fit to NA49 data at √s = 17.3 GeV, corresponding to Wroblewski coefficient λs = 0.52
- Wroblewski coefficient λs ≈ 0.25 in pp, pp̅ and e+e- collisions versus about 0.50 in central A+A collisions
- Production ratios K+/π and Λ/π increase by about factor 2 from p+p to central Pb+Pb at top SPS energy; Ξ/π and Ω/π increase by about 6 and 15 respectively
- Grand-canonical fit to RHIC Au+Au at √s = 200 GeV gives T = 160 ± 5 MeV with μB ≈ 20 MeV
- Omega to pion ratio of 10⁻³ at both SPS (√s = 17.3 GeV) and RHIC (√s = 200 GeV)
- Mean cluster mass from HERWIG simulation about 1.5 GeV with steep fall-off toward higher masses
- Critical energy density for deconfinement about 1 GeV/fm³ from lattice QCD

## Context

This work extends the statistical hadronization model tradition originating from Hagedorn (1960s-70s) and the color pre-confinement concept of Amati and Veneziano (1979), connecting it to the cluster hadronization models of Webber and collaborators (1980s) and the canonical ensemble fits by Becattini (1990s) for e+e- annihilation. The paper argues against the hypothesis that hadronic rescattering cascades (as considered by Bass and Dumitru, and others) are the principal mechanism establishing equilibrium in A+A collisions, instead proposing that hadrons are "born into equilibrium" directly from cluster decay. The transition from canonical to grand-canonical description with increasing system size builds on the work of Becattini and collaborators showing that the grand-canonical ensemble is the large volume limit of the canonical ensemble. The paper enables experimental determination of the QCD phase boundary from hadronization temperatures, and identifies the lower SPS and AGS energy regimes (where freeze-out temperatures fall below 125 MeV) as requiring a different dynamical description involving a finite expansion interval between hadronization and freeze-out.
