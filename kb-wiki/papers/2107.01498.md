# Machine learning approach to pattern recognition in nuclear dynamics from the ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell model

> A novel machine learning approach is used to provide further insight into atomic nuclei and to detect orderly patterns amidst a vast data of large-scale calculations. The method utilizes a neural network that is trained on ab initio results from the symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM) for light nuclei. We show that the SA-NCSM, which expands ab initio applications up to medium-mass nuclei by using dominant symmetries of nuclear dynamics, can reach heavier nuclei when coupled with the machine learning approach. In particular, we find that a neural network trained on probability amplitudes for $s$-and $p$-shell nuclear wave functions not only predicts dominant configurations for heavier nuclei but in addition, when tested for the $^{20}$Ne ground state, it accurately reproduces the probability distribution. The nonnegligible configurations predicted by the network provide an important input to the SA-NCSM for reducing ultra-large model spaces to manageable sizes that can be, in turn, utilized in SA-NCSM calculations to obtain accurate observables. The neural network is capable of describing nuclear deformation and is used to track the shape evolution along the $^{20-42}$Mg isotopic chain, suggesting a shape-coexistence that is more pronounced toward the very neutron-rich isotopes. We provide first descriptions of the structure and deformation of $^{24}$Si and $^{40}$Mg of interest to x-ray burst nucleosynthesis, and even of the extremely heavy nuclei such as $^{166,168}$Er and $^{236}$U, that build upon first principles considerations.

## Key claim

The neural network trained on ab initio SA-NCSM probability amplitudes for s- and p-shell nuclei can predict dominant configurations for heavier nuclei, but the accuracy depends on using sufficiently large training data sets; tests with networks trained only on a few nuclei result in larger deviations, and the network may neglect some slightly less significant modes entirely.

## Method

The authors trained a feedforward neural network with 11 input neurons (proton number, neutron number, total angular momentum, excitation quanta N, proton spin, neutron spin, total spin, deformation quantum numbers lambda and mu, Nmax, and hbar Omega) on SA-NCSM wave function probability amplitudes for s- and p-shell nuclei (4He, 8He, 6Li, 8Be, 10C, 12C, 14C, 16O) using the NNLOopt nucleon-nucleon potential. The network used a segmented first hidden layer to group related inputs, rectified linear unit activation functions for hidden layers, a sigmoid activation for the output layer, Poisson loss function, and Adam optimization over 200 epochs. The network was then applied to predict probability amplitudes for ground states of heavier nuclei not included in the training set.

## Key numbers

- Training data included 4He at Nmax = 6, 8, 10, 12, 14 with hbar Omega = 22 MeV (5.80 x 10^4 basis states total across these spaces)
- Training data for 8Be included Nmax = 8, 10, 12, and <8>14 with dimension 1.53 x 10^8 for the largest space
- For 20Ne validation, the Nmax = 10 model space consists of about 10^12 basis states
- For 24Si, the complete space in Nmax = 6 has dimension of approximately 10^11
- For 4He, the network predicted from Nmax = 12 (22,716 basis states) to Nmax = 14 (58,080 basis states)
- The network predicted configurations for 166Er, 168Er, and 236U at Nmax = 12, with leading configurations 0(78 0) for 166Er, 0(30 8) for 168Er, and 0(86 18) for 236U

## Context

This work extends the symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM) approach of Dytrych et al. (2020) and Launey et al. (2016, 2021), which uses inherent nuclear symmetries to enable ab initio calculations up to the calcium region, by coupling it with machine learning to reach heavier nuclei. The neural network approach builds on earlier applications of deep learning to nuclear physics, including extrapolations of nuclear observables by Negoita et al. (2019) and Neufcourt et al. The method enables first descriptions of nuclei such as 24Si and 40Mg relevant to x-ray burst nucleosynthesis, and extremely heavy nuclei like 166,168Er and 236U relevant to r-process simulations, which are currently impossible for full ab initio SA-NCSM calculations.
