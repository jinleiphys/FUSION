# Entanglement Structures in Quantum Field Theories: Negativity Cores and Bound Entanglement in the Vacuum

> The many-body entanglement between two finite (size-$d$) disjoint vacuum regions of non-interacting lattice scalar field theory in one spatial dimension -- a $(d_A \times d_B)_{\rm mixed}$ Gaussian continuous variable system -- is locally transformed into a tensor-product "core" of $(1_A \times 1_B)_{\rm mixed}$ entangled pairs. Accessible entanglement within these core pairs exhibits an exponential hierarchy, and as such identifies the structure of dominant region modes from which vacuum entanglement could be extracted into a spatially separated pair of quantum detectors. Beyond the core, remaining modes of the "halo" are determined to be AB-separable in isolation, as well as separable from the core. However, state preparation protocols that distribute entanglement in the form of $(1_A \times 1_B)_{\rm mixed}$ core pairs are found to require additional entanglement in the halo that is obscured by classical correlations. This inaccessible (bound) halo entanglement is found to mirror the accessible entanglement, but with a step behavior as the continuum is approached. It remains possible that alternate initialization protocols that do not utilize the exponential hierarchy of core-pair entanglement may require less inaccessible entanglement. Entanglement consolidation is expected to persist in higher dimensions and may aid classical and quantum simulations of asymptotically free gauge field theories, such as quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

The vacuum entanglement between two disjoint regions of a non-interacting lattice scalar field in one spatial dimension can be locally transformed into a tensor-product "core" of entangled mode pairs that capture all accessible entanglement, while the remaining "halo" modes are separable in isolation but require additional inaccessible (bound) entanglement when the core-halo separability structure is imposed.

## Method

The authors study the Gaussian continuous variable state formed by two disjoint regions of size d in the vacuum of a 1D free lattice scalar field, using the covariance matrix formalism. They construct local symplectic transformations from the partially transposed symplectic eigenvectors that consolidate the logarithmic negativity into a set of (1_A x 1_B)_mixed mode pairs (the core), using a symplectic Gram-Schmidt procedure to enforce unitarity. The separability structure of the post-consolidation system is analyzed through sequential applications of techniques from Giedke et al. (2001) to determine the core-halo decomposition and quantify the inaccessible entanglement in the halo.

## Key numbers

* For d = 30 and mass m = 0.003, the number of PT symplectic eigenvalues contributing to the logarithmic negativity (n_-) decreases rapidly with spatial separation between vacuum regions, reaching a single contribution at separation tilded r = 400.
* The logarithmic negativity contributions from core pairs exhibit an exponential hierarchy as a function of PT symplectic eigenvalue index, with the largest contribution corresponding to the first eigenvalue.
* The inaccessible halo entanglement is found to mirror the accessible core entanglement in magnitude and decay as a function of disjoint region separation, with approximate plateaus appearing as the continuum is approached.

## Context

This work extends prior analyses of entanglement in harmonic chains and lattice scalar fields, including studies of separability thresholds by Audenaert et al. (2002), Botero and Reznik (2004), and Calabrese et al. (2012), as well as the observation by Klco et al. (2020, 2021) that PT symplectic eigenvalues exhibit an exponential hierarchy. The consolidation technique builds on known entanglement reorganizations for Gaussian states with symmetries (Serafini et al., 2005; 2016) and pure-state decompositions (Botero and Reznik, 2003, 2004). The results enable improved design of quantum detectors for extracting vacuum entanglement and may inform classical and quantum simulations of asymptotically free gauge field theories such as quantum chromodynamics.
