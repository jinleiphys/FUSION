# Theoretical study of deeply virtual Compton scattering off $^4$He

> An interesting breakthrough in understanding the elusive inner content of nuclear systems in terms of partonic degrees of freedom is represented by deeply virtual Compton scattering processes. In such a way, tomographic view of nuclei and bound nucleons in coordinate space could be achieved for the first time. Moreover, nowadays experimental results for such a process considering $^4$He targets recently released at Jefferson Lab are available. In this talk, the recent results of our rigorous Impulse Approximation for DVCS off $^4$He, in terms of state-of-the-art models of the nuclear spectral function and of the parton structure of the bound proton, able to explain present data, has been shown.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a rigorous Impulse Approximation (IA) calculation for deeply virtual Compton scattering off $^4$He, using state-of-the-art models of the nuclear spectral function and the parton structure of the bound proton, can explain currently available Jefferson Lab data without invoking exotic nuclear effects such as dynamical off-shellness.

## Method

The authors computed the handbag contribution to both coherent and incoherent DVCS channels off $^4$He within the Impulse Approximation, where the virtual photon interacts with a single quark in one nucleon and final state interactions are neglected. For the coherent channel, the nuclear GPD was obtained as a convolution between nucleonic GPDs (using the Goloskokov-Kroll model) and an off-diagonal light-cone momentum distribution built from a realistic spectral function based on the Av18 NN interaction including three-body forces. For the incoherent channel, the cross section was computed by integrating the bound-proton amplitude over the diagonal spectral function, with the bound proton's off-shellness accounted for by rescaling the skewness parameter.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations for $^4$He DVCS by Liuti (1993) and Guzey (2003) into the kinematic regions probed by recent Jefferson Lab experiments (Hattawy 2017, 2018), and follows similar realistic few-body approaches previously applied to $^2$H by Cano (2004) and $^3$He by Scopetta (2004, 2009). The results enable a benchmark for interpreting the next generation of high-luminosity measurements at Jefferson Lab, with work in progress toward a fully realistic computation of the $^4$He spectral function.
