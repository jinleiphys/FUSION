# Three-body structures of low-lying nuclear states of $^8$Li

> The four nucleons in $^8$Li outside the $α$-particle ($α=^4$He) can be divided into pairs of one neutron ($n$) and 3 nucleons in the triton ($t=^3$H), or 2 in the deuteron ($d=^2$H) and two neutrons in a dineutron ($^2n$). The corresponding three-body structures, $α$+$t$+$n$ or $α$+$d$+$^2n$, are suggested to describe the bulk part of the low-energy ($<10$~MeV) states of $^8$Li. Several breakup thresholds influence the structures and possible decays. We calculate the three-body structures of the various $J^π$ states, where different clustering appear, e.g. $^7$Li*+$n$, $^6$Li*$+^2n$, $^6$He*$+d$. The experimental $^8$Li spectrum can be reproduced with fine tuning by a three-body potential parameter. Three unobserved $0^+$ and an excited 2$^+$ states are found. All states appear as bound states or resonances. The lowest or highest energies have cluster structures, $α$+$t$+$n$ or $α$+$d$+$^2n$, respectively. We give calculated energy and width (if possible), geometry, and partial wave decomposition for all states.

## Key claim

The low-energy spectrum of $^8$Li (below 10 MeV) can be described by two distinct three-body cluster configurations, $\alpha+t+n$ and $\alpha+d+^2n$, but the abstract's claim that these "suggest" the description glosses over the condition that the two configurations are restricted to states with total isospin 1, and that a total isospin 2 state (the highest $0^+$ resonance) cannot be described by either configuration and is explicitly excluded from the study.

## Method

The authors solved the Faddeev equations in coordinate space using the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method, with two-body interactions parameterized as sums of Gaussians fitted to reproduce experimental data for each subsystem (e.g., $t$-$n$, $\alpha$-$n$, $\alpha$-$t$, $\alpha$-$d$, $\alpha$-$^2n$, $d$-$^2n$). Resonances were identified by combining the hyperspherical expansion with complex scaling, where states with complex energy and $\theta$-independent positions separated from continuum cuts were classified as resonances. A Gaussian three-body potential depending only on the hyperradius was used for fine-tuning to match the experimental $^8$Li spectrum.

## Key numbers

- Ground state $2^+$ separation energy: $-4.50$ MeV (experimental $-4.49920$ MeV from Tilley (2004)), achieved with three-body strength $S_\mathrm{3b}=0.51$ MeV
- Ground state rms radius: 2.53 fm (experimental $2.51\pm0.03$ fm from Tanihata (1985) and $2.39\pm0.05$ fm from Fan (2014))
- Excited $1^+$ bound state separation energy: $-3.52$ MeV (0.98 MeV excitation energy), with three-body strength $S_\mathrm{3b}=-0.38$ MeV
- $1^+$ state rms radius: 2.76 fm
- $t$-$n$ $p$-wave resonances (poles of $S$-matrix): $(E_R,\Gamma_R)$ = (0.77, 6.72) MeV, (1.15, 6.38) MeV, (1.15, 3.49) MeV, and (1.22, 3.34) MeV
- $\alpha$-$t$ $\ell=3$ resonances: $\frac{7}{2}^-$ at 2.185 MeV with width 69 keV; $\frac{5}{2}^-$ at 4.137 MeV with width 0.918 MeV
- $\alpha$-$d$ $d$-wave resonances in $^6$Li: $3^+$ at $(E_R,\Gamma_R)=(0.712,0.024)$ MeV; $1^+$ at $(4.18,1.5)$ MeV; $2^+$ at $(2.71,0.75)$ MeV (experimental $2^+$ at $(2.84,1.30)$ MeV from Tilley (2002))
- $\alpha$-$^2n$ $2^+$ resonance in $^6$He: 0.83 MeV above two-neutron threshold with width 0.113 MeV
- $d$-$^2n$ $s$-wave scattering length: $a_{d,^2n} \simeq -7.5$ fm, corresponding to virtual state energy $\simeq 0.4$ MeV

## Context

This work extends the three-body cluster approach previously applied to $^6$He by Cobis (1998) and Fedorov (2003) and to $^4$H/$^5$H by Diego (2007), now to the more complex $^8$Li system with two competing cluster configurations. The calculations reproduce the experimental $^8$Li spectrum from Tilley (2004) and predict three unobserved $0^+$ states and an excited $2^+$ state, which can guide future experimental searches. The method enables systematic study of how different breakup thresholds (e.g., $^7$Li$+n$, $^6$Li$+^2n$, $^6$He$+d$) influence the structure of individual states, and the framework can be extended to the isospin 2 configuration $\alpha+^2n+np$ left for future work.
