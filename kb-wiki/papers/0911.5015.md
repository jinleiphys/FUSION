# Critical Opalescence: An Optical Signature for a QCD Critical Point

> Four possible scenarios are considered for a transition from a quark-gluon matter to hadronic matter, and their corresponding correlation signatures are discussed. Four criteria are highlighted for a definitive experimental search for a QCD critical point. An old-new experimental measure, the optical opacity (or its inverse the nuclear attenuation length) is determined, in terms of a combination of nuclear suppression factors and a measurement of the relevant fireball length scales. Length scale estimates using either the Hanbury Brown -- Twiss radii or that of the initial nuclear geometry for measurements of optical opacity with respect to the reaction plane yield, somewhat surprizingly, nearly the same nuclear attenuation lenght in 0-5 % most central 200 GeV Au+Au collisions, corresponding to 2.9 $\pm$ 0.3 fm. The necessity and the possibility of measuring critical exponents is also discussed in the context of determination of the universality class of the QCD critical point. Critical opalescence is proposed to locate such a critical point on the QCD phase diagram, corresponding to a maximum of optical opacity in heavy ion experiments.

# Critical Opalescence: An Optical Signature for a QCD Critical Point

## Key claim

Critical opalescence, defined as a maximum of optical opacity (or minimum of nuclear attenuation length) measured from the combination of nuclear suppression factors and femtoscopic length scales, is proposed as a smoking-gun signature to locate the QCD critical point on the phase diagram, provided that the critical exponents and universality class can also be determined experimentally to definitively characterize the transition.

## Method

The author defines optical opacity κ = -ln(R_AA)/R_HBT and nuclear attenuation length λ = -R_HBT/ln(R_AA) by combining the nuclear modification factor R_AA with Hanbury Brown-Twiss (HBT) radius measurements from two-particle Bose-Einstein correlation functions. Four possible scenarios for the transition from quark-gluon matter to hadronic matter are considered (first order, second order, cross-over, and non-equilibrium flash), and their corresponding correlation signatures are evaluated against existing experimental data from CERN SPS and RHIC. The opacity and attenuation length are evaluated as functions of centrality in 200 GeV Au+Au collisions using PHENIX data, and cross-checked against reaction-plane-dependent measurements with Glauber model length scale estimates.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear attenuation length in 0-5% most central 200 GeV Au+Au collisions: 2.9 ± 0.3 fm, evaluated from neutral pions at p_t = 4.75 GeV/c
- Opacity in 0-5% most central collisions: 0.35 ± 0.04 fm^(-1), decreasing to 0.15 ± 0.05 fm^(-1) in 50-60% centrality
- Attenuation length varies from 2.9 ± 0.3 fm (0-5% central) to 6.5 ± 2.0 fm (50-60% central) for p_t = 4.75 GeV/c neutral pions
- HBT radii scale as R_i = p_0 + p_1 N_part^(1/3) with p_0 = 0.83 ± 0.25 fm and p_1 = 0.54 ± 0.05 fm, averaged over all three directions
- Lattice QCD prediction for critical end point location: T_E = 164 ± 2 MeV and μ_E = 360 ± 40 MeV (Fodor, 2004), with statistical errors only
- Cross-over transition temperature from renormalized chiral susceptibility: T_c = 151 ± 3 MeV, with strange quark number susceptibility and Polyakov loop values higher by 24 ± 4 MeV and 25 ± 4 MeV respectively

## Context

This work extends the search for the QCD critical point by proposing an experimental observable (optical opacity) analogous to critical opalescence in statistical physics and physical chemistry, where a fluid becomes maximally opaque at its critical point due to density fluctuations on all length scales. The paper contrasts with the opinion expressed by Rajagopal (2009) that critical exponents cannot be measured in heavy ion experiments because correlation lengths are limited to 2-3 fm; the author argues that critical exponents have been successfully extracted from nuclear multifragmentation studies of the liquid-gas phase transition (Mahi, 1988; Srivastava, 2002), and similar measurements should be possible at RHIC and SPS energies. The approach enables a definitive experimental program to identify, locate, characterize, and cross-check the QCD critical point by measuring at least two critical exponents (recommended: η from Levy fits to HBT correlations and ν from multiplicity fluctuations) to determine the universality class, distinguishing between the 3d Ising model prediction and possible modifications from random fields or dynamical effects.
