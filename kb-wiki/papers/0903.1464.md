# Modeling Nuclear Pasta and the Transition to Uniform Nuclear Matter with the 3D-Skyrme-Hartree-Fock Method

> The first results of a new three-dimensional, finite temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS study of the properties of inhomogeneous nuclear matter at densities and temperatures leading to the transition to uniform nuclear matter are presented. A constraint is placed on the two independent components of the quadrupole moment in order to self-consistently explore the shape phase space of nuclear configurations. The scheme employed naturally allows effects such as (i) neutron drip, which results in an external neutron gas, (ii) the variety of exotic nuclear shapes expected for extremely neutron heavy nuclei, and (iii) the subsequent dissolution of these nuclei into nuclear matter. In this way, the equation of state can be calculated across phase transitions from lower densities (where one dimensional Hartree-Fock suffices) through to uniform nuclear matter without recourse to interpolation techniques between density regimes described by different physical models.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first results of a three-dimensional, finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS study that can self-consistently model the transition from inhomogeneous nuclear matter (including nuclear pasta phases) to uniform nuclear matter without interpolation between different physical models, but the results are preliminary and spurious shell effects from the finite computational cell have not yet been fully corrected.

## Method

The Skyrme-Hartree-Fock equations are solved in coordinate space within a single cubic unit cell of locally periodic nuclear matter, using periodic boundary conditions enforced via Fourier transforms. A constraint is placed on both independent components of the neutron quadrupole moment (parameterized as deformation coordinates beta and gamma) to systematically explore the shape phase space of nuclear configurations, while pairing is included at zero temperature via BCS with a contact potential and finite temperature effects are imposed through Fermi-Dirac occupation numbers. Calculations are performed at fixed baryon number density, temperature, and proton fraction, with the nucleon number A and (for neutron star matter) proton number Z varied to find free energy minima.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: n_s ≈ 2.5 × 10^14 g cm^-3
- At y_p = 0.3 and temperatures up to T = 5 MeV, canonical pasta shapes appear as absolute minima at densities: ~0.04 fm^-3 (spherical nuclei), ~0.06 fm^-3 (cylindrical nuclei), ~0.08 fm^-3 (slab nuclei), ~0.09 fm^-3 (cylindrical holes), ~0.10 fm^-3 (spherical holes)
- Free energy densities of two configurations in a cubic cell versus a double-length cell agree to within 1 part in 10^4
- At n_b = 0.083 fm^-3, y_p = 0.3, T = 2.5 MeV, different pasta shapes appear as local minima separated from absolute minima by less than 5 keV fm^-3
- For a typical pulsar surface magnetic field B ≈ 10^12 G, the magnetic field energy density is E_B ≈ 10^-5 keV fm^-3; for magnetar fields ≈ 10^15 G, E_B ≈ 10 keV fm^-3
- Calculations performed at y_p = 0.3 for T = 0, 2.5, 5, 7.5 MeV, nucleon numbers A = 100-3000 with step 100, and deformation parameters β = 0.0-2.0, γ = 0°, 30°, 60°
- The transition to uniform matter occurs at n_b ≈ 0.10-0.11 fm^-3 at T = 2.5 MeV

## Context

This work extends earlier 3D-Hartree-Fock studies by Magierski and Heenen (2002) and by Goegelein and Muther (2007), which calculated nuclear configurations at only limited densities, proton fractions, and nucleon numbers, and only the most recent attempted finite-temperature calculations. The new method constrains both independent quadrupole moments (rather than just one) and can self-consistently bridge between lower-density 1D calculations and uniform matter, avoiding interpolation between different physical models. The results enable construction of the equation of state across the pasta phase transition region for both supernova and neutron star matter, and the finding that the SkM* and Sly4 parameterizations give different pasta phase diagrams at beta-equilibrium (due to different symmetry energies) suggests that observations of neutron star crust properties could constrain the asymmetry energy of sub-nuclear matter.
