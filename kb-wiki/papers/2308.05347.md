# Comparing pion production in transport simulations of heavy-ion collisions at $270A$ MeV under controlled conditions

> Within the TMEP, we present a detailed study of the performance of different transport models in Sn+Sn collisions at $270A$ MeV, and put particular emphasis on the production of pions and $Δ$ resonances, which have been used as probes of the nuclear symmetry energy. We prescribe a common and rather simple physics model, and follow in detail the results of 4 BUU models and 6 QMD models. The nucleonic evolution of the collision and the nucleonic observables in these codes do not completely converge, but the differences among the codes can be understood as being due to several reasons: the basic differences between BUU and QMD models in the representation of the phase-space distributions, computational differences in the mean-field evaluation, and differences in the adopted strategies for the Pauli blocking in the collision integrals. For pionic observables, we find that a higher maximum density leads to an enhanced pion yield and a reduced $π^-/π^+$ yield ratio, while a more effective Pauli blocking generally leads to a slightly suppressed pion yield and an enhanced $π^-/π^+$ yield ratio. We specifically investigate the effect of the Coulomb force, and find that it increases the total $π^-/π^+$ yield ratio but reduces the ratio at high pion energies, although differences in its implementations do not have a dominating role in the differences among the codes. Taking into account only the results of codes that strictly follow the homework specifications, we find a convergence of the codes in the final charged pion yield ratio to a $1σ$ deviation of about $5\%$. However, the uncertainty is expected to be reduced to about $1.6\%$ if the same or similar strategies and ingredients, i.e., an improved Pauli blocking and calculation of the non-linear term in the mean-field potential, are similarly used in all codes.

## Key claim

Under a common prescribed physics model with fixed symmetry energy, the charged pion yield ratio from 10 different transport codes converges to a 1σ deviation of about 5% when only codes strictly following the homework specifications are considered, but this uncertainty could be reduced to about 1.6% if all codes adopt similar strategies for Pauli blocking and the non-linear term in the mean-field potential.

## Method

The study compared 4 Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) models and 6 quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) models simulating Sn+Sn collisions at 270A MeV, with all codes using a common simple physics model that included only nucleons, pions, and Δ resonances with momentum-independent mean-field potentials. The codes were prescribed identical input parameters including the nuclear equation of state, inelastic cross sections, Δ resonance spectral function, and Coulomb force treatment, allowing the authors to isolate differences arising from numerical implementation strategies rather than physics model choices.

## Key numbers

- The final charged pion yield ratio across codes strictly following homework specifications showed a 1σ deviation of about 5%
- The expected uncertainty could be reduced to about 1.6% if improved Pauli blocking and non-linear mean-field term calculations were uniformly adopted
- A higher maximum density leads to enhanced pion yield and reduced π⁻/π⁺ yield ratio
- More effective Pauli blocking leads to slightly suppressed pion yield and enhanced π⁻/π⁺ yield ratio
- The Coulomb force increases the total π⁻/π⁺ yield ratio but reduces the ratio at high pion energies

## Context

This work extends the Transport Model Evaluation Project (TMEP) activities that began in 2014, following earlier box calculations of nuclear matter (Zhang 2018, Ono 2019) that optimized implementation of elastic and inelastic collisions. It directly addresses the divergence of conclusions seen in the uncontrolled comparison of Spirit20, where different transport models using their "best" physics models produced conflicting predictions for pion observables. The results enable future benchmark calculations with more realistic physics models including momentum-dependent mean-field potentials and pion in-medium effects, and provide a framework for quantifying systematic uncertainties in transport model analyses of heavy-ion collisions.
