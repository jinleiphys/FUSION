# Incomplete absorption reactions at high energy

> The total reaction cross section of high-energy nucleus-nucleus collision refects the nuclear density profiles of the colliding nuclei and has been a standard tool to investigate the size properties of short-lived unstable nuclei. This basis relies on the assumption that the nucleus-nucleus collision is strongly absorptive in the sense of an optical model. However, this property does not hold completely, while incomplete absorption occurs when an overlap density of two colliding nuclei is low enough. In this paper, we propose a way to quantify this incompleteness, that is, the "blackness" of the total reaction cross section. A significance of this quantification is drawn by taking an example of the total reaction cross sections of proton-rich C isotopes.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the standard assumption of complete absorption in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions does not always hold; incomplete absorption occurs when the overlap density of two colliding nuclei is low enough, and the authors quantify this incompleteness by decomposing the total reaction cross section into complete and incomplete absorption parts, using the ratio S/R as a measure of "blackness."

## Method

The authors employ the Glauber model, a microscopic high-energy reaction theory, to calculate reaction probabilities and total reaction cross sections. They use the nucleon-target formalism (NTG) to incorporate multiple scattering effects, with two-parameter Fermi (2pF) density distributions for medium to heavy nuclei and harmonic-oscillator (HO) and Woods-Saxon (WS) type density distributions for light proton-rich C isotopes. The total reaction cross section is decomposed into complete absorption (σ_comp = πS²) and incomplete absorption (σ_incomp = πR²[1 − (S/R)²]) parts, where S is the impact parameter at which the reaction probability reaches approximately 0.99 and R = √(σ_R/π).

## Key numbers

- For 2pF density distributions on a carbon target at 1000 MeV/nucleon, S values are 4.9, 5.6, 7.0, and 8.2 fm for A = 40, 58, 120, and 208, respectively.
- For 2pF density distributions on a proton target at 1000 MeV/nucleon, S values are 0, 1.7, 3.7, and 5.1 fm for A = 40, 58, 120, and 208, respectively.
- For a carbon target, S/R values are large, about 0.6–0.8 across incident energies.
- For ⁹C on a carbon target at ∼720 MeV/nucleon, the theoretical total reaction cross section is 819 mb (HO) and 818 mb (WS), compared to the experimental value 834 ± 18 mb.
- For ¹⁰C on a carbon target at ∼720 MeV/nucleon, the theoretical total reaction cross section is 795 mb (HO) and 797 mb (WS), compared to the experimental value 795 ± 12 mb.
- For ¹¹C on a carbon target at ∼730 MeV/nucleon, the theoretical total reaction cross section is 776 mb (HO) and 771 mb (WS), compared to the experimental value 778 ± 40 mb.
- Changing the rms matter radius of ¹⁰C by +0.2 fm changes the total reaction cross section by +62 mb on a carbon target and +14 mb on a proton target; changing by −0.2 fm gives −59 mb and −17 mb, respectively.
- Adding one neutron to ¹⁰C while keeping the same rms matter radius changes the total reaction cross section by +19 mb on a carbon target and +23 mb on a proton target; removing one neutron gives −21 mb and −26 mb, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the black-sphere (BS) model of Kohama (2004, 2005, 2016) and the Glauber model analyses of Horiuchi (2006, 2007, 2010, 2012, 2014, 2015, 2016) by quantifying the degree of incomplete absorption in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions. The paper directly addresses an observation by Kaki (2017) that total reaction cross sections for proton-rich C isotopes on a proton target decrease despite enhanced nuclear matter radii, which contradicts the standard BS picture. The proposed decomposition into complete and incomplete absorption parts enables future studies to determine which density profile features (e.g., proton skin thickness) are probed by different targets, particularly for light unstable nuclei near the dripline.
