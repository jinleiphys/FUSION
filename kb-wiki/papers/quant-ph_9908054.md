# Effect of the measurement on the decay rate of a quantum system

> We investigated the electron tunneling out of a quantum dot in the presence of a continuous monitoring by a detector. It is shown that the Schrödinger equation for the whole system can be reduced to new Bloch-type rate equations describing the time-development of the detector and the measured system at once. Using these equations we find that the continuous measurement of the unstable system does not affect its exponential decay, $\exp (-Γt)$, contrary to expectations based on the Quantum Zeno effect . However, the width of the energy distribution of the tunneling electron is no more $Γ$, but increases due to the decoherence, generated by the detector.

## Key claim

Continuous measurement of an unstable quantum system undergoing pure exponential decay does not slow down its decay rate, contrary to expectations based on the Quantum Zeno effect; however, the measurement broadens the energy distribution of the emitted particle due to decoherence generated by the detector, and this result holds provided the density of states and transition amplitudes vary slowly with energy.

## Method

The authors model an electron tunneling out of a quantum dot into a continuum of reservoir states, with a nearby quantum point-contact detector that monitors the dot occupation via Coulomb blockade. They write the many-body time-dependent Schrodinger equation for the entire system (dot, point-contact, and their interaction) and use Laplace transforms to convert the infinite set of algebraic equations into generalized Bloch-type rate equations for the reduced density matrix, without explicitly solving for all amplitudes. By tracing out the detector states, they obtain final equations for the electron density matrix that yield the decay probability and the energy distribution of the tunneling electron.

## Key numbers

- The probability of finding the electron inside the dot is $\sigma_{00}(t) = \exp(-\Gamma t)$, showing the exponential decay rate $\Gamma$ is unaffected by continuous monitoring.
- The energy distribution of the tunneling electron is a Lorentzian: $P(E_\alpha) = \frac{|\Omega_\alpha|^2}{\Gamma} \frac{\Gamma+\Gamma_d}{(E_0-E_\alpha)^2+(\Gamma+\Gamma_d)^2/4}$, where the line-width becomes $\Gamma+\Gamma_d$ instead of $\Gamma$.
- The decoherence rate is $\Gamma_d = (\sqrt{D} - \sqrt{D'})^2$, where $D = (2\pi)|\Omega_{lr}|^2 \rho_L \rho_R (\mu_L - \mu_R)$ and $D' = (2\pi)|\Omega'_{lr}|^2 \rho_L \rho_R (\mu_L - \mu_R)$ are the tunneling rates through the point-contact with the dot empty and occupied, respectively.
- The tunneling rate out of the quantum dot is $\Gamma = 2\pi |\Omega_\alpha|^2 \rho$, where $\rho$ is the density of states in the reservoir.

## Context

This work extends the Bloch-type rate-equation approach developed by Gurvitz (1997) and Gurvitz and Prager (1996) for quantum transport in mesoscopic systems, applying it specifically to the question of the Quantum Zeno effect in exponentially decaying systems. The results contradict the expectation from Misra and Sudarshan (1977) that continuous observation would slow exponential decay, and instead align with the view that the projection postulate applied to transitions into a continuum (where $Q(\Delta t) \propto \Delta t$) does not change the lifetime, unlike the case of transitions between isolated levels studied by Itano et al. (1990). The work enables experimental tests using existing point-contact detectors in quantum dots, as demonstrated by Buks et al. (1998), and clarifies that line-width measurements do not directly correspond to lifetime when environmental decoherence is present.
