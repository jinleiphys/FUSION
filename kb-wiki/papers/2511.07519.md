# Jet quenching in out-of-equilibrium QCD matter

> We present the first study of jet substructure modifications during the bottom-up evolution that describes the early stages of heavy-ion collisions. To this end, we study the bremsstrahlung radiation rate of soft gluons from a hard parton propagating through out-of-equilibrium QCD matter. The gluon spectrum is computed within the Improved Opacity Expansion, which accounts for both multiple soft and single hard momentum exchanges between the hard probe and the medium. The background evolution is obtained from effective kinetic theory simulations that determine the jet quenching parameter, which in turn controls the radiation rate. We compute the radiation rate for initially under- and over-occupied systems, as well as for an expanding system undergoing hydrodynamization, which typically represents the initial stages of heavy-ion collisions. The results for these dynamical backgrounds are compared to static and thermally matched scenarios, allowing to gauge the importance of bulk expansion in the evolution of the jet cascade. Our findings show that the early stages of the bulk matter evolution in heavy-ion collisions leave a sizable imprint on the radiation pattern inside jets. These results establish a basis for incorporating pre-equilibrium dynamics into realistic descriptions of jet quenching and hard-probe evolution.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first study of jet substructure modifications during the bottom-up thermalization evolution that describes the early stages of heavy-ion collisions, showing that the early out-of-equilibrium stages leave a sizable imprint on the radiation pattern inside jets that does not converge to equilibrium expectations even at late times.

## Method

The authors compute the medium-induced bremsstrahlung radiation rate of soft gluons from a hard parton propagating through out-of-equilibrium QCD matter using the Improved Opacity Expansion (IOE), which accounts for both multiple soft and single hard momentum exchanges between the probe and the medium. The background evolution is obtained from effective kinetic theory (EKT) simulations that determine the jet quenching parameter \(\hat q\) and the effective screening mass \(\mu_*\), which control the radiation rate. The radiation spectrum is computed for three representative setups: initially under-occupied isotropic systems, initially over-occupied isotropic systems, and longitudinally expanding systems undergoing hydrodynamization, with results compared to static and thermally matched scenarios.

## Key numbers

* For the expanding system initialized with a CGC-inspired distribution, the saturation scale \(Q_s\) sets the momentum scale, with parameters \(\xi = 10\), \(A(\xi) = 5.24171\), and \(\langle p_T\rangle = 1.8\, Q_s\) used for the initial condition.
* The under-occupied isotropic system uses a Gaussian initial distribution centered at \(p = Q\) with \(Q = 50T\), where \(T\) is the equilibrium temperature after thermalization.
* The over-occupied isotropic system uses an initialization time \(Qt_0 = 10^{-4}\) with a self-similar scaling form parametrized as \(f(p,t_0) = (Qt_0)^{-4/7}/(\lambda \tilde p) \times (0.22 e^{-13.3\tilde p} + 2 e^{-0.92 \tilde p^2})\).
* The jet quenching parameter is parametrized as \(\hat q(\Lambda_\perp \gg T_\varepsilon, t) = a(t) \log(\Lambda_\perp / Q_s) + b(t)\), where the logarithmic dependence on the transverse-momentum cutoff \(\Lambda_\perp\) is explicitly extracted from EKT simulations.

## Context

This work extends the bottom-up thermalization scenario (Baier 2000) and previous EKT studies of the jet quenching parameter (Boguslavski 2023) by computing for the first time the full medium-induced gluon spectrum during pre-equilibrium evolution. The results establish a basis for incorporating pre-equilibrium dynamics into realistic descriptions of jet quenching and hard-probe evolution, directly challenging the conventional approach where \(\hat q = 0\) is assumed for timescales shorter than \(\sim 1\, {\rm fm}/c\) (Wang 2003). This work enables future studies of jet tomography in small collisional systems such as proton-nucleus or light-ion collisions, where the medium evolution is dominated by nonequilibrium dynamics.
