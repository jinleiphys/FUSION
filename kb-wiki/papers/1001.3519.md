# Contributions of different neutron pairs in different approaches for neutrinoless double beta decay

> The methods used till now to calculate the neutrinoless double beta decay matrix elements are: the Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA), the Shell Model (SM), the angular momentum projected Hartee-Fock-Bogoliubov approach (HFB) and the Interacting Boson Model (IBM). The different approaches are compared specifically concerning the the angular momenta and parities of the neutron pairs, which are changed into two protons by the $0νββ$ decay. The QRPA and SM involve about the same angular momentum and parity neutron pairs, while the HFB is restricted to $0^{+}, 2^{+}, 4^{+}, ...$, and IBM to $0^{+}$ and $2^{+}$ nucleon pairs. The differences in the seniority contributions for the QRPA and the SM are discussed.

# Contributions of different neutron pairs in different approaches for neutrinoless double beta decay

## Key claim
The Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) and Shell Model (SM) involve about the same angular momentum and parity neutron pairs for neutrinoless double beta decay transitions, while the angular momentum projected Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) approach is restricted to neutron pairs with positive parity and even angular momenta (0+, 2+, 4+, ...), and the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) is restricted to 0+ and 2+ nucleon pairs only. The authors show that the SM's restricted single-particle basis (typically 4-5 levels) leads to violation of the Ikeda Sum Rule, which the QRPA can satisfy with a larger basis, and that seniority 6 and 10 configurations included in the SM but absent in QRPA give only small contributions to the matrix elements.

## Method
The authors compared the treatment of neutron-pair angular momenta and seniority contributions across four many-body methods: QRPA, SM, angular momentum projected HFB, and IBM. For QRPA calculations, they systematically varied the single-particle basis size from 4 to 13 levels for nuclei including 82Se, 128Te, 130Te, and 76Ge, adjusting the particle-particle renormalization factor gpp to reproduce experimental 2νββ decay rates. They decomposed the total 0νββ nuclear matrix element M0ν into contributions from different angular momenta Jπ of the transforming neutron pairs and from different seniority components (number of broken quasiparticles) in the ground state wave functions.

## Key numbers
- For 82Se → 82Kr with a 4-level QRPA basis (matching SM), the total M0ν = 1.1 with Ikeda Sum Rule (ISR) exhausted by 50%; with a 6-level basis including spin-orbit partners, M0ν = 4.1 with ISR 100% fulfilled; with a 9-level basis, M0ν = 4.3.
- For 128Te → 128Xe with a 5-level QRPA basis, M0ν = 1.4 with ISR 60% exhausted; with a 7-level basis including spin-orbit partners, M0ν = 3.4 with ISR 100% fulfilled; with a 13-level basis, M0ν = 3.8.
- For 130Te → 130Xe with a 5-level QRPA basis, M0ν = 1.3; with a 7-level basis, M0ν = 3.2; with a 13-level basis, M0ν = 3.8.
- For 76Ge → 76Se with a 9-level QRPA basis, M0ν = 5.0 with ISR 100% fulfilled.
- In the QRPA ground state of 76Ge (9-level basis), the seniority composition is: s=0 (55.0%), s=4 (32.9%), s=8 (9.8%), s=12 (2.0%), s=16 (0.3%).
- For 128Te (13-level basis), the QRPA ground state composition is: s=0 (51.5%), s=4 (34.2%), s=8 (11.3%), s=12 (2.5%), s=16 (0.4%).
- The SM results for 82Se with 4 levels give M0ν = 2.0; with 5 levels including 1f7/2, M0ν = 2.5.
- The SM results for 128Te with 5 levels give M0ν = 2.2.

## Context
This work extends comparisons between QRPA (Rodin et al., 2005; Simkovic et al., 2008) and SM (Caurier et al., 2008; Poves et al., 2009) approaches to 0νββ matrix elements, specifically analyzing the angular momentum and seniority structure of the transforming neutron pairs. The authors show that the HFB approach (Rath et al., 2008; Tomoda et al., 1991) and IBM (Barea and Iachello, 2009) have more restrictive angular momentum content than QRPA and SM, which may affect the reliability of their matrix elements. The results enable a better understanding of systematic uncertainties in 0νββ matrix element calculations and highlight the importance of including spin-orbit partners to satisfy the Ikeda Sum Rule (Ikeda, 1964) for reliable predictions.
