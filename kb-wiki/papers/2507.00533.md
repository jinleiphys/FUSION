# Gravitational photon echo

> The generation, controls, and storage of the gravitationally induced photon echo using the 8.4 eV Thorium-229 nuclear clock transition on Earth are theoretically investigated. With its exceptionally narrow linewidth of approximately 1 mHz and high quality factor in the order of $10^{19}$, the Thorium-229 clock transition allows for the potential detection of gravitational-redshift effects at millimeter-scale altitude variations. Moreover, the 1740-second half lifetime of the Thorium-229 isomeric state allows for the slow movement of the system within its coherence time. Along this line, we explore the generation, controls, and storage of photon echo arising from a rotation induced inversion of the gravitational frequency shift in a single target or in multiple equally spaced samples. Our approach lays the foundation for the controllable gravitational quantum optics on Earth.

# Gravitational photon echo

## Key claim

The paper theoretically demonstrates that the 8.4 eV Thorium-229 nuclear clock transition, with its 1 mHz linewidth and 1740-second isomeric half-life, can generate a gravitationally induced photon echo on Earth through rotation-induced inversion of the gravitational frequency shift, but only under the condition that the system's coherence time (630 s in Th:CaF2 crystals) exceeds the control duration and that the vertical target thickness or spacing is optimized to match the gravitational redshift gradient.

## Method

The authors model the coupling between vacuum ultraviolet (VUV) light and two-level Thorium-229 nuclei (ground state and isomeric state) using optical Bloch equations in the perturbative regime, incorporating gravitational detuning that depends on altitude and rotation angle. They numerically solve these coupled equations for single-target systems (gravitational gradient photon echo, GGPE) and for arrays of multiple equally spaced targets (gravitational frequency comb, GFC), simulating rotations by polar angles of π/2 and π to control the rephasing and storage of nuclear polarization.

## Key numbers

- Thorium-229 clock transition energy: 8.4 eV
- Natural linewidth of the nuclear clock transition: Γ₀ = ln2/1740 rad·Hz (approximately 0.4 mHz)
- Quality factor of the Thorium-229 transition: approximately 10¹⁹
- Half-life of the Thorium-229 isomeric state: 1740 seconds
- Enhanced decoherence rate in Th:CaF₂ crystal: γ = ln2/630 rad·Hz, corresponding to a coherence time of 630 s
- Single-target GGPE simulation parameters: optical depth ξ = 386, target thickness L = 4.8 cm, input pulse duration τₛ = 100 s
- Two-target system parameters: optical depth ξ = 38.6, target thickness L = 1 mm, target separation 8.8 cm, initial detunings Δ₁ = 106.6Γ₀ and Δ₂ = -106.6Γ₀
- Five-target GFC system: target thickness L = 0.1 mm, target spacing 8 cm, initial altitudes z₁ = -16 cm, z₂ = -8 cm, z₃ = 0, z₄ = 8 cm, z₅ = 16 cm, with corresponding detunings Δ₁ = 387.6Γ₀, Δ₂ = 193.8Γ₀, Δ₃ = 0, Δ₄ = -193.8Γ₀, Δ₅ = -387.6Γ₀
- First GPE echo efficiency without rotation: R₁ = 46.44% with fidelity F₁ = 99.4% at delay time τ₁ = 53.8 s
- First GPE echo efficiency with full inversion: R₁ = 55.7% with fidelity F₁ = 98.8% at delay time τ₁ = 39.5 s
- First GPE echo efficiency with back-and-forth π/2 rotation: R₁ = 27% with fidelity F₁ = 93.6% at delay time τ₁ = 83.4 s
- First GPE echo efficiency with 0→π/2→π rotation: R₁ = 37% with fidelity F₁ = 97.2% at delay time τ₁ = 69 s
- Critical speed for transverse Doppler shift to equal gravitational redshift at z₅ = 16 cm: v_c = 1.77 m/s

## Context

This work extends the concept of gravitational redshift detection from passive observations, such as the Pound-Rebka experiment (Pound 1959) and modern atomic clock comparisons (Chou 2010, Bothwell 2022), toward active control of coherent quantum systems using the Thorium-229 nuclear clock transition proposed by Peik (2003) and recently realized in crystal hosts (Tiedau 2024). The gravitational gradient photon echo mechanism builds on gradient echo memory techniques developed by Hetet (2008) and Hosseini (2009), while the gravitational frequency comb approach extends spectral hole burning and frequency comb methods from Afzelius (2009) and Zhang (2019). The results enable potential applications in relativistic geodesy (Flury 2016) with millimeter-scale resolution and controllable gravitational quantum optics on Earth.
