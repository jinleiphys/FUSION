# Strangeness production in heavy-ion collisions

> A study of the "horn" in the particle ratio $K^+/ŽĆ^+$ for central heavy-ion collisions as a function of the collision energy $\sqrt{s}$ is presented. We analyse two different interpretations: the onset of deconfinement and the transition from a baryon- to a meson-dominated hadron gas. We use a realistic equation of state (EOS), which includes both hadron and quark degrees-of-freedom. The Taub-adiabate procedure is followed to determine the system at the early stage. Our results do not support an explanation of the horn as due to the onset of deconfinement. Using only hadronic EOS we reproduced the energy dependence of the $K^+/ŽĆ^+$ and $╬ø/ŽĆ^-$ ratios employing an experimental parametrisation of the freeze-out curve. We observe a transition between a baryon- and a meson-dominated regime; however, the reproduction of the $K^+/ŽĆ^+$ and $╬ø/ŽĆ^-$ ratios as a function of $\sqrt{s}$ is not completely satisfying. We finally propose a new idea for the interpretation of the data, the roll-over scheme, in which the scalar meson field $Žā$ has not reached the thermal equilibrium at freeze-out. The rool-over scheme for the equilibration of the $Žā$-field is based on the inflation mechanism. The non-equilibrium evolution of the scalar field influences the particle production, e.g. $K^+/ŽĆ^+$, however, the fixing of the free parameters in this model is still an open issue.

## Key claim
The "horn" structure in the \(K^+/\pi^+\) ratio at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} \approx 7\) GeV is not explained by the onset of deconfinement when using a realistic equation of state that includes both hadron and quark degrees of freedom; instead, a transition from a baryon-dominated to a meson-dominated hadron gas along the freeze-out curve provides a partial explanation, but the reproduction of the \(K^+/\pi^+\) ratio remains not completely satisfying.

## Method
The authors used a realistic equation of state (EOS) based on a relativistic mean-field Walecka model for hadrons (including baryon octet, \(\Delta\)-isobars, and mesons) and the MIT bag model for quarks, with a mixed phase constructed via Gibbs conditions for three conserved charges (baryon number, electric charge, strangeness). They applied the Taub-adiabate procedure to determine early-stage conditions and computed particle ratios along an experimental parametrization of the freeze-out curve. Additionally, they introduced a "roll-over" scheme where the scalar \(\sigma\) field evolves dynamically via an equation of motion with a friction term, analogous to inflation, to study non-equilibrium effects on particle production.

## Key numbers
- The "horn" in \(K^+/\pi^+\) appears at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} \approx 7\) GeV (from data cited in the paper).
- The mixed phase in the EOS ends at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} \sim 3\) GeV, not at the horn energy.
- The freeze-out temperature saturates at \(T \simeq 160\) MeV for \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} \sim 50\) GeV.
- The transition from baryon-dominated to meson-dominated regimes occurs at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} \lesssim 5\) GeV.
- The maximum value of \(\Lambda/\pi^-\) obtained is \(\sim 0.16\), comparable to the higher experimental point.
- The decay contributions suppress the \(K^+/\pi^+\) ratio by nearly \(10\%\).
- In the roll-over scheme, the parameters used are \(\tau = 2\) fm, \(\mu_1 = 750\) MeV, \(\mu_2 = 550\) MeV, and \(T = 156\) MeV; the field oscillates until \(t \sim 6\) fm before reaching equilibrium within \(\sim 10\%\) error.

## Context
This work extends the debate on the origin of the horn structure in \(K^+/\pi^+\) ratios, previously attributed to the onset of deconfinement by Gazdzicki and Gorenstein (1998) via the Statistical Model of the Early Stage (SMES), or to a baryon-to-meson transition in Hadron Resonance Gas models by Andronic et al. (2009) and Cleymans et al. (2004). The authors find that their realistic EOS does not support the deconfinement interpretation, and while the hadronic approach partially reproduces \(\Lambda/\pi^-\) data, it fails for \(K^+/\pi^+\). The proposed roll-over scheme, inspired by inflation mechanisms, offers a new direction for studying non-equilibrium effects in heavy-ion collisions, but the fixing of free parameters (initial velocity, expansion rate) remains an open issue.
