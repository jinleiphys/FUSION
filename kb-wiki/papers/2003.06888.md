# Exploring nuclear exotica at the limits

> The study of nuclear limits has been performed and new physical mechanisms and exotic shapes allowing the extension of nuclear landscape beyond the commonly accepted boundaries have been established. The transition from ellipsoidal to toroidal shapes plays a critical role in potential extension of nuclear landscape to hyperheavy nuclei. Rotational excitations leading to the birth of particle (proton or neutron) bound rotational bands provide a mechanism for an extension of nuclear landscape beyond spin zero proton and neutron drip lines.

# Exploring nuclear exotica at the limits

## Key claim
The transition from ellipsoidal to toroidal shapes in hyperheavy nuclei (Z ≥ 126) can substantially expand the nuclear landscape beyond the commonly accepted boundaries, and rotational excitations can create particle-bound rotational bands that extend the landscape beyond the spin-zero proton and neutron drip lines, but these extensions depend on specific conditions such as the magnitude of Coulomb repulsion and the high-j content of intruder orbitals.

## Method
The authors performed systematic axial relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations using the DD-PC1 covariant density functional, supplemented by triaxial RHB calculations for ground states and fission barriers of selected nuclei. For rotating nuclei near the drip lines, they employed the cranked relativistic mean field (CRMF) approach without pairing, analyzing single-particle routhians as functions of rotational frequency to identify transitions from particle-unbound to particle-bound configurations.

## Key numbers
- The two-neutron drip line for toroidal shapes in hyperheavy nuclei is close to the extrapolation of the ellipsoidal two-neutron drip line, but the proton-rich side shows substantial expansion: the area between the toroidal and ellipsoidal two-proton drip lines in Fig. 1 represents the extension.
- In the nucleus ³²⁴₁₃₈, the Fermi level for the lowest-energy toroidal minimum (β₂ ≈ -4.0) is at E_F ≈ -3.5 MeV, making this state particle bound, whereas ellipsoidal-like shapes have positive Fermi energies and are unstable against proton emission.
- For the toroidal nucleus ³⁵⁴₁₃₄, the saddle point of the fission barrier with respect to sausage deformations (λ=2) is at 4.4 MeV; for ³⁴⁸₁₃₈, it is at 8.54 MeV.
- In ⁴⁶Mg, the [3,0] configuration becomes particle bound at rotational frequency Ω_x = 1.03 MeV (I ≈ 16 ħ), where the 3/2[431](r=-i) orbital dives below zero energy.
- Rotational excitations allow extension of the Mg isotope landscape by four neutrons beyond the spin-zero neutron drip line (⁴⁸,⁵⁰Mg become bound at non-zero spin).
- In ⁴⁸Ni, the [0,2] configuration becomes proton bound at Ω_x = 2.04 MeV (I ≈ 26 ħ), where the 1/2[440](i=±) intruder orbitals dive into negative energy.
- For the Z=134 nucleus ³⁹²₁₃₄, axial RHB calculations give fission barriers of 10.24 MeV (ground state) and 7.55 MeV (excited state), but inclusion of triaxiality reduces these to 0.56 MeV and 2.08 MeV, respectively.

## Context
This work extends the authors' prior systematic studies of nuclear landscape limits using covariant density functional theory (Afanasjev et al., 2013, 2014, 2016) and their earlier investigations of hyperheavy nuclei (Afanasjev et al., 2018; Afanasjev et al., 2019). The discovery of rotational excitation mechanisms for extending nuclear landscape beyond spin-zero limits (Afanasjev et al., 2019) is applied here to both neutron-rich and proton-rich regions. The results contradict the traditional view that the nuclear landscape is bounded by the spin-zero drip lines, and they enable future experimental searches for toroidal hyperheavy nuclei and high-spin particle-bound rotational bands in exotic isotopes. The work also contrasts with non-relativistic Skyrme DFT calculations (Erler et al., 2012) where statistical and systematic uncertainties are comparable at nuclear limits, whereas in CDFT systematic uncertainties dominate.
