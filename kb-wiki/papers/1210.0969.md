# A Possible Higher Order Correction to the chiral Vortical Conductivity in a Gauge Field Plasma

> The two loop contributions to the chiral vortical conductivity are considered. The Kubo formula together with the anomalous Ward identity of the axial vector current suggest that there may be a nonzero correction to the coefficient of the $T^2$ term of the conductivity.

## Key claim
The two-loop contribution to the chiral vortical conductivity \(\sigma_5^V\) may produce a nonzero correction to the coefficient of the \(T^2\) term, contrary to earlier suggestions of nonrenormalization. The authors identify a specific diagram (Fig. 1(a)) not covered by the Coleman-Hill-like argument of Golkar et al., and compute its contribution to find a coupling-dependent correction.

## Method
The authors use the Kubo formula for the chiral vortical conductivity, expressed as a correlator between the axial current density and the energy flux density in thermal equilibrium. They evaluate the two-loop thermal diagrams in QED (and generalize to QCD) by applying the anomalous Ward identity to extract the anomaly contribution from the triangle diagram, then compute the resulting momentum integrals using dimensional regularization and Pauli-Villars regularization.

## Key numbers
- The two-loop correction to the chiral vortical conductivity in QED is \(\sigma_5^{V(2)} = \frac{e_0^2}{48\pi^2} T^2\).
- The coefficient \(c\) in the general expression \(\sigma_5^V = \frac{\mu_5^2}{2\pi^2} + c T^2\) becomes \(c = \frac{1}{12} + \frac{e_0^2}{48\pi^2}\) at two loops in QED.
- For QCD with \(N_c\) colors and \(N_f\) flavors, the coefficient generalizes to \(c = \frac{1}{12} + \frac{N_c^2-1}{2N_c} \frac{g_0^2}{48\pi^2}\).

## Context
This work extends the one-loop calculation of Landsteiner et al. (2008), which found \(c = 1/12\) from the Kubo formula, and challenges the nonrenormalization argument of Golkar et al. (2012) based on a generalized Coleman-Hill theorem. The result suggests that the chiral vortical conductivity receives coupling-dependent corrections, unlike the chiral magnetic conductivity, and opens the question of whether such corrections survive in the strong-coupling limit accessible via holographic methods. The authors note that a lattice calculation at \(\mu_5 = 0\) could provide a nonperturbative check.
