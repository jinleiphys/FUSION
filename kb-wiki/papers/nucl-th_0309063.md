# Isoscaling bearing information on the nuclear caloric curve

> We show that the qualitative behavior of the nuclear caloric curve can be inferred from the energy dependence of the isoscaling parameters. Since there are strong indications that the latter are not distorted by the secondary decay of primary hot fragments, in contrast to other observables, this suggests that valuable information on the nuclear caloric curve may be obtained through the analysis presented in this work.

## Key claim

The qualitative shape of the nuclear caloric curve (whether it rises monotonically or exhibits a plateau) can be inferred from the energy dependence of the isoscaling parameter α, because isoscaling parameters are robust against distortions from secondary decay of primary hot fragments, unlike other temperature observables.

## Method

The authors used the improved statistical multifragmentation model (ISMM) to simulate the decay of thermally equilibrated sources with different isospin (A₀=168, Z₀=75 for a proton-rich source; A₀=186, Z₀=75 for a neutron-rich source) at various excitation energies. They calculated the isoscaling parameter α from the primary fragment yields under two different assumptions for the breakup density: fixed density (breakup volume constant at 6 times normal density) and variable density (multiplicity-dependent breakup volume). They also compared microcanonical and grand-canonical ensemble predictions to verify the robustness of the qualitative behavior.

## Key numbers

- The breakup volume in the fixed-density scenario was kept constant at 6 times the normal density value.
- A plateau in the caloric curve was observed approximately between 3.5 and 7 MeV/A excitation energy per nucleon when the breakup volume was allowed to be multiplicity dependent.
- Changes in 1/α due to secondary decay of primordial fragments were of the order of 5% at E*/A = 3, 6, and 9 MeV.
- A billion events were generated for each excitation energy to suppress statistical fluctuations.
- The total binding energy deviations from empirical values in simple liquid drop mass formulae can be as large as 40 MeV for heavy nuclei and around 10 MeV for light fragments.

## Context

This work extends the study of isoscaling, previously observed in nuclear reactions by Xu (2000), Tsang (2001), Botvina (2002), Shetty (2003), and Souliotis (2003), by connecting it to the nuclear caloric curve debate. It contradicts an earlier study by Botvina (2002) that predicted a larger isospin dependence in the plateau region, attributing the discrepancy to the use of empirical binding energies rather than simple liquid drop mass formulae. The analysis enables experimental determination of the caloric curve's qualitative shape without the distortions from secondary decay that affect other temperature measurement methods, such as those based on excited-state populations or isotope yield ratios.
