# BBN And The CBR Probe The Early Universe

> Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) and the Cosmic Background Radiation (CBR) provide complementary probes of the early evolution of the Universe and of its particle content. Neutrinos play important roles in both cases, influencing the primordial abundances of the nuclides produced by BBN during the first 20 minutes, as well as the spectrum of temperature fluctuations imprinted on the CBR when the Universe is some 400 thousand years old. The physical effects relevant at these widely separated epochs are reviewed and the theoretical predictions are compared with observational data to explore the consistency of the standard models of cosmology and particle physics and to constrain beyond-the-standard-model physics and cosmology.

## Key claim

Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) and the Cosmic Background Radiation (CBR) provide complementary probes of the early Universe, and their combined predictions for the baryon density and expansion rate are consistent with the standard models of cosmology and particle physics, but only if deviations from the standard expansion rate are parameterized by an equivalent number of neutrinos (Delta N_nu) that is not necessarily related to extra or fewer neutrinos.

## Method

The paper reviews the physical effects of neutrinos on BBN during the first 20 minutes and on CBR temperature fluctuations at approximately 400,000 years, comparing theoretical predictions with observational data. It uses the baryon-to-photon ratio (eta_10 = 10^10 n_B/n_gamma) and the expansion rate parameter S = H'/H (quantified by Delta N_nu = N_nu - 3) to test consistency between the standard models of cosmology and particle physics and to constrain beyond-the-standard-model physics.

## Key numbers

- The neutron-proton mass difference is Delta m = 1.29 MeV, which controls the equilibrium n/p ratio.
- The n/p ratio decreases from approximately 1/6 at "freeze-out" to approximately 1/7 when BBN begins at about 200 seconds (T approximately 80 keV).
- The baryon-to-photon ratio is related to the baryon density by eta_10 = 274 Omega_B h^2.
- The CBR temperature at present is T = 2.725 K.
- Prior to e+ e- annihilation, the expansion rate parameter S is related to Delta N_nu by S = (1 + 7 Delta N_nu / 43)^(1/2).
- The BBN-predicted deuterium abundance scales as D/H proportional to eta_10^(-1.6).

## Context

This review extends the standard cosmological framework by comparing BBN and CBR constraints on baryon density and expansion rate, building on earlier work by the author (Steigman, recent review article cited as "bbnrev"). It enables future constraints on beyond-the-standard-model physics by showing that any deviation from the standard expansion rate can be parameterized as an effective number of neutrino species, and that consistency between BBN and CBR observations tests the standard models of cosmology and particle physics.
