# Dissipation near the QCD phase transition

> We set up a framework for field theoretical studies of systems out of thermal equilibrium and zoom in on the dissipation of disoriented chiral condensates. Short relaxation times are obtained in the phase transition region, jeopardizing the definiteness of a DCC signal.

# Dissipation near the QCD phase transition

## Key claim

Short relaxation times for disoriented chiral condensates (DCCs) are obtained in the phase transition region, making the observation of a possible DCC signal questionable. The result depends on the assumption that the system is slightly out of equilibrium and that linear response theory can be used to determine the heat bath response to the condensate.

## Method

The author decomposes the chiral field into a non-equilibrium condensate (soft modes with momentum below a cutoff scale Λ_c) and hard thermal fluctuations (modes with momentum above Λ_c) that constitute a heat bath. Classical effective equations for the non-thermal condensate are derived, with the effect of hard modes treated perturbatively and improved with resummation, using linear response theory to determine the heat bath response. The damping rates are computed from one-loop self-consistent numerical solutions for meson masses and the equilibrium condensate, considering processes such as π_s π_f → σ_f annihilation and π_s π_f → π_f π_f scattering.

## Key numbers

- At T ≃ 170 MeV, the damping from π_s π_f → σ_f annihilation is about 55% of the condensate's energy.
- At T = 200 MeV, the relaxation time of homogeneous condensates is t = 0.17 fm/c.
- At T = 235 MeV, the relaxation time is t = 1.36 fm/c.
- The dissipation from π_s π_f → σ_f annihilation reaches a maximum at T = 200 MeV, where the process becomes kinematically forbidden.

## Context

This work extends earlier estimates of DCC lifetimes by Biro (predicted 1 fm/c) and Steele (predicted 4-7 fm/c), finding significantly shorter relaxation times that make DCC signals questionable. The framework provides a consistent semi-classical description of the chiral condensate out of thermal equilibrium, enabling future studies of non-equilibrium dynamics in heavy ion collisions at RHIC. The results contradict the original quench scenario of Rajagopal and Wilczek, which assumed long-wavelength chiral fields completely decouple from the thermal background at the critical temperature.
