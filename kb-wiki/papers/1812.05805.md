# Superheavy Elements: Beyond the 7th Period in the Periodic Table

> What is the heaviest element? In order to address this question, the elements up to $Z=118$ have been synthesized by now by using heavy-ion fusion reactions. This has completed the 7th period in the periodic table of elements, and new attempts have been commenced in aiming at syntheses of the elements in the 8th period. In this article, we review the current status and future challenges in the research field of superheavy elements, putting some emphasis on perspectives from the nuclear reaction theory.

# Superheavy Elements: Beyond the 7th Period in the Periodic Table

## Key claim
The paper reviews the current status of superheavy element synthesis, arguing that while the 7th period of the periodic table is complete up to Z=118, going beyond to Z=119 and 120 requires heavier projectiles than ⁴⁸Ca (such as ⁵⁰Ti, ⁵¹V, or ⁵⁴Cr) because the necessary Es and Fm targets are unavailable, and the net effect on evaporation residue cross sections from using these heavier projectiles remains uncertain due to competing effects of friction, excitation energy, and asymmetry.

## Method
The paper reviews heavy-ion fusion reactions for superheavy element synthesis, describing the three-stage process: overcoming the Coulomb barrier to reach the touching configuration, diffusion through the fission saddle point to form a compound nucleus (described by Langevin dynamics or the dinuclear system model), and statistical decay of the compound nucleus with competition between fission and neutron evaporation. The author discusses quasi-elastic barrier distribution measurements for deformed targets (specifically the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm system) and compares hot fusion (using ⁴⁸Ca projectile) with cold fusion (using ²⁰⁸Pb or ²⁰⁹Bi targets) strategies.

## Key numbers
- Evaporation residue cross sections for the ⁴⁸Ca+²³⁸U reaction forming Cn (Z=112) are smaller than capture cross sections by about 11 orders of magnitude.
- For cold fusion reactions, cross sections drop rapidly as a function of Z of the compound nucleus; for hot fusion reactions, cross sections remain relatively large between Z=113 and 118.
- The heaviest Fl element (Z=114) synthesized so far is ²⁸⁹₁₇₅Fl, synthesized using the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁴Pu hot fusion reaction; 9 more neutrons are needed to reach the predicted magic number N=184.
- The predicted proton and neutron magic numbers for the island of stability are Z=114 and N=184, with modern Hartree-Fock calculations also predicting (Z,N)=(114,184), (120,172), and (126,184) as candidates for the next double magic nucleus beyond ²⁰⁸Pb.

## Context
This review extends the line of work on superheavy element synthesis that began in the 1950s, covering the completion of the 7th period with the addition of elements Z=113 (Nihonium), Z=115 (Moscovium), Z=117 (Tennessine), and Z=118 (Oganesson) to the periodic table in 2016. The paper builds on the cold fusion work of Hofmann (2000) and Hamilton (2013) and the hot fusion work of Oganessian (2004, 2013, 2015), and discusses the recent quasi-elastic barrier distribution measurement by Tanaka et al. (2018) for the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm system. The review enables future experimental campaigns toward Z=119 and 120 (already started at RIKEN using the ⁵¹V+²⁴⁸Cm reaction) and theoretical development of microscopic dynamical theories that can describe the heat-up process from the approaching phase to compound nucleus formation consistently.
