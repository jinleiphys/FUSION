# Analytic multi-Baryonic solutions in the SU(N)-Skyrme model at finite density

> We construct explicit analytic solutions of the $SU(N)$-Skyrme model (for generic $N$) suitable to describe different phases of nuclear pasta at finite volume in $(3+1)$ dimensions. The first type are crystals of Baryonic tubes (nuclear spaghetti) while the second type are smooth Baryonic layers (nuclear lasagna). Both, the ansatz for the spaghetti and the ansatz for the lasagna phases, reduce the complete set of Skyrme field equations to just one integrable equation for the profile within sectors of arbitrary high topological charge. We compute explicitly the total energy of both configurations in terms of the flavor number, the density and the Baryonic charge. Remarkably, our analytic results allow to compare explicitly the physical properties of nuclear spaghetti and lasagna phases. Our construction shows explicitly that, at lower densities, configurations with $N=2$ light flavors are favored while, at higher densities, configurations with $N=3$ are favored. Our construction also proves that in the high density regime (but still well within the range of validity of the Skyrme model) the lasagna configurations are favored while at low density the spaghetti configurations are favored. Moreover, the integrability property of the present configurations is not spoiled by the inclusion of the subleading corrections to the Skyrme model arising in the 't Hooft expansion. Finally, we briefly discuss the large $N$ limit of our configurations.

## Key claim

The paper constructs explicit analytic solutions of the SU(N)-Skyrme model for generic N that describe nuclear spaghetti and lasagna phases, claiming that at lower densities configurations with N=2 light flavors are favored while at higher densities N=3 configurations are favored, and that lasagna configurations are favored at high density while spaghetti configurations are favored at low density, but these conclusions depend on the specific choice of coupling constants and the assumption that the Skyrme model remains valid in the density regimes considered.

## Method

The authors reduce the full SU(N)-Skyrme field equations to a single integrable ordinary differential equation for the profile function by using a generalized Euler angle parameterization of the SU(N) group and imposing specific ansatze for the spaghetti and lasagna phases. For the spaghetti phase, the ansatz uses coordinates (r, θ, φ) with χ = χ(r), Θ = qθ, and Φ = p(t/L_φ − φ), while for the lasagna phase the field is expressed as U_L = e^{σΦk} e^{h(r)} e^{mθk} with a specific matrix k. The resulting profile equation is solved analytically in terms of generalized elliptic integrals, and the total energy is computed as a function of flavor number N, density, and baryonic charge B.

## Key numbers

- Baryonic charge for spaghetti phase: B = 2 n p N(N²−1)/12, where n is an integer from boundary conditions and p is a parameter related to baryon number per unit length
- Energy density formula: ℰ = (K/4) × [N(N²−1)p/(12 L_r L_θ L_φ²)] × (ρ₀ + 2 sin²(qθ) ρ₁), with explicit expressions for ρ₀ and ρ₁ given in terms of the profile χ and its derivative
- The profile equation (Eq. dchi) contains the combination q²λ/L_θ² and the constant E₀ must satisfy E₀ > 2L_r²q²/L_θ² for stability
- For numerical computations, the authors set K=2 and λ=1, with density defined as ρ = 1/(2πL)³ for a cubic box with L_r = L_θ = L_φ ≡ L

## Context

This work extends previous analytic constructions of baryonic tube and layer solutions in the SU(2)-Skyrme model (Crystal1 2015, Crystal2 2016, Crystal3 2017, Firstube 2018, Firsttube2 2019) to the SU(N) case for generic N, enabling the study of nuclear pasta phases with more than two flavors. The results enable direct analytic comparison of spaghetti and lasagna phase energies, which previously required challenging numerical simulations (Pasta1 2005, Pasta2 2008, Pasta10 2020). The authors note that the integrability property persists when subleading corrections from the 't Hooft expansion are included, based on results in Crystal3 (2017).
