# Automated Discovery of Jet Substructure Analyses

> The study of the substructure of collimated particles from quarks and gluons, or jets, has the promise to reveal the details how color charges interact with the QCD plasma medium created in colliders such as RHIC and the LHC. Traditional jet substructure observables have been constructed using expert knowledge, and are largely transplanted, unmodified, from the high-energy physics, where the goal is primarily the study of boosted hadronic decays. A novel neural network architecture is described that is capable of examining theoretical models, and constructs, on its own, an analysis procedure that is sensitive to the internal model features. This architecture, in combination with symbolic regression, further allows the extraction of closed-form algebraic expressions from the learned result -- enabling the automatically constructed jet substructure analysis to be subsequently understood and reproduced by humans. This system is then tasked to construct an analysis that infers the plasma temperature from observing jets, which is demonstrated using both JEWEL and the Linearized Boltzmann Transport model, and at the presence of a realistic remnant of the plasma, or underlying event, that the measurement has to overcome. In a demonstration how algorithms can produce original research in direct competition to human experts, the resulting jet substructure variables and analyses are capable of determining the initial temperature of the plasma medium from analyzing 1200--2500 jets, a performance not seen in existing, manually designed analyses. Comparison of an incidentally discovered observable with the existing literature further indicates that the system described is capable of examining the model phase spaces to a detail at least comparable to the current field of human experts.

## Key claim
The paper claims that a neural network architecture combined with symbolic regression can automatically discover jet substructure analyses that infer the initial temperature of the quark-gluon plasma from analyzing 1200-2500 jets, achieving a performance not seen in existing manually designed analyses. The hedge is that this demonstration is limited to two specific Monte Carlo models (JEWEL and Linearized Boltzmann Transport) and includes a realistic underlying event background that must be overcome.

## Method
The neural network processes energy flow polynomials (EFPs) computed from reconstructed jets in Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV, using a convolutional feature extraction layer followed by permutation symmetrization and dense statistical analysis layers. The network is trained to discriminate between different initial plasma temperatures using pseudoexperiments containing 1200-2500 jet events each, with regularization via an input complexity metric. After training, symbolic regression (ffx algorithm) extracts closed-form algebraic expressions approximating the learned analysis functions.

## Key numbers
- Jet reconstruction uses anti-kT algorithm with distance parameter D = 0.4, selecting jets with 100 < p_T,J < 500 GeV/c and |eta_J| < 1.4
- JEWEL training: 65 pseudoexperiments with 2500 jet-events each, achieving accuracy 0.973 +/- 0.013 (68% Jeffreys interval) after 1000 epochs with regularization parameters mu_c = mu_d = 0.01
- LBT training: 50 pseudoexperiments with 1200 jet-events each, achieving validation accuracy 0.975 +/- 0.014 with regularization mu_1 = mu_2 = 30
- JEWEL initial temperature range: T_i between 0.16 and 0.76 GeV/k, with initial time tau_i varied between 0.2 and 0.8 fm/c
- LBT integration constant hat_T_0 varied between 0.383 and 0.583 GeV/k
- 489 primitive energy flow polynomials with degree 1 through 7 are used as input features
- JEWEL symbolic regression analysis: d_1,SR = 8.99 - 0.176 N <c_16> - 0.175 N <c_1> with NMSE 10.9% (vs 28.3% for constant)
- LBT symbolic regression analysis: d_1,SR = 4.49 - 0.318 N <c_13> - 0.00653 N <c_13>^2 with NMSE 1.17% (vs 36.9% for constant)

## Context
This work extends automated scientific discovery methods beyond previous approaches that required domain-specific human knowledge as input, such as those by Zytkow (1990), King (2004), and others. The paper directly contrasts with existing jet substructure analyses in heavy ion physics (Mehtar-Tani 2017, Kunnawalkam Elayavalli 2017) that reuse variables developed for boosted object tagging in high-energy physics and show only moderate sensitivity to heavy ion collisions versus proton-proton baselines. The demonstrated capability to discover novel substructure variables that are sensitive to specific plasma model features enables future automated searches for observables sensitive to other quark-gluon plasma properties, and the extracted closed-form expressions allow human understanding and reproduction of the machine-discovered analyses.
