# Cosmology and Fundamental Physics and their Laboratory Astrophysics Connections

> The Decadal Survey of Astronomy and Astrophysics created five panels to identify the science themes that would define the field's research frontiers in the coming decade. I will describe the conclusions of one of these, the Panel on Cosmology and Fundamental Physics, and comment on their relevance to the discussions at this meeting of the NASA Laboratory Astrophysics community.

# Cosmology and Fundamental Physics and their Laboratory Astrophysics Connections

## Key claim
The Panel on Cosmology and Fundamental Physics identified four "big questions" for the coming decade, the mechanism behind inflation, the nature of dark energy, the identity of dark matter, and the properties of neutrinos, and argued that answering them will require an unconventional partnership where the universe itself serves as a laboratory for precision particle physics, complementing terrestrial experiments. The abstract's framing as a summary of panel conclusions glosses over the paper's specific emphasis that laboratory astrophysics in this context extends far beyond traditional nuclear cross-section measurements to include major accelerator facilities, underground detectors, and cosmological surveys.

## Method
The author, a member of the Panel on Cosmology and Fundamental Physics convened by the Decadal Survey of Astronomy and Astrophysics, summarizes the panel's conclusions based on community input provided as white papers on topics including the early universe, CMB, large-scale structure, cosmological parameters, dark matter, dark energy, tests of gravity, astrophysical measurements of physical constants, and astronomical messengers (neutrinos, gamma rays, cosmic rays). The paper then focuses on two of the four big questions, dark matter and neutrino properties, and discusses their specific connections to laboratory astrophysics and NASA missions.

## Key numbers
- Baryons comprise about 4% of the closure density Ω_c, as confirmed by CMB and Big Bang nucleosynthesis.
- WIMP annihilation cross sections naturally lead to the expectation that Ω_WIMP ~ 0.1.
- Representative WIMP spin-independent cross sections are σ_SI ~ 10^{-45} cm^2; current detectors in the ~10-100 kg range probe below 10^{-43} cm^2 for WIMP masses of ~100 GeV; next-generation 1-10 ton detectors aim for σ_SI ~ 10^{-47} cm^2.
- The right-handed Majorana mass needed to explain neutrino masses is M_R ~ 0.5 × 10^{15} GeV, close to the ~10^{16} GeV grand unified mass scale.
- The critical wave number for neutrino free-streaming effects on large-scale structure is k_free streaming ~ 0.004 √(m_ν/0.05 eV) Mpc^{-1}.
- Current best limits on neutrino mass correspond to 1.3% of Ω_c; detecting effects from m_ν ~ 0.05 eV requires sensitivity to dark matter at 0.1% of Ω_c.
- In core collapse supernovae, neutrinos decouple at density ~10^{11} g/cm^3 and encounter critical density at ~10^4 g/cm^3.
- The third mixing angle θ_13 is bounded only by reactor experiments; next-generation terrestrial experiments have a sensitivity goal of θ_13 ≳ 10^{-2}, while supernova effects require θ_13 ≳ 10^{-4}.

## Context
This paper extends the work of the Decadal Survey's Panel on Cosmology and Fundamental Physics (chaired by Spergel) and connects it specifically to the NASA Laboratory Astrophysics community. It builds on prior findings including the development of the Lambda Cold Dark Matter model, CMB confirmation of inflation predictions, supernova evidence for dark energy, and neutrino oscillation discoveries from Super-Kamiokande and SNO. The paper enables future coordination between laboratory experiments (LHC, underground detectors, neutrino beamlines on the 10^2-10^3 kiloton scale) and cosmological surveys (21-cm radio telescopes with ~0.1 km collecting areas, weak lensing studies) to jointly resolve questions of dark matter identity, neutrino mass hierarchy, and CP violation in the neutrino sector.
