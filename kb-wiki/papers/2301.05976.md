# Quantum Simulations in Effective Model Spaces (I): Hamiltonian Learning-VQE using Digital Quantum Computers and Application to the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick Model

> The utility of effective model spaces in quantum simulations of non-relativistic quantum many-body systems is explored in the context of the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model of interacting fermions. We introduce an iterative hybrid-classical-quantum algorithm, Hamiltonian learning variational quantum eigensolver (HL-VQE), that simultaneously optimizes an effective Hamiltonian, thereby rearranging entanglement into the effective model space, and the associated ground-state wavefunction. HL-VQE is found to provide an exponential improvement in Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model calculations, compared to a naive truncation without Hamiltonian learning, throughout a significant fraction of the Hilbert space. Quantum simulations are performed to demonstrate the HL-VQE algorithm, using an efficient mapping where the number of qubits scales with the $\log$ of the size of the effective model space, rather than the particle number, allowing for the description of large systems with small quantum circuits. Implementations on IBM's QExperience quantum computers and simulators for 1- and 2-qubit effective model spaces are shown to provide accurate and precise results, reproducing classical predictions. This work constitutes a step in the development of entanglement-driven quantum algorithms for the description of nuclear systems, that leverages the potential of noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices.

## Key claim

The Hamiltonian learning variational quantum eigensolver (HL-VQE) algorithm, which simultaneously optimizes an effective Hamiltonian and the associated ground-state wavefunction in a truncated model space, provides an exponential improvement in ground-state energy convergence for the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model compared to naive truncation without Hamiltonian learning, but only throughout a significant fraction of the Hilbert space rather than universally.

## Method

The authors introduce an iterative hybrid classical-quantum algorithm (HL-VQE) that simultaneously optimizes an effective Hamiltonian via orbital rotations parameterized by an angle beta and the ground-state wavefunction coefficients in the truncated model space using the variational quantum eigensolver. The effective model space is constructed by applying a unitary transformation to the single-particle basis that rotates individual quasi-spins around the y-axis, then truncating the many-body basis to include only up to Lambda-1 particle-hole excitations. Quantum simulations are performed on IBM's ibm_lagos quantum computer and the AER simulator using a mapping where the number of qubits scales with the logarithm of the effective model space size rather than the particle number.

## Key numbers

- For the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model with N fermions, the Hamiltonian matrix elements connect configurations with excitation orders differing by zero or two units, as imposed by parity symmetry
- The ground-state energy convergence from HL-VQE shows exponential improvement compared to naive truncation without Hamiltonian learning throughout a significant fraction of the Hilbert space
- Quantum simulations are demonstrated using 1-qubit and 2-qubit effective model spaces on IBM's ibm_lagos quantum processor and the AER simulator
- The number of qubits required scales with the log of the size of the effective model space, rather than the particle number N

## Context

This work extends previous studies of entanglement structures in nuclear systems by Robin (2020) and the application of VQE and ADAPT-VQE to the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model by PhysRevC.104.024305 and PhysRevC.105.064317. The HL-VQE algorithm is similar to the orbital-optimized VQE (oo-VQE) used in quantum chemistry by doi:10.1063/1.5141835 but differs in the truncation employed, more closely resembling the Wilsonian renormalization group organization used in effective field theories. The exponential scaling of counterterms observed suggests possible general applicability to other non-perturbative EFTs and enables more efficient quantum simulations of nuclear many-body systems on NISQ devices.
