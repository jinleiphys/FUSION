# Antisymmetrized molecular dynamics studies for exotic clustering phenomena in neutron-rich nuclei

> We present a review of recent works on clustering phenomena in unstable nuclei studied by antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD). The AMD studies in these decades have uncovered novel types of clustering phenomena brought about by the excess neutrons. Among them, this review focuses on the molecule-like structure of unstable nuclei. One of the earliest discussions on the clustering in unstable nuclei was made for neutron-rich Be and B isotopes. AMD calculations predicted that the ground state clustering is enhanced or reduced depending on the number of excess neutrons. Today, the experiments are confirming this prediction as the change of the proton radii. Behind this enhancement and reduction of the clustering, there are underlying shell effects called molecular- and atomic-orbits. These orbits form covalent and ionic bonding of the clusters analogous to the atomic molecules. It was found that this "molecular-orbit picture" reasonably explains the low-lying spectra of Be isotopes. The molecular-orbit picture is extended to other systems having parity asymmetric cluster cores and to the three cluster systems. O and Ne isotopes are the candidates of the former, while the $3α$ linear chains in C isotopes are the latter. For both subjects, many intensive studies are now in progress. We also pay a special attention to the observables which are the fingerprint of the clustering. In particular, we focus on the monopole and dipole transitions which are recently regarded as good probe for the clustering. We discuss how they have and will reveal the exotic clustering.

## Key claim

This review argues that antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) calculations predict that excess neutrons can either enhance or reduce ground-state clustering in neutron-rich nuclei depending on the isotope, with the effect driven by the formation of molecular and atomic orbits analogous to covalent and ionic bonding in molecules, and that these predictions are now being confirmed by experimental measurements of proton radii.

## Method

The authors employed antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) with a microscopic A-body Hamiltonian using effective nucleon-nucleon interactions (Volkov or modified Volkov central interactions combined with G3RS spin-orbit interactions, and Gogny D1S interaction in some sections). The intrinsic wave function was represented as a Slater determinant of Gaussian wave packets, projected to eigenstates of parity and angular momentum, with variational parameters determined by energy minimization using frictional cooling. Constrained energy minimization and the generator coordinate method (GCM) were used to obtain excited configurations and account for configuration mixing.

## Key numbers

- Proton radii of Be isotopes: relatively large in $^{9}$Be, decreases at $N=6$ for $^{10}$Be, and increases again in $^{11}$Be and $^{12}$Be, consistent with experimental data from isotope shift measurements
- Deformation parameters $\beta_p$ and $\beta_n$ for Be isotopes are smallest at $N=6$ ($^{10}$Be) rather than at $N=8$, indicating migration of the neutron magic number
- For B isotopes, $\beta_p$ and $\beta_n$ are smallest at $N=8$ ($^{13}$B), with enhanced deformation in $^{15}$B and $^{17}$B at $N=10$ and $N=12$, and decrease again in $^{19}$B at $N=14$ due to $d_{5/2}$ sub-shell closure
- Magnetic moments $\mu$ of $^{11-17}$B calculated with AMD reproduce experimental data; deviation from the Schmidt value for the $p_{3/2}$ proton orbit is smallest at $N=8$ for $^{13}$B
- Electric quadrupole moments $Q$ of B isotopes are smallest at $N=8$ for $^{13}$B, with slight increase in $^{15}$B and $^{17}$B

## Context

This review extends the Ikeda threshold rule (Ikeda 1968), which describes cluster states in stable nuclei based on energy and density saturation, to unstable nuclei where these saturation properties are broken. The work builds on earlier discussions of clustering in neutron-rich Be and B isotopes by Seya (1981), von Oertzen (1997, 2004), and Freer (2007), and on the molecular-orbit picture developed for Be isotopes. The AMD results predict that the breakdown of neutron magic numbers $N=8$ and $N=20$ plays a key role in enhancing clustering toward the neutron drip line, and these predictions are now being tested experimentally through measurements of proton radii via isotope shifts and charge-changing interaction cross sections. The findings motivate searches for exotic linear-chain states in neutron-rich C isotopes, where three $\alpha$ particles are linearly aligned, with recent experiments finding evidence for predicted linear-chain bands in $^{14}$C.
