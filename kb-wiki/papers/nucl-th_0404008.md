# Relaxation, Emission Modes and Limit Temperatures in Ni+Ni HIC

> A dynamical stability analysis is performed for Ni+Ni central collision at intermediate energies, showing that in chemical, thermal and dynamical equilibriums are reached at an early stage of system evolution. This is obtained by computing the relaxation times of the quadrupole momentum, speed of sound and electric charge density of the system. This way, fragment emission modes at low and high energies as well as the qualitative behavior of the limit temperatures are determined.

## Key claim
The paper claims that in Ni+Ni central collisions at intermediate energies, chemical, thermal, and dynamical equilibria are all reached at an early stage of system evolution, but this conclusion is qualified by the projectile energy: at low energies the thermal relaxation time dominates, while at high energies all three relaxation times become equal.

## Method
The authors performed semi-classical simulations of Ni+Ni central collisions using the LATINO model with Pandharipande potential, varying projectile kinetic energy from 800 MeV to 2000 MeV in the center-of-mass system with 200 replicas per energy. They computed relaxation times from three signatures: the quadrupole momentum for thermal equilibrium, the speed of sound for dynamical equilibrium, and the neutron-to-proton ratio parameter λ for chemical equilibrium. Fragment formation was analyzed using the Minimum Spanning Tree in Energy (MSTE) algorithm and the Microscopic Persistence Coefficient.

## Key numbers
- Projectile kinetic energy range: 800 MeV to 2000 MeV in the center-of-mass system
- 200 replicas were generated for each projectile energy
- Energy conservation in numerical integration was maintained to within 0.05%
- Parameters for the soft equation of state: a = -358.1 MeV, b = 304.8 MeV, σ = 7/6
- A specific low projectile energy of 1100 MeV was used to illustrate long quadrupolar relaxation time behavior
- A specific high projectile energy of 1400 MeV was used to illustrate the three instability signatures

## Context
This work extends prior studies of nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions and isospin effects in heavy-ion collisions, building on findings by Müller et al. regarding second-order phase transitions in asymmetric nuclear matter and by Bao An-Li et al. on density and isospin fluctuations. The results connect to experimental observations by Beaulieu et al. (2000) of different relaxation times for surface versus bulk fragment emission, and enable predictions of how limit temperatures and residual sizes depend on projectile energy through the transition from surface to bulk emission modes.
