# Revisiting proton-proton fusion in chiral effective field theory

> We calculate the $S$-factor for proton-proton fusion using chiral effective field theory interactions and currents. By performing order-by-order calculations with a variety of chiral interactions that are regularized and calibrated in different ways, we assess the uncertainty in the $S$-factor from the truncation of the effective field theory expansion and from the sensitivity of the $S$-factor to the short-distance axial current determined from three- and four-nucleon observables. We find that $S(0)=(4.100\pm0.019\mathrm{(syst)}\pm0.013\mathrm{(stat)}\pm0.008(g_A))\times10^{-23}~\mathrm{MeV\,fm}^2\,,$ where the three uncertainties arise, respectively, from the truncation of the effective field theory expansion, use of the two-nucleon axial current fit to few-nucleon observables and variation of the axial coupling constant within the recommended range. The increased value of $S(0)$ compared to previous calculations is mainly driven by an increase in the recommended value for the axial coupling constant and is in agreement with a recent analysis based on pionless effective field theory.

## Key claim

The paper calculates the proton-proton fusion S-factor using chiral effective field theory and finds S(0) = (4.100 ± 0.019(syst) ± 0.013(stat) ± 0.008(g_A)) × 10^{-23} MeV fm^2, where the three uncertainties arise from truncation of the EFT expansion, fitting the two-nucleon axial current to few-nucleon observables, and variation of the axial coupling constant within the recommended range. The increased value compared to previous calculations is mainly driven by an increase in the recommended value for the axial coupling constant, not by changes in the nuclear physics treatment.

## Method

The authors calculated the astrophysical S-factor for proton-proton fusion using chiral effective field theory interactions and currents at different orders in the chiral expansion. They employed four different model approaches: model A used the semi-local SMS-RS potential with Lippmann-Schwinger equation wave functions; model B used the NNLOsim family of 42 interactions with refitted parameters; model C used a Bayesian posterior probability distribution for the c_D-c_E parameters; and model D used the Idaho-N3LO potential with variational method wave functions. The two-body axial current counterterm d_R was related to the three-nucleon force parameter c_D through the corrected relation, and the S-factor was evaluated at center-of-mass energies between 1 and 30 keV with polynomial fits to extract threshold values.

## Key numbers

- S(0) = (4.100 ± 0.019(syst) ± 0.013(stat) ± 0.008(g_A)) × 10^{-23} MeV fm^2 from the SMS-RS interaction at regulator cutoff Λ = 500 MeV
- S(0) = 4.091 × 10^{-23} MeV fm^2 as the average value for the 42 refitted NNLOsim interactions, spanning a range from 4.081 × 10^{-23} to 4.095 × 10^{-23} MeV fm^2
- S(0) = (4.155 ± 0.013(stat)) × 10^{-23} MeV fm^2 from model C using the Bayesian posterior distribution with g_A = 1.2724 and c_D ∈ [-0.615, 0.615] at 1σ
- S(0) values from model D (Idaho-N3LO, Λ = 500 MeV) range from 4.101 × 10^{-23} to 4.118 × 10^{-23} MeV fm^2 depending on the tritium β-decay half-life used (Fit-1: 1134.6 ± 3.1 s, Fit-2: 1129.6 ± 3 s, Fit-3: 1132.1 ± 4.3 s)
- The S(0) value is approximately 2.2% larger than the value from Marcucci (2013), with the difference arising from improved convergence of nuclear wave functions and increased g_A value
- The EFT truncation uncertainty of 0.024 × 10^{-23} MeV fm^2 was obtained using Q = pion mass and Λ_b = 500 MeV

## Context

This work extends and corrects previous chiral EFT calculations of proton-proton fusion from Marcucci (2013) and Acharya (2016) by using the corrected relationship between the axial current parameter d_R and the three-nucleon force parameter c_D, as established by PhysRevLett.121.049901. The results show an upward revision from the recommendation made by Adelberger (2010) and are in agreement with a recent pionless EFT analysis by De-Leon (2022) that quoted S(0) = (4.14 ± 0.01 ± 0.005 ± 0.06) × 10^{-23} MeV fm^2. The improved precision enables more reliable inputs for solar astrophysics simulations and can help resolve inconsistencies between spectroscopic determinations of solar abundances and helioseismology.
