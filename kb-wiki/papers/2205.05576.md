# Quantum Computing Calculations for Nuclear Structure and Nuclear Data

> Model calculations of nuclear properties are peformed using quantum computing algorithms on simulated and real quantum computers. The models are a realistic calculation of deuteron binding based on effective field theory, and a simplified two-level version of the nuclear shell model known as the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model. A method of reducing the number of qubits needed for practical calculation is presented, the reduction being with respect to the number needed when the standard Jordan-Wigner encoding is used. Its efficacy is shown in the case of the deuteron binding and shell model. A version of the variational quantum eigensolver in which all eigenstates in a spectrum are targetted on an equal basis is shown. The method involves finding the minima of the variance of the Hamiltonian. The method's ability to find the full spectrum of the simplified shell model is presented.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that quantum computing algorithms can perform nuclear structure calculations on both simulated and real quantum computers, but with the important caveat that the calculations are limited to simplified models (deuteron binding and a two-level shell model) and that the method of minimizing Hamiltonian variance to find all eigenstates works only on simulated quantum computers, with real quantum computer investigations still underway.

## Method

The authors encoded the deuteron Hamiltonian (derived from effective field theory in a harmonic oscillator basis) and the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick (LMG) shell model Hamiltonian onto qubits using a reduced encoding method that requires fewer qubits than the standard Jordan-Wigner encoding. They applied the variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) algorithm on IBM quantum computers (ibmq_manila) and simulators, using hardware-general wave function ansatzes with variable rotation parameters, and introduced a variance-minimization variant of VQE that targets all eigenstates rather than just the ground state.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron Hamiltonian H₂ with reduced encoding: 5.906709 I₀ - 6.343291 Z₀ - 4.286607 X₀ (one qubit), compared to Jordan-Wigner encoding requiring two qubits
- Ground state energy of deuteron H₂ from energy landscape: approximately -1.75 MeV at (θ₁,θ₂) ≈ (3π/16, 0)
- Ground state energy of deuteron H₄ on ibmq_manila: -2.108 MeV ± 0.080 MeV, equal within errorbars to exact value of -2.144 MeV
- Second eigenstate energy of deuteron H₂ from variance minimization: 13.586 MeV, close to analytic value of 13.563 MeV
- Potential parameter V₀ = -5.68658111 MeV used in deuteron Hamiltonian matrix elements

## Context

This work extends prior quantum computing calculations of the deuteron by Dumitrescu et al. (2018), who used Jordan-Wigner encoding on IBM cloud quantum computers, and of the LMG model by Cervia et al. (2021) and Robbins et al. (2021). The reduced qubit encoding method enables calculations of larger basis sizes (H₄) than previously attempted on real quantum hardware, while the variance-minimization VQE variant provides a path toward obtaining excited state spectra on quantum computers, which has been explored in quantum chemistry by other groups (2020, 2021).
