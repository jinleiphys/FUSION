# Physics perspectives of heavy-ion collisions at very high energy

> Heavy-ion collisions at very high colliding energies are expected to produce a quark-gluon plasma (QGP) at the highest temperature obtainable in a laboratory setting. Experimental studies of these reactions can provide an unprecedented range of information on properties of the QGP at high temperatures. We report theoretical investigations of the physics perspectives of heavy-ion collisions at a future high-energy collider. These include initial parton production, collective expansion of the dense medium, jet quenching, heavy-quark transport, dissociation and regeneration of quarkonia, photon and dilepton production. We illustrate the potential of future experimental studies of the initial particle production and formation of QGP at the highest temperature to provide constraints on properties of strongly interaction matter.

## Key claim

Heavy-ion collisions at future colliders with center-of-mass energies of tens to hundreds of TeV per nucleon pair can produce a quark-gluon plasma at the highest laboratory temperatures, but whether the interaction strength among bulk partons and between hard probes and the medium will continue to weaken toward the weakly coupled regime predicted by perturbative QCD remains an open question that requires experimental verification.

## Method

The authors present a theoretical review of selected physics topics for heavy-ion collisions at very high energies, including initial parton production via pQCD-based models (HIJING, rcBK, IP-Glasma) and relativistic viscous hydrodynamics for collective expansion. They extrapolate from existing RHIC and LHC data using Monte Carlo simulations and lattice QCD calculations to estimate initial energy densities, temperatures, multiplicities, and transport coefficients at future collider energies.

## Key numbers

- Pseudo-critical QCD crossover temperature at zero baryon chemical potential: T_c ≈ 155 MeV (from lattice QCD)
- Initial energy density at center of Pb+Pb collisions at √s = 30 TeV: ε₀ = 136 GeV/fm³ at thermalization time τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c
- Initial temperature at center of Pb+Pb collisions at √s = 30 TeV: T₀ = 517 MeV at τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c
- Charged hadron rapidity density in central Pb+Pb collisions at √s = 30 TeV: dN_ch/dη ≈ 2700
- Charged hadron rapidity density in central Pb+Pb collisions at √s = 2.76 TeV: dN_ch/dη ≈ 1700
- Charged hadron rapidity density in central Au+Au collisions at √s = 0.20 TeV: dN_ch/dη ≈ 745
- Hadron chemical freeze-out temperature upper bound: 148 ± 4 MeV (from lattice QCD matching)
- All charmonia dissociate at T ≳ 1.5 T_c in gluonic plasma (from lattice QCD spectral functions)
- S-wave bottomonium states exist up to at least 2 T_c; P-wave states melt just above T_c (from lattice NRQCD)

## Context

This paper extends the theoretical framework developed for RHIC and LHC heavy-ion physics to future collider energies, building on the discoveries of strongly-coupled QGP at RHIC (Adcox 2004, Adams 2005) and its confirmation at LHC (Muller 2012). It addresses open questions about the QCD equation of state at high temperatures, thermalization mechanisms, and whether transport properties approach weak-coupling values predicted by perturbative QCD. The work enables quantitative predictions for future facilities such as the proposed VLHC or CEPC, and identifies key measurements needed to constrain initial-state models like the Color Glass Condensate (McLerran 1993) and the rcBK approach (Balitsky 1995, Kovchegov 1999).
