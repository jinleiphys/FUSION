# How atomic nuclei cluster

> Nucleonic matter displays a quantum liquid structure, but in some cases finite nuclei behave like molecules composed of clusters of protons and neutrons. Clustering is a recurrent feature in light nuclei, from beryllium to nickel. For instance, in $^{12}$C the Hoyle state, crucial for stellar nucleosynthesis, can be described as a nuclear molecule consisting of three alpha-particles. The mechanism of cluster formation, however, has not yet been fully understood. We show that the origin of clustering can be traced back to the depth of the confining nuclear potential. By employing the theoretical framework of energy density functionals that encompasses both cluster and quantum liquid-drop aspects of nuclei, it is shown that the depth of the potential determines the energy spacings between single-nucleon orbitals, the localization of the corresponding wave functions and, therefore, the degree of nucleonic density clustering. Relativistic functionals, in particular, are characterized by deep single-nucleon potentials. When compared to non-relativistic functionals that yield similar ground-state properties (binding energy, deformation, radii), they predict the occurrence of much more pronounced cluster structures. More generally, clustering is considered as a transitional phenomenon between crystalline and quantum liquid phases of fermionic systems.

# How atomic nuclei cluster

## Key claim
The origin of clustering in atomic nuclei can be traced to the depth of the confining single-nucleon mean-field potential, which determines the energy spacings between single-nucleon orbitals and the localization of their wave functions. Relativistic energy density functionals, which produce deeper single-nucleon potentials than non-relativistic functionals that otherwise yield similar ground-state properties, predict much more pronounced cluster structures.

## Method
The authors employed self-consistent mean-field calculations based on nuclear energy density functionals (EDFs), comparing the non-relativistic Skyrme SLy4 functional with the relativistic DD-ME2 functional. They calculated ground-state densities and single-nucleon spectra for deformed alpha-conjugate nuclei including 20Ne, 24Mg, 28Si, and 32S, and analyzed the depth of the self-consistent single-nucleon potentials and the corresponding localization of single-particle wave functions.

## Key numbers
- The depth of the DD-ME2 single-neutron potential at the centre of 20Ne is -78.6 MeV, compared to -69.5 MeV for SLy4.
- The depth of the DD-ME2 single-proton potential at the centre of 20Ne is -72.8 MeV, compared to -64.6 MeV for SLy4.
- The central density calculated with SLy4 is approximately 0.16 fm^-3, while DD-ME2 predicts density spikes as large as approximately 0.2 fm^-3.
- Both functionals reproduce the experimental binding energy of 20Ne (160.6 MeV), the proton distribution radius (2.90 fm), and the matter distribution radius (2.85 fm) with typical accuracy of about 1 percent.
- The dimensionless clustering parameter alpha = b/r0, where b is the dispersion of the nucleon wave function and r0 is the typical inter-nucleon distance (approximately 1.2 fm), is less than 1 for the relativistic functional and greater than 1 for the non-relativistic functional.

## Context
This work extends the qualitative explanation of clustering based on deformation-induced degeneracies (associated with the Nilsson model from the 1960s) by providing a microscopic mechanism rooted in the depth of the mean-field potential. It builds on previous EDF studies of cluster structures in alpha-conjugate nuclei by various groups (Ebran, Khan, Niksic, Vretenar and others, 2010-2012) and connects to the broader concept of quantality introduced by Mottelson (1999) for classifying fermionic systems. The results enable a unified description of cluster and quantum liquid-drop aspects of nuclei within the EDF framework, and suggest that cluster states become less likely in heavier nuclei as the parameter alpha increases with nucleon number.
