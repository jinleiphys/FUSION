# Fluid Dynamics for Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

> 49 pages, 22 figures, style files, Proceedings of the 11th Chris Engelbrecht Summer School in Theoretical Physics, Cape Town, February 4-13, 1998

## Key claim

This lecture series provides an introductory foundation for understanding relativistic fluid dynamics as applied to heavy-ion collisions, but explicitly avoids comparison to experimental data and omits applied topics such as transverse collective flow; the author states the aim is not a complete review but rather a basis for reading current research literature.

## Method

The paper derives the conservation equations for energy, momentum, and net charge currents, then presents the ideal fluid approximation where dissipative terms (heat flow, stress tensor, charge diffusion) are set to zero and the system is closed by specifying an equation of state. It then develops dissipative fluid dynamics via both a phenomenological approach based on the second law of thermodynamics and a kinetic-theory approach using the first three moments of the Boltzmann equation, leading to the Israel-Stewart equations. Multi-fluid dynamics is introduced where separate fluids (e.g., target and projectile nucleons) exchange charge and energy-momentum through source terms derived from microscopic collision rates.

## Key numbers

- The ideal fluid approximation reduces the number of unknown functions from 10 + 4n (for n conserved charges) to 5 + n, closed by an equation of state.
- The dissipative fluid dynamics framework introduces 6 + 3n additional equations to close the system, with coefficients for bulk viscosity ζ, shear viscosity η, and thermal conductivity κ.
- The criterion for local thermodynamical equilibrium is Γ ≫ θ, where Γ ≃ σ n is the microscopic reaction rate (σ cross section, n particle density) and θ ≡ ∂·u is the expansion scalar.
- In the Eckart frame, the simplest dissipative equations give Π = ζ θ, q^μ = κ T Δ^{μν} (∂_ν ln T - ˙u_ν), and π^{μν} = 2η [½(Δ^μ_α Δ^ν_β + Δ^μ_β Δ^ν_α) - ⅓ Δ^{μν} Δ_{αβ}] ∂^α u^β.

## Context

This work extends the long tradition of fluid-dynamical modeling in heavy-ion physics reviewed by Stoecker, Greiner, and collaborators, and by Csernai. It provides the theoretical foundation for studying the deconfinement and chiral symmetry restoration transition using lattice QCD equations of state, as computed by Laermann and others. The paper enables future applications to collisions at CERN SPS, Brookhaven AGS, and RHIC (beginning Fall 1999), and specifically notes that the time delay in expansion due to equation-of-state softening in a phase transition region may have experimental consequences at RHIC energies.
