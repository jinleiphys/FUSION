# Statistical aspects of nuclear mass models

> We study the information content of nuclear masses from the perspective of global models of nuclear binding energies. To this end, we employ a number of statistical methods and diagnostic tools, including Bayesian calibration, Bayesian model averaging, chi-square correlation analysis, principal component analysis, and empirical coverage probability. Using a Bayesian framework, we investigate the structure of the 4-parameter Liquid Drop Model by considering discrepant mass domains for calibration. We then use the chi-square correlation framework to analyze the 14-parameter Skyrme energy density functional calibrated using homogeneous and heterogeneous datasets. We show that a quite dramatic parameter reduction can be achieved in both cases. The advantage of Bayesian model averaging for improving uncertainty quantification is demonstrated. The statistical approaches used are pedagogically described; in this context this work can serve as a guide for future applications.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the information content of nuclear mass models can be dramatically reduced through statistical analysis, showing that the 4-parameter Liquid Drop Model effectively has only 2 independent degrees of freedom and the 14-parameter Skyrme energy density functional can be reduced to 5-7 effective parameters, though these conclusions depend on the specific calibration dataset used.

## Method

The authors applied Bayesian calibration, chi-square correlation analysis, principal component analysis, and Bayesian model averaging to two nuclear mass models: the 4-parameter Liquid Drop Model (LDM) and the 14-parameter Skyrme energy density functional. For the LDM, they calibrated four variants using different subsets of 595 even-even nuclei from AME2003 divided into light (Z<40, N<50), heavy (Z>50, N>80), intermediate, and combined domains, then tested predictions on held-out intermediate nuclei. For the Skyrme functional, they used chi-square correlation analysis to study parametrizations obtained from homogeneous and heterogeneous calibration datasets.

## Key numbers

* LDM fitted to all 595 even-even nuclei (LDM(A)): volume parameter a_vol = 15.162 +/- 0.051 MeV, surface parameter a_surf = 15.960 +/- 0.160 MeV, symmetry parameter a_sym = 21.995 +/- 0.131 MeV, Coulomb parameter a_C = 0.680 +/- 0.004 MeV, with error scale sigma = 3.698 MeV
* LDM fitted to light nuclei only (LDM(L)): a_vol = 14.050 +/- 0.097 MeV, a_surf = 13.877 +/- 0.230 MeV, a_sym = 17.054 +/- 0.347 MeV, a_C = 0.534 +/- 0.013 MeV, sigma = 2.936 MeV
* LDM fitted to heavy nuclei only (LDM(H)): a_vol = 15.221 +/- 0.176 MeV, a_surf = 15.873 +/- 0.624 MeV, a_sym = 22.502 +/- 0.390 MeV, a_C = 0.690 +/- 0.010 MeV, sigma = 2.690 MeV
* Root-mean-square deviation on the intermediate domain for LDM(A): 3.205 MeV (chi-square) and 3.206 MeV (Bayesian)
* Root-mean-square deviation on the intermediate domain for LDM(L): 8.170 MeV (chi-square) and 8.176 MeV (Bayesian)
* Root-mean-square deviation on the intermediate domain for LDM(H): 3.817 MeV (chi-square) and 3.811 MeV (Bayesian)
* Principal component analysis showed that for all LDM variants, 99% of data variation is captured by only 2 effective parameters; for LDM(H) and LDM(A), a single principal component explains about 95% of variability
* For the Skyrme functional, the number of effective parameters was found to be 5-7 out of 14

## Context

This work extends the statistical analysis of nuclear mass models previously applied to the LDM by Bertsch (2005), Toivanen (2008), and others, and to Skyrme functionals by Dobaczewski (2014) and the ISNET collaboration. The Bayesian model averaging approach builds on recent Bayesian studies of nuclear density functional theory by Neufcourt (2018, 2019, 2020) and McDonnell (2015). The results enable more efficient parameter estimation and uncertainty quantification for nuclear mass models by identifying which parameter combinations are actually constrained by data, and demonstrate how Bayesian model averaging can improve predictions by combining models calibrated on different nuclear mass domains.
