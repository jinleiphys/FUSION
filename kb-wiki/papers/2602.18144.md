# The hyperfine interaction as a probe of the microscopic structure of the atomic nucleus

> The study of highly charged electronic and muonic hydrogen-like ions, provides an intriguing way to probe the internal structure of their atomic nuclei. In this work, we use nuclear structure calculations to accurately calculate the hyperfine splitting of electronic and muonic hydrogen-like ions, focusing in particular on the incorporation of finite-volume corrections, such as Bohr-Weisskopf and Breit-Rosenthal, due to the penetration of the electron and muon wavefunction into the nuclear electric charge and magnetic dipole densities. These corrections are essential for refining our understanding of the nuclear magnetic dipole and electric quadrupole moments. Our simulations use a Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-BCS model known for its effectiveness in modeling well-deformed nuclei such as ${}^{159}\mathrm{Tb}^{64+}$ and ${}^{165}\mathrm{Ho}^{66+}$, with particular emphasis on ${}^{161,163}\mathrm{Dy}^{65+}$ isotopes. It can also be generalised to multi-electron ions by studying the hyperfine anomaly between two isotopes.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that microscopic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-BCS nuclear densities, when used to compute Bohr-Weisskopf and Breit-Rosenthal finite-volume corrections to hyperfine splitting in hydrogen-like ions, can distinguish orbital and spin contributions to nuclear magnetism and capture deformation effects that simple spherical or Fermi-distribution models miss, but the authors caution that QED corrections (vacuum polarization and self-energy) must be added separately to match experimental precision, and that the present work focuses only on the finite-nucleus corrections.

## Method
The authors performed Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-BCS calculations for the deformed nuclei 159Tb, 161Dy, 163Dy, and 165Ho to obtain spatially-resolved nuclear charge densities and current densities (orbital and spin contributions). These microscopic densities were then used as input to solve the Dirac equation for hydrogen-like electronic and muonic ions, computing the magnetic dipole hyperfine constant a and electric quadrupole constant b, including the finite-nucleus corrections (Bohr-Weisskopf and Breit-Rosenthal effects) that arise from the penetration of the lepton wavefunction into the nuclear volume.

## Key numbers
- The study focuses on isotopes 161Dy and 163Dy (Z=66), with 159Tb (Z=65) and 165Ho (Z=67) as comparative baselines.
- The muon-to-electron mass ratio (m_mu/m_e) provides a factor of approximately 10^3 enhancement of nuclear overlap in muonic versus electronic ions.
- QED corrections (vacuum polarization and self-energy) are stated to be of the order of a few percent of the total hyperfine transition energy for highly charged heavy ions.
- The 229Th nuclear isomer energy is cited as approximately 8 eV, relevant to the discussion of nuclear clocks.

## Context
This work extends the tradition of Bohr-Weisskopf (1950) and Breit-Rosenthal (1932) finite-size corrections, which previously relied on simple spherical or Fermi-distribution nuclear models (e.g., Indelicato, 2013), by introducing a fully microscopic nuclear-structure framework. It enables systematic study of how deformation, shell structure, and the interplay of orbital versus spin magnetism affect hyperfine observables, and proposes muonic hydrogen-like lanthanide ions as benchmarks for testing nuclear models. The approach can be generalized to multi-electron ions via the hyperfine anomaly between isotopes, and connects to quantum computing applications in lanthanide molecular complexes (e.g., TbPc2, studied by Moreno and Wernsdorfer groups at KIT).
