# Calculating the nuclear mass at finite angular momenta

> Mean field methods to calculate the nuclear mass are extended into the high spin regime to calculate the nuclear binding energy as a function of proton number, neutron number and angular momentum. Comparing the trend as a function of mass number for a selection of high-spin states, a similar agreement between theory and experiment is obtained as for ground state masses.

## Key claim
Mean field methods for calculating nuclear masses are extended to the high-spin regime, and the agreement between theory and experiment for high-spin states is similar to that obtained for ground state masses, but only when pairing correlations are negligible and a specific liquid-drop model (LSD) with a diffuse-surface moment of inertia is used.

## Method
The authors use a macroscopic-microscopic approach combining a rotating liquid-drop energy (either the finite-range liquid drop model, FRLDM, or the LSD model with an A^{1/3} curvature term) with cranked Nilsson-Strutinsky (CNS) shell corrections from a modified oscillator potential. The rigid moment of inertia is calculated with a Yukawa-folding diffuse surface, with parameters r0 and a fitted to experimental charge density distributions for 116 nuclei. Pairing is ignored, and the total energy is minimized over deformation for each angular momentum I.

## Key numbers
- The FRLDM fit to 1654 ground-state masses gives a root mean square error of σ_th = 0.779 MeV; a new fit without zero-point energy and with pairing in microscopic terms gives σ_th = 0.778 MeV (standard error 0.783 MeV).
- The LSD model fitted to 2766 masses gives a root mean square error of 0.698 MeV, decreasing to 0.610 MeV when only 1654 masses are considered.
- The moment of inertia parameters fitted from charge densities are r0 = 1.1599 fm and a = 0.5984 fm, with a standard deviation of 0.0454 fm.
- Typical errors between calculated and experimental high-spin energies are in the range of ±1 MeV for the mass range A = 20–194.
- For the terminating state in 36Ar, the FRLDM predicts ε2 = 0.415 and ε4 = 0.188, while the LSD model predicts ε2 = 0.356 and ε4 = 0.065.
- For the terminating state in 20Ne, the FRLDM predicts ε2 = 0.120 and ε4 = 0.210, while the LSD model predicts ε2 = 0.092 and ε4 = 0.033.
- The diffuse moment of inertia for spherical shape is about 13% bigger for A = 36 and more than 20% bigger for A = 20 compared to a sharp surface.
- For the 8+ state in 20Ne, the energy difference between diffuse and sharp surface is about 3 MeV; for the 16+ state in 36Ar, the difference is about 1.5 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic mass calculations of Möller et al. (FRLDM, 1995) and the LSD model of Pomorski and Dudek (2003) into the high-spin regime, building on the cranked Nilsson-Strutinsky formalism of Afanasjev et al. (1999). It enables systematic comparisons of absolute binding energies across the (Z,N,I) space, which is necessary for predicting prompt particle decay from high-spin states and for studying how angular momentum is built from collective rotation versus single-particle excitations. The approach could be extended to a global mass fit in three dimensions and to include pairing effects at lower spins.
