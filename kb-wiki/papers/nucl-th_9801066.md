# Semi-relativistic charge-current density operator

> The charge-current density and two-photon operators consistent with a single-particle semi-relativistic Hamiltonian are derived within a suitable functional derivative formalism which preserves gauge invariance. An application to electron scattering is presented and results are compared with a fully relativistic case and the non-relativistic cases corrected through fourth order in M^{-1}.

## Key claim

The paper derives a charge-current density operator consistent with a single-particle semi-relativistic Hamiltonian (kinetic energy given by the relativistic square root) using a functional derivative formalism that preserves gauge invariance; the resulting operator is valid for energies up to the threshold of particle-antiparticle production, substantially increasing the range of applicability compared to any non-relativistic reduction, but it cannot reproduce the Darwin-Foldy term because the semi-relativistic Hamiltonian excludes negative-energy states from the outset.

## Method

The authors start from a semi-relativistic Hamiltonian where the kinetic energy is the relativistic square-root operator \(T = \sqrt{\vec{p}^2 + M^2}\) and the potential is energy- and momentum-independent. They apply minimal substitution to introduce the electromagnetic field and then use a functional derivative formalism to expand the resulting Hamiltonian in a Taylor series in the field, identifying the first-order charge-current density operator \(j_\mu(\vec{x})\) and the second-order two-photon operator \(b_{ij}(\vec{x},\vec{y})\). The spin contribution is treated by writing the Hamiltonian in terms of Pauli matrices and applying the same method. The resulting operators are then used to compute the four structure functions \(g_{\lambda\lambda'}\) for quasi-elastic electron-proton scattering, and these are compared with the fully relativistic case (using the cc1 current) and with non-relativistic reductions through fourth order in \(M^{-1}\).

## Key numbers

- The non-relativistic Foldy-Wouthuysen reduction becomes meaningless for values of \(p/M\) larger than 0.5, where \(p\) is a typical momentum.
- For ejectile momentum \(p' = 0.5\) GeV, the non-relativistic result corrected to order \(M^{-4}\) is within a few percent of the relativistic case in both parallel and perpendicular kinematics.
- The semi-relativistic result merges into the relativistic one for increasing \(q\) and \(p'\), while the non-relativistic result corrected to order \(M^{-4}\) rapidly deteriorates for larger momenta (e.g., \(p' = 0.75\) GeV and \(p' = 1\) GeV).
- The semi-relativistic structure functions are obtained from the non-relativistic ones by substituting the mass \(M\) with \(\frac{1}{2}(E_{\vec{p}} + E_{\vec{p}'})\), so they coincide in the low-energy limit \(E \approx M\).

## Context

This work extends the semi-relativistic approach to electromagnetic interactions, where the kinetic energy operator is taken as the relativistic square root (as used in relativized quark models by Godfrey and Isgur (1985) and in nuclear models by Glockle (1983) and Carlson (1987)), and provides the consistent charge-current density operator that was missing. It contrasts with the Foldy-Wouthuysen method (Foldy and Wouthuysen (1950)) and the direct Pauli reduction, which both include negative-energy states and produce the Darwin-Foldy term; the present approach excludes such states from the beginning. The results are compared with the non-relativistic reductions through fourth order in \(M^{-1}\) by Giusti and Pacati (1980) and with the fully relativistic cc1 current of de Forest (1983). The derived operators can be used to describe electromagnetic interactions of many-body systems at intermediate energies whenever the impulse approximation applies.
