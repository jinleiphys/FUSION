# The Vlasov formalism for extended relativistic mean field models: the crust-core transition and the stellar matter equation of state

> The Vlasov formalism is extended to relativistic mean-field hadron models with non-linear terms up to fourth order and applied to the calculation of the crust-core transition density. The effect of the nonlinear $ωρ$ and $σρ$ coupling terms on the crust-core transition density and pressure, and on the macroscopic properties of some families of hadronic stars is investigated. For that purpose, six families of relativistic mean field models are considered. Within each family, the members differ in the symmetry energy behavior. For all the models, the dynamical spinodals are calculated, and the crust-core transition density and pressure, and the neutron star mass-radius relations are obtained. The effect on the star radius of the inclusion of a pasta calculation in the inner crust is discussed. The set of six models that best satisfy terrestrial and observational constraints predicts a radius of 13.6$\pm$0.3 km and a crust thickness of $1.36\pm 0.06$km for a 1.4 $M_\odot$ star.

## Key claim

The Vlasov formalism is extended to relativistic mean-field hadron models including all non-linear meson terms up to fourth order, and applied to calculate the crust-core transition density and pressure in neutron stars. The dynamical spinodal method predicts a lower limit for the crust-core density, and for β-equilibrium matter the result is very similar to the Thomas-Fermi estimate.

## Method

The authors extended the Vlasov formalism to include all non-linear self-interaction and mixing terms involving the σ, ω, and ρ mesons up to quartic order in the Lagrangian density. They constructed six families of relativistic mean-field models (based on TM1, NL3, and Z271 as "head" models) by adding σρ or ωρ coupling terms with different strengths, keeping isoscalar properties fixed while varying isovector properties. For each model, dynamical spinodals were calculated to determine the crust-core transition density and pressure, and the complete stellar matter equation of state was built by combining outer crust, inner crust, and core EoS to integrate the TOV equations for neutron star mass-radius relations.

## Key numbers

- For the set of six models best satisfying terrestrial and observational constraints: radius of 13.6 ± 0.3 km and crust thickness of 1.36 ± 0.06 km for a 1.4 M⊙ star
- NL3 head model: transition density ρt = 0.055 fm⁻³, transition pressure Pt = 0.258 MeV/fm³, symmetry energy Esym = 37.34 MeV, slope L = 118 MeV
- TM1 head model: transition density ρt = 0.060 fm⁻³, transition pressure Pt = 0.328 MeV/fm³, symmetry energy Esym = 36.84 MeV, slope L = 111 MeV
- Z271 head model: transition density ρt = 0.068 fm⁻³, transition pressure Pt = 0.405 MeV/fm³, symmetry energy Esym = 35.81 MeV, slope L = 99 MeV
- NL3 σρ6 model: transition density ρt = 0.081 fm⁻³, transition pressure Pt = 0.382 MeV/fm³, symmetry energy Esym = 31.47 MeV, slope L = 55 MeV
- NL3 ωρ6 model: transition density ρt = 0.084 fm⁻³, transition pressure Pt = 0.516 MeV/fm³, symmetry energy Esym = 31.66 MeV, slope L = 55 MeV
- NL3 saturation density ρ0 = 0.148 fm⁻³, binding energy per nucleon E/A = -16.24 MeV, incompressibility K = 270/269 MeV
- TM1 saturation density ρ0 = 0.145 fm⁻³, binding energy per nucleon E/A = -16.26 MeV, incompressibility K = 280 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous Vlasov formalism calculations for the Walecka model (Nielsen 1991, 1993) and earlier non-linear extensions (Avancini 2005, Providencia 2006, Pais 2009, 2010) to include all meson terms up to fourth order. It builds on prior crust-core transition studies using dynamical spinodals (Avancini 2010, 2012) and Skyrme interactions (Pethick 1995), and addresses the need for unified equations of state as highlighted by Fortin (2016). The results enable systematic identification of correlations between the symmetry energy slope L and crust-core transition properties, and inform whether the glitch mechanism in pulsars can be attributed solely to the crust, as discussed by Link (1999) and Chamel (2013).
