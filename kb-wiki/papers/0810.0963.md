# Comparison of statistical treatments for the equation of state for core-collapse supernovae

> Neutrinos emitted during the collapse, bounce and subsequent explosion provide information about supernova dynamics. The neutrino spectra are determined by weak interactions with nuclei and nucleons in the inner regions of the star, and thus the neutrino spectra are determined by the composition of matter. The composition of stellar matter at temperature ranging from $T=1-3$ MeV and densities ranging from $10^{-5}$ to 0.1 times the saturation density is explored. We examine the single-nucleus approximation commonly used in describing dense matter in supernova simulations and show that, while the approximation is accurate for predicting the energy and pressure at most densities, it fails to predict the composition accurately. We find that as the temperature and density increase, the single nucleus approximation systematically overpredicts the mass number of nuclei that are actually present and underestimates the contribution from lighter nuclei which are present in significant amounts.

## Key claim

The single-nucleus approximation (SNA) is accurate for predicting the energy and pressure of stellar matter at most densities relevant to core-collapse supernovae, but it systematically overpredicts the mass number of the representative heavy nucleus and underestimates the contribution from lighter nuclei, particularly as temperature and density increase.

## Method

The authors constructed a Grand Canonical Approximation (GCA) model that treats an ensemble of nuclei in nuclear statistical equilibrium, and compared it to a companion Single Nucleus Approximation (SNA) model using the same mass formula and level densities for two different liquid-drop parametrizations (LDM1 with surface symmetry energy, LDM2 without). They also constructed an improved SNA model (ISNA) that includes nucleon interactions, quantum statistics, a more complete Coulomb energy, and a density-dependent surface energy, following the approach of Lattimer and Swesty (1991). The composition and thermodynamic properties were computed for baryon densities from \(10^{-5}\) to \(0.1\) times saturation density, temperatures from 1 to 3 MeV, and electron fractions from 0.2 to 0.4.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density: \(\rho_0 \sim 2.7 \times 10^{14}~\mathrm{g/cm}^3\)
- Temperature range explored: \(T = 1-3\) MeV
- Density range explored: \(10^{-5}\) to \(0.1\) times saturation density
- Electron fraction values used: \(Y_e = 0.2\) and \(Y_e = 0.4\)
- The largest differences between SNA and ISNA models are a few tenths of an MeV in the energy per baryon at higher densities, with comparable differences in entropy
- The critical temperature for symmetric matter used in ISNA: \(T_C(x=1/2) = 20.085\) MeV
- The free volume fraction used: \(\Omega \equiv V_f/V = 1 - \rho_B/\rho_0\)
- Upper cutoff for nuclear mass number in GCA sums: \(A = 1000\)

## Context

This work extends the single-nucleus approximation approach developed by Lattimer and Swesty (1991) and Shen (1998) by directly comparing it to a grand canonical treatment of the nuclear ensemble. The authors find that while the SNA adequately predicts bulk thermodynamic quantities, the composition differences matter for weak interaction rates, consistent with findings by Hix (2003) that electron capture rates on a distribution of nuclei increase the potential for explosion, and by Arcones (2008) that light nuclei are important for neutrino spectra. The results enable improved modeling of neutrino spectra and electron capture rates in core-collapse supernova simulations by quantifying when the SNA is insufficient.
