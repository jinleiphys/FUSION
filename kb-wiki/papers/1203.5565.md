# Microscopic sub-barrier fusion calculations for the neutron star crust

> Fusion of very neutron rich nuclei may be important to determine the composition and heating of the crust of accreting neutron stars. Fusion cross sections are calculated using time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory coupled with density-constrained Hartree-Fock calculations to deduce an effective potential. Systems studied include 16O+16O, 16O+24O, 24O+24O, 12C+16O, and 12C+24O. We find remarkable agreement with experimental cross sections for the fusion of stable nuclei. Our simulations use the SLy4 Skyrme force that has been previously fit to the properties of stable nuclei, and no parameters have been fit to fusion data. We compare our results to the simple São Paulo static barrier penetration model. For the asymmetric systems 12C+24O or 16O+24O we predict an order of magnitude larger cross section than those predicted by the São Paulo model. This is likely due to the transfer of neutrons from the very neutron rich nucleus to the stable nucleus and dynamical rearrangements of the nuclear densities during the collision process. These effects are not included in potential models. This enhancement of fusion cross sections, for very neutron rich nuclei, can be tested in the laboratory with radioactive beams.

# Microscopic sub-barrier fusion calculations for the neutron star crust

## Key claim

For asymmetric systems involving a very neutron-rich nucleus (24O) and a stable nucleus (12C or 16O), the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations predict fusion cross sections an order of magnitude larger than those from the São Paulo static barrier penetration model, likely due to neutron transfer and dynamical density rearrangements not included in potential models.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) simulations in full 3D geometry using the SLy4 Skyrme energy-density functional, coupled with density-constrained Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) calculations to extract ion-ion interaction potentials. Fusion cross sections were then obtained by solving the two-body Schrödinger equation with the incoming wave boundary condition (IWBC) method, using a coordinate-dependent mass parameter transformed to a scaled potential. For comparison, cross sections were also calculated using the São Paulo barrier penetration model with Woods-Saxon static densities and WKB tunneling.

## Key numbers

- For 16O+16O, the DC-TDHF potential barrier height is 10.00 MeV; for 16O+24O it is 8.54 MeV; for 24O+24O it is 7.95 MeV; for 16O+28O it is 7.95 MeV (Fig. 5).
- For 12C+16O, the DC-TDHF potential barrier peak is 7.77 MeV; for 12C+24O it is 6.64 MeV (Fig. 8).
- On average, about one neutron is transferred from 24O to 16O in the region of R values relevant for fusion cross sections, while two neutrons are transferred from 28O to 16O in the same region (Fig. 6).
- The TDHF calculations reproduce experimental fusion cross sections for 16O+16O and 12C+16O with no parameters fit to fusion data.
- For the asymmetric systems 12C+24O and 16O+24O, the TDHF results predict an order of magnitude larger cross section than the São Paulo model.

## Context

This work extends the DC-TDHF method previously applied by the same group to systems such as 132Sn+64Ni (Umar and Oberacker, 2007), 64Ni+64Ni (Umar and Oberacker, 2008), and 16O+208Pb (Umar and Oberacker, 2009) to light neutron-rich nuclei relevant for neutron star crust physics. The results contradict the simpler São Paulo model predictions for asymmetric neutron-rich systems, as previously tabulated by Beard et al. (2010) for astrophysical S factors. The findings enable improved modeling of pycnonuclear fusion reactions in accreting neutron star crusts, potentially affecting predictions for superburst ignition conditions, and can be tested experimentally with radioactive beams at facilities such as GANIL-SPIRAL, TRIUMF ISAC, and NSCL ReA3-6.
