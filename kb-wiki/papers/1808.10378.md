# Digitization of Scalar Fields for Quantum Computing

> Qubit, operator and gate resources required for the digitization of lattice $λϕ^4$ scalar field theories onto quantum computers are considered, building upon the foundational work by Jordan, Lee and Preskill, with a focus towards noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices. The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, introduced in this context by Macridin, Spentzouris, Amundson and Harnik building on the work of Somma, provides a guide with which to evaluate the efficacy of two field-space bases, the eigenstates of the field operator, as used by Jordan, Lee and Preskill, and eigenstates of a harmonic oscillator, to describe $0+1$- and $d+1$-dimensional scalar field theory. We show how techniques associated with improved actions, which are heavily utilized in Lattice QCD calculations to systematically reduce lattice-spacing artifacts, can be used to reduce the impact of the field digitization in $λϕ^4$, but are found to be inferior to a complete digitization-improvement of the Hamiltonian using a Quantum Fourier Transform. When the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem is satisfied, digitization errors scale as $|\log|\log |ε_{\rm dig}|||\sim n_Q$ (number of qubits describing the field at a given spatial site) for the low-lying states, leaving the familiar power-law lattice-spacing and finite-volume effects that scale as $|\log |ε_{\rm latt}||\sim N_Q$ (total number of qubits in the simulation). For localized(delocalized) field-space wavefunctions, it is found that $n_Q\sim4(7)$ qubits per spatial lattice site are sufficient to reduce theoretical digitization errors below error contributions associated with approximation of the time-evolution operator and noisy implementation on near-term quantum devices.

## Key claim

When the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem is satisfied, digitization errors in lattice scalar field theory scale as $|\log|\log |\epsilon_{\rm dig}|||\sim n_Q$ (number of qubits per spatial site) for low-lying states, leaving the familiar power-law lattice-spacing and finite-volume effects that scale as $|\log |\epsilon_{\rm latt}||\sim N_Q$ (total number of qubits). The authors find that improved action techniques are inferior to a complete digitization-improvement of the Hamiltonian using a Quantum Fourier Transform.

## Method

The authors digitize lattice $\lambda\phi^4$ scalar field theory onto qubit degrees of freedom, examining both 0+1 and 1+1 dimensional systems. They evaluate two field-space bases: eigenstates of the field operator (as used by Jordan, Lee and Preskill) and eigenstates of a harmonic oscillator, using the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem as a guide to determine required qubit resources. They compare improved action techniques from Lattice QCD against a Quantum Fourier Transform approach for reducing digitization artifacts, and analyze the operator and gate resources required for implementation with 3, 4, and 5 qubits per spatial site.

## Key numbers

- For localized field-space wavefunctions, $n_Q\sim 4$ qubits per spatial lattice site are sufficient to reduce theoretical digitization errors below error contributions from time-evolution approximation and noisy implementation on near-term quantum devices
- For delocalized field-space wavefunctions, $n_Q\sim 7$ qubits per spatial lattice site are sufficient for the same threshold
- A 4-qubit representation of the scalar field with $\bar{\phi}_{\rm max}=4.7$ achieves precision better than $10^{-3}\%$ on the energies of the lowest 5 eigenstates of the harmonic oscillator
- The optimal $\bar{\phi}_{\rm max}$ values for digitization are 3.1, 4.7, and 6.9 for $n_Q=3$, $n_Q=4$, and $n_Q=5$ qubits respectively

## Context

This work builds upon the foundational framework for scalar field theory on quantum computers developed by Jordan, Lee and Preskill (2011-2017), and extends the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem analysis introduced by Macridin, Spentzouris, Amundson and Harnik (2018) building on work by Somma (2016). The authors connect digitization improvement techniques to the Symanzik improvement program used extensively in Lattice QCD calculations, and show that the Quantum Fourier Transform approach provides exponential convergence superior to polynomial improvement. This work enables resource estimates for near-term quantum simulations of scalar field theories on noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices, with implications for eventual simulations of gauge theories such as quantum electrodynamics and quantum chromodynamics.
