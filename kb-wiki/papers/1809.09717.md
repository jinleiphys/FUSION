# Representations of relativistic particles of arbitrary spin in Poincaré, Lorentz, and Euclidean covariant formulations of relativistic quantum mechanics

> Relativistic treatments of quantum mechanical systems are important for understanding hadronic structure and dynamics at sub-nucleon distance scales. Hadronic states in different inertial reference frames are needed to compute current matrix elements that probe hadronic structure and dynamics. Relativistic invariance is an important consideration as the resolution of the probe is increased. Many different treatments of relativistic dynamics are used in practice, including Poincaré covariant methods, Lorentz covariant methods, Euclidean covariant methods and methods based on quantum fields. Wave functions are typically matrix elements of interacting relativistic states in a basis of non-interacting relativistic states. The purpose of this work is to develop the relation between these different representations of relativistic states that are used in different applications from a unified point of view, starting with positive mass irreducible representations of the Poincaré group.

## Key claim

The paper systematically develops the explicit relations between Poincaré covariant, Lorentz covariant, and Euclidean covariant representations of relativistic quantum states for particles of arbitrary spin, showing they are equivalent by constructing the transformations that connect them, but only for positive-mass positive-energy irreducible representations of the Poincaré group.

## Method

The author starts with positive-mass irreducible representations of the Poincaré group for a particle of mass \(m\) and spin \(j\), constructing basis states as simultaneous eigenstates of a maximal set of commuting observables. Equivalent Lorentz covariant irreducible representations are then built by decomposing the \(SU(2)\) Wigner rotations into products of \(SL(2,\mathbb{C})\) matrices, absorbing momentum-dependent parts into the wave functions. Finally, Euclidean covariant Green functions are exhibited that yield all the Lorentz covariant representations without requiring analytic continuation.

## Key numbers

* The Lorentz metric tensor is defined as \(\eta^{\mu \nu} = \text{diag}(-1,1,1,1)\) (equation p.2).
* The Lorentz group has four topologically disconnected components distinguished by \(\det(\Lambda) = \pm 1\) and \(\Lambda^0{}_0 \geq 1\) or \(\leq -1\) (equations p.7–p.10).
* The Poincaré group has ten infinitesimal generators: the four-momentum \(P^\mu\) and the antisymmetric rank-2 tensor \(J^{\mu\nu}\) (equations p.11–p.12).
* The two independent Casimir invariants are \(M^2 = -P^\mu P_\mu\) and \(W^2 = W^\mu W_\mu = -M^2 \mathbf{j}^2\) (equation p.16).
* The normalization convention for single-particle basis states is \(\langle (m,j)\mathbf{p}',\mu' | (m,j)\mathbf{p},\mu \rangle = \delta(\mathbf{p}'-\mathbf{p}) \delta_{\mu'\mu}\) (equation p.30).
* The Lorentz invariant measure is \(\int d^4p \, \delta(p^2+m^2) \theta(p^0) = \int d\mathbf{p} / (2\omega_m(\mathbf{p}))\) (equation p.36).
* The light-front representation has the largest kinematic subgroup, with seven parameters (section on kinematic subgroups).
* The canonical boost has the distinguishing property that the Wigner rotation of a rotation is the rotation (section on canonical boost properties).

## Context

This work extends the foundational classification of Wigner (1939) on unitary irreducible representations of the Poincaré group and Dirac's (1949) identification of forms of dynamics with kinematic subgroups. It unifies results scattered across references including Wigner (1939), Dirac (1949), Weinberg (1964), and Osterwalder and Schrader (1973, 1974) into a single systematic exposition. The relations derived enable practitioners to convert wave functions between different relativistic formulations (Poincaré covariant, Lorentz covariant, Euclidean covariant) once a dynamical model is solved in one representation, which is relevant for computing current matrix elements in hadronic structure calculations.
