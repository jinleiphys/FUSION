# Nuclear Astrophysics in the New Era of Multimessenger Astronomy

> Neutron stars are unique cosmic laboratories for the exploration of matter under extreme conditions of density and neutron-proton asymmetry. Due to their enormous dynamic range, neutron stars display a myriad of exotic states of matter that are impossible to recreate under normal laboratory conditions. In these three lectures I will discuss how the strong synergy that has developed between nuclear physics and astrophysics will uncover some of the deepest secrets behind these fascinating objects. In particular, I will highlight the enormous impact that the very first detection of gravitational waves from the binary neutron-star merger GW170817 is having in constraining the composition, structure, and dynamics of neutron stars.

## Key claim

The detection of gravitational waves from the binary neutron-star merger GW170817 is providing fundamental new constraints on the composition, structure, and dynamics of neutron stars, but the full impact depends on continued synergy between nuclear physics theory, laboratory experiments, and multimessenger astronomical observations.

## Method

The author presents three lecture-style chapters covering: (1) a historical perspective on neutron-star theory from Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939) through the discovery of pulsars by Bell (1967); (2) the anatomy of neutron stars from the outer crust through the liquid core, including the role of nuclear masses and the symmetry energy; and (3) the connections between terrestrial nuclear experiments and astrophysical observations, particularly how the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations relate the equation of state to observable stellar properties. The work uses relativistic mean-field models calibrated to finite nuclei properties to compute mass-radius relations, and applies Bayesian Neural Network refinements to nuclear mass predictions for neutron-rich nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The Oppenheimer-Volkoff free Fermi gas model predicted a maximum neutron star mass of 0.7 solar masses (0.7 M_sun), while observed neutron stars reach at least 2 M_sun (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013)
- The Crab pulsar has a mass of 1.4 M_sun, radius of 10 km, central density of 10^15 g/cm^3, pressure of 10^29 atm, and rotation period of 33 ms
- Nuclear matter saturation density is approximately 0.15 fm^-3, with average inter-nucleon separation of about 1.2 fm
- The BNN-improved Duflo-Zuker mass model achieved a root-mean-square deviation of 369 keV for impactful r-process nuclei, compared to approximately 1 MeV for other models (WS3, FRDM-2012, DZ)
- PSR J164-2230 has a measured mass of 1.97 +/- 0.04 M_sun, ruling out equations of state too soft to support a 2 M_sun neutron star
- Photometric radius extractions have shown systematic discrepancies as large as 5-6 km between different analyses (Ozel 2010, Steiner 2010, Suleimanov 2010)

## Context

This lecture series extends the long line of work connecting nuclear physics to neutron-star structure that began with Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939) and Chandrasekhar (1931). The work builds on recent observations of massive neutron stars by Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013), and on the first gravitational-wave detection of a binary neutron-star merger by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration (2017). The results enable improved constraints on the symmetry energy and its slope L, which correlates with both the neutron-skin thickness in 208Pb and neutron-star radii, and will be further refined by upcoming missions such as NICER and additional gravitational-wave detections.
