# Proton inelastic scattering to continuum studied with antisymmetrized molecular dynamics

> Intermediate energy (p,p$'$x) reaction is studied with antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) in the cases of $^{58}$Ni target with $E_p = 120$ MeV and $^{12}$C target with $E_p = $ 200 and 90 MeV. Angular distributions for various $E_{p'}$ energies are shown to be reproduced well without any adjustable parameter, which shows the reliability and usefulness of AMD in describing light-ion reactions. Detailed analyses of the calculations are made in the case of $^{58}$Ni target and following results are obtained: Two-step contributions are found to be dominant in some large angle region and to be indispensable for the reproduction of data. Furthermore the reproduction of data in the large angle region $θ\agt 120^\circ$ for $E_{p'}$ = 100 MeV is shown to be due to three-step contributions. Angular distributions for $E_{p'} \agt$ 40 MeV are found to be insensitive to the choice of different in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections $σ_{NN}$ and the reason of this insensitivity is discussed in detail. On the other hand, the total reaction cross section and the cross section of evaporated protons are found to be sensitive to $σ_{NN}$. In the course of the analyses of the calculations, comparison is made with the distorted wave approach.

# Proton inelastic scattering to continuum studied with antisymmetrized molecular dynamics

## Key claim

The antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) model reproduces angular distributions for (p,p'x) reactions on 58Ni and 12C targets without any adjustable parameters, but the agreement depends on having sufficient event statistics (around 10,000 events for 58Ni) and the insensitivity of angular distributions to the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section σNN only holds for outgoing proton energies E_p' > 40 MeV, while total reaction cross sections and evaporated proton cross sections remain sensitive to σNN.

## Method

The AMD framework describes the A-nucleon system as a Slater determinant of Gaussian wave packets with complex coordinate parameters representing spatial and momentum centers, using the Gogny effective force with incompressibility K = 228 MeV. The time evolution is determined by a time-dependent variational principle for the mean-field dynamics combined with stochastic two-nucleon collisions, where the physical nucleon coordinates are defined through a transformation that accounts for antisymmetrization and Pauli blocking is enforced when the back-transformation from physical to wave-packet coordinates fails. After the dynamical stage (switching time 150 fm/c), primordial fragments undergo statistical decay via evaporation calculated with a code similar to Pühlhofer's.

## Key numbers

- 58Ni target: E_p = 120 MeV; 12C target: E_p = 200 and 90 MeV
- Width parameter ν = 0.16 fm^{-2} used for all calculations
- Gogny force gives nuclear matter incompressibility K = 228 MeV
- Calculated binding energies: 92.6 MeV for 12C (observed 92.2 MeV), 507.6 MeV for 58Ni (observed 506.5 MeV)
- Calculated r.m.s. radii: 2.55 fm for 12C, 3.85 fm for 58Ni
- Impact parameter range: 0 to 8 fm for 12C, 0 to 10 fm for 58Ni
- Total events: 4,000 for 12C, 14,000 for 58Ni with case-1 σNN, 3,000 for case-2 and case-3 σNN
- Three-step contributions reproduce data for 58Ni at θ > 120° for E_p' = 100 MeV
- Estimated σ_R(58Ni, 120 MeV) lies in the range 650-800 mb based on systematics from Nada (1990) and Bauhoff (1984)
- Observed σ_R(58Ni) at E_p = 60.8 MeV is 807 mb (Bauhoff, 1984)
- Observed σ_R(59Co) = 780 mb at E_p = 98.5 MeV and σ_R(Cu) = 751 mb at E_p = 113 MeV (Bauhoff, 1984)

## Context

This work extends the AMD approach, previously successful for heavy-ion collisions (Ono 1992, 1993), to light-ion reactions of the (p,p'x) type, providing a unified theoretical description that contrasts with the intra-nuclear cascade model (Kikuchi 1968, Goldberger 1948) and the semi-classical distorted-wave model of Kawai (1994). The results enable systematic studies of in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections through comparison with total reaction cross section data, and establish AMD as a tool for analyzing multi-step processes in nucleon-induced reactions up to three-step contributions, which the distorted-wave approach of Kawai (1994) had not extended beyond two-step processes.
