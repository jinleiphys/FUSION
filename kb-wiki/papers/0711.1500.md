# Quantum Monte Carlo Calculations of Light Nuclei

> During the last 15 years, there has been much progress in defining the nuclear Hamiltonian and applying quantum Monte Carlo methods to the calculation of light nuclei. I describe both aspects of this work and some recent results.

## Key claim

Quantum Monte Carlo methods, specifically Variational Monte Carlo (VMC) and Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC), can accurately solve the many-nucleon Schrödinger equation for light nuclei up to A=12 using realistic two-nucleon (Argonne v18) and three-nucleon (Illinois-2) potentials, but the calculations are limited by the exponential growth of the spin-isospin vector dimension with nucleon number, making A=16 impractical with current methods.

## Method

The authors use a two-step Quantum Monte Carlo approach: first, a Variational Monte Carlo calculation with a trial wave function containing central, tensor, and spin-orbit correlations derived from the Argonne v18 potential; second, a Green's Function Monte Carlo propagation in imaginary time to project out the exact ground state. The three-nucleon potential (Illinois-2) includes two-pion exchange (Fujita-Miyazawa P-wave and S-wave terms), three-pion ring terms, and a phenomenological repulsive term, with coupling constants adjusted to fit 17 nuclear levels for A ≤ 8.

## Key numbers

- The Argonne v18 potential fit to 1787 pp and 2514 np data points (0-350 MeV) yields χ²/d.o.f. = 1.09
- The three-nucleon potential expectation value ranges from 0.02 to 0.09 of the two-nucleon potential expectation value in light nuclei
- The AV18+IL2 Hamiltonian reproduces experimental energies with an rms deviation of approximately 0.75 MeV
- The spin-isospin vector dimension for ¹²C is 2048 × 132 = 270,336 components, requiring solution of 270,336 coupled equations in 33 variables
- For ¹⁶O the computational scaling relative to ⁸Be is 224,000 times larger, making such calculations impractical

## Context

This work extends the Green's Function Monte Carlo method developed by Carlson and collaborators (1988) from A=4 to A=12 nuclei, building on earlier Faddeev calculations for A=3 (1975-1985) and No-core Shell Model calculations for A=6-10 (1994-2002). The paper demonstrates that realistic nuclear structure requires nearly the full complexity of the Argonne v18 potential, as simplified versions (AV8', AV6', AV4', AV1') fail to reproduce essential features such as spin-orbit splittings, nuclear saturation, and the A=5,8 mass gaps crucial for big-bang nucleosynthesis. The results enable future calculations of scattering states, densities, and transition amplitudes, though four-nucleon potential effects are estimated to be comparable to current calculation accuracy (0.5-2.0 MeV).
