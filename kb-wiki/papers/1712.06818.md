# Exotic atoms at extremely high magnetic fields: the case of neutron star atmosphere

> The presence of exotic states of matter in neutron stars (NSs) is currently an open issue in physics. The appearance of muons, kaons, hyperons, and other exotic particles in the inner regions of the NS, favored by energetic considerations, is considered to be an effective mechanism to soften the equation of state (EoS). In the so-called two-families scenario, the softening of the EoS allows for NSs characterized by very small radii, which become unstable and convert into a quark stars (QSs). In the process of conversion of a NS into a QS material can be ablated by neutrinos from the surface of the star. Not only neutron-rich nuclei, but also more exotic material, such as hypernuclei or deconfined quarks, could be ejected into the atmosphere. In the NS atmosphere, atoms like H, He, and C should exist, and attempts to model the NS thermal emission taking into account their presence, with spectra modified by the extreme magnetic fields, have been done. However, exotic atoms, like muonic hydrogen $(p\,μ^-)$ or the so-called Sigmium $(Σ^+\,e^-)$, could also be present during the conversion process or in its immediate aftermath. At present, analytical expressions of the wave functions and eigenvalues for these atoms have been calculated only for H. In this work, we extend the existing solutions and parametrizations to the exotic atoms $(p\,μ^-)$ and $(Σ^+\,e^-)$, making some predictions on possible transitions. Their detection in the spectra of NS would provide experimental evidence for the existence of hyperons in the interior of these stars.

## Key claim

The paper extends existing analytical solutions and parametrizations for hydrogen atoms in extremely strong magnetic fields to the exotic atoms muonic hydrogen (p μ⁻) and Sigmium (Σ⁺ e⁻), predicting possible spectral transitions that could serve as observational evidence for hyperons in neutron star interiors, provided these atoms form during the conversion of a neutron star into a quark star.

## Method

The authors rescale the reduced mass and redefine the magnetic field parameter b in the existing hydrogen energy level equations for the exotic atoms, using the condition that the approach is valid for 100 ≤ b ≤ 10⁶. They apply the one-dimensional Schrödinger equation with an average potential derived from Landau wave functions to classify tightly-bound and weakly-bound states, and include center-of-mass motion corrections via the pseudomomentum K⊥. Transition energies and wavelengths are then calculated for different values of K⊥ using the rescaled parametrizations.

## Key numbers

- For muonic hydrogen, the ratio b/b^H is approximately 10⁻², requiring an increase of the magnetic field to 10¹⁶ G for the solutions to remain valid.
- For Sigmium, the ratio b/b^H is approximately 1, requiring no change in magnetic field relative to the hydrogen case.
- For hydrogen at K⊥ = 0, the ν=1⁻ → ν=0⁺ transition (m=0) produces radiation at approximately 147.5877 eV, corresponding to a wavelength of 8.4 nm (UV region).
- For muonic hydrogen at K⊥ = 0, the transition energy is 17848.5 eV, corresponding to a wavelength of 0.06946 nm (X-ray region).
- For Sigmium at K⊥ = 0, the transition energy is 149.832 eV, corresponding to a wavelength of 8.27 nm (UV region).
- For muonic hydrogen at K⊥ = 100 a.u., the transition energy is 17885.1 eV, corresponding to a wavelength of 0.069323 nm.
- For Sigmium at K⊥ = 100 a.u., the transition energy is 91.7076 eV, corresponding to a wavelength of 13.52 nm.

## Context

This work extends the analytical solutions for hydrogen in strong magnetic fields developed by Lai (2001) and earlier work by Ruder (1994) to exotic atoms that could form during the neutron star to quark star conversion process described in the two-families scenario of Drago (2016). The predicted spectral lines could provide new observables to discriminate between different equations of state, complementing existing atmospheric models for H, He, and C developed by Potekhin (2009) and the study of molecular ions by Turbiner (2007). Future work requires dynamical simulations of the conversion process and numerical solutions of the one-dimensional Schrödinger equation for exotic atoms.
