# The Generative Programs Framework

> Recently there has been significant interest in using causal modelling techniques to understand the structure of physical theories. However, the notion of `causation' is limiting - insisting that a physical theory must involve causal structure already places significant constraints on the form that theory may take. Thus in this paper, we aim to set out a more general structural framework. We argue that any quantitative physical theory can be represented in the form of a generative program, i.e. a list of instructions showing how to generate the empirical data; the information-processing structure associated with this program can be represented by a directed acyclic graph (DAG). We suggest that these graphs can be interpreted as encoding relations of `ontological priority,' and that ontological priority is a suitable generalisation of causation which applies even to theories that don't have a natural causal structure. We discuss some applications of our framework to philosophical questions about realism, operationalism, free will, locality and fine-tuning.

## Key claim

The paper argues that any quantitative physical theory can be represented as a "generative program" (a list of instructions for generating empirical data) encoded in a directed acyclic graph (DAG), and that the relations in these graphs should be interpreted as "ontological priority" rather than causation, because causation is too restrictive for fundamental physics where the temporal asymmetry characteristic of causation may not apply.

## Method

The authors define a generative program as any ordered set of instructions that produces empirical data conditional on inputs, with the information flow represented by a directed acyclic graph (DAG) where nodes correspond to variables (observable or unobservable) and arrows indicate the order of generation. They introduce "original vertices" as inputs that are taken as given with no further explanation, and show how probabilistic variables can be modeled by combining an original vertex with a vertex representing the probability distribution. The framework is then applied to Bell-type experiments, with DAGs showing different possible relations between hidden variables, measurement settings, and outcomes.

## Key numbers

- No specific numerical results are presented; the paper is a theoretical and philosophical framework proposal with no experimental data or quantitative predictions.

## Context

This work extends the causal modelling approach to quantum foundations developed by Pearl (2009), Spirtes, Glymour, and Scheines (2000), and Woodward (2005), as well as quantum causal modelling frameworks by Spekkens and Wood, Schmid et al. (2020), Chaves, Miklin (2017), and Pienaar (2017). The authors argue that causal modelling is too restrictive for fundamental physics because causation may be a macroscopic emergent phenomenon (as argued by Lewis (1979), Price (2005), and others), and propose ontological priority as a more general relation. The framework enables a unified treatment of operationalist and realist interpretations of physical theories, and provides new ways to analyze concepts like locality, contextuality, and free will without presupposing causal structure.
