# Investigation of the formation of superheavy elements with atomic numbers 116 and 120 through $^{50}$Ti-induced reaction

> The synthesis of superheavy elements provides crucial insights into the stability and structure of nuclei at the limits of the periodic table. This study investigates the $^{50}$Ti$+^{244}$Pu and $^{50}$Ti$+^{251}$Cf reactions as pathways to form superheavy elements (SHEs) 116 (Livermorium) and 120, respectively. Using the dinuclear system model, key parameters such as fusion probability and fusion cross section were calculated. These findings were used to examine neutron emission and survival probability through a statistical approach. The reaction $^{50}$Ti$+^{244}$Pu is explored as a continuation of experimental efforts to extend the known isotopic range of element 116, while the $^{50}$Ti$+^{251}$Cf reaction represents a frontier for the synthesis of element 120. The role of shell effects, excitation energy, and angular momentum on the production and stability of these nuclei is discussed. The results provide theoretical predictions to guide future experimental efforts aimed at advancing our understanding of the island of stability and the limits of nuclear existence.

# Investigation of the formation of superheavy elements with atomic numbers 116 and 120 through $^{50}$Ti-induced reaction

## Key claim

The $^{50}$Ti+$^{251}$Cf reaction may be more favorable for producing element 120 than the previously studied $^{50}$Ti+$^{249}$Cf reaction, with predicted evaporation residue cross sections reaching 0.34 pb for the 3n channel, contingent on the treatment of fusion barrier height, fission barrier modifications with excitation energy, and orbital angular momentum in the survival probability calculation.

## Method

The dinuclear system (DNS) model was employed to calculate capture cross sections, compound nucleus formation probabilities, and evaporation residue cross sections for the $^{50}$Ti+$^{244}$Pu and $^{50}$Ti+$^{251}$Cf reactions. The capture probability was determined by solving the equation of relative motion for colliding nuclei, including orientation-dependent nucleus-nucleus potentials with quadrupole and octupole deformations. Compound nucleus formation probabilities were obtained by solving a master equation for the evolution of mass and charge distributions of the DNS, and survival probabilities against fission during neutron evaporation were calculated using the KEWPIE2 statistical model code with Kramers correction and Strutinsky factor.

## Key numbers

- For $^{50}$Ti+$^{244}$Pu reaction: maximum evaporation residue cross section of 0.58 pb for the 4n channel at center-of-mass energy $E_{\rm c.m.}=218.8$ MeV (excitation energy $E_{\rm CN}^*=40.3$ MeV), compared to experimental value of $0.44^{+0.58}_{-0.28}$ pb at $E_{\rm CN}^*=41$ MeV from Gates (2024)
- For $^{50}$Ti+$^{244}$Pu reaction: 3n channel cross section of 0.13 pb at $E_{\rm c.m.}=216.5$ MeV ($E_{\rm CN}^*=38.3$ MeV); 5n channel cross section of 0.034 pb at $E_{\rm c.m.}=226.4$ MeV ($E_{\rm CN}^*=48.2$ MeV)
- For $^{50}$Ti+$^{251}$Cf reaction: 3n channel cross section of 0.34 pb at $E_{\rm c.m.}=221.6$ MeV ($E_{\rm CN}^*=43.4$ MeV); 4n channel cross section of 0.23 pb at $E_{\rm c.m.}=229.4$ MeV ($E_{\rm CN}^*=51.2$ MeV); 5n channel cross section of 0.12 pb at $E_{\rm c.m.}=237.1$ MeV ($E_{\rm CN}^*=58.9$ MeV)
- Optimal incident energy for $^{50}$Ti+$^{244}$Pu reaction: $E_{\rm c.m.}=218.1$ MeV; for $^{50}$Ti+$^{251}$Cf reaction: $E_{\rm c.m.}=222.3$ MeV
- Fusion barrier for $^{50}$Ti+$^{244}$Pu reaction located at charge number $Z=15$ for angular momenta $0\hbar$, $15\hbar$, and $25\hbar$; for $^{50}$Ti+$^{251}$Cf reaction, fusion barrier located at $Z=9$ for angular momenta $0\hbar$-$25\hbar$

## Context

This work extends theoretical predictions for superheavy element synthesis using $^{50}$Ti projectiles, following experimental efforts at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory reported by Gates (2024) for the $^{244}$Pu($^{50}$Ti,$xn$) reaction. The study builds on previous DNS model calculations by Nasirov (2011), Wang (2012), and Liu (2016) for element 120 synthesis, but uses the $^{251}$Cf target instead of $^{249}$Cf, finding higher predicted cross sections. The orientation-dependent fusion results agree with experimental observations by Nishio (2000), Oganessian (2004), and Nishio (2012) that equatorial collisions contribute significantly to fusion while polar collisions do not. The results provide theoretical guidance for upcoming experiments at RIKEN, GSI, and other heavy-ion accelerator facilities.
