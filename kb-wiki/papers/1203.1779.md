# The Renormalization Group in Nuclear Physics

> Modern techniques of the renormalization group (RG) combined with effective field theory (EFT) methods are revolutionizing nuclear many-body physics. In these lectures we will explore the motivation for RG in low-energy nuclear systems and its implementation in systems ranging from the deuteron to neutron stars, both formally and in practice. Flow equation approaches applied to Hamiltonians both in free space and in the medium will be emphasized. This is a conceptually simple technique to transform interactions to more perturbative and universal forms. An unavoidable complication for nuclear systems from both the EFT and flow equation perspective is the need to treat many-body forces and operators, so we will consider these aspects in some detail. We'll finish with a survey of current developments and open problems in nuclear RG.

## Key claim

The renormalization group (RG) flow equation approach, applied to nuclear Hamiltonians, can decouple low- and high-momentum degrees of freedom through a series of infinitesimal unitary transformations, thereby transforming interactions to more perturbative and universal forms while preserving all observables.

## Method

The method uses flow equations (also called similarity renormalization group, SRG) to evolve the Hamiltonian matrix elements as a function of a flow parameter \(s\) (or \(\lambda = s^{-1/4}\)), with the generator chosen as \(\eta_s = [G_s, H_s]\) where \(G_s\) is typically the kinetic energy operator. The evolution drives the Hamiltonian toward band-diagonal form in momentum space, effectively decoupling low- and high-momentum components without truncating the matrix, and the transformation is unitary so that all observables remain unchanged.

## Key numbers

- The typical relative momentum in the Fermi sea of any large nucleus is of order \(1\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) or 200 MeV.
- A dividing line between low and high momentum for nuclei is adopted at \(2\ \text{fm}^{-1}\).
- For the two-body potential in a partial-wave momentum basis, the flow equation includes a term proportional to \((\epsilon_k - \epsilon_{k'})^2 V_\lambda(k,k')\) where \(\epsilon_k \equiv \hbar^2 k^2/M\).
- The width of the band in kinetic energy \(k^2\) after SRG evolution is roughly given by \(\lambda^2\).
- For \(^4\)He, the Hamiltonian matrix dimension at \(\Nmax = 8\) is about 50,000; for \(^6\)Li at the same \(\Nmax\) it is over one million.

## Context

This work extends the application of RG methods from condensed matter (Wegner, 1994) and light-front QCD (Glazek and Wilson, 1993) to low-energy nuclear physics, building on earlier low-momentum interaction approaches such as \(V_{\text{low}\,k}\) (Bogner, 2003). The SRG evolution makes nuclear many-body calculations more like quantum chemistry by improving convergence of harmonic oscillator basis expansions, enabling techniques such as importance sampling (Roth, 2007) and opening the door to using RG-evolved potentials with quantum Monte Carlo methods.
