# Bubble nucleation in the two-flavor quark-meson model

> We investigate the dynamics of a first-order quark-hadron transition via homogeneous thermal nucleation in the two-flavor quark-meson model. The contribution of the fermionic vacuum loop in the effective thermodynamics potential and phase diagram together with the location of critical end point (CEP) have been obtained in the temperature and chemical potential plane. For a weak and strong first-order phase transition, by taking the temperature as a variable, the critical bubble profiles, the evolutions of the surface tension and the saddle-point action in the presence of a nucleation bubble are numerically calculated in detail when fixing the chemical potentials at $μ=306 \mathrm{MeV}$ and $μ=309 \mathrm{MeV}$. Our results show that the system could be trapped in the metastable state for a long time as long as the temperature is between the metastable region characterized by the up and low spinodal lines. Moreover, the surface tension at criticality will rise up to about $4 \mathrm{MeV/fm^2}$ when the chemical potential is very high. Such a small value of the surface tension would favor a mixed phase in the cores of compact stars and may have an important implication in astrophysics.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for a first-order quark-hadron phase transition in the two-flavor quark-meson model, the system can remain trapped in a metastable state for a long time when the temperature lies between the upper and lower spinodal lines, and that the surface tension at criticality reaches only about 4 MeV/fm² at very high chemical potential, which would favor a mixed phase in compact star cores. This result depends on including the fermionic vacuum loop contribution, which softens the first-order transition and reduces the surface tension compared to earlier studies that omitted this term.

## Method

The authors computed the effective thermodynamic potential at finite temperature and chemical potential in the mean-field approximation, including the renormalized fermionic vacuum one-loop contribution via dimensional regularization. They located the critical end point (CEP) by analyzing the shape of the effective potential as a function of the chiral order parameter sigma. For fixed chemical potentials of μ=306 MeV (weak first-order transition) and μ=309 MeV (strong first-order transition), they numerically solved the O(3)-symmetric bounce equation using the AnyBubble package to obtain critical bubble profiles, and from these calculated the saddle-point action S₃ and the surface tension as functions of temperature.

## Key numbers

- Critical end point (CEP) located at (T_E, μ_E) ≃ (30, 301) MeV in the T-μ plane.
- For μ=306 MeV: critical temperature T_c ≃ 20.6 MeV; lower spinodal temperature T_c1 ≃ 14.7 MeV; upper spinodal temperature T_c2 ≃ 23.1 MeV.
- For μ=309 MeV: critical temperature T_c ≃ 13.3 MeV; upper spinodal temperature T_c2 ≃ 18.8 MeV; no lower spinodal temperature exists (strong first-order transition).
- Critical chemical potential separating weak from strong first-order transitions: μ_c ≃ 308 MeV in the hadron phase.
- Surface tension at criticality rises to about 4 MeV/fm² when the chemical potential is very high.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of homogeneous bubble nucleation in the quark-meson model by Scavenius et al. (2000), which used an unphysical coupling constant and were restricted to temperatures below T_c, and by Palhares et al. (2010), Kroff et al. (2014), Mintz et al. (2012), and Stiele et al. (2016), who employed the thin-wall approximation. The authors emphasize that the thin-wall approximation breaks down far from T_c and for weak first-order transitions near the spinodal line, making exact numerical methods necessary. The inclusion of the fermionic vacuum loop, following Skokov et al. (2010) and Gupta et al. (2011), softens the transition and reduces the surface tension, which has implications for the equation of state of matter in compact stars and for gravitational wave signals from first-order quark-hadron transitions in the multimessenger era.
