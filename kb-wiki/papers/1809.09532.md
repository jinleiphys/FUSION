# Test of the Porter-Thomas distribution with the total cross section autocorrelation function

> On the basis of experiments conducted in the 1970s and earlier, it is believed that, in a compound nucleus (CN), fluctuations in transition strengths are drawn from a Porter-Thomas distribution (PTD), a $χ^2$ distribution of one degree of freedom. However, work done post-1990 at the Los Alamos Neutron Science Center (LANSCE) and Oak Ridge Electron Linear Accelerator (ORELA) facilities has yielded instances of neutron resonance data sets of superior quality that are inconsistent with the PTD. In view of the importance of the status of the PTD to the foundations of statistical reaction theory, the question arises whether other data acquired at these facilities can be mined for evidence of deviations from the PTD? To date, the focus has been on data taken in the regime of isolated resonances. In this letter, arguments are put forward in support of the analysis of measurements of the total cross section in the regime where resonances are weakly overlapping.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the total cross section autocorrelation function \(R_\text{tot}(\varepsilon)\) in the regime of weakly overlapping resonances can be used to test the Porter-Thomas distribution (PTD) for fluctuations in reduced neutron widths, potentially revealing deviations from the PTD that have been suggested by post-1990 data from LANSCE and ORELA. The hedge is that this test is proposed as a new analysis method, not yet performed, and its feasibility depends on the availability of archived total cross section data in the unresolved resonance regime.

## Method

The author advocates analyzing archived total cross section data from LANSCE and ORELA in the unresolved resonance regime, where resonances are weakly overlapping, using the autocorrelation function \(R_\text{tot}(\varepsilon) = \langle \sigma_\text{tot}(E+\varepsilon/2) \sigma_\text{tot}(E-\varepsilon/2) \rangle / \langle \sigma_\text{tot} \rangle^2 - 1\). This function is linked via the optical theorem to the two-point measure \(C_{ab}(\varepsilon)\) of fluctuations in elastic \(S\)-matrix elements, which in turn involves the variance of partial widths for the entrance channel. The author derives an expression for the dominant contribution to \(C_{aa}(0)\) within the statistical Breit-Wigner (SBW) model, assuming partial widths follow a \(\chi^2\) distribution with \(\nu\) degrees of freedom, and compares it to the Heidelberg model (GOE-based) prediction for \(\nu=1\) (the PTD limit).

## Key numbers

- For values of \(\nu\) comparable to those found in the statistical analysis of reduced neutron widths in Ref. [KBK10] (which implies \(\nu \approx 1/2\)), Fig. 1 suggests that \(R_\text{tot}(0)\) could deviate from its value in the Heidelberg model by more than 20%.
- The dominant contribution to \(C_{aa}(0)\) in the SBW model is given by \(C_{ab}^{(d)}(0) = \left( 1 + \frac{2}{\nu}\delta_{ab} \right) T_a T_b \, I_{ab}^{(\nu)}\), where \(I_{ab}^{(\nu)}\) is an integral over \(\tau\) involving transmission coefficients \(T_c\) and the number of open channels.
- In the comparison with experimental data for the reaction \({}^{37}\text{Cl}(p,\alpha){}^{34}\text{S}\), only 51 of an estimated 120 or so resonances were observed in the energy interval of interest.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing debate about the validity of the Porter-Thomas distribution, which has been challenged by post-1990 analyses of neutron resonance data from LANSCE and ORELA (Koehler et al., 2010; Koehler et al., 2012; Koehler et al., 2013; Koehler et al., 2013). Previous tests have focused on the resolved resonance regime, but the author proposes using the unresolved resonance regime via the autocorrelation function, building on Ericson's (1963) seminal treatment of cross section fluctuations and the Heidelberg model (Mitchell et al., 2010). The approach is enabled by exact integral representations for \(C_{ab}(\varepsilon)\) derived by Verbaarschot et al. (1984, 1985) and recent work by Kumar et al. (2017) on the characteristic function of cross section fluctuations, though the author notes that a parameter-free comparison with data is still needed.
