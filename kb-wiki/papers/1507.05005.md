# Time evolution of the anisotropies of the hydrodynamically expanding sQGP

> In high energy heavy ion collisions of RHIC and LHC, a strongly interacting quark gluon plasma (sQGP) is created. This medium undergoes a hydrodynamic evolution, before it freezes out to form a hadronic matter. The initial state of the sQGP is determined by the initial distribution of the participating nucleons and their interactions. Due to the finite number of nucleons, the initial distribution fluctuates on an event-by-event basis. The transverse plane anisotropy of the initial state can be translated into a series of anisotropy coefficients or eccentricities: second, third, fourth-order anisotropy etc. These anisotropies then evolve in time, and result in measurable momentum-space anisotropies, to be measured with respect to their respective symmetry planes. In this paper we investigate the time evolution of the anisotropies. With a numerical hydrodynamic code, we analyze how the speed of sound and viscosity influence this evolution.

## Key claim
The paper investigates how the speed of sound and viscosity influence the time evolution of spatial anisotropies in a hydrodynamically expanding strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma (sQGP), using numerical hydrodynamics rather than exact analytic solutions that assume constant anisotropies.

## Method
The authors solved the equations of nonrelativistic hydrodynamics with viscosity and relativistic perfect hydrodynamics using a finite volume method with a multi-stage (MUSTA) predictor-corrector flux estimation. Initial conditions were set with higher-order anisotropies in the scaling variable, and the time evolution of anisotropy coefficients (epsilon_n) for density, energy density, and velocity fields was extracted.

## Key numbers
- Example relation between scale variable anisotropies and density anisotropies: for epsilon_2 = 0.1, the direct calculation gives epsilon_2 = -0.04927, while the first-order estimate is -0.05.
- Example relation: for epsilon_3 = 0.05, direct calculation gives epsilon_3 = -0.02533, first-order estimate is -0.025.
- Example relation: for epsilon_4 = 0.02, direct calculation gives epsilon_4 = -0.00769, first-order estimate is -0.01.
- Viscous case used mu = 10 MeV·fm for the dynamic viscosity coefficient.

## Context
This work extends prior analytic hydrodynamic solutions by Csorgo (2003) and Csanad (2014) that assumed 3D Hubble-flow with no pressure gradients, where anisotropies remain constant in time. The numerical approach allows inclusion of pressure gradients and viscosity, showing that a stiffer equation of state (larger speed of sound) makes pressure anisotropies disappear faster, while viscosity makes flow anisotropies disappear faster but pressure anisotropies persist longer. This enables more realistic modeling of sQGP evolution in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
