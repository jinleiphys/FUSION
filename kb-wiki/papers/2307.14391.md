# Nuclear Reactions in Evolving Stars

> This chapter will go through the important nuclear reactions in stellar evolution and explosions, passing through the individual stellar burning stages and also explosive burning conditions. To follow the changes in the composition of nuclear abundances requires the knowledge of the relevant nuclear reaction rates. For light nuclei (entering in early stellar burning stages) the resonance density is generally quite low and the reactions are determined by individual resonances, which are best obtained from experiments. For intermediate mass and heavy nuclei the level density is typically sufficient to apply statistical model approaches. For this reason, while we discuss all burning stages and explosive burning, focusing on the reactions of importance, we will for light nuclei refer to the chapters by M. Wiescher, deBoer & Reifarth (Experimental Nuclear Astrophysics) and P. Descouvement (Theoretical Studies of Low-Energy Nuclear Reactions), which display many examples, experimental methods utilized, and theoretical approaches how to predict nuclear reaction rates for light nuclei. For nuclei with sufficiently high level densities we discuss statistical model methods used in present predictions of nuclear reaction cross sections and thermonuclear rates across the nuclear chart, including also the application to nuclei far from stability and fission modes.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a comprehensive overview of nuclear reactions across all stellar burning stages and explosive conditions, but explicitly defers detailed treatment of light-nuclei reactions to other chapters (Wiescher, deBoer & Reifarth; Descouvement), focusing instead on statistical model methods applicable to intermediate-mass and heavy nuclei where level densities are sufficiently high.

## Method

The authors derive the formalism for calculating abundance changes via nuclear reaction networks, starting from the definition of mass fractions and abundances, then presenting the reaction rate integrals for Maxwell-Boltzmann-distributed particles, photodisintegrations, electron captures, neutrino reactions, and decays. They introduce the stellar reaction rate concept accounting for thermal excitation of nuclei, and then systematically survey each stellar burning stage (hydrogen burning via PP-cycles and CNO-cycles, helium burning, and subsequent stages) identifying the key reaction sequences and their temperature-density regimes.

- The pep reaction (three-body) has τ=3.7×10¹² years, the slowest of all PP-cycle reactions
- ⁷Be terrestrial half-life is 53 days (τ=77 days), but under fully ionized stellar conditions at the tabulated density and temperature the lifetime becomes 106 days
- Solar CNO mass fraction X_CNO changed from 1.4×10⁻² (Anders & Grevesse 1989) to 9.33×10⁻³ (Asplund, Grevesse et al. 2009; Amarsi, Grevesse et al. 2021)
- CNO-cycles dominate over PP-cycles beyond T=1.75×10⁷ K for solar CNO abundances
- ⁸Be ground state decays on a timescale of 2.6×10⁻¹⁶ s via alpha emission
- Triple-alpha reaction Q-value is 6.445 MeV; ¹²C(α,γ)¹⁶O Q-value is 7.161 MeV
- Helium burning typical conditions: T=(1-2)×10⁸ K and ρ=3×10²-10⁴ g cm⁻³

## Context

This chapter serves as a pedagogical review within a contributed volume on nuclear astrophysics, extending the foundational reaction-rate formalism of Fowler, Caughlan & Zimmerman (1967) and the stellar rate treatment of Holmes, Woosley et al. (1976) and Ward & Fowler (1980). It complements the experimental and theoretical approaches for light nuclei covered in the chapters by Wiescher, deBoer & Reifarth and by Descouvement, while providing the statistical-model framework needed for the thousands of reactions involving intermediate-mass and heavy nuclei across the nuclear chart, including applications to nuclei far from stability and fission modes.
