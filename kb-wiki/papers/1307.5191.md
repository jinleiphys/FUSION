# Prompt merger collapse and the maximum mass of neutron stars

> We perform hydrodynamical simulations of neutron-star mergers for a large sample of temperature-dependent, nuclear equations of state, and determine the threshold mass above which the merger remnant promptly collapses to form a black hole. We find that, depending on the equation of state, the threshold mass is larger than the maximum mass of a non-rotating star in isolation by between 30 and 70 per cent. Our simulations also show that the ratio between the threshold mass and maximum mass is tightly correlated with the compactness of the non-rotating maximum-mass configuration. We speculate on how this relation can be used to derive constraints on neutron-star properties from future observations.

## Key claim

The threshold total binary mass for prompt collapse to a black hole is larger than the maximum mass of a nonrotating neutron star in isolation by between 30 and 70 per cent, depending on the equation of state; this ratio is tightly correlated with the compactness of the maximum-mass configuration, not simply with the radius of a 1.4 solar mass star as earlier work had suggested.

## Method

The authors performed 3D relativistic smoothed particle hydrodynamics simulations of equal-mass neutron-star mergers for 12 microphysical, fully temperature-dependent nuclear equations of state, using the conformal flatness approximation and a gravitational-wave backreaction scheme. For each equation of state they determined the threshold binary mass by running simulations with total gravitational mass incremented by 0.1 solar masses, identifying the most massive binary with a dynamically stable remnant and the least massive binary that triggers prompt collapse, then taking the average. They also tested asymmetric binaries with mass ratio 0.9 for three equations of state and compared their results against fully relativistic simulations from the literature.

## Key numbers

- Threshold mass exceeds the maximum mass of a nonrotating star by 30 to 70 per cent across the 12 equations of state.
- Maximum masses of the nonrotating configurations range from 1.95 to 2.79 solar masses; radii of those maximum-mass configurations range from 10.32 to 13.43 km.
- Threshold binary masses range from 2.95 to 3.85 solar masses across the sample.
- The linear fit between the ratio k and the compactness C*_{1.6} has slope j = -3.606 and intercept a = 2.380, with a maximum residual of 0.025.
- For the DD2 equation of state, the threshold mass determination was verified with resolutions of 731,000 and 1,202,000 SPH particles.
- For a hypothetical 1.5-1.5 solar mass binary that collapses promptly, the minimum possible k value of about 1.3 yields an upper limit on the maximum mass of 2.3 solar masses.

## Context

This work extends earlier simulations by Hotokezaka et al. (2011) that used six barotropic equations of state with a hybrid thermal treatment and suggested a relation between the threshold mass ratio and the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star; the present study shows that relation was a selection effect and that the compactness of the maximum-mass configuration provides a tighter correlation. The results enable future gravitational-wave detections of neutron-star mergers to constrain the maximum mass of nonrotating neutron stars, either from a single detection of prompt or delayed collapse combined with radius measurements, or from multiple detections that bracket the threshold mass.
