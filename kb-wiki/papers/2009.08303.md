# Detailed $β$ spectrum calculations of $^{214}$Pb for new physics searches in liquid Xenon

> We present a critical assessment of the calculation and uncertainty of the $^{214}$Pb $\to$ $^{214}$Bi ground state to ground state $β$ decay, the dominant source of background in liquid Xenon dark matter detectors, down to below 1 keV. We consider contributions from atomic exchange effects, nuclear structure and radiative corrections. For each of these, we find changes much larger than previously estimated uncertainties and discuss shortcomings of the original calculation. Specifically, through the use of a self-consistent Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater calculation, we find that the atomic exchange effect increases the predicted flux by $10(3)\%$ at 1 keV relative to previous exchange calculations. Further, using a shell model calculation of the nuclear structure contribution to the shape factor, we find a strong disagreement with the allowed shape factor and discuss several sources of uncertainty. In the 1-200 keV window, the predicted flux is up to 20$\%$ lower. Finally, we discuss omissions and detector effects in previously used QED radiative corrections, and find small changes in the slope at the $\gtrsim 1\%$ MeV$^{-1}$ level, up to $3\%$ in magnitude due to omissions in $\mathcal{O}(Zα^2, Z^2α^3)$ corrections and $3.5\%$ uncertainty from the neglect of as of yet unavailable higher-order contributions. Combined, these give rise to an increase of at least a factor 2 of the uncertainty in the 1-200 keV window. We comment on possible experimental schemes of measuring this and related transitions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the previously estimated uncertainties for the $^{214}$Pb $\beta$ spectrum, the dominant background in liquid Xenon dark matter detectors, are underestimated by at least a factor of two in the 1-200 keV window, with changes in predicted flux up to 20% lower than earlier calculations and a 10(3)% increase at 1 keV from atomic exchange effects alone.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater (DHFS) calculations of atomic exchange effects, integrating numerically over the full nuclear volume and including all $\kappa$ values, without the approximations used in prior work. They carried out shell model calculations using the NuShellX@MSU code with the unconstrained `jj67pn` model space and `khpe` interaction to compute the forbidden shape factor for the $^{214}$Pb ($0^+$) $\to$ $^{214}$Bi ($1^-$) first-forbidden $\beta$ decay. They also re-evaluated QED radiative corrections including $\mathcal{O}(Z\alpha^2)$ and $\mathcal{O}(Z^2\alpha^3)$ terms and estimated finite energy resolution effects for calorimetric detection systems.

## Key numbers

* Atomic exchange correction reaches 13% at 2 keV and 27% at 0.1 keV, compared to 4% at 2 keV from the prior calculation (Aprile 2020)
* The exchange effect increases predicted flux by 10(3)% at 1 keV relative to previous exchange calculations
* Predicted flux is up to 20% lower in the 1-200 keV window due to nuclear structure effects
* Shape factor contains an upward shift of more than 5% in the 1-5 keV window from higher-order Coulomb effects not included in prior work (Haselschwardt 2020)
* Radiative corrections introduce changes in slope at the $\gtrsim 1\%$ MeV$^{-1}$ level, up to 3% in magnitude from omitted $\mathcal{O}(Z\alpha^2, Z^2\alpha^3)$ corrections
* Combined uncertainty from radiative corrections estimated at 3.5% due to high Z value and unknown detector response
* Uncertainty in the 1-200 keV window increases by at least a factor of 2 compared to the prior estimate (Aprile 2020)

## Context

This work extends and corrects the background calculations used by the XENON1T collaboration (Aprile 2020) for their observed low-energy excess, finding significantly larger uncertainties and spectral shape changes. The atomic exchange calculations improve upon the fitted-potential approach of Salvat (1987) and the work of Mougeot (2014), while the nuclear structure calculations confirm and extend the shell model results of Haselschwardt (2020) by including higher-order Coulomb effects. The improved calculations enable more accurate background modeling for dark matter and axion-like particle searches in liquid Xenon detectors, and the authors suggest experimental schemes for measuring these transitions to validate the theoretical predictions.
