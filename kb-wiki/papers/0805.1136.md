# Rotation of the swing plane of Foucault's pendulum and Thomas spin precession: Two faces of one coin

> Using elementary geometric tools, we apply essentially the same methods to derive expressions for the rotation angle of the swing plane of Foucault's pendulum and the rotation angle of the spin of a relativistic particle moving in a circular orbit (Thomas precession effect).

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the rotation of Foucault's pendulum's swing plane and Thomas spin precession are geometrically identical phenomena, both arising from parallel transport of vectors over curved surfaces (the Earth's surface and the relativistic velocity space hyperboloid, respectively). The author explicitly qualifies that this geometric identity "does not necessarily contradict the different physical natures of these systems."

## Method
The author uses the tangent-cone geometric method, previously applied to Foucault's pendulum by Somerville and by Hart, Miller, and Mills, and generalizes it to the relativistic velocity space hyperboloid. For Foucault's pendulum, the Earth's surface is treated as a sphere and a cone tangent at the pendulum's latitude is cut, unfolded onto a plane, and parallel transport of the swing-plane normal vector is computed. For Thomas precession, the same cone method is applied to the hyperboloid of physical relativistic velocities embedded in Minkowski spacetime, where rapidity replaces the polar angle.

## Key numbers
- Foucault's pendulum rotation rate at Paris (latitude α = 41.15°, polar angle ϑ = 48.85°): φ̇_E ≈ -11.3° per hour (clockwise), compared to -15° per hour for a 24-hour day and -11° per hour observed.
- Foucault's pendulum rotation angle relative to remote stars: φ_S = 2π(1 - cos ϑ), where ϑ is the polar angle.
- Thomas precession rotation angle for one period of uniform circular motion: φ_S = 2π(1 - cosh θ), where θ is rapidity and cosh θ = γ = (1 - v²/c²)^(-1/2).
- Earth's flattening f ≈ 1/300 (where f = (a-c)/a, a = equatorial radius, c = polar radius), treated as negligible.

## Context
This work extends the geometric analogy between Foucault's pendulum and Thomas precession first noted by Émile Borel (1913), who pointed out the noncommutativity of non-collinear Lorentz transformations and provided the lowest-order estimate of kinematic precession. The exact expression for Thomas precession was derived by Ludwig Föppl and Percy John Daniell (1913), and the effect was later recognized by Llewellyn Thomas (1926) as reducing spin-orbit splitting in atomic spectra by the factor 1/2 (the "Thomas half"). The paper enables a unified pedagogical treatment of these two phenomena using elementary geometric methods, building on the parallel transport formalism of Fermi (1922) and Walker (1932).
