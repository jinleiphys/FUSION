# Probing Strangeness in Hard Processes

> Since the discovery of strangeness almost five decades ago, interest in this degree of freedom has grown up and now its investigation spans the scales from quarks to nuclei. Measurements with identified strange hadrons can provide important information on several hot topics in hadronic physics: the strange distribution and fragmentation functions, the nucleon tomography and quark orbital momentum, accessible through the study of the {\it generalized} parton distribution and the {\it transverse momentum dependent} parton distribution functions, the quark hadronization in the nuclear medium, the hadron spectroscopy and the search for exotic mesons. The CLAS12 large acceptance spectrometer in Hall B at the Jefferson Laboratory upgraded with a RICH detector together with the 12 GeV CEBAF high intensity, high polarized electron beam can open new possibilities to study strangeness in hard processes allowing breakthroughs in all those areas. This paper summarizes the physics case for a RICH detector for CLAS12. Many topics have been intensively discussed at the International Workshop "Probing Strangeness in Hard Processes" (PSHP2010) \cite{PSHP-workshop} held in Frascati, Italy in October 2010. The authors of this papers like to thank all speakers and participants of the workshop for their contribution and very fruitful discussion.

## Key claim

The CLAS12 spectrometer at Jefferson Lab, upgraded with a RICH detector and using the 12 GeV CEBAF electron beam, can enable breakthroughs in studying strangeness in hard processes, particularly for measuring strange quark distributions, fragmentation functions, generalized parton distributions (GPDs), and transverse momentum dependent (TMD) parton distribution functions, but the paper presents this as a physics case and motivation rather than reporting completed experimental results.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical and experimental status of strangeness physics in hard processes, summarizing discussions from the International Workshop "Probing Strangeness in Hard Processes" (PSHP2010). It examines how kaon identification over the momentum range 1-6 GeV with a RICH detector for CLAS12 would enable measurements of exclusive phi-meson production (for gluon GPDs), semi-inclusive deep-inelastic scattering (SIDIS) kaon production (for strange quark distributions and fragmentation functions), and exclusive kaon-hyperon production (for transversity GPDs). The authors compare existing data from HERMES, COMPASS, and global QCD analyses (CTEQ, NNPDF, MSTW, DSSV, DSS groups) to demonstrate current uncertainties and the need for new measurements.

## Key numbers

- Exclusive phi-meson production: kaons from phi decay reach momenta up to 6 GeV with 11 GeV electrons, requiring kaon identification over 1-6 GeV range
- Transverse single-spin asymmetries predicted for K+Lambda and K+Sigma0 channels at JLab12 kinematics: up to 0.4 in magnitude
- Longitudinal target/beam asymmetries predicted for same channels: about 0.05 to 0.1 in magnitude
- The strange distribution S(x) from HERMES LO analysis shows deviation from light sea quarks most pronounced around x=0.1, which is well covered by CLAS12
- NNPDF strange distribution uncertainty improved by a factor of two by comparing Drell-Yan cross section data above and below charm threshold
- Phi-meson production cross section described by GPD model over two orders of magnitude in W down to about 2 GeV

## Context

This paper extends the physics program of the CLAS12 spectrometer at Jefferson Lab, building on pioneering measurements from HERMES at DESY, H1 and ZEUS, and earlier JLab experiments (Hall A and CLAS). It directly addresses limitations in current global QCD analyses of parton distributions, particularly the poorly constrained strange quark distributions from CTEQ (CTEQ6.5Si sets), NNPDF (NNPDF1.0, 1.2, 2.0, 2.1), and MSTW (MSTW08) groups, as well as the strange helicity distributions from the DSSV group. The paper enables future measurements that would provide flavor-tagged information on GPDs through exclusive phi-meson and kaon-hyperon production, and would improve fragmentation function extractions beyond the assumptions used in KRE and AKK analyses, following the approach pioneered by the DSS group.
