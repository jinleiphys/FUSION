# Brief critical analysis of the Darwin-Fowler method

> We present a brief numerical study of the Darwin-Fowler method applied to the analysis of the energy partition of essembles of bosons and fermions. We analyze the assertion of the existence of a "strong maximum" made in the original paper of Darwin and Fowler and other studies and show that although the presumed saddle point along the real axis of the grand canonical parameters may exist it cannot, in general, be characterized as "strong", in the sense of having much larger magnitude than the other points along the path of integration. We show that in some cases the saddle point is not even present and the various approximations of the method can be interpreted as a tricky reformulation of usual thermodynamic relations. The close connection of the method with the formalism of the Laplace transform may produce wrong results if the internal energy of the components of the ensemble is not large enough. Therefore, although useful in many applications the Darwin-Fowler method may not be suitable, in general, for a detailed microscopic analysis of the nuclear structure in connection with the Shell Model approach, as it is usually done in studies of the pre-equilibrium stage of nuclear reactions.

# Brief critical analysis of the Darwin-Fowler method

## Key claim

The Darwin-Fowler method's assertion of a "strong maximum" at the saddle point along the integration contour does not hold in general for bosonic or fermionic partition functions; although a saddle point may exist on the positive real axis of the grand canonical parameters, the maximum along the integration path is typically not much larger in magnitude than other points on the contour, and in some cases no saddle point is present at all.

## Method

The author performs a numerical study of the generating functions for ensembles of bosons (Planck vibrators) and fermions, using simplified partition functions where all single-particle levels are degenerate or equally spaced. The ratio of the maximum value of the generating function at the saddle point to the minimum value along the same circular contour is computed analytically for simple cases and numerically for more realistic cases with 20 single-particle states, using the Newton-Raphson method to locate the minimum along the real axis.

## Key numbers

- For bosons with fixed total energy E and particle number M, the ratio of maximum to minimum along the contour is ρ = ((κ+2)/κ)^M exp(2κ/(κ+2)) for κ = M/E, which diverges as M→∞ for fixed κ>1 but gives lim_{M→∞} ρ = exp(2(E+1)) for fixed E, which is "not necessarily a very large ratio."
- For fermions with degenerate single-particle levels, the analogous ratio gives lim_{M→∞} |f_max/f_min| = exp(2(E+1)), identical to the bosonic fixed-energy case.
- For the simplified bosonic generating function with M=20 particles in the ground state (E=0), the maximum along the real axis is no more than 10 times greater than the minimum along the circumference containing the saddle point.
- For more realistic partition functions with M=20 and non-degenerate levels (ε_λ = λ), the surfaces are even flatter than the simplified cases, with the maximum-to-minimum ratio smaller than in the degenerate case.

## Context

This work critically re-examines the foundational assumption of Darwin and Fowler (1922) that the saddle point in the generating function provides a "strong maximum" sufficient for the method of steepest descents. It extends the concerns raised in the author's earlier work (Guimaraes, arXiv) about the applicability of the Darwin-Fowler method to microscopic nuclear structure calculations in the Shell Model approach, as used by Bloch (1968), Williams (1971), and others for pre-equilibrium nuclear reaction theory. The results suggest that while the method yields correct thermodynamic relations in many applications, its use for detailed microscopic analysis of nuclear structure may be unsuitable because the approximations can obscure details of the microscopic interaction.
