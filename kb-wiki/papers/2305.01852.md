# $Σ$ Resonances from a Neural Network-based Partial Wave Analysis on $K^-p$ Scattering

> We implement a convolutional neural network to study the $Σ$ hyperons using experimental data of the $K^-p\toπ^0Λ$ reaction. The averaged accuracy of the NN models in resolving resonances on the test data sets is ${\rm 98.5\%}$, ${\rm 94.8\%}$ and ${\rm 82.5\%}$ for one-, two- and three-additional-resonance case. We find that the three most significant resonances are $1/2^+$, $3/2^+$ and $3/2^-$ states with mass being ${\rm 1.62(11)~GeV}$, ${\rm 1.72(6)~GeV}$ and ${\rm 1.61(9)~GeV}$, and probability being $\rm 100(3)\%$, $\rm 72(24)\%$ and $\rm 98(52)\%$, respectively, where the errors mostly come from the uncertainties of the experimental data. Our results support the three-star $Σ(1660)1/2^+$, the one-star $Σ(1780)3/2^+$ and the one-star $Σ(1580)3/2^-$ in PDG. The ability of giving quantitative probabilities in resonance resolving and numerical stability make NN potentially a life-changing tool in baryon partial wave analysis, and this approach can be easily extended to accommodate other theoretical models and/or to include more experimental data.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a convolutional neural network can perform partial wave analysis of $K^-p \to \pi^0 \Lambda$ scattering data to identify $\Sigma$ resonance quantum numbers and properties, achieving quantitative probability estimates for resonance classification that conventional $\chi^2$ fitting methods cannot provide, though the main limitation remains the uncertainties in the experimental data.

## Method

The authors constructed a convolutional neural network with 8 layers (convolutional, pooling, and fully-connected feedforward layers) trained on 20 million simulated data sets generated from an effective Lagrangian model of $K^-p \to \pi^0 \Lambda$ scattering. The network simultaneously performs classification (identifying the quantum number $J^P$ of additional resonances) and regression (determining resonance parameters including mass, width, and coupling constants), with the loss function combining cross-entropy and mean squared error terms. The analysis was performed sequentially for one-, two-, and three-additional-resonance cases, with background contributions from established $t$-channel $K^*$, $u$-channel proton, and $s$-channel $\Sigma(1189)1/2^+$, $\Sigma(1385)3/2^+$, $\Sigma(1670)3/2^-$, and $\Sigma(1775)5/2^-$ always included.

## Key numbers

- Averaged classification accuracy on test data sets: 98.5% for one-additional-resonance case, 94.8% for two-additional-resonance case, and 82.5% for three-additional-resonance case
- Most significant resonance: $1/2^+$ state with mass 1.62(11) GeV and probability 100(3)%
- Second most significant resonance: $3/2^+$ state with mass 1.72(6) GeV and probability 72(24)%
- Third most significant resonance: $3/2^-$ state with mass 1.61(9) GeV and probability 98(52)%
- In the 1R case, the $1/2^+$ mass was determined to be 1.58(6)(1)(2) GeV (three errors: two systematic and one statistical)
- In the 2R case, the $3/2^+$ mass was determined to be 1.70(6) GeV for the 1p3p combination
- The product probability for the three states being $1/2^+$, $3/2^+$, and $3/2^-$ is 70(33)%

## Context

This work extends the conventional $\chi^2$ fitting-based partial wave analysis of $\Sigma$ resonances previously performed by Gao (2010) and Gao (2012) using the same theoretical framework and Crystal Ball experimental data from Prakhov (2008). The neural network approach provides quantitative probability estimates for resonance quantum numbers, which conventional methods cannot offer, and shows greater numerical stability when multiple additional resonances are included. The results support the existence of the three-star $\Sigma(1660)1/2^+$, the one-star $\Sigma(1780)3/2^+$, and the one-star $\Sigma(1580)3/2^-$ listed in the Particle Data Group, and the method can be extended to accommodate coupled-channel effects and additional experimental data.
