# QCD equation of state at finite isospin density from the linear sigma model with quarks: The cold case

> We use the two-flavor linear sigma model with quarks to study the phase structure of isospin asymmetric matter at zero temperature. The meson degrees of freedom provide the mean field chiral- and isospin-condensates on top of which we compute the effective potential accounting for constituent quark fluctuations at one-loop order. Using the renormalizability of the model, we absorb the ultraviolet divergences into suitable counter-terms that are added respecting the original structure of the theory. These counter-terms are determined from the stability conditions which require the effective potential to have minima in the condensates directions at the classical values, as well as the transition from the non-condensed to the condensed phase to be smooth as a function of the isospin chemical potential. We use the model to study the evolution of the condensates as well as the pressure, energy and isospin densities and the sound velocity as functions of the isospin chemical potential. The approach does a good average description up to isospin chemical potentials values not too large as compared to the vacuum pion mass.

## Key claim

The linear sigma model with quarks (LSMq), when renormalized via stability conditions that preserve the tree-level minima, provides a good average description of the QCD equation of state at finite isospin density for isospin chemical potentials not too large compared to the vacuum pion mass, but only when fermion quantum fluctuations are included at one-loop order and the expansion is truncated at order \(\mu_I^2\).

## Method

The authors used the two-flavor linear sigma model with constituent quarks, shifting the sigma and charged pion fields by their classical condensates \(v\) and \(\Delta\). They computed the one-loop effective potential from constituent quark fluctuations using dimensional regularization in the \(\overline{\text{MS}}\) scheme, expanding the fermion energies to order \(\mu_I^4\) but retaining only terms up to \(\mathcal{O}(\mu_I^2)\) in the divergent part. Ultraviolet divergences were absorbed by counter-terms determined from stability conditions that fix the minima in the \(v\) and \(\Delta\) directions to their tree-level values and enforce a smooth transition from the non-condensed to the condensed phase as a function of \(\mu_I\).

## Key numbers

* Constituent quark mass \(m_q = 235\) MeV, sigma mass \(m_\sigma = 400\) MeV, vacuum pion mass \(m_0 = 140\) MeV, and pion decay constant \(f_\pi = 93\) MeV were used as input parameters, yielding \(\lambda = 8.12\), \(a = 225\) MeV, and \(g = 2.53\).
* The onset of pion condensation occurs at \(\mu_I = m_0 = 140\) MeV, identified as the critical isospin chemical potential for the second-order phase transition.
* The model results were compared to lattice QCD data from Brandt et al. (2017, 2018) using scaled variables \(\mu_I / m_0\), with lattice sizes \(24^3 \times 32\) (lattice spacing \(\approx 0.22\) fm) and \(32^3 \times 48\) (lattice spacing \(\approx 0.15\) fm).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of isospin-asymmetric matter using chiral perturbation theory (Son and Stephanov, 2000; Splittorff, 2000) and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model (Frank et al., 2003; Toublan and Kogut, 2003) by employing the renormalizable linear sigma model with dynamical quarks, where mesons are treated as mean-field backgrounds rather than quantum fluctuations. The approach differs from the Cornwall-Jackiw-Tomboulis formalism used by Mao (2013) and from the counter-term scheme of Herpay et al. (2008), which allowed a shift of the condensation onset. The results enable future inclusion of meson quantum fluctuations and finite-temperature effects, and provide a benchmark for comparing effective model predictions to sign-problem-free lattice QCD calculations at finite isospin density.
