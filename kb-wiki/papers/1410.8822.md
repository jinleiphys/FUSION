# J/psi-Pair Production at Large Momenta: Indications for Double-Parton Scatterings and Large alpha_s^5 Contributions

> We demonstrate that the recent studies of J/psi-pair production by CMS at the LHC and by D0 at the Tevatron reveal the presence of different production mechanisms in different kinematical regions. We find out that next-to-leading-order single parton scattering contributions at alpha_s^5 dominate the yield at large transverse momenta of the pair. Our analysis further emphasises the importance of double parton scatterings --which are expected to dominate the yield at large J/psi-rapidity differences-- at large invariant masses of the pair in the CMS acceptance, and thereby solve a large discrepancy between the theory and the CMS data. In addition, we provide the first exact --gauge-invariant and infrared-safe-- evaluation of a class of leading-P_T (P_T^-4) next-to-next-to-leading-order contributions at alpha_s^6, which can be relevant in the region of large values of P_T,min=min(P_T1,P_T2). Finally, we derive simple relations for the feed-down fractions from the production of an excited charmonium state with a J/psi in the case of the dominance of the double parton scatterings, which significantly deviate from those for single parton scatterings. Such relations can be used to discriminate these extreme scenari, either DPS or SPS dominance.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that next-to-leading-order single parton scattering (SPS) contributions at α_s^5 dominate J/ψ-pair yields at large transverse momenta, while double parton scatterings (DPS) dominate at large invariant masses in the CMS acceptance, thereby resolving a large discrepancy between theory and CMS data. The authors provide the first exact gauge-invariant and infrared-safe evaluation of a class of leading-P_T (P_T^{-4}) next-to-next-to-leading-order contributions at α_s^6, which may be relevant at large values of P_T,min but are found to dominate only in regions currently inaccessible to experiments.

## Method
The authors computed SPS contributions to J/ψ-pair production in the colour-singlet model using HELAC-Onia, evaluating LO (α_s^4), NLO* (α_s^5 real-emission), and a class of NNLO (α_s^6, gg→J/ψJ/ψc c̅) contributions. DPS yields were modeled using equation σ^{DPS} = (m/2)(σ_{ψ1}σ_{ψ2})/σ_{eff}, with single-J/ψ cross sections from three different fits to LHCb and CDF data, and σ_{eff} extracted from fits to CMS differential distributions. Feed-down fractions for excited charmonium states were derived analytically for both DPS and SPS dominance scenarios.

## Key numbers
- CMS integrated cross section: 5.25 ± 0.52 pb (√s=7 TeV, with P_T-dependent cuts between 4.5-6.5 GeV and |y_ψ|∈[0,2.2])
- D0 SPS extracted yield: 70 ± 23 fb; DPS extracted yield: 59 ± 23 fb (√s=1.96 TeV, P_T^ψ>4 GeV, |η_ψ|<2.0)
- σ_{eff} fitted to CMS data: 5.3 ± 1.4 mb (Fit 3, using only Tevatron data), 8.2 ± 2.2 mb (Fit 2), 11 ± 2.9 mb (Fit 1 from Kom et al. (2011))
- D0 extracted σ_{eff}: approximately 5.0 ± 2.75 mb
- LHCb integrated cross section: 18 ± 5.3 pb (√s=7 TeV, P_T^ψ<10 GeV, 2<y_ψ<5)
- SPS NLO* prompt yield in CMS acceptance: 1.5^{+2.2}_{-0.87} pb; DPS prompt yield: 0.69^{+0.24}_{-0.14} pb (with σ_{eff} from CMS fit)
- Feed-down fractions under DPS dominance: F^{χ_c}_{ψψ} ≈ 50%, F^{ψ'}_{ψψ} ≈ 20%, F^{direct}_{ψψ} ≈ 35%
- Feed-down fraction under SPS dominance: F^{ψ'}_{ψψ} ≈ 46%
- Ratio of ψ' to direct SPS: 0.85, derived from |R_{ψ'}(0)|^2=0.53 GeV^3, |R_{J/ψ}(0)|^2=0.81 GeV^3, and B(ψ'→J/ψ)=55%

## Context
This work extends the analysis of Kom et al. (2011) on DPS in J/ψ-pair production and addresses the puzzle identified by Sun et al. (2014) that CMS data severely overshoot SPS predictions even at NLO. The authors provide a coherent description of both CMS and D0 results by including DPS contributions with a fitted σ_{eff} compatible between the two experiments. The derived feed-down relations for DPS versus SPS dominance enable future experimental discrimination between these mechanisms, and the first evaluation of α_s^6 contributions from gg→J/ψJ/ψc c̅ shows that NLO* accuracy is sufficient for current experimental reach.
