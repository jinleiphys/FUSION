# Influence of the hadronic phase on observables in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions

> The hadronic phase in ultrarelativistic nuclear collisions has a large influence on final state observables like multiplicity, flow and $p_t$ spectra, as studied in the UrQMD approach. In this model one assumes that a non-equilibrium decoupling phase follows a fluid dynamical description of the high density phase. Hadrons are produced assuming local thermal equilibrium and dynamically decouple during the hadronic rescattering until the particles are registered in the detectors. This rescattering of hadrons modifies every hadronic bulk observable. The apparent multiplicity of resonances is suppressed as compared to a chemical equilibrium freeze-out model, because the decay products rescatter. Therefore the resonances, which decay in the early hadronic phase, cannot be identified anymore by the invariant mass method. Stable and unstable particles change their momentum distribution by more than 30$\%$ through rescattering and their multiplicity is modified by resonance production and annihilation on a similar magnitude. These findings show that it is all but trivial to conclude from the final state observables on the properties of the system at an earlier time where it may have been in local equilibrium.

## Key claim

The hadronic rescattering phase in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions modifies every hadronic bulk observable by roughly 30%, meaning it is not trivial to infer properties of the system at earlier times (when it may have been in local equilibrium) from final-state observables.

## Method

The authors used the UrQMD hybrid model, which combines a 3+1D ideal fluid dynamical simulation of the hot and dense QGP phase with a microscopic transport description of the non-equilibrium hadronic decoupling phase. Hadrons are produced from the fluid via the Cooper-Frye equation on an iso-energy density hypersurface at approximately 350 MeV/fm\(^3\), then undergo hadronic rescattering (elastic scattering, resonance excitations, string excitations, strangeness exchange, and baryon-antibaryon annihilation) according to cross sections from the Particle Data Group and the additive quark model. The analysis focused on central and mid-central Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV, with 10000 events generated per centrality bin.

## Key numbers

- The hadronic rescattering phase lasts approximately 10 fm/\(c\) after hadronization (from about 10 fm/\(c\) to 20 fm/\(c\) total evolution time), after which the number of scatterings does not change essentially.
- Stable and unstable particles change their momentum distribution by more than 30% through rescattering; multiplicity is modified by resonance production and annihilation on a similar magnitude.
- The \(\rho(770)^0\) meson has a lifetime of 1.335 fm/\(c\); the \(K^*(892)^0\) has 4.16 fm/\(c\); the \(\phi(1020)\) has 46.26 fm/\(c\); the \(\Sigma(1385)^+\) has 5.48 fm/\(c\); the \(\Lambda(1520)\) has 12.54 fm/\(c\); the \(\Xi(1530)^0\) has 22 fm/\(c\).
- Only 42% of all annihilated baryons are actual nucleons; less than 25% of all annihilation reactions occur between two ground-state nucleons.
- The proton loss rate from annihilation is maximal at a transverse momentum of roughly 600–700 MeV/\(c\), corresponding to the maximum in the proton transverse momentum distribution.
- The detection probability for resonances shows a strong dependence on transverse momentum; for example, the \(\phi\) has the highest and the \(\rho\) the lowest probability of being detectable.

## Context

This work extends previous studies on the importance of hadronic rescattering during the freeze-out phase, including earlier work by the same group on fluctuations of conserved charges (Steinheimer, 2016) and studies by Knospe (2015) and Ilner (2016) on resonance production and particle number fluctuations. The results confirm that the hadronic phase significantly modifies resonance yields, momentum spectra, and elliptic flow, and that comparisons with experimental data from the STAR experiment (Adams, 2004; Abelev, 2008; Adams, 2006) require the full hadronic rescattering treatment. The findings enable experimental verification of the hadronic phase through resonance measurements, particularly for short-lived resonances like the \(K^*\) and \(\Lambda^*\), and highlight the need for improved treatment of multi-particle scattering and baryon-antibaryon annihilation with excited resonance states.
