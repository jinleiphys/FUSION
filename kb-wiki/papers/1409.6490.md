# Observational constraints on spinning, relativistic Bose-Einstein condensate stars

> Bose-Einstein condensates (BECs) have been proposed as candidate states of matter for the interior of neutron stars. Specifically, Chavanis and Harko obtained the mass-radius relation for a BEC star and proposed that the recently discovered neutron stars with masses around 2$M_\odot$ are BEC stars. They employed a barotropic equation of state (EOS), with one free parameter, that was first found by Colpi, Wasserman, and Shapiro (CSW), to describe them and derive stable equilibrium configurations of spinning BEC stars in General Relativity. In this work we show that while it is true that BECs allow for compact object masses as heavy as the heaviest observed ones, such stars cannot simultaneously have radii that are small enough to be consistent with the latest observations, in spite of the flexibility available in the EOS in the form of the free parameter. In fact, our conclusion applies to any spinning relativistic boson star that obeys the CSW EOS.

## Key claim

While Bose-Einstein condensate stars can reach masses as high as the heaviest observed neutron stars (around 2 solar masses), they cannot simultaneously have radii small enough to be consistent with the latest astronomical observations, even when allowing for rapid spin and the free parameter in the equation of state.

## Method

The authors used the publicly available "Rapidly Rotating Neutron Star" (RNS) code to numerically compute stationary axisymmetric equilibrium configurations of BEC stars obeying the Colpi-Wasserman-Shapiro (CSW) equation of state. They computed gravitational mass and both equatorial and polar radii for spin frequencies of 0 Hz, 250 Hz, 500 Hz, 750 Hz, and 1 kHz, for a hundred different central density values, and compared the resulting mass-radius relations against observational constraints from thermonuclear X-ray bursts and quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries.

## Key numbers

- The minimum value of the free parameter K allowed by the maximum observed neutron star mass is K = 3.475 × 10^5 cm^5 g^−1 s^−2; for K < 3.475 × 10^5 cm^5 g^−1 s^−2, the maximum mass allowed by the CSW EOS is 1.89 M_⊙ (the −3σ lower limit of the observed 2.01 ± 0.04 M_⊙ pulsar).
- The smallest radius allowed by the CSW EOS for any spin ≤ 1 kHz and masses as high as 1.89 M_⊙ is 17.5 km.
- Observed neutron star radii from thermonuclear X-ray bursts lie in the range ≈ 8–12 km (Ozel 2010), with one specific measurement giving M = 1.58 ± 0.06 M_⊙ and R = 9.11 ± 0.4 km (Guver 2010).
- Quiescent LMXB observations give neutron star radii in the range R = 7.6–10.4 km (Guillot 2013).
- The fastest known pulsar spin frequency is 716 Hz (PSR J1748-2446ad); the authors considered spins up to 1 kHz.

## Context

This work tests the hypothesis of Chavanis and Harko (2012) that recently discovered massive neutron stars (Antoniadis 2013; Demorest 2010) are BEC stars obeying the CSW equation of state. The authors extend that work by including spin and find that the predicted radii (≥ 17.5 km) are inconsistent with multiple independent observational constraints on neutron star radii (Ozel 2010; Guver 2010; Guillot 2013; Lattimer and Steiner 2013), which favor radii below about 12 km. The conclusion applies to any spinning relativistic boson star that obeys the CSW EOS, and therefore also constrains cosmological models that invoke boson stars as dark matter candidates.
