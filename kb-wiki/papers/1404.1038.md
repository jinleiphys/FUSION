# Life Cycle of Superfluid Vortices and Quantum Turbulence in the Unitary Fermi Gas

> The unitary Fermi gas (UFG) offers an unique opportunity to study quantum turbulence both experimentally and theoretically in a strongly interacting fermionic superfluid. It yields to accurate and controlled experiments, and admits the only dynamical microscopic description via time-dependent density functional theory (DFT) - apart from dilute bosonic gases - of the crossing and reconnection of superfluid vortex lines conjectured by Feynman in 1955 to be at the origin of quantum turbulence in superfluids at zero temperature. We demonstrate how various vortex configurations can be generated by using well established experimental techniques: laser stirring and phase imprinting. New imagining techniques demonstrated by the MIT group [Ku et al. arXiv:1402.7052] should be able to directly visualize these crossings and reconnections in greater detail than performed so far in liquid helium. We demonstrate the critical role played by the geometry of the trap in the formation and dynamics of a vortex in the UFG and how laser stirring and phase imprint can be used to create vortex tangles with clear signatures of the onset of quantum turbulence.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the conversion of vortex rings into vortex lines in the unitary Fermi gas (UFG) is highly sensitive to trap geometry, specifically the anisotropy and anharmonicity induced by gravity, and that this conversion can explain the "heavy solitons" observed in the MIT experiment (Yefsah 2013, Ku 2014) without requiring thermal fluctuations or finite-temperature effects.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (SLDA) simulations of the unitary Fermi gas on a 32^2 x 128 lattice with approximately 560 particles, incorporating gravitational effects through an anisotropic trapping potential with asymmetry parameter delta and anharmonicity parameter C/C0. They modeled the phase imprint technique used in experiments to generate vortex configurations, and compared results with the extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) model, a bosonic Gross-Pitaevskii-like density functional theory.

## Key numbers

- Anisotropy parameter delta = 1 - omega_y/omega_x approximately 5% in the MIT experiment (Ku 2014), with the authors using values of approximately 10%, 0.5%, and -10% in simulations
- Anharmonicity parameter C/C0 approximately 3% for moderate asymmetry simulations, and approximately 0.15% for weak asymmetry simulations
- Simulations performed on a 32^2 x 128 lattice with approximately 560 particles for the main results, and a 48^2 x 128 box with 1410 fermions for the quantum turbulence demonstration
- The experimental trap parameters from Yefsah (2013) and Ku (2014) give C/C0 approximately delta approximately 5%

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous SLDA simulations (Bulgac 2013) that showed vortex rings could explain the "heavy solitons" observed in the MIT experiment (Yefsah 2013), and directly addresses the updated tomography results (Ku 2014) that identified these objects as vortex segments. The simulations demonstrate that gravity-induced asymmetries in the trapping potential, rather than stochastic noise as suggested by Scherpelz (2014), provide a systematic mechanism for converting vortex rings into horizontally aligned vortex lines. The work enables future experimental studies of vortex reconnection and quantum turbulence in the UFG using improved imaging techniques (Ku 2014), with direct applications to understanding superfluid dynamics in neutron stars.
