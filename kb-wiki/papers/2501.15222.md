# Explainable autoencoder for neutron star dense matter parameter estimation

> We present a physics-informed autoencoder designed to encode the equation of state of neutron stars into an interpretable latent space. In particular the input will be encoded in the mass, radius, and tidal deformability values of a neutron star. Unlike traditional black-box models, our approach incorporates additional loss functions to enforce explainability in the encoded representations. This method enhances the transparency of machine learning models in physics, providing a robust proof-of-concept tool to study compact stars data. Our results demonstrate that the proposed autoencoder not only accurately estimates the EoS parameters and central density/pressure but also offers insights into the physical connection between equation of state and observable physical quantities. This framework conceptualizes the physical differential equations themselves as the ``encoders", allowing interpretability of the latent space.

## Key claim
The paper presents a physics-informed autoencoder (PIA) that encodes the equation of state (EoS) of neutron stars into an interpretable latent space, where the latent variables correspond to physically meaningful quantities like mass, radius, and tidal deformability. The key claim is that by using algebraic physics-informed loss functions (rather than solving differential equations directly), the model can accurately estimate EoS parameters while providing transparency, but this is demonstrated as a proof-of-concept using a simplified piecewise-polytropic EoS model and may not hold for EoSs with strong first-order phase transitions.

## Method
The authors constructed a dataset by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations and the tidal deformability equation for a set of piecewise-polytropic EoSs, varying the polytropic indices and the pressure at the first density threshold. They trained a custom autoencoder where the encoder maps EoS parameters (p1, gamma1, gamma2, gamma3, rho0) to a latent space of macroscopic observables (mass, radius, tidal deformability, compactness), and the decoder reconstructs the EoS parameters. The training loss combines a standard reconstruction loss with three physics-informed terms: one enforcing consistency between latent variables and TOV solutions, one enforcing the compactness relation (C = M/R), and one enforcing an empirical universal relation between tidal deformability and compactness.

## Key numbers
* The dataset was built by sampling polytropic indices gamma_i uniformly from the range [1.3, 4] and log10(p1) from [34.001, 34.900].
* The density thresholds for the piecewise polytrope were fixed at rho1 = 10^14.7 g/cm^3 and rho2 = 10^15 g/cm^3.
* The optimal training dataset size was found to be between 3000 and 5000 instances; training on a dataset of 5000 samples took from approximately 20 minutes to 5 hours on NVIDIA Tesla V100 and A100 GPUs.
* The model was trained for at least 2000 epochs with an initial learning rate of 10^-3, using weighting factors [alpha, beta, delta] = [100, 1, 0.1] for the physics-informed loss terms.

## Context
This work extends prior applications of neural networks for inferring neutron star properties from observations, such as those by Ferreira (2019), Fujimoto (2017), and Soma (2022), by addressing the "black box" nature of those models. Unlike traditional physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) which incorporate physics at the level of solving partial differential equations, the PIA framework incorporates physics as algebraic constraints, making the latent space interpretable. This proof-of-concept enables future work to apply the framework to more complex EoS models and potentially to real observational data, though the authors note the universal relation used may not hold for EoSs with strong first-order phase transitions as discussed by Raithel (2023).
