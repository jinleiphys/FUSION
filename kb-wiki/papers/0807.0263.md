# The "ab initio" approach to the nuclear equation of state: review and discussion

> We review the main components of our microscopic model of nuclear matter, which we have recently extended to incorporate isospin asymmetry. Some frequently discussed issues concerning nuclear many-body approaches are revisited and critically analysed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) method provides a reliable and internally consistent microscopic framework for calculating the nuclear equation of state, because it effectively incorporates three-nucleon forces from virtual nucleon-antinucleon pair excitations while avoiding the consistency problems that arise when explicit three-nucleon forces are added to a two-body sector described by one-boson-exchange potentials.

## Method

The author reviews the DBHF approach to nuclear matter, starting from a realistic free-space nucleon-nucleon potential (Bonn B, chosen for its pseudovector coupling and relativistic scattering equation) and applying it in the medium without readjusting parameters. The many-body calculation includes the lowest-order Brueckner series (two-hole-line diagrams) with particle-particle correlations, and describes single-nucleon propagation in the medium through a Dirac equation with an effective mass that accounts for a repulsive scalar potential. This effective-mass treatment is extended to isospin-asymmetric matter by treating protons and neutrons with different concentrations.

## Key numbers

- The repulsive effect on the energy per particle in symmetric nuclear matter from the DBHF method scales approximately as \(\Delta E \propto (\rho/\rho_0)^{8/3}\), where \(\rho_0\) is nuclear saturation density.
- The Bonn B potential is cited as the standard choice for the two-body input, though no specific numerical values for its fit to NN data are given in the text.

## Context

This review extends the author's previous work on the microscopic model of nuclear matter (Sammarruca, 2008) to incorporate isospin asymmetry, and critically analyzes issues concerning popular three-nucleon forces such as the Fujita-Miyazawa force (Fujita and Miyazawa, 1957) and the Tucson-Melbourne force. The paper contrasts the DBHF approach with other microscopic models that include explicit three-nucleon forces (such as those from Catania or the APR model) and with phenomenological approaches like Skyrme forces (referenced as Sk) and relativistic mean-field models (referenced as RMF). The work aims to complement experimental efforts to constrain the symmetry energy through heavy-ion collisions and parity-violating electron scattering experiments.
