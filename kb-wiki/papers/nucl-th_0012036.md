# Unblocking of the Gamow-Teller strength in stellar electron capture on neutron-rich Germanium isotopes

> We propose a new model to calculate stellar electron capture rates for neutron-rich nuclei. These nuclei are encountered in the core-collapse of a massive star. Using the Shell Model Monte Carlo approach, we first calculate the finite temperature occupation numbers in the parent nucleus. We then use these occupation numbers as a starting point for calculations using the random phase approximation. Using the RPA approach, we calculate electron capture rates including both allowed and forbidden transitions. Such a hybrid model is particularly useful for nuclei with proton numbers Z<40 and neutron numbers N>40, where allowed Gamow-Teller transitions are only possible due to configuration mixing by the residual interaction and by thermal unblocking of $pf$-shell single-particle states. Using the even germanium isotopes Ge-68 to Ge-76 as examples, we demonstrate that the configuration mixing is strong enough to unblock the Gamow-Teller transitions at all temperatures relevant to core-collapse supernovae.

# Unblocking of the Gamow-Teller strength in stellar electron capture on neutron-rich Germanium isotopes

## Key claim

The authors propose a hybrid model combining Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) and random phase approximation (RPA) to calculate stellar electron capture rates on neutron-rich nuclei with Z<40 and N>40, and demonstrate that configuration mixing from the residual interaction is strong enough to unblock Gamow-Teller transitions even at temperatures as low as T=0.5 MeV, contrary to earlier RPA-only studies that found significant unblocking only at T~1.5 MeV.

## Method

The authors first perform Shell Model Monte Carlo calculations in the complete (pfg9/2) shell-model space using a pairing+quadrupole residual interaction to obtain temperature-dependent single-particle occupation numbers for the parent nucleus. They then use these occupation numbers as input for a random phase approximation calculation that evaluates the electron capture cross section, including both allowed Gamow-Teller (λ=1+) and forbidden (λ=1-, 2-) multipole transitions, with the Brink hypothesis applied to the thermally averaged initial state described by a Slater determinant with partial occupation numbers.

## Key numbers

- For 74Ge at T=0.5 MeV, the SMMC calculation predicts an average of 0.52 protons in the g9/2 orbital and 2.0 neutron holes in the pf shell
- For 68Ge at T=0.5 MeV, the SMMC calculation yields 0.72 protons in the g9/2 orbital and 5.8 neutron holes in the pf shell (compared to 4 in the independent particle model)
- For 70Ge at T=0.5 MeV: 0.65 protons in g9/2, 4.3 neutron holes in pf shell
- For 72Ge at T=0.5 MeV: 0.59 protons in g9/2, 3.1 neutron holes in pf shell
- For 76Ge at T=0.5 MeV: 0.48 protons in g9/2, 1.1 neutron holes in pf shell
- The energy splitting between the g9/2 orbital and the pf-shell is assumed to be 3 MeV
- For 72Ge and 76Ge at moderate electron energies (Ee < 15 MeV), the hybrid model increases the capture cross section by nearly two orders of magnitude compared to the independent particle model
- The GT transition matrix element is multiplied by a quenching factor of 0.7

## Context

This work extends the earlier RPA study of Cooperstein and Wambach (1990), which found that Gamow-Teller transitions on neutron-rich nuclei with N>40 required temperatures T~1.5 MeV for thermal unblocking via excitation of neutrons from the pf-shell into the g9/2 orbital. The present hybrid model shows that configuration mixing from the residual interaction, captured by SMMC, unblocks GT transitions at all temperatures relevant to core-collapse supernovae (T=0.5-1.5 MeV), contradicting the independent particle model assumption used in collapse simulations by Bruenn (1985) and Mezzacappa (1993) that block all capture on nuclei with N≥40. This enables improved electron capture rate calculations for neutron-rich nuclei beyond the iron mass range, which are needed for presupernova evolution models where the electron fraction Ye is approximately 0.44 at T=0.5 MeV.
