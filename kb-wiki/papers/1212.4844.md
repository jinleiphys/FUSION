# The Cosmic MeV Neutrino Background as a Laboratory for Black Hole Formation

> Calculations of the cosmic rate of core collapses, and the associated neutrino flux, commonly assume that a fixed fraction of massive stars collapse to black holes. We argue that recent results suggest that this fraction instead increases with redshift. With relatively more stars vanishing as "unnovae" in the distant universe, the detectability of the cosmic MeV neutrino background is improved due to their hotter neutrino spectrum, and expectations for supernova surveys are reduced. We conclude that neutrino detectors, after the flux from normal SNe is isolated via either improved modeling or the next Galactic SN, can probe the conditions and history of black hole formation.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the fraction of core-collapse events that form black holes (unnovae) increases with redshift due to lower stellar metallicity at earlier cosmic times, contrary to the common assumption of a fixed fraction. This means the cosmic MeV neutrino background (CMNB) could be dominated by black hole formation even if local surveys find few unnovae, because the distant universe contributes more of these hotter-spectrum events.

## Method

The authors model the cosmic core-collapse rate using star formation rate data from Hopkins (2006) and parametrize the unnova fraction as 10% locally, evolving as (1+z) based on gamma-ray burst observations from Kistler (2009, 2013). They adopt neutrino spectra from Nakazato et al. (2012) for both supernovae (13 solar mass, solar metallicity, 100 ms revival) and black hole formation (30 solar mass, low metallicity), plus a spectrum inferred from SN 1987A data. The positron spectrum from inverse beta decay in a water Cherenkov detector is computed by convolving these spectra with the evolving rate history and cosmological parameters.

## Key numbers

- Local unnova fraction: 10% of total core-collapse rate, evolving as (1+z) based on GRB data
- SN 1987A spectrum: average antineutrino energy 12 MeV, total antineutrino luminosity 6 × 10^52 erg
- Fermi-Dirac comparison spectrum: average antineutrino energy 15 MeV, luminosity 5 × 10^52 erg
- Core-collapse rate normalization: 0.0074 per solar mass from star formation rate, assuming Salpeter IMF to 100 solar masses
- Event rates per 22.5 kton-year in Super-Kamiokande: CCSN model gives 0.95 events (4-10 MeV), 0.54 (10-18 MeV), 0.14 (18-26 MeV); CCSN plus black hole model gives 1.34, 1.25, and 0.65 events in the same ranges
- SN 1987A model gives 2.09, 1.40, 0.56 events per 22.5 kton-year; SN 1987A plus black hole gives 2.26, 1.97, 1.00 events
- For a 560 kton detector (Hyper-Kamiokande) over 10 years: CCSN model yields 238 events (4-10 MeV), 135 (10-18 MeV), 36 (18-26 MeV); CCSN plus BH yields 335, 314, 162 events

## Context

This work extends prior CMNB studies (Lunardini 2009, Lien 2010, Keehn 2010) that assumed a uniform unnova fraction, by introducing metallicity-dependent evolution motivated by gamma-ray burst observations (Stanek 2006, Graham 2012) and theoretical expectations (Heger 2002, Zhang 2007, O'Connor 2010). The authors show that the CMNB can probe black hole formation even if local searches for disappearing stars (Kochanek 2008) find few events, because the high-redshift contribution dominates. This enables next-generation detectors like Hyper-Kamiokande to distinguish unnova contributions from the diffuse supernova neutrino background, especially after a Galactic supernova provides a template for subtraction.
