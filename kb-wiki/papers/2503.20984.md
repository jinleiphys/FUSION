# Host-dependent frequency offsets in $^{229}$Th nuclear clockwork

> Recent advances in laser excitation of the low-energy nuclear isomer transition in $^{229}$Th have opened avenues for developing nuclear clocks, a novel quantum technology with exceptional performance and sensitivity to exotic physics. Here we explore the host-dependence of the nuclear clock frequency, focusing on the isomer shift induced by the difference in the nuclear charge distribution between the ground and excited nuclear states. We combine relativistic many-body methods of atomic structure with periodic density functional theory to evaluate the isomer shifts in solid-state hosts. We elucidate the critical importance of the ``relaxation'' effect in evaluating the isomer shifts. Our analysis predicts nuclear clock frequencies for various solid-state and trapped ion platforms: $ ω_\text{clk}(\text{solid state}) = 2,020,407,384(40) \, \text{MHz}$, $ω_\text{clk}(^{229}\text{Th}^{4+}) = 2,020,407,648(70) \, \text{MHz}$, and $ ω_\text{clk}(^{229}\text{Th}^{3+}) = 2,020,407,114(70) \, \text{MHz}$. We also determine the nuclear transition energy for the bare $^{229}$Th nucleus to be $ω_\text{nuc} = 8.272(22) \,\text{eV}$. Our calculated valence-band isomer shifts for different host materials constrain the nuclear transition frequencies to an 80 MHz-wide frequency window, aiding experimental searches for the $^{229}$Th nuclear transition in novel materials.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that the nuclear clock frequency for the $^{229}$Th isomer transition varies between different solid-state and trapped-ion hosts by up to approximately 80 MHz, a shift that becomes resolvable only below the 30 GHz accuracy level of current measurements but is several orders of magnitude larger than demonstrated kHz-level VUV frequency comb resolution.

## Method

The authors combine relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock atomic structure calculations with periodic density functional theory (DFT) using the VASP code to evaluate isomer shifts in solid-state hosts. They compute the valence-band isomer shift by integrating the Th-projected density of states (IPDOS) weighted by atomic isomer shifts of $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$ valence orbitals, accounting for the "relaxation" effect where the change in nuclear potential primarily affects deeply bound core electrons, which then modify the self-consistent electrostatic potential experienced by valence electrons.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear clock frequency for solid-state hosts: $\omega_\text{clk}(\text{solid state}) = 2,020,407,384(40) \, \text{MHz}$
- Nuclear clock frequency for $^{229}$Th$^{4+}$ trapped ion: $\omega_\text{clk}(^{229}\text{Th}^{4+}) = 2,020,407,648(70) \, \text{MHz}$
- Nuclear clock frequency for $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$ trapped ion: $\omega_\text{clk}(^{229}\text{Th}^{3+}) = 2,020,407,114(70) \, \text{MHz}$
- Bare $^{229}$Th nuclear transition energy: $\omega_\text{nuc} = 8.272(22) \, \text{eV}$
- Valence-band isomer shift for $^{229}$Th:LiSrAlF$_6$ GM site (4x4x2 supercell): $-234 \, \text{MHz}$
- Valence-band isomer shift for $^{229}$Th:CaF$_2$ F-$90^\circ$-F site: $-264 \, \text{MHz}$
- Valence-band isomer shift for $^{229}$ThF$_4$ site 1: $-266 \, \text{MHz}$
- Spread in valence-band isomer shifts across considered hosts and doping sites: approximately 60 MHz, widening to approximately 80 MHz with the 30% uncertainty in $\Delta R$
- Difference in nuclear charge radii between isomer and ground states: $\delta \langle r^2 \rangle^{229m,229} = 0.0097(26) \, \text{fm}^2$
- Ground state nuclear rms charge radius: $R_g = 5.756(14) \, \text{fm}$
- Predicted frequency offset between the two doping sites in CaF$_2$: 14(4) MHz
- Predicted isomer shift between the two nonequivalent sites in ThF$_4$: 4(1) MHz

## Context

This work extends prior calculations of isomer shifts in $^{229}$Th ions by Safronova et al. (2018) and Dzuba and Flambaum (2023) to solid-state hosts, which had not been evaluated previously. The predictions constrain the nuclear transition frequency to an 80 MHz-wide window for novel materials, aiding experimental searches such as the recent measurement in ThO$_2$ crystals by Elwell et al. (2025), which is consistent with the authors' solid-state prediction. The formalism also enables future ab initio determination of temperature-dependent isomer shifts, relevant for identifying nuclear clock materials with low temperature sensitivity.
