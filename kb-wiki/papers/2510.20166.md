# Probing Neutron Skin through Event-by-Event Pion Asymmetry in Heavy-ion collisions

> In this work, we propose a novel approach for probing the neutron skin thickness of gold (Au) by analyzing the event-by-event distribution of $π^{-}$ and $π^{+}$ yield differences. This is achieved through SMASH simulations of ultra-peripheral Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=3$ GeV. Our results demonstrate that the mean value of $Δn_π = n_{π^{-}} - n_{π^{+}}$, along with the Pearson correlation and mutual information between $(π^{-}+π^{+})$ and $(π^{-}-π^{+})$, all scale linearly with the neutron skin thickness. Moreover, the slope of the line connecting two distinct $Δn_π$ values in the event-by-event distribution also exhibits a linear dependence on the neutron skin thickness. The most sensitive $Δn_π$ pairs are identified as $(-1, 1)$, $(-1, 2)$, $(0, 1)$, and $(0, 2)$. These findings establish a new pathway for determining the neutron skin thickness. Finally, by comparing SMASH and UrQMD simulations under identical initial conditions, we observe that individual slope values depend on the specific collision model. However, by extracting slopes from multiple $Δn_π$ pairs in experimental event-by-event data and inferring the corresponding neutron skin thickness, one can assess which model better aligns with the true physical value.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the event-by-event distribution of the difference between π⁻ and π⁺ yields (Δnπ = nπ⁻ − nπ⁺) in ultra-peripheral Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 3 GeV can serve as a probe of the neutron skin thickness of gold, but the method retains model dependence that must be reduced by comparing slopes from multiple Δnπ pairs against experimental data.

## Method

The authors performed SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons) transport model simulations of Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 3 GeV, sampling 118 neutrons and 79 protons per gold nucleus using a 3-parameter Woods-Saxon distribution. The neutron skin thickness was varied from 0.14 fm to 0.26 fm by adjusting the neutron surface diffusion parameter dn while keeping the proton half-density radius fixed at r0 = 6.638 fm and proton diffusion parameter dp = 0.535 fm. The 20% most peripheral collisions were selected for analysis, and the event-by-event distribution of Δnπ was constructed. For comparison, UrQMD simulations were also performed for three neutron skin thicknesses (0.16 fm, 0.20 fm, and 0.24 fm) under identical initial conditions.

## Key numbers

* Neutron skin thickness values studied: 0.14, 0.16, 0.18, 0.20, 0.22, 0.24, and 0.26 fm for gold nuclei
* Collision energy: √sNN = 3 GeV for Au+Au ultra-peripheral collisions
* Proton half-density radius: r0 = 6.638 fm; proton surface diffusion parameter: dp = 0.535 fm
* Each SMASH simulation run contained 600,000 events with impact parameters from bmin to 16 fm; after selecting the 20% most peripheral collisions, approximately 210,000 events were retained per run
* Approximately 96% of π⁻ and π⁺ mesons are produced through resonance decays, primarily from Δ resonance states
* The slope for Δnπ pairs (−1, 1), (−1, 2), (0, 1), and (0, 2) shows the strongest linear sensitivity to neutron skin thickness; for example, a measured slope of 0.090 for Δnπ = (−1, 1) corresponds to a neutron skin thickness of 0.20 fm

## Context

This work extends prior studies using the π⁻/π⁺ yield ratio to constrain neutron skin thickness (Wei, 2013; Hartnack, 2019) by introducing event-by-event analysis of the yield difference rather than the ratio, which avoids issues from zero π⁺ counts in individual events. The approach contrasts with parity-violating electron scattering measurements used by PREX (2021) and CREX (2022), which yielded contradictory predictions for the symmetry energy slope. The method also differs from the STAR Collaboration's (2022) nuclear tomography approach using ρ⁰ photoproduction, which reported a neutron skin thickness of 0.17 fm for gold. By comparing SMASH and UrQMD simulations, the authors demonstrate that individual slope values depend on the collision model, but extracting slopes from multiple Δnπ pairs in experimental data can help assess which model better aligns with the true physical value.
