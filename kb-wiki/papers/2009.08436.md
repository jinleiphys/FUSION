# A global microscopic description of nucleon-nucleus scattering with quantified uncertainties

> We develop for the first time a microscopic global nucleon-nucleus optical potential with quantified uncertainties suitable for analyzing nuclear reaction experiments at next-generation rare-isotope beam facilities. Within the improved local density approximation and without any adjustable parameters, we begin by computing proton-nucleus and neutron-nucleus optical potentials from a set of five nuclear forces from chiral effective field theory for 1800 target nuclei in the mass range 12 $\leq$ A $\leq$ 242 for energies between 0 MeV $<$ E $\lesssim$ 150 MeV. We then parameterize a global optical potential for each chiral force that depends smoothly on the projectile energy as well as the target nucleus mass number and isospin asymmetry. Uncertainty bands for elastic scattering observables are generated from a full covariance analysis of the parameters entering in the description of our global optical potential and benchmarked against existing experimental data for stable target nuclei. Since our approach is purely microscopic, we anticipate a similar quality of the model for nucleon scattering on unstable isotopes.

## Key claim
The authors construct the first microscopic global nucleon-nucleus optical potential with quantified uncertainties, built from chiral effective field theory forces without any adjustable parameters fitted to scattering data. The hedge is that while the model reproduces experimental data for stable nuclei within its uncertainty bands, the authors "anticipate" (rather than demonstrate) similar quality for unstable isotopes, and the maximum energy of 150 MeV is "heuristically estimated" as the point above which theoretical uncertainties become uncontrolled.

## Method
The authors computed nucleon self-energies (equivalent to optical potentials) up to second order in many-body perturbation theory for homogeneous nuclear matter, using five different chiral effective field theory interactions at N2LO and N3LO orders with different cutoff values. They applied the improved local density approximation to fold these nuclear-matter self-energies with density distributions from Skyrme mean-field calculations for 1800 target nuclei with mass numbers 12 to 242 and projectile energies up to 150 MeV. The resulting position- and energy-dependent optical potentials were parameterized into Woods-Saxon forms with smooth dependence on energy, mass number, and isospin asymmetry, and uncertainty bands were generated from a covariance analysis of the global potential parameters using 5000 random samples.

## Key numbers
- 1800 target nuclei in the mass range 12 ≤ A ≤ 242 were used to build the global optical potential.
- Projectile energy range: 0 MeV < E ≤ 150 MeV (maximum heuristically estimated).
- Range parameters for the improved local density approximation: t_C = 1.22 fm for central terms, t_SO = 0.98 fm for the spin-orbit term.
- The real volume depth undergoes an isospin inversion where the isovector term changes sign near E = 115 MeV.
- The imaginary surface parameterization is valid only for neutron projectile energies E ≤ 40 MeV and proton projectile energies E ≤ 20 MeV.
- Uncertainty estimates were generated from 5000 random samples of the global optical potential parameters.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous microscopic optical potentials for stable calcium isotopes (Whitehead 2019, 2020) to a global framework covering 1800 nuclei, and contrasts with phenomenological potentials such as Koning and Delaroche (2003) and Varner et al. (1991) that are fitted directly to scattering data for stable nuclei. The purely microscopic approach, based on chiral effective field theory forces, aims to provide predictive power for reactions on exotic isotopes relevant to rare-isotope beam facilities (FRIB, RIBF, FAIR, Spiral2) and to r-process nucleosynthesis calculations, where neutron-capture rates on neutron-rich isotopes cannot be directly measured. The authors suggest the WLH potential can serve as a prior distribution for Bayesian uncertainty quantification incorporating empirical data.
