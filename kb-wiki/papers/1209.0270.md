# Phase diagram of neutron-rich nuclear matter and its impact on astrophysics

> Dense matter as it can be found in core-collapse supernovae and neutron stars is expected to exhibit different phase transitions which impact the matter composition and equation of state, with important consequences on the dynamics of core-collapse supernova explosion and on the structure of neutron stars. In this paper we will address the specific phenomenology of two of such transitions, namely the crust-core solid-liquid transition at sub-saturation density, and the possible strange transition at super-saturation density in the presence of hyperonic degrees of freedom. Concerning the neutron star crust-core phase transition at zero and finite temperature, it will be shown that, as a consequence of the presence of long-range Coulomb interactions, the equivalence of statistical ensembles is violated and a clusterized phase is expected which is not accessible in the grand-canonical ensemble. A specific quasi-particle model will be introduced to illustrate this anomalous thermodynamics and some quantitative results relevant for the supernova dynamics will be shown. The opening of hyperonic degrees of freedom at higher densities corresponding to the neutron stars core modifies the equation of state. The general characteristics and order of phase transitions in this regime will be analyzed in the framework of a self-consistent mean-field approach.

# Phase diagram of neutron-rich nuclear matter and its impact on astrophysics

## Key claim

The paper argues that the crust-core transition in neutron stars is not a first-order phase transition as commonly assumed, but rather a continuous clusterization process driven by Coulomb frustration that violates ensemble equivalence; the existence of a strangeness phase transition at supra-saturation densities is model-dependent and cannot be affirmed as a general feature, though it appears in many models.

## Method

The authors analyze two phase transitions in dense stellar matter using complementary theoretical approaches. For the sub-saturation crust-core transition, they employ an extended nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) model with excluded-volume interactions and self-consistent Hartree-Fock mean-field for unbound nucleons, supplemented by microscopic Hartree-Fock calculations in the Wigner-Seitz cell to extract in-medium modified mass formula parameters. For the supra-saturation strangeness transition, they study a neutron-Lambda mixture at zero and finite temperature using the Balberg-Gal energy density functional, performing Gibbs phase equilibrium constructions and analyzing the free-energy curvature matrix to identify spinodal instabilities.

## Key numbers

- The supernova core explores baryonic densities from about rho > 10^10 g cm^-3 (approximately 10^-4 rho_0) to several times normal nuclear density rho_0, with temperatures between some hundreds of keV and around 20 MeV, and proton fractions between approximately 0.5 and 0.1
- In the in-medium modified mass formula, the surface symmetry coefficient a_{Ssym} is -32 MeV with exponents gamma_1 = gamma_2 = 2
- The critical temperature for the strangeness phase transition in the schematic n-Lambda model is estimated as T_c = 14.8 MeV
- The zero-temperature instability region of the n-Lambda mixture shows three distinct zones: an isoscalar instability below nuclear saturation density, a strangeness-driven instability at high neutron density, and a symmetric counterpart at high Lambda density

## Context

This work extends the understanding of phase transitions in dense matter beyond the standard liquid-gas transition picture for uncharged nuclear matter developed by Siemens (1983) and the Lattimer-Swesty equation of state (1991). The authors build on the concept of Coulomb frustration introduced by Horowitz (2005) and Campa (2009) from condensed matter physics, and apply it to stellar matter following their own previous work (Raduta 2010, Ducoin 2011). The strangeness transition analysis extends the Balberg-Gal (1997) model and addresses the tension between hyperon softening of the equation of state and the two-solar-mass neutron star observation by Demorest (2010). The results enable improved modeling of core-collapse supernova dynamics and neutron star structure by providing a more accurate treatment of cluster distributions and phase transitions that affect electron capture rates and neutrino interactions.
