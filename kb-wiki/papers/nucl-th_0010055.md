# Incorporating Radial Flow in the Lattice Gas Model for Nuclear Disassembly

> We consider extensions of the lattice gas model to incorporate radial flow. Experimental data are used to set the magnitude of radial flow. This flow is then included in the Lattice Gas Model in a microcanonical formalism. For magnitudes of flow seen in experiments, the main effect of the flow on observables is a shift along the $E^*/A$ axis.

# Incorporating Radial Flow in the Lattice Gas Model for Nuclear Disassembly

## Key claim

For flow energies between 30 to 50% of thermal energy, as suggested by experiments, the inclusion of radial flow does not affect fragment production in a profound way; the main effect is a shift along the E*/A axis, but this shift is not constant and the flow does more than simply remove energy available for thermalization.

## Method

The authors extended the microcanonical Lattice Gas Model (LGM) by adding radial flow momenta proportional to each nucleon's distance from the center of mass, with the total flow energy taken from experimental data by Williams et al. (1997) relating flow energy per nucleon (E_f/A) to excitation energy per nucleon (E*/A). Metropolis Monte-Carlo simulations were performed on a 6^3 lattice for a system of A=84, N=48, Z=36, with neutron-proton bonds of -5.33 MeV and Coulomb interactions included. After generating thermal momenta from phase-space sampling, radial flow momenta were added vectorially, and cluster decomposition was determined by whether the relative kinetic energy of nearest neighbors could overcome the attractive bond.

## Key numbers

- System size: A=84, N=48, Z=36 on a 6^3 lattice
- Neutron-proton bond strength: -5.33 MeV; like-particle bonds set to 0
- Flow energy range considered: 30 to 50% of thermal energy, as suggested by experiments
- The exponent tau_Z (from Y(Z) proportional to Z^(-tau_Z)) was extracted and plotted against E*/A for both models with and without flow

## Context

This work extends the Lattice Gas Model (LGM) approach of Pan and Das Gupta (1995) and Samaddar and Das Gupta (2000) by incorporating radial flow, which is automatically included in transport models such as BUU (Bertsch, 1988) but must be added a posteriori in statistical models like the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (Bondorf et al., 1995), the thermodynamic model (Das Gupta and Mekjian, 1998), and the microcanonical model (Gross, 1997). The study quantitatively tests the conjecture by Kunde et al. (1995) that radial flow may do more than simply reduce the energy available for thermalization. The results enable future calculations for specific experimental cases, including very large systems such as those considered by Kunde et al. (1995).
