# Rotating quantum turbulence in the unitary Fermi gas

> Quantized vortices carry the angular momentum in rotating superfluids, and are key to the phenomenon of quantum turbulence. Advances in ultra-cold atom technology enable quantum turbulence to be studied in regimes with both experimental and theoretical control, unlike the original contexts of superfluid helium experiments. While much work has been performed with bosonic systems, detailed studies of fermionic quantum turbulence are nascent, despite wide applicability to other contexts such as rotating neutron stars. In this paper, we present the first large-scale study of quantum turbulence in rotating fermionic superfluids using an accurate orbital based time-dependent density functional theory (DFT) called the superfluid local density approximation (SLDA). We identify two different modes of turbulent decay in the dynamical equilibration of a rotating fermionic superfluid, and contrast these results with a computationally simpler orbital-free DFT, which we find can qualitatively reproduce these decay mechanisms if dissipation is explicitly included. These results demonstrate that one-body dissipation mechanisms intrinsic to fermionic superfluids play a key role differentiating fermionic from bosonic turbulence, but also suggest that simpler orbital-free theories may be corrected so that these more efficient techniques can be used to model extended physical systems such as neutron superfluids in neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first large-scale study of quantum turbulence in rotating fermionic superfluids using an accurate microscopic density functional theory, identifying two distinct regimes of turbulent decay; however, the authors note that the limited system size precludes quantitative extraction of decay parameters, and the TDASLDA still requires quantitative validation for dynamical situations against experiments.

## Method

The authors simulated the unitary Fermi gas near zero temperature using two approaches: the time-dependent asymmetric superfluid local density approximation (TDASLDA), a fermionic density functional theory that explicitly accounts for Pauli exclusion through Bogoliubov quasiparticle orbitals, and a computationally simpler orbital-free Gross-Pitaevskii equation (GPE) that models the condensate with a single wave function. Turbulence was generated by starting from a self-consistent configuration of 14 vortices in a rotating lattice, then dynamically perturbing the system by imprinting four dark solitons with phase shifts, which decayed via snaking instabilities to produce a vortex tangle. Both methods evolved the system on three-dimensional spatial grids of size 50 × 50 × 100 with lattice spacing dx = k_F^{-1} ≈ 0.78 ξ, where ξ is the BCS coherence length, trapping approximately 3500 atoms in a cylindrical geometry.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of the pairing gap to the Fermi energy in the unitary Fermi gas is Δ/ε_F ≈ 0.5, the largest known value.
- The Bertsch parameter used in the GPE model is ξ_Bertsch = 0.373.
- The initial vortex lattice contained 14 vortices; the equilibrium length L_∞ = 12L_0 where L_0 is the cylinder length.
- The vortex tangle reached maximum length at t_max ≈ 100 ε_F^{-1}, with the boundary-dominated decay regime ending at t_b ≈ 250 ε_F^{-1}.
- The GPE dissipation parameter η ≈ 0.01–0.02 was required to match the TDASLDA qualitative behavior.
- The system contained N_↑ + N_↓ ≈ 3500 atoms with Fermi momentum k_F ≈ 1.
- The maximum local spin polarization in the P = 10% case was 40–45%.
- Angular momentum L_z was conserved to an accuracy of about 1% in TDASLDA simulations.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of quantum turbulence in superfluid helium (Eltsov, 2006; Finne, 2003) and bosonic cold-atom systems (Pethick, 2002) into the fermionic regime, where detailed studies were previously nascent despite relevance to rotating neutron stars. The TDASLDA approach builds on earlier validation against experiments and quantum Monte Carlo calculations (Forbes, 2012; ASLDA-LOFF, 2012) and has been applied to ultracold atomic gases (Science, 2011; PRL, 2014), atomic nuclei (PRL, 2016), and neutron star crusts (VortexPinning, 2017). The results enable calibration of simpler orbital-free theories for modeling quantum turbulence in extended systems like neutron stars, where experimental validation is impossible, and suggest that the TDASLDA may provide a parameter-free microscopic theory for studying mutual friction between superfluid and normal components.
