# The mass of $^{101}$Sn and Bayesian extrapolations to the proton drip line

> The favorable energy configurations of nuclei at magic numbers of ${N}$ neutrons and ${Z}$ protons are fundamental for understanding the evolution of nuclear structure. The ${Z=50}$ (tin) isotopic chain is a frontier for such studies, with particular interest at and around the doubly-magic \textsuperscript{100}Sn isotope, for which the mass is a topic of debate. Precise mass values for neutron-deficient isotopes provide necessary anchor points for mass models to test extrapolations near the proton drip line, where experimental studies remain out of reach. In this work, we report the first Penning trap mass measurement of \textsuperscript{101}Sn. The determined mass excess of $-59\,889.89(96)$~keV for \textsuperscript{101}Sn represents a factor of 300 improvement over the current precision and indicates that \textsuperscript{101}Sn is less bound than previously thought. Mass predictions from a recently developed Bayesian model combination (BMC) framework employing statistical machine learning and nuclear masses computed within seven global models based on nuclear Density Functional Theory (DFT) agree within 1$σ$ with experimental masses from the $48 \le Z \le 52$ isotopic chains. The framework's resilience to new mass data gave confidence in the extrapolation of tin masses down to $N=46$. Our calculations suggest that \textsuperscript{96}Sn is a two-proton drip line nucleus and predict a mass excess of $-58\,090(800)$~keV for $^{100}$Sn, showing a preference within 1$σ$ for the mass of \textsuperscript{100}Sn derived from the $β$-delayed $Q$-value measured at GSI.

## Key claim

The first Penning trap mass measurement of 101Sn yields a mass excess of -59,889.89(96) keV, a factor of 300 improvement in precision, showing the nucleus is less bound than previously thought; Bayesian Model Combination (BMC) extrapolations suggest 96Sn is a two-proton drip line nucleus and predict a mass excess for 100Sn that agrees within 1σ with the GSI β-decay Q-value measurement but shows only a minor preference over the RIKEN value.

## Method

The mass of 101Sn was measured using the phase-imaging ion-cyclotron-resonance (PI-ICR) technique in a 9.4 T Penning trap at the LEBIT facility, following production via projectile fragmentation of a 228 MeV/u 124Xe beam on a 12C target at FRIB. Eight PI-ICR measurements over 60 hours were interwoven with calibrant measurements of 50Ti+ and 51V+, yielding a weighted average frequency ratio of 0.989635524(10) relative to 50Ti+. Bayesian Model Combination (BMC) was applied using seven global energy density functionals (three covariant, two Skyrme, two Fayans) trained on experimental binding energies for nuclei with 48 ≤ Z ≤ 52 and 50 ≤ N ≤ 58, excluding 100Sn due to the mass discrepancy, to extrapolate tin masses down to N=46.

## Key numbers

- Mass excess of 101Sn: -59,889.89(96) keV, representing a factor of 300 improvement over the AME2020 value of -60,310(300) keV
- Frequency ratio R = 0.989635524(10) for 101Sn2+ relative to 50Ti+
- Electron-capture decay energy Q_EC for 101Sn: 8626.1(13) keV
- Available energy for β-delayed proton emission (Q_EC - S_1p): 6987(13) keV
- BMC predicted mass excess for 100Sn: -58,090(800) keV, compared to -57,150(240) keV from GSI (Hinke 2012) and -56,490(160) keV from RIKEN (Lubos 2019)
- BMC predicted binding energies: 761.11(77) MeV for 96Sn, 777.02(78) MeV for 97Sn, 794.16(78) MeV for 98Sn, 809.26(78) MeV for 99Sn
- BMC predicted two-proton separation energy S_2p for 96Sn: 0.17(60) MeV
- Systematic shift from half-mass difference: δ_R = -2(2)×10^{-9}

## Context

This work extends the study of neutron-deficient tin isotopes near the doubly-magic 100Sn, addressing the mass discrepancy between the GSI (Hinke 2012) and RIKEN (Lubos 2019) β-decay Q-value measurements highlighted by Mougeot (2021). The BMC framework, developed by Giuliani (2024), is applied here for the first time to real-world rare-isotope mass data, improving upon previous Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) work by Neufcourt (2020) that placed the Z=50 two-proton drip line at 97Sn. The precise 101Sn mass constrains the 109Xe→105Te→101Sn α-decay chain and enables refined extrapolations to the proton drip line, with the magnetic moment of 101Sn and the precise mass of 100Sn identified as key future measurements.
