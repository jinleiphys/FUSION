# Three-Dimensional Simulations of SASI- and Convection-Dominated Core-Collapse Supernovae

> We investigate the effect of dimensionality on the transition to explosion in neutrino-driven core-collapse supernovae. Using parameterized hydrodynamic simulations of the stalled supernova shock in one-, two- (2D), and three spatial dimensions (3D), we systematically probe the extent to which hydrodynamic instabilities alone can tip the balance in favor of explosion. In particular, we focus on systems that are well into the regimes where the Standing Accretion Shock Instability (SASI) or neutrino-driven convection dominate the dynamics, and characterize the difference between them. We find that SASI-dominated models can explode with up to ~20% lower neutrino luminosity in 3D than in 2D, with the magnitude of this difference decreasing with increasing resolution. This improvement in explosion conditions is related to the ability of spiral modes to generate more non-radial kinetic energy than a single sloshing mode, increasing the size of the average shock radius, and hence generating better conditions for the formation of large-scale, high-entropy bubbles. In contrast, convection-dominated explosions show a smaller difference in their critical heating rate between 2D and 3D (<8%), in agreement with previous studies. The ability of our numerical implementation to maintain arbitrary symmetries is quantified with a set of SASI-based tests. We discuss implications for the diversity of explosion paths in a realistic supernova environment.

# Three-Dimensional Simulations of SASI- and Convection-Dominated Core-Collapse Supernovae

## Key claim

SASI-dominated core-collapse supernova models can explode with up to approximately 20% lower neutrino luminosity in 3D than in 2D, but the magnitude of this improvement decreases with increasing angular resolution. In contrast, convection-dominated explosions show a much smaller difference in critical heating rate between 2D and 3D (less than 8%), consistent with previous studies.

## Method

The study uses parameterized hydrodynamic simulations of stalled supernova shocks in 1D, 2D, and 3D using the FLASH3.2 code in spherical polar coordinates, with a gamma-law equation of state and parameterized neutrino heating and cooling source terms. Two model sequences are evolved with different nuclear dissociation energies at the shock to place systems well into either the SASI-dominated regime (chi much less than 3) or the convection-dominated regime (chi much greater than 3), where chi is the convection parameter measuring the ratio of advection rate to convective growth rate. Models are evolved around the critical heating rate for explosion, with random velocity perturbations or imposed dipole perturbations, and the baseline angular resolution uses 56 cells in theta and 192 cells in phi (approximately 2 degrees at the equator).

## Key numbers

- SASI-dominated models can explode with up to approximately 20% lower neutrino luminosity in 3D than in 2D
- Convection-dominated explosions show less than 8% difference in critical heating rate between 2D and 3D
- The marginally exploding SASI-dominated model S08dv (B = 0.008, chi = 1.1) explodes at t = 306 t0 in 3D
- The 2D SASI-dominated model s2d-10 (B = 0.010, chi = 1.6) explodes at t = 238 t0, while the 3D model S09dv (B = 0.009, chi = 1.3) explodes at t = 204 t0
- The convection-dominated model C07dv (B = 0.007, chi = 9.1) explodes at t = 336 t0 in 3D, while the 2D equivalent c2d-07 explodes at t = 423 t0
- The high-resolution SASI model S08L1-hr (112 cells in theta, 384 in phi) does not explode within the simulated time, while the standard-resolution version S08L1 explodes at t = 172 t0
- The transition from SASI- to convection-dominance occurs around chi approximately equal to 3

## Context

This work extends the 2D parametric study of Fernandez (Paper I, 2014) which characterized SASI- and convection-dominated explosions in axisymmetry, and builds on the linear stability analysis of Foglizzo (2006, 2007) and the parametric models of Hanke (2013) and Iwakami (2008, 2014) that straddled both instability regimes. The study systematically probes the differences introduced by the third spatial dimension in bona fide SASI-dominated explosions, which had not been previously explored. The results enable better understanding of how the SASI can contribute to explosion diversity in realistic supernova environments, particularly given that the susceptibility to SASI depends on progenitor compactness and protoneutron star contraction rate, and that current models may be underestimating SASI prevalence due to uncertainties in neutrino physics, stellar models, and the equation of state.
