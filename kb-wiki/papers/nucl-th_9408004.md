# Color Transparency Effects in Quasi-elastic Nuclear Reactions

> 7 pages, Report # DOE/ER/40427-12-N94, to be published in the Proceedings of CEBAF Workshop "CEBAF at Higher Energies"

## Key claim

Color transparency (CT) effects in quasi-elastic nuclear reactions are expected to become observable only at sufficiently high momentum transfer (Q^2 up to 12 or 15 GeV^2 for (e,e'p) reactions), and the lack of a significant CT signal in the SLAC NE18 experiment (Q^2 up to 6.8 GeV^2) is consistent with models that do predict substantial CT for the BNL (p,pp) experiment.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical treatments of color transparency, focusing on the production of point-like configurations (PLC) in high-Q^2 exclusive reactions and their subsequent expansion as they propagate through the nucleus. The time development of the PLC is modeled using a baryonic basis and an exponentiated Lippman-Schwinger equation, with the transverse size (and therefore the forward scattering amplitude) growing roughly linearly with distance traveled from the hard interaction point. The author compares these models to existing data from the BNL (p,pp) experiment at beam momenta of 6, 10, and 12 GeV/c and the SLAC NE18 (e,e'p) experiment on carbon targets at Q^2 values of 1, 3, 5, and 6.8 GeV^2.

## Key numbers

- BNL (p,pp) experiment used proton beams of momenta 6, 10, and 12 GeV/c on nuclear targets, with elastic scattering at a center-of-mass angle of 90 degrees.
- SLAC NE18 experiment measured (e,e'p) cross sections on carbon, iron, and silver targets at Q^2 values of 1, 3, 5, and 6.8 GeV^2.
- The energy transfer to the recoil nuclear system must be kept small (less than or equal to 70-100 MeV) to ensure no extra pions are produced.
- Expansion effects are vital for intermediate energies with laboratory momenta less than about 20 GeV/c.
- The DWIA describes similar (p,pp) data at intermediate energies of E_p = 1 GeV with accuracy better than 20 percent.
- The 90-degree pp elastic scattering angular distribution has an energy dependence of the form 1/s^10 R(s), where R(s) oscillates between 1 and 3 over the energy range of the BNL experiment.
- For (e,e'p) measurements, the author recommends pushing to Q^2 values up to 12 or 15 GeV^2; if no effect is seen at 15 GeV^2, CT can be regarded as inconsequential.
- For double scattering reactions (e,e'pp) and (e,e'Delta++), substantial CT effects may be observable at Q^2 values from 1 or 2 up to about 6 GeV^2.
- Spin-dependent CT calculations show the normal component of ejectile polarization approaches zero very slowly as energy increases, making polarization measurements difficult at any foreseeable energy.
- The normal-transverse response in lead-208 (from the 1H_11/2 orbital) is sensitive to CT effects at momentum transfers as low as about 1 GeV/c.

## Context

This paper reviews and extends the theoretical framework for color transparency developed by Farrar et al. (1989), Jennings and Miller (1990, 1991, 1992), and others, and compares it with the BNL (p,pp) experiment by Carroll et al. (1988) and the SLAC NE18 (e,e'p) experiment. The work enables future experimental searches at CEBAF and its higher-energy upgrade, particularly by identifying that (e,e'p) measurements must reach Q^2 of 12-15 GeV^2 to see clear CT effects, while double-scattering reactions like (e,e'pp) and (e,e'Delta++) may show effects at more modest Q^2 values (4-6 GeV^2). The paper also introduces the concept of "chiral transparency" involving pionic degrees of freedom, proposed by Frankfurt, Miller, and Strikman (1992).
