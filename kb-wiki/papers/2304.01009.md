# Relativistic second-order spin hydrodynamics: an entropy-current analysis

> We present a new derivation of Israel-Stewart-like relativistic second-order dissipative spin hydrodynamic equations using the entropy current approach. In our analysis, we consider a general energy-momentum tensor with symmetric and anti-symmetric parts. Moreover, the spin tensor, which is not separately conserved, has a simple phenomenological form that is antisymmetric only in the last two indices. Apart from the evolution equations for energy density, fluid flow, and spin density, we also find relaxation-type dynamical equations for various dissipative currents. The latter are consistently derived within the second-order theory as gradient corrections to the energy-momentum and spin tensors. We argue that this approach correctly reproduces the corresponding Navier-Stokes limit of spin hydrodynamic equations. Throughout our analysis, the spin chemical potential is considered a $\mathcal{O}(\partial)$ quantity in the hydrodynamic gradient expansion and reduces to thermal vorticity in the global equilibrium. New coefficients appearing in the generalized spin hydrodynamic equations are undetermined and can only be evaluated within a proper underlying microscopic theory of a given system.

## Key claim

The paper derives second-order Israel-Stewart-like relativistic dissipative spin hydrodynamic equations using an entropy-current analysis, where the spin chemical potential is treated as a first-order quantity in the hydrodynamic gradient expansion (O(delta)) and reduces to thermal vorticity in global equilibrium; the new transport coefficients appearing in the generalized equations remain undetermined and can only be evaluated within a proper underlying microscopic theory.

## Method

The authors start from the conservation of energy-momentum tensor and total angular momentum tensor, using a phenomenological form of the spin tensor that is antisymmetric only in the last two indices (S^{lambda mu nu} = u^{lambda} S^{mu nu}). They construct an entropy current ansatz for the second-order theory that includes a general four-vector Q^{mu} containing all second-order combinations of dissipative currents, and impose the second law of thermodynamics (partial_mu s^{mu} >= 0) to derive relaxation-type dynamical equations for the dissipative currents. The first-order correction to the spin tensor S^{lambda mu nu}_{(1)} is decomposed into irreducible tensors (Phi, tau^{mu nu}_{(s)}, tau^{mu nu}_{(a)}, Theta^{mu alpha beta}) which become new dissipative spin currents.

## Key numbers

* The spin chemical potential omega^{mu nu} is considered an O(delta) quantity in the hydrodynamic gradient expansion, consistent with thermal vorticity scaling as O(delta) in global equilibrium.
* The first-order (Navier-Stokes) constitutive relations contain five positive transport coefficients: kappa >= 0, lambda >= 0, eta >= 0, zeta >= 0, and gamma >= 0.
* The shear stress tensor is given by pi^{mu nu} = 2 eta sigma^{mu nu}, where sigma^{mu nu} = nabla^{(mu} u^{nu)} - (1/3) theta Delta^{mu nu}.
* The bulk pressure is Pi = zeta theta.
* The antisymmetric dissipative tensor is phi^{mu nu} = gamma (Omega^{mu nu} + 2 beta omega^{<mu><nu>}) = tilde{gamma} (2 nabla^{[mu} u^{nu]} + 4 omega^{<mu><nu>}), with tilde{gamma} = beta gamma/2.

## Context

This work extends the first-order (Navier-Stokes) spin hydrodynamic framework developed by Hattori (2019), Fukushima (2020), and Daher (2022), which was shown to be unstable under linear perturbations in the fluid rest frame by Daher (2022) and Sarwar (2022). The second-order Israel-Stewart approach is motivated by the fact that second-order theories for spinless fluids have been argued to be free of stability and causality issues (Hiscock 1983, 1987; Kovtun 2019; Bemfica 2019). This entropy-current derivation provides a model-independent complement to the kinetic-theory approach of Weickgenannt (2022) for deriving second-order spin hydrodynamic equations, and the resulting framework enables future stability and causality analyses as well as phenomenological applications to relativistic heavy-ion collisions.
