# Non-congruent phase transitions in strongly interacting matter within the Quantum van der Waals model

> The non-congruent liquid-gas phase transition (LGPT) in asymmetric nuclear matter is studied using the recently developed Quantum van der Waals model in the grand canonical ensemble. Different values of the electric-to-baryon charge ratio, $Q/B$, are considered. This non-congruent LGPT exhibits several features which are not present in the congruent LGPT of symmetric nuclear matter. These include a continuous phase transformation, a change in the location of the critical point, and the separation of the critical point and the endpoints. The effects which are associated with the non-congruent LGPT become negligible for the following cases: when $Q/B$ approaches its limiting values, $0.5$ or $0$, or if quantum statistical effects can be neglected. The latter situation is realized when the particle degeneracy attains large values, $g\gtrsim 10$.

## Key claim

The non-congruent liquid-gas phase transition in asymmetric nuclear matter, studied within the Quantum van der Waals model, exhibits a continuous phase transformation, a shift in the critical point location, and a separation of the critical point from the temperature and pressure endpoints; these non-congruent effects become negligible only when the electric-to-baryon charge ratio approaches 0.5 or 0, or when quantum statistical effects are suppressed by large degeneracy factors (g ≳ 10).

## Method

The authors employed the Quantum van der Waals (QvdW) model in the grand canonical ensemble, treating neutrons and protons as interacting Fermi gases with equal masses (938 MeV) and equal interaction parameters (attraction a = 329 MeV fm³, repulsion b = 3.42 fm³). For a fixed electric-to-baryon charge ratio Q/B, they solved a self-consistent system of four transcendental equations, for pressure, shifted baryon chemical potential, baryon density, and the charge-ratio constraint, to determine the thermodynamic functions and phase coexistence boundaries. The non-congruent phase transition was analyzed by imposing Gibbs equilibrium conditions between gaseous and liquid phases while conserving total B and Q charges, with the local asymmetry in the infinitesimal fractions allowed to differ from the global Q/B.

## Key numbers

- Critical point for symmetric nuclear matter (Q/B = 0.5): Tc = 19.7 MeV, nc = 0.072 fm⁻³, pc = 0.52 MeV fm⁻³.
- Critical point for pure neutron matter (Q/B = 0): Tc = 10.8 MeV, nc = 0.051 fm⁻³, pc = 0.197 MeV fm⁻³.
- Ground state binding energy for symmetric nuclear matter: ε^GS / n_B^GS ≅ 922 MeV (Eb = −16 MeV relative to nucleon mass).
- Ground state baryon density for symmetric nuclear matter: n_B^GS ≅ 0.16 fm⁻³.
- Ground state for pure neutron matter: ε^GS / n_B^GS ≅ 938.33 MeV (Eb = +0.33 MeV, not self-bound), n_B^GS ≅ 0.10 fm⁻³.
- Critical point for Q/B = 0.3: Tc = 18.72 MeV, nc = 0.068 fm⁻³, pc = 0.49 MeV fm⁻³.
- Boltzmann approximation critical point: Tc^boltz = 28.54 MeV, nc^boltz = 0.098 fm⁻³, pc^boltz = 1.045 MeV fm⁻³.
- For large degeneracy g = 1000: Tc = 28.50 MeV, nc = 0.098 fm⁻³, pc = 1.042 MeV fm⁻³, close to the Boltzmann limit.
- For g = 10: Tc = 27.76 MeV, showing quantum effects become negligible for g ≳ 10.

## Context

This work extends the Quantum van der Waals model, previously applied to symmetric nuclear matter by Vovchenko (2015), to asymmetric nuclear matter with a fixed electric-to-baryon charge ratio, following the theoretical framework for non-congruent (Gibbs) phase transitions established by Iosilevskiy (2010) and Glendenning (1992). The results demonstrate isospin distillation, the liquid fraction becomes more symmetric than the gaseous fraction, analogous to the strangeness distillation mechanism proposed by Greiner (1987, 1988, 1991) for the hadron-to-quark-gluon plasma transition. The findings enable future studies of fluctuation susceptibilities in asymmetric matter and applications to neutron star mergers and heavy-ion collisions, where non-congruent phase transitions with multiple conserved charges are relevant.
