# The GSI anomaly

> Recently, an experiment at GSI Darmstadt has observed oscillating decay rates of heavy ions. Several controversial attempts have been made to explain this effect in terms of neutrino mixing. We briefly describe the experimental results, give an overview of the literature, and show that the effect cannot be due to neutrino mixing. If the effect survives, it could, however, be explained by hypothetical internal excitations of the mother ions (~ 10^(-15) eV).

## Key claim

The oscillating decay rates of heavy ions observed at GSI cannot be caused by neutrino mixing, because the detection process sums over neutrino mass eigenstates incoherently rather than coherently. If the effect is real, it could instead be explained by hypothetical internal excitations of the mother ions with energy splittings on the order of 10^(-15) eV.

## Method

The authors use the density matrix formalism and Gaussian wave packet techniques to compute the transition amplitude for electron capture decay of hydrogen-like ions, treating the unobserved neutrino as a final state that must be summed incoherently. They expand the complex phases in the momentum integrals to first order, neglect wave packet spreading, and evaluate the resulting Gaussian integrals to obtain the decay probability. They then construct a hypothetical scenario where the mother ion is produced in a coherent superposition of sublevels, leading to quantum beats that could produce the observed oscillation.

## Key numbers

- The observed oscillation period in the GSI experiment is T ~ 7 s.
- To explain the GSI oscillations via quantum beats, the required mass splitting between mother ion sublevels is |m^(2) - m^(1)| ~ 8.4 x 10^(-16) eV, corresponding to Δm^2 ~ 2.2 x 10^(-4) eV^2.
- The GSI experiment reported evidence for the anomalous decay law at 99% confidence level.
- The experiment studied electron capture decays of hydrogen-like 140Pr58+ and 142Pm60+ ions.

## Context

This work refutes earlier attempts by Ivanov (2008), Faber (2008), and Lipkin (2008) to explain the GSI anomaly through neutrino mixing, showing that those calculations incorrectly sum amplitudes coherently rather than incoherently. The analysis agrees with arguments by Giunti (2008), Burkhardt (2008), and Peshkin (2008) that neutrino mixing cannot produce the observed oscillations. The paper enables future searches for new physics in nuclear decay experiments, particularly the possibility of internal excitations of mother ions, while noting that subsequent experiments with stopped atoms by Vetter (2008) and Faestermann (2008) found no oscillatory signature.
