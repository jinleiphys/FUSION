# The full weak charge density distribution of 48Ca from parity violating electron scattering

> Background: The ground state neutron density of a medium mass nucleus contains fundamental nuclear structure information and is at present relatively poorly known. Purpose: We explore if parity violating elastic electron scattering can provide a feasible and model independent way to determine not just the neutron radius but the full radial shape of the neutron density $ρ_n(r)$ and the weak charge density $ρ_W(r)$ of a nucleus. Methods: We expand the weak charge density of $^{48}$Ca in a model independent Fourier Bessel series and calculate the statistical errors in the individual coefficients that might be obtainable in a model parity violating electron scattering experiment. Results: We find that it is feasible to determine roughly six Fourier Bessel coefficients of the weak charge density of 48Ca within a reasonable amount of beam time. However, it would likely be much harder to determine the full weak density of a significantly heavier nucleus such as 208Pb. Conclusions: Parity violating elastic electron scattering can determine the full weak charge density of a medium mass nucleus in a model independent way. This weak density contains fundamental information on the size, surface thickness, shell oscillations, and saturation density of the neutron distribution in a nucleus. The measured $ρ_W(r)$, combined with the previously known charge density $ρ_{ch}(r)$, will literally provide a detailed textbook picture of where the neutrons and protons are located in an atomic nucleus.

## Key claim

Parity violating elastic electron scattering can determine the full radial shape of the weak charge density of a medium mass nucleus like ⁴⁸Ca in a model independent way, but this approach would likely be much harder for a significantly heavier nucleus such as ²⁰⁸Pb because more Fourier Bessel coefficients would be needed.

## Method

The authors expand the weak charge density ρ_W(r) of ⁴⁸Ca in a model independent Fourier Bessel series truncated at n_max=6 terms, with a cutoff radius R_max=7 fm. They calculate the statistical errors in the individual Fourier Bessel coefficients that could be obtained from measuring the parity violating asymmetry A_pv at five specific momentum transfers q_i, using experimental parameters similar to the CREX experiment at Jefferson Laboratory, with a total measurement time of 60 days.

## Key numbers

- R_max = 7 fm is chosen as the cutoff radius for the Fourier Bessel expansion, since the weak charge density from many density functionals is small for r > 7 fm
- n_max = 6 Fourier Bessel coefficients are used; for the FSU-Gold model, expansion coefficients |a_i| for i > 6 are all < 7×10⁻⁴ fm⁻³
- The total weak charge of ⁴⁸Ca is Q_W = -26.216 (including radiative corrections Q_n = -0.9878, Q_p = 0.0721)
- The model FSU-Gold weak density has 1.8×10⁻³ of the weak charge at r > R_max = 7 fm
- Measurement times for the five q_i points range from 5 days (q=0.90 fm⁻¹) to 23 days (q=2.69 fm⁻¹), totaling 60 days
- Fractional statistical errors Δa_i/a_i range from 5.9% (a₂) to 90% (a₆) for the Fourier Bessel coefficients
- Beam energy ranges from 2.06 GeV to 4 GeV across the five measurement points
- The cross section at the highest momentum transfer (q=2.69 fm⁻¹) is 9.7×10⁻⁶ mb

## Context

This work extends the program of measuring neutron radii via parity violating electron scattering, which was pioneered by the PREX experiment at Jefferson Laboratory that measured the neutron radius of ²⁰⁸Pb (Horowitz et al., 2001; PREX collaboration, 2012). The authors propose going beyond a single radius measurement to determine the full radial shape of the weak density, building on the approved CREX experiment for ⁴⁸Ca. The resulting weak density can be directly compared to modern microscopic calculations using Chiral effective field theory interactions (Hagen et al., 2015), and when combined with the known charge density from elastic electron scattering (De Vries et al., 1987), will provide a detailed picture of where neutrons and protons are located in the nucleus.
