# Nuclear Theory and Science of the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams

> The Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) will be a world-leading laboratory for the study of nuclear structure, reactions and astrophysics. Experiments with intense beams of rare isotopes produced at FRIB will guide us toward a comprehensive description of nuclei, elucidate the origin of the elements in the cosmos, help provide an understanding of matter in neutron stars, and establish the scientific foundation for innovative applications of nuclear science to society. FRIB will be essential for gaining access to key regions of the nuclear chart, where the measured nuclear properties will challenge established concepts, and highlight shortcomings and needed modifications to current theory. Conversely, nuclear theory will play a critical role in providing the intellectual framework for the science at FRIB, and will provide invaluable guidance to FRIB's experimental programs. This article overviews the broad scope of the FRIB theory effort, which reaches beyond the traditional fields of nuclear structure and reactions, and nuclear astrophysics, to explore exciting interdisciplinary boundaries with other areas. \keywords{Nuclear Structure and Reactions. Nuclear Astrophysics. Fundamental Interactions. High Performance Computing. Rare Isotopes. Radioactive Beams.

## Key claim

The Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) will enable access to nearly 80% of all possible isotopes of elements up to uranium, but current theoretical models diverge significantly when extrapolating into the neutron-rich regions that FRIB will explore, meaning that the interplay between new experimental data and improved theory is essential for achieving predictive capability with quantified uncertainties.

## Method

The paper surveys the theoretical framework needed to support the FRIB experimental program, covering ab initio methods (such as coupled cluster and no-core shell model), nuclear density functional theory, configuration interaction approaches, and reaction theory. It describes how these methods will be applied to nuclear structure, reactions, nuclear astrophysics, and fundamental symmetries, with emphasis on uncertainty quantification through statistical tools and high-performance computing. The authors also outline organizational structures for the FRIB theory community, including the UNEDF and NUCLEI SciDAC projects that integrate nuclear theorists with applied mathematicians and computer scientists.

## Key numbers

- FRIB will deliver primary beams with 400 kW minimum power at energies of at least 200 MeV/u for rare isotope production
- Reaccelerated beam energies of 12–20 MeV/u will be reached, enabling full complement of direct reactions including high-ℓ transfer and deep-inelastic reactions
- Nearly 80% of all isotopes of elements up to uranium (Z=92) may become available for study at FRIB
- In-flight production and reacceleration will provide isotope availability with efficiencies approaching 10 to 20%
- For nickel isotopes, useful yields will be available from ⁴⁸Ni to ⁸⁴Ni, spanning the full range of neutrons in the pfg-shells
- The neutron drip line for zirconium isotopes is predicted between N=84 (FRDM and SLy4 models) and N=96 (UNEDF0 model), showing a model systematic error of 12 neutrons
- A neutron star mass measurement of M=1.97(4) M_solar provides a strong constraint on theoretical models
- The typical mass of a neutron star is about 1.4 solar masses, and the typical radius is thought to be about 12 km
- For elastic n-³H scattering, uncertainties in the differential cross section need to be of order 5% to reliably infer fuel density for inertially confined fusion experiments

## Context

This paper extends the science case developed in the U.S. National Academy of Science decadal survey "Exploring the Heart of Matter" (2012) and the earlier FRIB planning documents such as the NSAC Long Range Plan (2007) and the Rare Isotope Science Assessment Committee (RISAC) report. It builds on the computational framework established by the UNEDF SciDAC project and its successor NUCLEI, which joined nuclear theorists with computer scientists and applied mathematicians. The paper enables a coordinated theory effort to guide the FRIB experimental program, particularly for determining the equation of state of neutron-rich matter, understanding the r-process nucleosynthesis sites (supernovae and neutron star mergers), and providing quantified nuclear structure input for fundamental symmetry tests and applications in medicine and stockpile stewardship.
