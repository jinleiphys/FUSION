# Assessing continuum channel importance in continuum-discretized coupled-channels via dynamic polarization potential decomposition

> A recurring question in continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) calculations is which continuum channels carry the breakup coupling, both to interpret the reaction and to decide which channels a model space can safely omit. The standard answer is bin deletion: remove a channel, re-solve the coupled equations, and read the change in the elastic $S$-matrix. We show that this cannot isolate an individual channel's contribution. Deleting a channel forces the surviving model space to reorganize, the neighboring bins rerouting through the off-diagonal Green's function, so the recorded change mixes the channel's own effect with the readjustment of all the others. Working instead from the channel-resolved Feshbach dynamic polarization potential (DPP), whose full-coupling Green's function is a fixed reference shared by every channel, we define an exclusion that removes one channel while holding that reference fixed, returning its contribution to the intact system. The two operations disagree on which continuum bins matter most, for $d$+$^{58}$Ni at 21.6~MeV even reversing their order of importance. The DPP further separates each channel's action into a direct path, virtual excitation and return, and a bridge path, relayed through neighboring bins, a split deletion cannot make; it shows the bins act on the elastic channel mainly as bridges, robustly across angular momentum, and that it is this bridge coupling that reorganizes under deletion. A deletion-based channel importance is therefore best read as the truncated calculation's sensitivity to a channel's removal, not as the channel's intrinsic coupling strength.

# Assessing continuum channel importance in continuum-discretized coupled-channels via dynamic polarization potential decomposition

## Key claim

Standard bin deletion in continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) calculations cannot isolate an individual continuum channel's contribution to breakup coupling, because deleting a channel forces the surviving model space to reorganize through the off-diagonal Green's function, mixing the channel's own effect with the readjustment of all other channels. The authors show that a channel-resolved Feshbach dynamic polarization potential (DPP) decomposition, which holds the full-coupling Green's function fixed as a shared reference, provides an intrinsic measure of each channel's contribution that disagrees with bin deletion on which continuum bins matter most, for d+⁵⁸Ni at 21.6 MeV even reversing their order of importance.

## Method

The authors compute the exact, fully nonlocal Feshbach dynamic polarization potential (DPP) using the full-coupling Green's function for d+⁵⁸Ni elastic scattering at 21.6 MeV, then decompose the DPP into contributions from individual continuum bins. For each bin, they separate the contribution into a direct path (virtual excitation and return through the diagonal propagator) and a bridge path (relayed through neighboring bins via off-diagonal Green's function elements), and compare this intrinsic importance measure to standard CDCC bin deletion where the channel is removed and the coupled equations are re-solved in the reduced space.

## Key numbers

- For d+⁵⁸Ni at Elab = 21.6 MeV, J = 8, l = 2 sector: the intrinsic DPP measure ranks the [0.36–0.45] fm⁻¹ bin most important at 25.9%, while standard bin deletion ranks it fourth at 10.7%; deletion instead ranks the [0.28–0.36] fm⁻¹ bin first at 22.8%, while the intrinsic measure ranks it fourth at 9.3%.
- The per-bin ratio of intrinsic importance to deletion importance at J = 8 ranges from 0.4 (for the [0.28–0.36] fm⁻¹ bin) to 2.4 (for the [0.36–0.45] fm⁻¹ bin).
- The DPP reconstructs the full CDCC elastic S-matrix to better than 0.5%.
- The near-threshold [0.02–0.10] fm⁻¹ bin shows negligible importance at 0.1% (at the reconstruction floor).
- The bridge-only exclusion exceeds the direct-only exclusion in every interpreted l = 2 bin at J = 8: for the [0.28–0.36] fm⁻¹ bin, bridge-only gives 24.2% versus direct-only 18.3%; for the [0.36–0.45] fm⁻¹ bin, bridge-only gives 37.5% versus direct-only 15.9%.
- The continuum is discretized into six bins of equal width Δk = 0.087 fm⁻¹ in n-p relative momentum, spanning relative energies from 0 to 12 MeV (k from 0.02 to 0.54 fm⁻¹).
- The n-p interaction is a central Gaussian of depth 72.15 MeV and range 1.484 fm; coupled equations are integrated to a 60 fm matching radius with a 0.05 fm step.

## Context

This work extends the Feshbach projection formalism (Feshbach, 1958, 1962) and the dynamic polarization potential approach first applied to breakup by Sakuragi, Yahiro, and Kamimura (1986), who found the breakup-induced DPP repulsive and weakly absorptive. It builds on the exact, fully nonlocal DPP constructed in the authors' companion work (Lei, Liu, and Ren, 2026) using the full-coupling Green's function, and on prior bin-deletion studies by Hirabayashi (1991), Gomez Camacho et al. (2015, 2016, 2018), Canto et al. (2009), and others. The work enables a rigorous distinction between a channel's intrinsic coupling strength and a truncated calculation's sensitivity to that channel's removal, and provides a pathway to assess continuum channel importance in halo systems such as ⁶He, ¹¹Li, and ¹¹Be where the effects are expected to be strongest.
