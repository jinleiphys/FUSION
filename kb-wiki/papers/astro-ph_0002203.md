# Nuclear Matter and its Role in Supernovae, Neutron Stars and Compact Object Binary Mergers

> The equation of state (EOS) of dense matter plays an important role in the supernova phenomenon, the structure of neutron stars, and in the mergers of compact objects (neutron stars and black holes). During the collapse phase of a supernova, the EOS at subnuclear densities controls the collapse rate, the amount of deleptonization and thus the size of the collapsing core and the bounce density. Properties of nuclear matter that are especially crucial are the symmetry energy and the nuclear specific heat. The nuclear incompressibility, and the supernuclear EOS, play supporting roles. In a similar way, although the maximum masses of neutron stars are entirely dependent upon the supernuclear EOS, other important structural aspects are more sensitive to the equation of state at nuclear densities. The radii, moments of inertia, and the relative binding energies of neutron stars are, in particular, sensitive to the behavior of the nuclear symmetry energy. The dependence of the radius of a neutron star on its mass is shown to critically influence the outcome of the compact merger of two neutron stars or a neutron star with a small mass black hole. This latter topic is especially relevant to this volume, since it stems from research prompted by the tutoring of David Schramm a quarter century ago.

## Key claim

The equation of state (EOS) of dense matter controls the supernova collapse rate, deleptonization, core size, and bounce density, but the paper emphasizes that the supernova phenomenon and many aspects of neutron star structure may not depend upon ultradense matter, focusing instead on properties at and below nuclear saturation density.

## Method

The authors employ a compressible liquid droplet model for nuclei in thermal, mechanical, and chemical equilibrium with an external nucleon vapor, using a free energy expansion that includes bulk, surface, Coulomb, and translational terms. They analyze the dependence of neutron star radii on the matter pressure at fiducial densities of 1, 1.5, and 2 times nuclear saturation density, and compare mass-radius relations from 20 different equations of state spanning nonrelativistic potential models, field-theoretical models, and relativistic Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approaches.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: \(n_s = 0.16\) baryons fm\(^{-3}\) (or about \(3 \times 10^{14}\) g cm\(^{-3}\))
- Energy per particle of bulk nuclear matter at saturation: approximately \(-16\) MeV
- Incompressibility \(K_s\): range 190–250 MeV; experimental RPA analyses give \(K_s \cong 230\) MeV
- Symmetry energy coefficient \(S_v\): range 25–36 MeV
- Skewness parameter \(K_s^\prime\): range 1780–2380 MeV (from the correlation \(K_s^\prime = -0.0860 K_s^2 + (28.37 \pm 2.65) K_s\))
- Neutron drip density: \(n_d \simeq 1.6 \times 10^{-3}\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Neutron star maximum mass range across models: 1.5–2.5 M\(_\odot\)
- Known neutron star masses from binaries: 1.25–1.44 M\(_\odot\)
- Radius of 1.4 M\(_\odot\) stars varies by up to \(\sim 5\) km across EOSs
- Correlation between radius and pressure: \(R \simeq \text{constant} \cdot [P(n)]^{0.23-0.26}\)
- For RX J185635-3754: X-ray blackbody fit gives \(R_\infty \approx 7.3 (D/120\text{ pc})\) km; with heavy-element atmosphere \(R_\infty \approx 21.6 (D/120\text{ pc})\) km
- Proper motion of RX J185635-3754: 0.34 arcsec yr\(^{-1}\)

## Context

This work extends the compressible liquid droplet model originally developed by Lattimer et al. (1985) and modified by Lattimer and Swesty (1991), building on the Wigner-Seitz approximation of Baym, Bethe and Pethick (1971). The paper shows that the correlation between neutron star radius and pressure at nuclear densities can be understood via a relativistic generalization of the \(n=1\) polytrope due to Buchdahl (1967). The results enable future observations of neutron star radii (e.g., from RX J185635-3754 with Chandra and XMM) and moments of inertia (from pulsar glitches) to constrain the dense matter EOS, and suggest that large neutrino detectors such as Super-Kamiokande or SNO may observe enhanced early neutrino luminosity from nearby galactic supernovae.
