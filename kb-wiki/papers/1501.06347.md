# Analytical formula for numerical evaluations of the Wigner rotation matrices at high spins

> The Wigner d function, which is the essential part of an irreducible representation of SU(2) and SO(3) parameterized with Euler angles, has been know to suffer from a serious numerical errors at high spins, if it is calculated by means of the Wigner formula as a polynomial of cos and sin of half of the second Euler angle. This paper shows a way to avoid this problem by expressing the d functions as the Fourier series of the half angle. A precise numerical table of the coefficients of the series is obtainable from a web site.

## Key claim

The Wigner \(d\) function can be evaluated without catastrophic loss of precision at high spins by expressing it as a Fourier series in the half-angle \(\theta/2\), provided that the Fourier coefficients are precomputed using high-precision arithmetic (e.g., with a formula-manipulation system such as MAXIMA) and then stored as 64-bit floating-point numbers. The method is a perfect remedy only when the coefficients are computed with sufficient precision; the Wigner formula itself remains more accurate for very small \(d\)-function values near \(\theta=0\) or \(\pi\).

## Method

The author derives an analytical expression for the Fourier-series coefficients \(t_{\nu}^{jmk}\) of the Wigner \(d\) function, starting from the Wigner formula and using product-to-sum trigonometric identities. Because the direct evaluation of this coefficient formula also suffers from severe cancellation, the coefficients are computed in high-precision floating-point arithmetic (up to 74 decimal digits for \(j=100\)) using the MAXIMA system, then truncated to 64-bit floating-point numbers. The \(d\) function is then evaluated as a sum over \(\nu\) of \(t_{\nu}^{jmk}\) times \(\cos(\nu\theta)\) or \(\sin(\nu\theta)\), where the trigonometric functions are computed via a stable recursion relation.

## Key numbers

* For \(j=40\) and \(\theta=90^\circ\), the Wigner formula gives errors up to \(\sim 10^{-5}\) for \(m\sim k\sim 0\), while the Fourier-series expression gives 15 correct digits for all \(m,k\).
* For \(j=100\), the Fourier-series expression provides precision of \(\sim 10^{-14}\).
* The maximum error of the Wigner formula grows as \(\log_{10}|\text{error}| \approx 0.294 j - 17.2\) for \(20 \le j \le 100\); the Fourier-series error grows as \(\log_{10}|\text{error}| \approx 0.006 j - 14.8\) over the same range.
* Computing all coefficients for \(j=100\) takes 43 hours on a single core of an Intel Core i7-3960X at 3.3 GHz.
* The stored coefficient data for \(j_{\text{max}}=100\) occupies 404 MiB as 64-bit floating-point numbers (compressed text files of similar size).

## Context

This work addresses the well-known loss of significance in the Wigner formula for large angular-momentum quantum numbers \(j\), a problem that had been partially mitigated by earlier proposals such as those of CIG99 and Dac06, but not completely eliminated. The Fourier-series approach provides a practical, precomputed-table-based method for nuclear-structure calculations that require \(d\) functions for \(j\) up to 100 or more, for example in angular-momentum projection from deformed mean-field solutions. The author provides supplemental data files and a sample FORTRAN90 program to enable immediate use by the community.
