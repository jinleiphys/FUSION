# Nuclear $α$-cluster structures from valence-space microscopic cluster model

> Alpha clustering is an important dynamic in nuclear physics, with growing interest to its study in heavy nuclei in recent years. Theoretically, the microscopic cluster models taking nucleons as relevant degrees of freedom have been widely used to study $α$-cluster structures in light nuclei. However, a straightforward application on same footing in heavy nuclei is obstructed by the complexity of handling numerous nucleons. As a simplified alternative, the macroscopic cluster models built upon cluster degrees of freedom are usually employed in heavy nuclei, though these approaches typically lose several critical structural details. In this work, we propose to study the $α$-cluster structures within the framework of valence-space microscopic cluster model (VS-MCM), which is a hybrid between microscopic and macroscopic cluster models and inherits features from both models, making it capable to investigate the $α$-cluster structures in heavy nuclei from a relatively microscopic viewpoint. In VS-MCM, the valence $α$ clusters are described by antisymmetrized microscopic wave functions, with single-particle orbits in core nuclei removed systematically from the model space via the Pauli projection to simulate the antisymmetrization between $α$ clusters and doubly magic cores. As a proof of principle, we apply the VS-MCM to study the $α$-cluster structures in ${}^{20}$Ne and ${}^{44}$Ti at first, with the theoretical energy levels of the $K^π=0_1^{\pm}$ bands for ${}^{20}$Ne and ${}^{44}$Ti showing reasonable agreement with experimental data. These calculations lay the foundation for future applications of VS-MCM in general cluster structures across the nuclide chart, where more $α$ clusters and valence nucleons can exist outside the heavy doubly magic core, opening new avenues to study the $α$ and cluster decays in heavy nuclei microscopically.

## Key claim

The valence-space microscopic cluster model (VS-MCM) is proposed as a hybrid between microscopic and macroscopic cluster models that can study alpha-cluster structures in heavy nuclei from a relatively microscopic viewpoint, but as a proof of principle the authors first apply it only to the lighter nuclei 20Ne and 44Ti, where the theoretical energy levels of the Kπ=0₁± bands show "reasonable agreement" with experimental data rather than exact reproduction.

## Method

The authors treat the target nuclei as a doubly magic core plus four valence nucleons (2p+2n) that form an alpha cluster, described by a modified Brink wave function with Pauli projection to remove core orbits from the model space. They optimize the core-nucleon potential parameters to experimental single-particle energies of 17O/17F and 41Ca/41Sc using χ² minimization, and determine the width parameters α and β by reproducing experimental RMS charge radii and performing variational calculations for the ground states. The energy levels are then obtained by solving the Hill-Wheeler equation with spin-projected trial wave functions using generator coordinates along the z-axis.

## Key numbers

- For 20Ne: optimized width parameter α = 0.486 fm⁻² (cluster size b = 1.43 fm), β = 0.3391 fm⁻², with minimum energy at s_z = 2.38 fm for the ground 0⁺ state
- For 44Ti: optimized width parameter α = 0.433 fm⁻², β = 0.2662 fm⁻², with minimum energy at s_z = 3.64 fm for the ground 0⁺ state
- For 20Ne positive band: deviations of bound 2⁺ and 4⁺ states from experimental data are less than 30 keV
- For 20Ne: the calculated quasibound 8⁺ state exceeds the experimental value by 2.813 MeV
- For 20Ne negative band: theoretical energies are slightly larger than experimental ones by 0.718 MeV
- The α⊗¹⁶O break threshold for 20Ne is 4.730 MeV; the α⊗⁴⁰Ca break threshold for 44Ti is 5.127 MeV
- RMS charge radii used: r(¹⁶O) = 2.6991 fm, r(⁴⁰Ca) = 3.4776 fm

## Context

This work extends the cluster-configuration shell model approach first explored by Varga (1992) for alpha decay in ²¹²Po, applying it systematically to 20Ne and 44Ti as a proof of principle. The VS-MCM sits between fully microscopic cluster models (which are computationally prohibitive for heavy nuclei) and macroscopic cluster models (which lose structural details), offering a middle ground that preserves antisymmetrization between the alpha cluster and core via Pauli projection. The calculations lay the foundation for future applications to heavier systems such as ⁵²Ti, ¹⁰⁴Te, and ²¹²Po, and enable microscopic studies of alpha and cluster decays in heavy nuclei where multiple alpha clusters and valence nucleons can exist outside doubly magic cores.
