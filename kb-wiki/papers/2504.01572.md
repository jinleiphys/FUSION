# A microscopic calculation of fission cross sections with the non-equilibrium Green function method

> We apply the non-equilibrium Green function (NEGF) method to microscopically evaluate fission cross sections for the neutron induced $^{235}$U$(n,f)$ reaction. While the model space was restricted only to seniority zero configurations in the previous applications of the NEGF method, we remove this restriction and include seniority non-zero configurations as well. In such model space, a proton-neutron interaction is active, for which we introduce a random interaction. We find that the seniority non-zero configurations significantly increase the fission cross sections, and thus the fission-to-capture branching ratios, even though they are still underestimated by about one order of magnitude as compared to the experimental data. In addition, we also find that the fission dynamics is governed by only a small number of eigenstates of the model Hamiltonian.

## Key claim

The non-equilibrium Green function (NEGF) method, when extended to include seniority non-zero configurations and a random proton-neutron interaction, yields fission cross sections for the neutron-induced ²³⁵U(n,f) reaction that are significantly larger than those obtained with only seniority zero configurations, yet the calculated values still underestimate experimental data by about one order of magnitude.

## Method

The authors generated many-body basis states using deformed Hartree-Fock calculations with the UNEDF1 Skyrme energy density functional along a fission path on the potential energy surface of ²³⁶U, discretized from Q=14 b to Q=84 b. They constructed a Hamiltonian including monopole pairing, diabatic coupling, and a random particle-hole interaction, then computed the retarded Green function using a shift-invert Lanczos method with GPU-accelerated LSMR algorithm to solve the linear equations for transmission coefficients. The fission cross section was obtained from the transmission coefficient T_{n,fis} using the s-wave formula σ_{n,fis} = (π/k²) T_{n,fis} at a neutron energy of 10 keV.

## Key numbers

- Fission cross section at E_max=8 MeV: σ_{n,fis} = 0.354 barn, compared to the JENDL5.0 library value of 2.938 barn (underestimation by about one order of magnitude)
- Fission-to-capture branching ratio at E_max=8 MeV: α⁻¹ = 0.190, compared to the nuclear library value of 2.831 (Moore 1984)
- Maximum possible cross section at this energy: π/k² = 64.9 barn
- Total Hamiltonian matrix dimension at E_max=8 MeV: 6×10⁵
- Neutron transmission coefficient from RIPL: T_n = 0.0628; capture transmission coefficient: T_cap = 0.25
- Fission barrier height used after scaling: 5.7 MeV (from Leal 1999)
- Energy cut-off values tested: 6.0, 6.5, 7.0, 7.5, and 8.0 MeV
- Pairing strength G ranged from 0.1412 to 0.1465 MeV depending on E_max
- Random interaction strength: v = 0.025 MeV for proton-neutron channel, v = 0.02 MeV for like-particle channels
- Diabatic coupling strength: h₂ = 1.5 MeV (from Bertsch 2023)
- Fission width used: Γ̅_fis = 150 keV (where branching ratio converges)
- Energy averaging window: ΔE = 5 keV; ensemble average over 96 random seeds

## Context

This work extends previous NEGF applications to induced fission (Bertsch 2023, Uzawa 2024) that were restricted to seniority zero configurations, by including seniority non-zero configurations and a random proton-neutron interaction motivated by the work of Bush (1992) on fission decay rates. The method contrasts with phenomenological approaches such as transition state theory (Bohr 1939) and Langevin dynamics (Abe 1996), and aims to provide a microscopic framework applicable to both pre-barrier and post-barrier fission dynamics, particularly relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis where statistical approaches may fail for neutron-rich nuclei with low level densities (Mathews 1983, Xu 2014). The results enable future investigations into the role of triaxiality, deformation-dependent pairing strength, and momentum components in the generator coordinate method to resolve the remaining order-of-magnitude discrepancy with experimental data.
