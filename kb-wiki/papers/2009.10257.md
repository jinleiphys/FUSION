# Ab-initio no-core shell model study of $^{10-14}$B isotopes with realistic NN interactions

> We report a comprehensive study of $^{10-14}$B isotopes within the \textit{ab-initio} no-core shell model (NCSM) using realistic nucleon-nucleon (\textit{NN}) interactions. In particular, we have applied the inside non-local outside Yukawa (INOY) interaction to study energy spectra, electromagnetic properties and point-proton radii of the boron isotopes. The NCSM results with the charge-dependent Bonn 2000 (CDB2K), the chiral next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N$^3$LO) and optimized next-to-next-to-leading order (N$^2$LO$_{opt}$) interactions are also reported. We have reached basis sizes up to $N_{\mbox{max}}$ = 10 for $^{10}$B, $N_{\mbox{max}}$ = 8 for $^{11,12,13}$B and $N_{\mbox{max}}$ = 6 for $^{14}$B with m-scheme dimensions up to 1.7 billion. We also compare the NCSM calculations with the phenomenological YSOX interaction using the shell model to test the predictive power of the \textit{ab-initio} nuclear theory. Overall, our NCSM results are consistent with the available experimental data. The experimental ground state spin $3^{+}$ of $^{10}$B has been reproduced using the INOY \textit{NN} interaction. Typically, the 3\textit{N} interaction is required to correctly reproduce the aforementioned state.

## Key claim

The INOY nucleon-nucleon interaction alone reproduces the experimental ground state spin \(3^{+}\) of \(^{10}\)B, a result that typically requires the inclusion of a three-nucleon (3N) force; however, the ordering of the \(3^{+}\) and \(1^{+}\) states in \(^{10}\)B is not fully converged even at the largest basis size, with the calculated splitting (1.250 MeV) still larger than the experimental value (0.718 MeV).

## Method

The authors performed ab-initio no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations for \(^{10-14}\)B isotopes using four realistic nucleon-nucleon (NN) interactions: INOY, CDB2K, chiral N\(^{3}\)LO, and optimized N\(^{2}\)LO\(_{opt}\). The Okubo-Lee-Suzuki (OLS) renormalization method was applied to soften the INOY, CDB2K, and N\(^{3}\)LO interactions, while the softer N\(^{2}\)LO\(_{opt}\) interaction was used without renormalization. Basis sizes up to \(N_{\text{max}} = 10\) for \(^{10}\)B, \(N_{\text{max}} = 8\) for \(^{11,12,13}\)B, and \(N_{\text{max}} = 6\) for \(^{14}\)B were reached, with m-scheme dimensions up to 1.7 billion. The NCSM results were compared with phenomenological shell model calculations using the YSOX interaction.

## Key numbers

*   For \(^{10}\)B with the INOY interaction at \(N_{\text{max}} = 10\), the calculated splitting between the \(3^{+}\) ground state and the \(1^{+}\) first excited state is 1.250 MeV (experimental value: 0.718 MeV).
*   For \(^{10}\)B with the N\(^{2}\)LO\(_{opt}\) interaction at \(N_{\text{max}} = 10\), the splitting between the \(1^{+}\) and \(3^{+}\) states is 0.035 MeV, with \(1^{+}\) incorrectly predicted as the ground state.
*   For \(^{10}\)B with the CDB2K interaction at \(N_{\text{max}} = 10\), the \(3^{+}_{1}\) state lies 1.069 MeV above the \(1^{+}_{1}\) state.
*   For \(^{10}\)B with the N\(^{3}\)LO interaction at \(N_{\text{max}} = 10\), the \(3^{+}_{1}\) state lies 1.594 MeV above the \(1^{+}_{1}\) state.
*   The optimal harmonic oscillator frequencies used for the largest basis calculations are \(\hbar\Omega = 20\) MeV (INOY), 14 MeV (CDB2K), 12 MeV (N\(^{3}\)LO), and 22 MeV (N\(^{2}\)LO\(_{opt}\)).
*   The m-scheme dimension for the largest calculation (\(^{10}\)B, \(N_{\text{max}} = 10\)) is \(1.7 \times 10^{9}\).

## Context

This work extends previous NCSM studies of \(^{10}\)B that used CDB2K (NCSM up to \(N_{\text{max}} = 8\), Ref. CDB2Knocore) and chiral N\(^{2}\)LO\(_{opt}\) (NCSM up to \(N_{\text{max}} = 10\), Ref. N2LOopt) interactions, which both predicted the incorrect \(1^{+}\) ground state. It also contrasts with earlier NCSM results that required explicit three-nucleon forces to obtain the correct \(3^{+}\) ground state of \(^{10}\)B (Refs. 10B_PRL, Nmax10). The present work is the first NCSM application of the INOY interaction to boron isotopes, and it enables future comparisons with NCSMC calculations for \(^{10,11}\)B that are currently in progress.
