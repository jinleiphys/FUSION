# Potential turning points in cluster radioactivity

> Effects of various nuclear interaction potentials on the decay lifetimes and the turning points of the WKB action integral has been studied. The microscopic nuclear potential obtained by folding in the density distribution functions of the two clusters with a realistic effective interaction has also been used to calculate the turning points. Half lives of alpha and ^{20}O cluster emissions from ^{228}Th have been calculated within the superasymmetric fission model using various phenomenological and the microscopic double folding potentials. Calculations of half lives with the microscopic double folding potentials are found to be in good agreement with the observed experimental data. Present calculations put the superasymmetric fission model on a firm theoretical basis.

## Key claim

The superasymmetric fission model (SAFM) using a microscopic double-folding nuclear potential (M3Y effective interaction) yields half-life predictions for alpha and cluster radioactivity that agree well with experimental data, whereas SAFM calculations using phenomenological potentials (proximity, Christensen-Winther, Igo) fail to reproduce observed lifetimes. The microscopic approach places the SAFM on a firmer theoretical basis, although the first turning points and potentials between turning points differ substantially from the parabolic approximation used in the analytical SAFM (ASAFM).

## Method

The total interaction energy between the emitted cluster and daughter nucleus was computed as the sum of a microscopic nuclear potential (obtained by double-folding the density distributions of the two fragments with the finite-range M3Y effective nucleon-nucleon interaction) and the Coulomb potential. The WKB action integral was evaluated numerically between the two turning points determined from this total interaction energy, and the half-life was calculated using the assault frequency derived from the zero-point vibration energy. Results were compared for alpha decay from 228Th and 20O cluster emission from 228Th using the microscopic double-folding potential, the parabolic potential of ASAFM, the proximity potential, the Christensen-Winther semiempirical heavy-ion potential, and the Igo potential.

## Key numbers

- For alpha decay from 228Th (Q = 5.53 MeV): DF potential with normalization 0.9 gave log10 T(s) = 8.08; experimental value is 7.781 ± 0.001.
- For 20O cluster emission from 228Th (Q = 44.72 MeV): DF potential with normalization 0.9 gave log10 T(s) = 21.05; experimental value is 20.86 ± 0.30.
- First turning point for alpha decay from 228Th: DF potential gave 6.71 fm; parabolic potential gave 6.20 fm; proximity potential gave 8.90 fm; Christensen-Winther gave 9.33 fm; Igo gave 17.27 fm.
- First turning point for 20O cluster emission from 228Th: DF potential gave 7.69 fm; parabolic potential gave 5.79 fm; proximity potential gave 9.80 fm; Christensen-Winther gave 10.75 fm; Igo gave 17.01 fm.
- Second turning point for both decays was 41.47 fm (alpha) and 19.87 fm (20O), nearly identical across potentials except for Igo.
- Chi-squared per degree of freedom for alpha-decay half-lives across many nuclei: DF with normalization 0.9 gave 6.0; DF with normalization 1.0 gave 7.8; Viola-Seaborg with Sobiczewski constants gave 8.2; DF with normalization 0.8 gave 26.9.

## Context

This work extends the superasymmetric fission model (SAFM) of Poenaru and Ivascu (1984) and Poenaru et al. (1986, 1991) by replacing the phenomenological parabolic nuclear potential with a microscopic double-folding potential derived from the M3Y effective interaction. The results contradict the adequacy of proximity-type potentials (Blocki et al., 1977; Myers and Swiatecki, 2000), the Christensen-Winther potential (1976), and the Igo potential (Rasmussen, 1959) for cluster-decay half-life calculations. The study enables more reliable predictions of cluster radioactivity lifetimes and puts the SAFM on a firmer theoretical footing, while also providing a benchmark for comparing microscopic and phenomenological nuclear potentials in decay calculations.
