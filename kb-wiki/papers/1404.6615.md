# QCD Critical Point: The Race is On

> A critical point in the phase diagram of Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), if established either theoretically or experimentally, would be as profound a discovery as the good-old gas-liquid critical point. Unlike the latter, however, first-principles based approaches are being employed to locate it theoretically. Due to the short lived nature of the concerned phases, novel experimental techniques are needed to search for it. The Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) in USA has an experimental program to do so. This short review is an attempt to provide a glimpse of the race between the theorists and the experimentalists as well as that of the synergy between them.

## Key claim

The QCD critical point, if it exists, would be as profound a discovery as the gas-liquid critical point, and first-principles lattice QCD methods are now converging on its location in the temperature-baryonic chemical potential plane, though the results still depend on lattice cut-off, quark masses, and simulation method.

## Method

The author reviews lattice QCD simulations using Taylor expansion of the QCD partition function in powers of the baryonic chemical potential \(\mu_B/T\) around \(\mu_B=0\), where the fermion determinant is real and standard importance sampling works. The coefficients (nonlinear susceptibilities) are computed on \(N_t \times N_s^3\) lattices with two flavors of staggered fermions at quark mass \(m/T_c = 0.1\), for \(N_t = 4, 6, 8\) and spatial sizes up to \(N_s = 32\). The radius of convergence of the series for the baryonic susceptibility is estimated using ratio and \(n\)th-root methods to locate the critical endpoint, and Padé approximants are used to resum the series and predict fluctuation observables along the experimental freeze-out curve.

## Key numbers

* Critical endpoint coordinates on the \(N_t = 6\) lattice: \(T^E/T_c = 0.94 \pm 0.01\) and \(\mu_B^E/T^E = 1.8 \pm 0.1\) (Gavai 2014, citing Ref. [our2]).
* On the \(N_t = 8\) lattice, the \(\mu_B/T\) estimate agrees within errors with the \(N_t = 6\) result, while the coarser \(N_t = 4\) lattice gave a somewhat smaller \(\mu_B/T\) (Gavai 2014, citing Refs. [our1, our2, our4]).
* The Fodor-Katz reweighting method on \(4^3-8^3 \times 4\) lattices found \(\mu_E/m_{\text{nucleon}} \sim 0.77\) for one quark mass, and a factor 2 smaller \(\mu_E\) with a more realistic mass spectrum (Gavai 2014, citing Fodor and Katz).
* The MILC collaboration determined for the \(N_t = 4\) lattice: \(m_\rho/T_c = 5.4 \pm 0.2\) and \(m_\pi/m_\rho = 0.31 \pm 0.01\), corresponding to a pion mass of about 230 MeV (Gavai 2014, citing MILC).
* The STAR collaboration results for the ratio \(m_1\) (equivalent to \(S\sigma\)) show good agreement with the lattice predictions at higher colliding energies (Gavai 2014, citing STAR).

## Context

This review extends the lattice QCD program for locating the QCD critical point, building on earlier model studies using effective chiral Nambu-Jona-Lasinio type models (ErLy, WilRaj) and the pioneering lattice reweighting method of Fodor and Katz (Fodor and Katz). The TIFR group's Taylor expansion approach (Gavai 2014, citing Refs. [our1, our2, our4]) provides systematic control over finite-volume and continuum limits, and the results are used to predict baryon number fluctuation ratios (\(m_1, m_2, m_3\)) along the experimental freeze-out curve. These predictions enable direct comparison with the RHIC beam energy scan data from the STAR collaboration, offering a non-perturbative test of QCD in hot, dense matter and a potential experimental route to discover the critical point.
