# Describing SGRs/AXPs as fast and magnetized white dwarfs

> Over the last decade, observational evidence has mounted that SGRs/AXPs belong to a particular class of pulsars. Furthermore, fast and very magnetic white dwarfs have been observed, and recently two SGRs with low magnetic fields $B\sim(10^{12}-10^{13})$ G, namely SGR 0418+5729 and Swift J1822.3-1606 were discovered with a period of $P\sim9.08$ s and $P\sim8.44$ s, respectively. These new discoveries opens the question of the nature of SGRs/AXPs, emerging alternative scenarios, in particular the white dwarf (WD) pulsar model. These astronomical observations have based an alternative description of the SGRs/AXPs expressed on rotating highly magnetized and very massive WDs. In this new description, several observational properties are easy understood and well explained as a consequence of the large radius of a massive white dwarf that manifests a new scale of mass density, moment of inertia, rotational energy, and magnetic dipole moment in comparison with the case of neutron stars. In this contribution, we will show that these recent discoveries of SGRs with low magnetic field share some properties with the recent detected fast WD pulsar AE Aquarii, and also with RXJ 0648.0-4418, and EUVE J0317-855, supporting the understanding of at least these SGRs with low-B as belonging to a class of very fast and magnetic massive WDs. Furthermore, these recent astronomical observations suggest that we should revisit the real nature of AXP/SGRs: are they really magnetars or very fast and massive white dwarfs?

# Describing SGRs/AXPs as fast and magnetized white dwarfs

## Key claim
The paper argues that at least the two low-magnetic-field SGRs (SGR 0418+5729 and Swift J1822.3-1606) can be described as rotating, highly magnetized, and very massive white dwarfs rather than neutron-star magnetars, because their observed properties (period, spin-down rate, X-ray luminosity) are quantitatively similar to those of known fast and magnetic white dwarfs such as AE Aquarii, RX J0648.0-4418, and EUVE J0317-855.

## Method
The authors apply the standard rotation-powered pulsar formalism, equating the magnetic dipole radiation power to the rotational energy loss rate, to compute the magnetic dipole moment and surface magnetic field for both a canonical neutron star model (mass 1.4 solar masses, radius 10 km, moment of inertia 10^45 g cm^2) and a fiducial white dwarf model (mass 1.4 solar masses, radius 3000 km, moment of inertia 1.26 × 10^50 g cm^2). They then compare the derived white-dwarf magnetic fields and dipole moments for the two low-B SGRs with those of three known fast, magnetic white dwarfs.

## Key numbers
- SGR 0418+5729: period P = 9.08 s, spin-down rate Pdot = 0.4 × 10^(-14), characteristic age 24 Myr, quiescent X-ray luminosity L_X ~ 1.0 × 10^30 erg/s; as a white dwarf: B_WD ~ 8.02 × 10^7 G, magnetic dipole moment m_WD ~ 2.17 × 10^33 emu; as a neutron star: B_NS ~ 6.10 × 10^12 G, m_NS ~ 6.10 × 10^30 emu.
- Swift J1822.3-1606: period P = 8.44 s, spin-down rate Pdot = 8.3 × 10^(-14), characteristic age 1.6 Myr, quiescent X-ray luminosity L_X ~ 4.2 × 10^32 erg/s; as a white dwarf: B_WD ~ 3.52 × 10^8 G, m_WD ~ 0.95 × 10^34 emu; as a neutron star: B_NS ~ 2.70 × 10^13 G, m_NS ~ 2.70 × 10^31 emu.
- AE Aquarii: period P = 33.08 s, spin-down rate Pdot = 5.64 × 10^(-14), characteristic age 9.3 Myr, L_X ~ 10^31 erg/s, B_WD ~ 5 × 10^7 G, m_WD ~ 1.35 × 10^33 emu.
- RX J0648.0-4418: period P = 13.2 s, spin-down rate Pdot < 90 × 10^(-14), characteristic age 0.23 Myr, L_X ~ 10^32 erg/s, B_WD < 1.45 × 10^9 G, m_WD ~ 3.48 × 10^34 emu.
- EUVE J0317-855: period P = 725 s, B_WD ~ 4.5 × 10^8 G, m_WD ~ 1.22 × 10^34 emu.
- For the white dwarf model: moment of inertia I ~ 1.26 × 10^50 g cm^2, radius R = 3000 km, mass M = 1.4 solar masses.
- For the neutron star model: I ~ 10^45 g cm^2, R = 10 km, M = 1.4 solar masses.
- The white dwarf magnetic dipole moment is ~10^3 times larger than for neutron stars; the white dwarf surface magnetic field is ~10^5 times smaller than for neutron stars.

## Context
This work extends the white dwarf pulsar model proposed by Malheiro et al. (2012) and Coelho and Malheiro (2012) as an alternative to the magnetar model of Duncan and Thompson for soft gamma repeaters (SGRs) and anomalous X-ray pulsars (AXPs). The paper directly compares the two recently discovered low-B SGRs (reported by N. Rea et al., 2010 and 2012) with three known fast, magnetic white dwarfs: AE Aquarii (the first spin-powered white dwarf pulsar, studied by Terada and Angel), RX J0648.0-4418 (Mereghetti et al., 2009), and EUVE J0317-855 (Barstow et al., 1995; Kulebi et al.). The results suggest that at least these two SGRs may belong to a class of very fast and massive white dwarfs, and motivate revisiting whether all SGRs/AXPs are magnetars or instead rotating white dwarfs.
