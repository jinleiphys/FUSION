# High-order isospin-dependent surface tension contribution to the fourth-order symmetry energy of finite nuclei

> The relation between the fourth-order symmetry energy $E_{\rm{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ of nuclear matter at saturation density $ρ_0$ and its counterpart $a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)$ of finite nuclei in a semiempirical nuclear mass formula is revisited by considering the high-order isospin-dependent surface tension contribution to the latter. We derive the full expression of $a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)$, which includes explicitly the high-order isospin-dependent surface tension effects, and find that the value of $E_{\rm{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ cannot be extracted from the measured $a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)$ before the high-order surface tension is well constrained. Our results imply that a large $a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)$ value of several MeVs obtained from analyzing nuclear masses can nicely agree with the empirical constraint of $E_{\rm{sym,4}}(ρ_0)\lesssim 2$ MeV from mean-field models and does not necessarily lead to a large $E_{\rm{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ value of $\approx 20$ MeV obtained previously without considering the high-order surface tension. Furthermore, we also give the expression for the sixth-order symmetry energy $a_{\rm{sym,6}}(A)$ of finite nuclei, which involves more nuclear matter bulk parameters and the higher-order isospin-dependent surface tension.

## Key claim

The fourth-order symmetry energy coefficient of finite nuclei \(a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)\) includes a previously neglected high-order isospin-dependent surface tension contribution, meaning that a large measured \(a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)\) of several MeV does not necessarily imply a large \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0)\) of about 20 MeV; instead, \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0)\) can remain consistent with the empirical constraint of \(\lesssim 2\) MeV once the surface term is accounted for.

## Method

The authors derived the full expression for the fourth-order symmetry energy of finite nuclei by extending the nuclear surface tension to include a quartic term in the neutron-proton chemical potential difference: \(\sigma/\sigma_0 \approx 1 - \theta \mu_{\rm{a}}^2 + \kappa \theta^2 \mu_{\rm{a}}^4\). Using a variational method and the leptodermous expansion of the nuclear binding energy, they separated the bulk contribution (previously derived by Wang et al. (2017)) from the new surface contribution. The total \(a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)\) was then expressed as a function of the mass number \(A\), the bulk symmetry energy \(E_{\rm{sym}}(\rho_0)\), the surface symmetry energy \(\beta\), the slope parameter \(L\), the incompressibility \(K_0\), and the high-order surface parameter \(\kappa\).

## Key numbers

* The surface fourth-order symmetry energy \(a^{\rm s}_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)\) reaches a maximum value of approximately 7.5 MeV at \(A_{\max} \approx 216\), near the mass of \(^{208}\)Pb, using \(\alpha/\beta = 2\) and \(\kappa = 0.5\).
* The bulk term from Wang et al. (2017) contains the combination \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0) - L^2/(2K_0)\), where \(L^2/(2K_0)\) is about 4.2 MeV for \(L \approx 45\) MeV and \(K_0 \approx 240\) MeV.
* The empirical constraint on the fourth-order symmetry energy of nuclear matter is \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0) \lesssim 2\) MeV from mean-field models.
* The fourth-order symmetry energy coefficient of finite nuclei extracted from nuclear masses is \(a_{\rm{a,4}} \approx 3.28 \pm 0.50\) MeV (Jiang et al., 2014) or \(a_{\rm{a,4}} \approx 8.33 \pm 1.21\) MeV (Tian et al., 2016).

## Context

This work extends the droplet model treatment of Danielewicz (2003) and the variational calculation of Wang et al. (2017) by including the next-order isospin-dependent surface tension term. The authors resolve an apparent contradiction: Wang et al. (2017) had concluded that a measured \(a_{\rm{a,4}} \approx 3.28\) MeV implies \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0) \approx 20\) MeV, which conflicts with the \(\lesssim 2\) MeV constraint from mean-field models (Cai et al., 2012; Gonzalez-Boquera et al., 2017; Pu et al., 2017). The present result reconciles these by showing that the surface contribution can account for the large finite-nucleus value while keeping the bulk fourth-order symmetry energy small. This enables future studies to constrain the high-order surface parameter \(\kappa\) using neutron skin thickness measurements from experiments such as PREX and CREX.
