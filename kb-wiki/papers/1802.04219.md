# On the nature of the shape coexistence and the quantum phase transition phenomena: lead region and Zr isotopes

> The goal of this contribution is to analyze the connection between shape coexistence and quantum phase transition, two seemingly unrelated phenomena that share common aspects, namely, the rapid change in the ground state structure along an isotope chain or the presence of several minima at the mean-field level. To illustrate the similarities and differences between both phenomena, we will focus in the Pb region, in particular in Pt and Hg isotopes, as well as in Zr isotopes.

## Key claim

Shape coexistence and quantum phase transitions (QPTs) are related phenomena that can be distinguished by the height of the barrier between coexisting minima and the degree of mixing between regular and intruder configurations, but a QPT can be induced from shape coexistence when the intruder configuration becomes the ground state and the mixing between sectors is weak.

## Method

The authors analyzed the systematics of the two-neutron separation energy (S₂ₙ) and the energy ratio E(4₁⁺)/E(2₁⁺) for Zr, Pt, and Hg isotopes as indicators of first-order QPTs. They compared these experimental observables with Interacting Boson Model (IBM) calculations that separate regular and intruder configurations, switching off the interaction between the two sectors to isolate the underlying structural evolution.

## Key numbers

- In Zr, E(4₁⁺)/E(2₁⁺) changes from values close to 2 (even 1.5 for ⁹⁶⁻⁹⁸Zr) to values close to 3.3 across the QPT region around A=100.
- The mid-shell (where intruder configurations are lowest) occurs at A=106 for Zr, A=182 for Pt, and A=184 for Hg.
- In Zr and Pt, an intruder state becomes the ground state around the mid-shell, while this never happens for Hg.
- The mixing between regular and intruder states is rather small in Zr and Hg, but much larger in Pt.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of shape coexistence, originally proposed by Morinaga in the 1950s and reviewed extensively by Heyde (2011), and connects it to the QPT framework developed using the Interacting Boson Model (Iachello, 1987). The paper builds on prior IBM calculations for Pt (García-Ramos, 2009), Hg (García-Ramos, 2014), and Zr (García-Ramos, 2017), and shows that the strong mixing between configurations in Pt (García-Ramos, 2014a) can hide QPT signatures that would otherwise be expected from the crossing of regular and intruder bands. This enables future work to identify other mechanisms by which shape coexistence can induce QPTs.
