# The effect of quark coalescence on conical signals

> We explore the effect of hadronization by partonic coalescence on a "conical" signal at the partonic level. We show that, by transferring partons from a lower to a higher $p_T$, coalescence makes the conical signal stronger and hence less susceptible to thermal smearing, provided the signal is integrated over a large momentum bin and effects such as non-collinearity and a finite Wigner function width are taken into account. We explore the role of this effect in baryon/meson scaling and calculate the effect of resonances decays on such a conical signal.

## Key claim
Quark coalescence can strengthen a conical signal (Mach cone) at the hadron level even when thermal smearing would wash it out at the parton level, provided the signal is integrated over a sufficiently wide transverse momentum bin and non-collinear coalescence with a finite Wigner function width is taken into account.

## Method
The authors used a Monte Carlo coalescence model (developed in Greco et al., 2007) that incorporates constituent quark masses, non-collinear coalescence, and a finite Wigner function width, tuned to reproduce baryon/meson and elliptic flow data. They applied this model to a parameterized quark distribution function exhibiting a generic conical flow in a static medium, with flow components \(U_0, U_L, U_T\) and angles \(\phi_r, \phi\) in configuration and momentum space. They compared the angular distributions of quarks and hadrons (mesons and baryons) after coalescence, and also included resonance decays (e.g., \(\rho \rightarrow \pi \pi\)) to assess their effect on the signal.

## Key numbers
- The simplest collinear coalescence model amplifies an existing conical signal by a factor of 2 for mesons and 3 for baryons.
- Mesons at \(p_T\) 1–2 GeV show two peaks even when they come from quarks at 0–1 GeV that have only one peak.
- At high enough \(p_T\) (\(>2\) GeV), the effect of coalescence disappears because coalescence becomes nearly collinear and thermal and coalescing distributions become identically exponential.
- The Mach angle is independent of quark number in the hadron, but the depth of the signal scales with quark number.

## Context
This work extends the study of Mach cone signals in heavy ion collisions, which were proposed as evidence for a perfect fluid (Stoecker, 2005; Shuryak and Mach, 2006), but were shown to be suppressed by thermal broadening in Cooper-Frye freeze-out (Betz et al., 2008; Betz et al., 2009; Heinz and Mach, 2008) and by diffusion wake formation. The authors argue that quark coalescence (Molnar and Voloshin, 2003; Mueller and Xu, 2006; Hwa and Yang, 2003; Greco et al., 2003) may be responsible for the observed conical signal at momenta above 1–1.5 GeV, where hydrodynamics with Cooper-Frye freeze-out fails. This work enables future experimental discrimination between coalescence and Cooper-Frye freeze-out scenarios using identified baryon/meson correlations and binning by the angle between the hard trigger and the reaction plane.
