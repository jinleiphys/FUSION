# Microscopic model for the collective enhancement of nuclear level densities

> A microscopic method for calculating nuclear level densities (NLD) is developed, based on the framework of energy density functionals. Intrinsic level densities are computed from single-quasiparticle spectra obtained in a finite-temperature self-consistent mean-field (SCMF) calculation that takes into account nuclear deformation, and is specified by the choice of the energy density functional (EDF) and pairing interaction. The total level density is calculated by convoluting the intrinsic density with the corresponding collective level density, determined by the eigenstates of a five-dimensional quadrupole or quadrupole plus octupole collective Hamiltonian. The parameters of the Hamiltonian (inertia parameters, collective potential) are consistently determined by deformation-constrained SCMF calculations using the same EDF and pairing interaction. The model is applied in the calculation of NLD of $^{94,96,98}$Mo, $^{106,108}$Pd, $^{106,112}$Cd, $^{160,162,164}$Dy, $^{166}$Er, and $^{170,172}$Yb, in comparison with available data. It is shown that the collective enhancement of the intrinsic level density, consistently computed from the eigenstates of the corresponding collective Hamiltonian, leads to total NLDs that are in excellent agreement with data over the whole energy range of measured values.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a fully microscopic calculation of nuclear level densities, where the collective enhancement is determined from eigenstates of a collective Hamiltonian rather than from phenomenological factors, yields total level densities in excellent agreement with experimental data across the entire measured energy range, but only when the intrinsic and collective degrees of freedom are treated consistently using the same energy density functional and pairing interaction.

## Method

Intrinsic level densities were computed from single-quasiparticle spectra obtained in finite-temperature self-consistent mean-field calculations using the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov model with the DD-PC1 energy density functional and a separable pairing force. The total level density was calculated by convoluting the intrinsic density with collective level densities determined from eigenstates of either a five-dimensional quadrupole collective Hamiltonian (for medium-mass nuclei) or an axial quadrupole-octupole collective Hamiltonian (for heavy nuclei), with all Hamiltonian parameters (inertia parameters, collective potential) consistently determined by deformation-constrained zero-temperature RHB calculations using the same functional and pairing interaction.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for pairing collapse: \(T_c = 0.6 \sim 0.8\) MeV for Mo, Pd, and Cd isotopes; \(T_c = 0.5 \sim 0.6\) MeV for Dy, Er, and Yb isotopes
- For \(^{94}\)Mo: predicted \(2_1^+\) energy 0.92 MeV (experimental 0.87 MeV), predicted \(0_2^+\) energy 1.94 MeV (experimental 1.74 MeV), predicted \(4_1^+\) energy 2.07 MeV (experimental 1.57 MeV)
- For \(^{162}\)Dy: collective enhancement factor \(K_{\text{coll}} = 39.8\) at neutron separation energy \(S_n = 8.20\) MeV
- For \(^{164}\)Dy: collective enhancement factor \(K_{\text{coll}} = 42.5\) at neutron separation energy \(S_n = 7.63\) MeV
- Equilibrium deformations: \(^{96}\)Mo at \((\beta, \gamma) \sim (0.2, 21^\circ)\), \(^{108}\)Pd at \((\beta, \gamma) \sim (0.2, 13^\circ)\), heavy nuclei global minima at \(\beta_2 = 0.3 \sim 0.4\) and \(\beta_3 = 0\)

## Context

This work extends microscopic approaches to nuclear level densities beyond the phenomenological or semi-empirical multiplicative factors for rotational and vibrational degrees of freedom used in earlier studies such as Demetriou (2001) and Rahmatinejad (2020). It provides a fully self-consistent framework where both intrinsic and collective contributions are derived from the same energy density functional, enabling predictions for nuclei far from stability where phenomenological methods cannot be applied quantitatively. The approach can be extended to include additional collective degrees of freedom and to compute other nuclear properties such as fission cross sections, building on the HFB-based NLD framework of Hilaire (2001) and Goriely (2008).
