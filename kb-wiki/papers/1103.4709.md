# Phase diagram for a Cubic Consistent-Q Interacting Boson Model Hamiltonian: signs of triaxiality

> An extension of the Consistent-Q formalism for the Interacting Boson Model that includes the cubic QxQxQ term is proposed. The potential energy surface for the cubic quadrupole interaction is explicitly calculated within the coherent state formalism using the complete chi-dependent expression for the quadrupole operator. The Q-cubic term is found to depend on the asymmetry deformation parameter gamma as a linear combination of cos(3gamma) and cos^2(3γ) terms, thereby allowing for triaxiality. The phase diagram of the model in the large N limit is explored, it is described the order of the phase transition surfaces that define the phase diagram, and moreover, the possible nuclear equilibrium shapes are established. It is found that, contrary to expectations, there is only a very tiny region of triaxiality in the model, and that the transition from prolate to oblate shapes is so fast that, in most cases, the onset of triaxiality might go unnoticed.

## Key claim

The cubic Consistent-Q Hamiltonian can produce triaxial nuclear shapes only in an extremely narrow region of parameter space, contrary to earlier expectations that the inclusion of a $\cos^2(3\gamma)$ term via the $\chi$-dependent quadrupole operator would yield a broad triaxial region; the transition between prolate and oblate shapes is so rapid that triaxiality would likely go undetected in most practical cases.

## Method

The authors extended the Consistent-Q formalism of the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) by adding a cubic $(\hat{Q}\times\hat{Q}\times\hat{Q})^{(0)}$ term to the standard Hamiltonian, keeping the same $\chi$ parameter in all quadrupole operators. They calculated the potential energy surface (PES) analytically using the coherent state formalism with a symbolic computer code, obtaining the matrix element of the cubic term as a function of deformation parameters $\beta$ and $\gamma$. The phase diagram in the large-$N$ (thermodynamic) limit was then mapped by minimizing the energy per boson with respect to $\beta$ and $\gamma$ across the three-dimensional parameter space defined by $\xi$, $\chi$, and $k_3$, and the order of each phase transition surface was determined from the behavior of the energy expansion around $\beta=0$.

## Key numbers

* The spherical-deformed phase transition is first order except along the line $k_3 = -\sqrt{5}\chi/2$, where it becomes second order; this line occurs at $\xi = 4/5$.
* For $\xi=0$ and $\chi=-\sqrt{7}/2$, the critical value separating prolate and oblate minima is $k_3 = \sqrt{35}/3 \approx 1.972$, where both minima become degenerate with energy $E = -2/3$.
* The prolate minimum for $\xi=0$ and $\chi=-\sqrt{7}/2$ lies at $\beta = \sqrt{2}$ and $\gamma = 0^\circ$; the oblate minimum lies at $\beta = 1/\sqrt{2}$ and $\gamma = 60^\circ$.
* The condition for triaxial extrema, $|-b/2a| \le 1$, is satisfied only in a very narrow range of $\beta$ around $\beta \approx 0.934$ (for $k_3=1$, $\chi=-0.5$), which falls in a region where $\beta$ drops rapidly with $\xi$.

## Context

This work extends the Consistent-Q formalism (Warner and Casten, 1983) by adding a cubic quadrupole term, building on earlier studies of three-body $[d^\dagger d^\dagger d^\dagger]^{(L)}\cdot[\tilde d\tilde d\tilde d]^{(L)}$ interactions that produced broader triaxial regions (Heyde and collaborators, 1984; García-Ramos and collaborators, 2000). The authors explicitly contrast their results with those of Van Isacker (1999) and Rowe and Thiamova (2009), who studied the cubic term with $\chi=0$ and found only axial shapes; the present work shows that even with the full $\chi$-dependent quadrupole operator, triaxiality remains negligible. The phase diagram enables future studies of transitional nuclei near the prolate-oblate shape coexistence region within the IBM framework.
