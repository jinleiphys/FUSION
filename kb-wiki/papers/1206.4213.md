# Decaying hadrons within constituent-quark models

> Within conventional constituent-quark models hadrons come out as stable bound states of the valence (anti)quarks. Thereby the resonance character of hadronic excitations is completely ignored. A more realistic description of hadron spectra can be achieved by including explicit mesonic degrees of freedom, which couple directly to the constituent quarks. We will present a coupled-channel formalism that describes such hybrid systems in a relativistically invariant way and allows for the decay of excited hadrons. The formalism is based on the point-form of relativistic quantum mechanics. If the confining forces between the (anti)quarks are described by instantaneous interactions it can be formally shown that the mass-eigenvalue problem for a system that consists of dynamical (anti)quarks and mesons reduces to a hadronic eigenvalue problem in which the eigenstates of the pure confinement problem (bare hadrons) are coupled via meson loops. The only point where the quark substructure enters are form factors at the meson-(bare) hadron vertices. The physical picture that emerges resembles the kind of hadronic resonance model that has been developed by Sato and Lee and is now heavily used at the Excited Baryon Analysis Center (EBAC) to fix $N^\ast$ properties. Our approach, however, is in a certain sense inverse to the one of Sato and Lee. Whereas they want to undress physical resonances to end up with bare quantities, we rather want to dress the bound-states resulting from a pure constituent quark model to end up with quantities that can be directly compared with experiment. The way how our approach works will be exemplified by means of a simple quark-antiquark-meson system.

# Decaying hadrons within constituent-quark models

## Key claim

The authors present a relativistically invariant coupled-channel formalism that allows excited hadrons to decay by dressing the stable bound states from a pure constituent-quark model with explicit mesonic degrees of freedom, but only under the condition that the confining forces between (anti)quarks are described by instantaneous interactions. This approach is inverse to the Sato-Lee model used at EBAC: whereas Sato and Lee undress physical resonances to obtain bare quantities, this work dresses bound states from constituent-quark models to produce quantities comparable with experiment.

## Method

The formalism uses the point-form of relativistic quantum mechanics with a Bakamjian-Thomas construction to factorize the 4-momentum operator into a free velocity operator and an interaction-dependent mass operator. For a system of valence (anti)quarks plus one meson, the mass-eigenvalue problem is formulated as a 2-channel problem; the meson-containing channel is eliminated to yield an optical potential on the valence-quark level. By expanding the valence-quark wavefunction in eigenstates of the pure confinement problem (bare hadrons), the problem reduces to an algebraic eigenvalue equation on the hadronic level where bare hadrons are coupled via meson loops, with all quark substructure absorbed into form factors at the meson-(bare) hadron vertices.

## Key numbers

- Quark and antiquark masses were prefixed to $m_q = m_{\bar{q}} = 0.34$ GeV; pion mass $m_\pi = 0.135$ GeV.
- Oscillator strength of the confinement potential: $a = 0.28$ GeV; parameter $V_0 = 0.1$ GeV fixing the ground-state mass.
- Maximum decay width of the first excited state: $\Gamma_1 = 0.026$ GeV, reached at $g^2/4\pi = 1.19$ GeV$^2$.
- The decay threshold of the first excited state occurs when $\mathrm{Re}(\mathbf{m}_1) = \mu_0 + m_\pi$, where $\mu_0$ is the bare ground-state mass.

## Context

This work extends the point-form relativistic coupled-channel approach of Krassnigg (2003) by allowing meson absorption by the emitting (anti)quark, not just meson exchange between different hadronic constituents. It contrasts with leading-order perturbative treatments of strong decays (such as the $^3P_0$ model of Le Yaouanc (1974) or the elementary emission model of Melde (2008)), which typically underestimate experimental partial decay widths by at least an order of magnitude. The formalism enables a bottom-up approach where the dressed hadron masses and vertex form factors from the hybrid quark model could serve as input for dynamical coupled-channel calculations a la Sato and Lee (2009), providing a constituent-quark understanding of hadron resonances beyond pure hadron reaction models.
