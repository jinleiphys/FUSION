# Shrinking $\hbar$ as a recipe for revealing classical-like contributions in optical potential cross sections

> A simple recipe for revealing classical-like contributions in optical potential cross sections is proposed. The recipe is based on the fact that the classical-like properties are not expected to depend on the actual value of $\hbar$. This allows us to identify the classical-like characteristics of an optical potential cross section by simply repeating the calculation with different values of $\hbar$, and observing which properties of the cross section are invariant. This method is applied to the cross sections of a few optical potentials used to describe the recent data of light heavy-ion elastic scattering.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that classical-like contributions in optical potential cross sections can be identified by repeating quantum calculations with artificially reduced values of Planck's constant \(\hbar\), under the condition that for complex potentials the imaginary part must be scaled by the same factor as \(\hbar\) to keep the survival probability constant. The method is presented as a simpler alternative to full semiclassical analyses, though the author notes that deviations from classical behavior are expected near rainbow angles and in extreme backward directions.

## Method

The author performs quantum mechanical scattering calculations using conventional optical potential codes, systematically replacing the physical value of \(\hbar\) with \(\hbar/f\) where \(f\) is a reduction factor (values from 0.5 to 4.0 are used). For complex optical potentials, the imaginary potential strength is scaled by the same factor \(f\) to maintain constant survival probabilities. The quantum deflection function \(\Theta_Q(\lambda)\) is estimated from the scattering function \(S(\lambda)\) using finite differences, and the resulting cross sections for different \(\hbar\) values are compared to identify features that remain invariant under \(\hbar\) variation, which are interpreted as classical-like contributions.

## Key numbers

- For the real Woods-Saxon potential (\(V_0 = 282.2\) MeV, \(R_v = 2.818\) fm, \(d_v = 0.978\) fm): classical deflection function shows a maximum of about \(7^\circ\) at impact parameter \(b_C \simeq 9.3\) fm (\(\lambda_C \simeq 40.2\)) and a minimum of about \(-310^\circ\) at \(b_n \simeq 5.4\) fm (\(\lambda_n \simeq 23.5\))
- For the complex potential at \(E_{\rm Lab} = 132\) MeV: imaginary part parameters \(W_0 = 13.86\) MeV, \(R_w = 5.6894\) fm, \(d_w = 0.656\) fm
- The \(\hbar\) reduction factors used range from \(f = 0.5\) (doubled \(\hbar\)) to \(f = 4.0\) (quarter \(\hbar\))
- The quantum deflection function points for different \(f\) values (0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0) lie on the same curve when plotted against impact parameter \(b\), with the exclusion of a small range around \(b \simeq 5.5\) fm

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical analysis of heavy-ion elastic scattering, specifically addressing optical potentials used to describe \({}^{16}\)O + \({}^{12}\)C data at laboratory energies of 132 and 200 MeV from Ogloblin et al. (2000). The method provides a practical alternative to more sophisticated semiclassical techniques such as the Brink and Takigawa (1977) multireflection expansion and the uniform approximation methods reviewed by Brink (1985). The approach enables identification of classical-like contributions without requiring full semiclassical calculations, and the author demonstrates its application to distinguish between diffractive (quantum) and classical contributions in the far-side cross section.
