# Mean field description of electron induced quasi-elastic excitation in nuclei

> The validity of the approximations done in the mean field description of the quasi-elastic excitation of medium-heavy nuclei is discussed. A test of the reliability of the plane wave Born approximation is presented. The uncertainty related to the choice of the electromagnetic nucleon form factors is discussed. The effects produced by the meson exchange currents generated by the exchange of a single pion are studied, as well as the need of including relativistic corrections to the one-body currents. The results of the continuum shell model and those of the Fermi gas model are compared and the need of treating the emitted nucleon within a relativistic framework is studied. We analyze the results of Random Phase Approximation calculations not only in terms of their effects on the mean field responses, but also in terms of the theoretical consistency in the choice of the effective residual interaction. The role of the final state interaction is investigated. A microscopic justification of the use of complex optical potential to describe the final state interaction is provided. The effects of the final state interaction on the mean field responses are studied with a sum rule conserving model. A comparison with the experimental data measured in the C12 and Ca40 nuclei is shown.

## Key claim

The mean field description of quasi-elastic electron-nucleus scattering, while qualitatively successful, requires careful treatment of several approximations, including Coulomb distortion, nucleon form factor uncertainties, meson exchange currents, relativistic corrections, and final state interactions, before quantitative agreement with experimental data can be achieved, and the commonly used Fermi gas model fits obscure nuclear structure physics behind effective parameters.

## Method

The authors performed continuum shell model and Fermi gas model calculations of the longitudinal and transverse response functions for electron-induced quasi-elastic excitation, comparing results with experimental data for 12C and 40Ca. They systematically tested the validity of the plane wave Born approximation by comparing with distorted wave Born approximation calculations, evaluated the sensitivity to different nucleon electromagnetic form factor parametrizations, and studied the effects of meson exchange currents from single-pion exchange, relativistic corrections to one-body currents, and final state interactions using a sum-rule-conserving model and random phase approximation calculations.

## Key numbers

- The commonly accepted value of the Fermi momentum \(k_{\rm F}\) is about 270 MeV/\(c\), obtained from nuclear matter density \(\rho = 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\) with \(r_0 = 1.18\) fm from elastic electron scattering
- For heavy nuclei, the fit procedure to quasi-elastic data provided a roughly constant \(k_{\rm F}\) of about 260 MeV/\(c\)
- The uncertainty in nuclear responses from different nucleon form factor choices can be as big as 12% at the peak values
- For 40Ca, the effective momentum transfer in the eikonal approximation is given by \(q_{\rm eff} = q \left(1 + 1.5 Z \alpha \hbar c / (\varepsilon_i R_{\rm eq})\right)\) with \(R_{\rm eq} = 1.24 A^{1/3}\)
- The Darwin-Foldy correction factor for the charge operator is \(f_{\rm DF} = \left(1 + (q^2 - \omega^2)/(4M^2)\right)^{-1/2}\)
- For a typical Fermi momentum of \(k_{\rm F} = 250\) MeV/\(c\), the ratio \(P/M \le 1/4\), justifying expansion in powers of \(P/M\)
- Relativistic effects become large at momentum transfer larger than 500 MeV/\(c\)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of quasi-elastic electron scattering beyond the pioneering Fermi gas model fits of Moniz (1971), which successfully described total cross sections but failed to separately describe the longitudinal and transverse response components measured by Altemus (1980). The paper systematically evaluates approximations in the mean field description, building on earlier DWBA studies for 12C and 40Ca by Co' (1987) and for 40Ca and 208Pb by Traini (1988, 1993), and on the relativistic current operator development by Amaro (1996). The results enable more reliable extraction of nuclear structure information from quasi-elastic electron scattering data and provide guidance for future experiments and calculations that must account for Coulomb distortion, form factor ambiguities, and relativistic effects.
