# The quantum mechanics of particle-correlation measurements in high-energy heavy-ion collisions

> The Hanbury Brown--Twiss (HBT) effect in two-particle correlations is a fundamental wave phenomenon that occurs at the sensitive elements of detectors; it is one of the few processes in elementary particle detection that depends on the wave mechanics of the produced particles. We analyze here, within a quantum mechanical framework for computing correlations among high-energy particles, how particle detectors produce the HBT effect. We focus on the role played by the wave functions of particles created in collisions and the sensitivity of the HBT effect to the arrival times of pairs at the detectors, and show that the two detector elements give an enhanced signal when the single-particle wave functions of the detected particles overlap at both elements within the characteristic atomic transition time of the elements. The measured pair correlation function is reduced when the delay in arrival times between pairs at the detectors is of order of or larger than the transition time.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Hanbury Brown-Twiss (HBT) effect in high-energy heavy-ion collisions is fundamentally a wave phenomenon occurring at the detectors, not at the source, and that the crucial timescale for observing HBT correlations is the atomic transition time of the detector material (approximately 10^{-16} seconds), rather than the detector resolution time. The measured pair correlation function is reduced when the delay in arrival times between pairs at the detectors is of order or larger than this atomic transition time.

## Method

The authors develop a quantum mechanical framework for computing two-particle correlations by analyzing how particle detectors respond to the wave functions of produced particles. They model pions as relativistic wave packets from Gaussian sources, describe the detector response through a spectrometer function that accounts for the atomic current-current correlation function of detector atoms, and compute the overlap between pion wave functions and detector atoms. They then apply this framework to compare directly produced pions with pions from resonance decay (specifically Lambda decay) to determine how emission time delays affect the measured correlation.

## Key numbers

- Source lifetime in high-energy collisions: approximately 1-10 fm/c (0.3-3 × 10^{-23} seconds) in its own rest frame
- Characteristic lifetime of elementary pion sources (coherence time): less than or approximately 1 fm/c
- Atomic transition time for ionization of a gas atom: approximately 10^{-16} seconds (derived from ℏ/10 eV)
- Detector resolution time for a wire chamber: approximately 10^{-9} seconds (nanosecond scale)
- Exposure time for momentum measurement in a 10-meter magnetic spectrometer: approximately 3 × 10^{-8} seconds
- For a 1 GeV pion with source-detector distance of 1 meter, the wave packet envelope at the detector is approximately 20 cm in the transverse direction and 0.2 cm thick in the longitudinal direction
- Average energy loss for creating electron-ion pairs in noble gases: 10-50 eV
- Energy bandwidth of the Gaussian part of the ionization energy distribution: approximately 10 eV
- Time delay between a directly produced pion and a pion from Lambda decay at rapidity 3: approximately 9.7 × 10^{-12} seconds (0.037 times the Lambda lifetime)

## Context

This work extends the quantum mechanical understanding of the Hanbury Brown-Twiss effect in particle physics, building on the original photon correlation studies of Hanbury Brown and Twiss (1950s) and Purcell's observation that the maximum correlation height for chaotic stationary sources is approximately the ratio of coherence time to observational binning time. The paper clarifies that the atomic transition time, not the detector resolution time, is the relevant timescale for HBT measurements, and shows that pions from long-lived resonance decays (such as Lambda decay) can experience arrival time delays much larger than this atomic timescale, thereby reducing the measured correlation. This analysis enables more accurate interpretation of HBT measurements in heavy-ion collisions, particularly regarding the contribution of resonance decay pions to the correlation function.
