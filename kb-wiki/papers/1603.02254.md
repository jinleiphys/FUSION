# Fluctuations as a test of chemical non-equilibrium at the LHC

> It is shown that large chemical potential leads to the significant increase of multiplicity fluctuations for bosons, and makes the fluctuations infinite in the case of Bose-Einstein condensation. It allows to distinguish between the models that explain the anomalous proton to pion ratio and the low transverse momentum enhancement of pion spectra in Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC within chemical equilibrium or non-equilibrium models. The effects of resonance decays, finite size of the system, requirements to the event statistics, different momentum cuts, and limited detector acceptance are considered. The obtained results show the possibility to observe a substantial increase of the normalized kurtosis for positively or negatively charged pions in the case of non-equilibrium or partial pion condensation using currently measured data.

## Key claim

Large positive pion chemical potential (close to the pion mass) leads to a substantial increase in multiplicity fluctuations for bosons, and makes fluctuations infinite in the case of Bose-Einstein condensation in an infinite system; this effect can distinguish between chemical equilibrium and non-equilibrium models that both explain the anomalous proton-to-pion ratio and low transverse momentum enhancement of pion spectra in Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC, provided the normalized kurtosis for charged pions is measured.

## Method

The author calculates multiplicity fluctuations (scaled variance, normalized skewness, normalized kurtosis) for primary pions using susceptibilities derived from the pressure of a pion gas with finite chemical potential, including finite-volume corrections and the zero-momentum condensate term. The primary fluctuations are then combined with resonance decay contributions (simulated using the THERMINATOR model, assuming Poisson statistics for resonances) via formulas for uncorrelated distributions, and the effects of limited detector acceptance and transverse momentum cuts are evaluated analytically.

## Key numbers

- For equilibrium pions at $\mu=0$ and $T=140$ MeV: $\omega \simeq 1.1$, $S\cdot\sigma \simeq 1.2$, $\kappa\cdot\sigma^2 \simeq 1.9$.
- For pions from resonance decays at the most peripheral centrality ($c=80-90\%$): $\omega_{\rm res} \lesssim 1.05$, $S_{\rm res}\cdot\sigma_{\rm res} \lesssim 1.1$, $\kappa_{\rm res}\cdot\sigma_{\rm res}^2 \lesssim 1.3$.
- For the non-equilibrium scenario with a $p_T$ cut of $100-200$ MeV, the normalized kurtosis reaches values $\sim 300$ (three times larger than the $\sim 100$ obtained for the measured $p_T>100$ MeV range).
- To achieve a 10% relative error on the normalized kurtosis for the most central collisions at the LHC, one needs $N_{\rm ev} \sim 10^9$ events.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous studies of pion condensation in non-equilibrium thermal models (Begun 2014, 2015) and the analysis of multiplicity fluctuations in Bose systems (Begun 2005, 2006). It directly addresses the open problem of the low-$p_T$ pion enhancement at the LHC, which cannot be explained by standard thermal models (e.g., Floris 2014, Stachel 2013) or by known resonances with masses below 2.5 GeV. The proposed fluctuation measurements can distinguish between the non-equilibrium model (Petran 2013, Begun 2013) and alternative explanations such as Hagedorn-like states or disoriented chiral condensate (Blaizot 1992, Mohanty 2005). The results enable experimental tests using currently available ALICE data, with the specific $p_T$ cut of $100-200$ MeV proposed to enhance the condensate signal.
