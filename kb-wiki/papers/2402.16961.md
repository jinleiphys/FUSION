# Resolving the Ultracollinear Paradox with Effective Field Theory

> Naive intuition about scale decoupling breaks down in the presence of fermion masses. Kinematic enhancements can greatly extend the range where one needs to keep a finite mass in calculations to obtain a correct result at even the O(1) level. Treating a light fermion as massive though, leads to a known but somewhat obscure paradox, a seeming leading-order sensitivity to an arbitrarily small mass. We show how a proper formulation in effective field theory not only resolves the physical conundrum, but answers the very practical question of when fermion masses are required. This has important implications for the development of shower Monte Carlo above the weak scale.

## Key claim

The paper resolves the "ultracollinear paradox", where a fermion mass term in a propagator numerator appears to give a leading-order sensitivity to an arbitrarily small mass, by showing that the Sudakov factor from jet evolution dynamically generates an infrared cutoff that can be parametrically larger than the fermion mass, so that one can systematically decide when to treat a fermion as massive or massless based on whether the no-split probability exceeds 50%.

## Method

The authors work in a toy Yukawa theory with one massive fermion ("top"), one massless fermion ("bottom"), and one massless scalar ("higgs"), using Soft-Collinear Effective Theory (SCET) in two regimes: SCET1 where the top mass is negligible, and SCET2 where the top mass is treated as a scale comparable to the soft scale. They compute fragmentation functions at leading order and next-to-leading order, including finite-width effects for the top quark via the complex mass scheme, and use renormalization group evolution to resum large logarithms across scales.

## Key numbers

- The integral \(\int_0^\infty \frac{m^2}{(k^2 + m^2)^2} dk^2 = 1\) demonstrates the noncommutation of integration and the \(m \to 0\) limit.
- The lower cutoff on emission angle from a finite flight path gives \(k_{\perp \text{min}} \sim \sqrt{E/L}\), leading to sensitivity \(\sim m^2 L / E\).
- The minimum fermion mass measurable by the LHC is estimated as \(m_{\text{LHC, min}} \sim \mathcal{O}(100)\) eV.
- The top quark width at leading order is \(\Gamma_t = \frac{\alpha_y M_t}{4}\).
- The fragmentation function \(D_{th}(z)\) at \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha_y)\) in SCET2 contains a term \(\frac{\pi M_t}{\Gamma_t}\) from on-shell top production.
- The SCET1 splitting functions reproduce standard massless splitting functions from Ciafaloni (2010).
- Improper treatment of fermion masses affects observables at the few-percent level for \(\mathcal{O}(10\text{--}100)\) TeV colliders, growing with energy.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of ultracollinear corrections to splitting functions first noted by Ciafaloni (2010) and later encountered in electroweak shower Monte Carlo by Chen (2016), who found that proper handling of fermion masses affects observables at the few-percent level but did not explore the conceptual consequences. The paper provides a systematic EFT framework using SCET to decide when fermion masses must be kept, with implications for developing shower Monte Carlo above the weak scale and for indirect-detection of dark matter at high energies.
