# Go Green: Selected Configuration Interaction as a More Sustainable Alternative for High Accuracy

> Recently, a new distributed implementation of the full configuration interaction (FCI) method has been reported [Gao et al. J. Chem Theory Comput. 2024, 20, 1185]. Thanks to a hybrid parallelization scheme, the authors were able to compute the exact energy of propane (\ce{C3H8}) in the minimal basis STO-3G. This formidable task involves handling an active space of 26 electrons in 23 orbitals or a Hilbert space of \SI{1.3d12} determinants. This is, by far, the largest FCI calculation reported to date. Here, we illustrate how, from a general point of view, selected configuration interaction (SCI) can achieve microhartree accuracy at a fraction of the computational and memory cost, via a sparse exploration of the FCI space. The present SCI calculations are performed with the \textit{Configuration Interaction using a Perturbative Selection made Iteratively} (CIPSI) algorithm, as implemented in a determinant-driven way in the \textsc{quantum package} software. The present study reinforces the common wisdom that among the exponentially large number of determinants in the FCI space, only a tiny fraction of them significantly contribute to the energy. More importantly, it demonstrates the feasibility of achieving comparable accuracy using more reasonable and sustainable computational resources, hence reducing the ever-growing carbon footprint of computational chemistry.

## Key claim

Selected configuration interaction (SCI) can achieve microhartree accuracy for the energy of propane (C3H8) in the STO-3G basis at a fraction of the computational and memory cost of the recent full configuration interaction (FCI) calculation by Gao et al. (2024), provided that the variational space is iteratively expanded and the energy is extrapolated to the FCI limit.

## Method

The authors used the Configuration Interaction using a Perturbative Selection made Iteratively (CIPSI) algorithm, a determinant-driven SCI method implemented in the quantum package software. They first performed a preliminary CIPSI run with orbital optimization up to 1.8 million determinants, then a second CIPSI run with those optimized orbitals. The variational energy was corrected by a second-order perturbative correction computed within Epstein-Nesbet perturbation theory, and the final FCI estimate (exFCI) was obtained by a 3-point linear extrapolation of the variational energy as the perturbative correction approaches zero.

## Key numbers

- The FCI calculation of propane by Gao et al. (2024) involved a Hilbert space of 1.3e12 determinants, requiring 512 processes on 256 nodes, 19 TB of memory, and a wall time of 113.6 hours.
- The CCSDTQ energy of propane was -117.100120230 hartree, with an error of +2.451 microhartree relative to FCI, requiring 42 minutes wall time and 508 MB of memory.
- The SCI+PT2 energy with 32 million determinants was -117.10012066 hartree, with an error of +2.021 microhartree relative to FCI.
- The exFCI energy with 32 million determinants was -117.10012289(6) hartree, with an error of -0.21(6) microhartree relative to FCI.
- Achieving microhartree accuracy required approximately 16 million determinants, 25 GB of RAM, 3 hours 12 minutes wall time, and 1.1 kilowatt-hour of energy consumption.
- The largest CIPSI calculation (127 million determinants) required 138 hours 44 minutes wall time, 138 GB memory, and 42 kilowatt-hour of energy consumption.

## Context

This work directly extends the recent FCI calculation of propane by Gao et al. (2024), which broke the one-trillion determinant barrier, by demonstrating that SCI methods (specifically CIPSI) can match FCI accuracy with far more modest resources. The study reinforces the common wisdom, established by earlier SCI methods such as those of Bender (1969) and Whitten (1969) and revived by Giner et al. (2013), that only a tiny fraction of determinants in the FCI space contribute significantly to the energy. The results enable routine production of reference-quality energies for benchmarking other methods (such as CCSD, CCSD(T), CCSDT, and CCSDTQ) while reducing the carbon footprint of computational chemistry.
