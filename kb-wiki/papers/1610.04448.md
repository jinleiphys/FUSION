# Role of core excitation in (d,p) transfer reactions

> [Background:] Recent work found that core excitation can be important in extracting structure information from (d,p) reactions. [Purpose:] Our objective is to systematically explore the role of core excitation in (d,p) reactions, and understand the origin of the dynamical effects. [Method:] Based on the particle-rotor model of $n+^{10}$Be, we generate a number of models with a range of separation energies ($S_n=0.1-5.0$ MeV), while maintaining a significant core excited component. We then apply the latest extension of the momentum-space based Faddeev method, including dynamical core excitation in the reaction mechanism to all orders, to the $^{10}$Be(d,p)$^{11}$Be like reactions, and study the excitation effects for beam energies from $E_d=15-90$ MeV. [Results:] We study the resulting angular distributions and the differences between the spectroscopic factor that would be extracted from the cross sections, when including dynamical core excitation in the reaction, to that of the original structure model. We also explore how different partial waves affect the final cross section. [Conclusions:] Our results show a strong beam energy dependence of the extracted spectroscopic factors that become smaller for intermediate beam energies. This dependence increases for loosely bound systems.

## Key claim

Dynamical core excitation in (d,p) reactions can significantly distort the spectroscopic factor extracted from cross sections, with the effect being strongly dependent on beam energy and most pronounced for loosely bound systems at intermediate beam energies (around 60-70 MeV), where the extracted spectroscopic factor can drop to half its original value.

## Method

The authors generated a series of n+¹⁰Be particle-rotor models with neutron separation energies ranging from 0.1 to 5.0 MeV while maintaining a significant core-excited component. They applied the momentum-space Faddeev method extended to include dynamical core excitation to all orders, calculating elastic, inelastic, and transfer cross sections for ¹⁰Be(d,p)¹¹Be-like reactions at deuteron beam energies from 15 to 90 MeV. The spectroscopic factor that would be extracted from the cross sections was obtained by taking the ratio of the full Faddeev calculation (including core excitation) to the single-particle Faddeev prediction.

## Key numbers

- Neutron separation energies studied: S_n = 0.1 to 5.0 MeV, with core excitation energy E_x = 3.368 MeV (physical ¹⁰Be 2⁺ state) and also E_x = 0.5 MeV for comparison
- For the realistic S_n = 0.5 MeV model at E_d = 20 MeV, the normalization needed to match single-particle cross sections to core-excited predictions was SF = 0.76; at E_d = 80 MeV, it was SF = 0.48
- The spectroscopic factor ratio R_x for S_n = 0.5 MeV starts at R_x = 0.94 for E_d = 15 MeV, decreases to a minimum around R_x = 0.54 at E_d = 60-70 MeV, then rises again for higher beam energies
- For S_n = 5 MeV, the dependence of R_x on beam energy is weaker than for S_n = 0.5 MeV
- Calculations using a simple Gaussian NN interaction instead of CD-Bonn produced R_x values within 2% of those with the full CD-Bonn potential
- The theoretical spectroscopic factors for the s₁/₂ component (S^{th}_{s1/2}) ranged from 94.2% (S_n = 0.1 MeV) to 57.7% (S_n = 5.0 MeV) for models with E_x = 3.368 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Faddeev formalism including core excitation developed by Deltuva (2013, 2015), which was previously applied to ¹⁰Be(d,p)¹¹Be and showed that dynamical core excitation can be very important for the ground state population. The study systematically explores the beam energy and separation energy dependence of these effects, going beyond the single-particle structure assumptions used in earlier analyses such as Schmitt (2013). The results indicate that for reliable extraction of spectroscopic factors from (d,p) reactions at intermediate beam energies (40-90 MeV), dynamical core excitation must be explicitly included in the reaction mechanism, and the authors suggest experimental investigation of ¹⁰Be(d,p)¹¹Be at E_d = 80 MeV to test these predictions.
