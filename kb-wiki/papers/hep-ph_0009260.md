# Skyrmion-anti-Skyrmion Annihilation with Omega Mesons

> We study numerically the annihilation of an omega-stabilized Skyrmion and an anti-Skyrmion in three spatial dimensions. To our knowledge this is a first successful simulation of Skyrmion-anti-Skyrmion annihilation which follows through to the point where the energy is carried by outgoing meson waves. We encounter instabilities similar to those encountered is earlier calculations, but in our case these are not fatal and we are able to simulate through this process with a global energy loss of less than 8%, and to identify robust features of the final radiation pattern. The system passes through a singular configuration at the time of half-annihilation. This is followed by the onset of fast oscillations which are superimposed on the smoother process which leads to the appearence of outgoing spherical waves. We investigate the two prominent features of this process, the proliferation of small, fast oscillations, and the singular intermediate configuration. We find that our equations of motion allow for a regime in which the amplitude of certain small perturbations increases exponentially. This regime is similar but not identical to the situation pointed out earlier regarding the original Skyrme model. We argue that the singularity may be seen as the result of a pinch effect similar to that encountered in plasmas.

## Key claim

The authors report the first successful three-dimensional numerical simulation of Skyrmion-anti-Skyrmion annihilation that follows through to outgoing meson waves, using omega meson stabilization instead of the Skyrme term; the simulation succeeds despite encountering instabilities similar to those in earlier calculations, with a global energy loss of less than 8 percent.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the coupled equations of motion for an SU(2) pion field and a U(1) omega vector field in three spatial dimensions, using both a pion-field parameterization and a Cartesian Psi-field parameterization with the chiral constraint enforced via projection. They discretized the fields on a uniform three-dimensional grid, used a cluster of IBM SP-2 machines, and simulated head-on annihilation of a hedgehog Skyrmion and its charge-conjugated anti-Skyrmion with initial velocity beta = 0.5 each, taking advantage of symmetry to simulate only one-eighth of the physical box.

## Key numbers

- Global energy loss of less than 8 percent over the course of the simulation (from approximately 1500 MeV total initial energy)
- Initial soliton separation of 3.0 fm, with each soliton boosted at beta = 0.5
- Pion decay constant f_pi = 64 MeV, pion mass m_pi = 139 MeV, omega meson mass m_omega = 385 MeV
- Skyrmion mass of 650 MeV, Skyrmion diameter of approximately 1.1 fm
- Total initial energy of the two-soliton system: 1500 MeV (1300 MeV in solitons, approximately 200 MeV kinetic energy)
- Annihilation begins at t = 1.66 fm/c (point A), half-annihilation occurs at approximately t = 2.3 fm/c (point B), baryon number reaches zero at t = 2.85 fm/c (point C), and radiation regime begins at approximately t = 5.5 fm/c (point E)
- Unwinding of the field from points A to C takes approximately 1.2 to 1.3 fm/c; total process from touching to complete disappearance of baryon number takes about 3.5 fm/c
- Grid resolution of 12 points per fermi for main runs, with timesteps varying from 100 per fermi to 3200 per fermi in the violent regime

## Context

This work extends earlier attempts to simulate Skyrmion-anti-Skyrmion annihilation using the original Skyrme Lagrangian, which encountered numerical difficulties traced to a deviation from hyperbolic equations of motion (Livermore, 1990s). The omega-stabilized model replaces the Skyrme term with a coupling to a U(1) vector field, providing gentler dynamics that allow the simulation to proceed through the singular intermediate configuration to outgoing spherical waves. The results enable future studies of off-center annihilation, dependence on initial conditions, and a more detailed understanding of the singularity mechanism, which the authors compare to a pinch effect in plasmas.
