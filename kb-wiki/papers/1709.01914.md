# The Magnetic Field Distribution in Strongly Magnetized Neutron Stars

> In this work, we expand on a previously reported realistic calculation of the magnetic field profile for the equation of state inside strongly magnetized neutron stars. In addition to showing that magnetic fields increase quadratically with increasing baryon chemical potential of magnetized matter (instead of exponentially, as previously assumed), we show here that the magnetic field increase with baryon number density is more complex and harder to model. We do so by the analysis of several different realistic models for the microscopic description of matter in the star (including hadronic, hybrid and quark models) combined with general relativistic solutions by solving Einstein-Maxwell's field equations in a self-consistent way for stars endowed with a poloidal magnetic field.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the magnetic field profile inside strongly magnetized neutron stars increases quadratically with baryon chemical potential (not exponentially, as previously assumed), but that the increase with baryon number density is more complex and model-dependent, making a universal fit impossible for that variable.

## Method

The authors solved Einstein-Maxwell field equations self-consistently for neutron stars with a poloidal magnetic field using the LORENE numerical relativity library, combining this with three different microscopic equations of state: a hadronic model (G-model), a hybrid hadron-quark model (D-model), and a pure quark NJL model (H-model). They computed the magnetic field distribution along the stellar polar direction for stars of baryonic masses 1.6 and 2.2 solar masses at two different dipole magnetic moments, then translated the field into a function of baryon chemical potential and baryon number density.

## Key numbers

- The magnetic field profile as a function of baryon chemical potential is fit by a quadratic polynomial: \(B^*(\mu_B) = (a + b \mu_B + c \mu_B^2) / B_c^2 \cdot \mu\), with \(B_c = 4.414 \times 10^{13}\) G (the critical field for the electron).
- For a 2.2 M\(_\odot\) star: \(a = -7.69 \times 10^{-1}\) G\(^2\)/(A m\(^2\)), \(b = 1.20 \times 10^{-3}\) G\(^2\)/(A m\(^2\) MeV), \(c = -3.46 \times 10^{-7}\) G\(^2\)/(A m\(^2\) MeV\(^2\)).
- For a 1.6 M\(_\odot\) star: \(a = -1.02\) G\(^2\)/(A m\(^2\)), \(b = 1.58 \times 10^{-3}\) G\(^2\)/(A m\(^2\) MeV), \(c = -4.85 \times 10^{-7}\) G\(^2\)/(A m\(^2\) MeV\(^2\)).
- The dipole magnetic moments used were \(\mu = 3 \times 10^{32}\) Am\(^2\) and \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{30}\) Am\(^2\) for the 2.2 M\(_\odot\) star, and \(\mu = 2 \times 10^{32}\) Am\(^2\) and \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{30}\) Am\(^2\) for the 1.6 M\(_\odot\) star.
- The central stellar magnetic field reaches close to the code's upper limit of \(\sim 10^{18}\) G; the surface field reaches \(\sim 10^{15}\) G, the maximum observed on a star surface (Melatos 1999).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous calculation (Dexheimer 2016) by providing a numerical fit for the magnetic field profile as a function of baryon chemical potential, and for the first time as a function of baryon number density. It directly contradicts the ad hoc exponential profile of Bandyopadhyay (1997), which was used in approximately one hundred publications (including Menezes 2008, Menezes 2009, Barkovich 2004, Dexheimer 2011) but does not satisfy Maxwell's equations as noted by Menezes (2016). The quadratic fit enables future studies of magnetic field effects on equation of state stiffness, particle populations, phase transitions, temperature, and transport properties, but the authors caution that the field distribution is direction-dependent and requires the full Einstein-Maxwell formalism rather than a simple TOV treatment.
