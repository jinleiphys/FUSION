# Orthogonality Effects in Relativistic Models of Nucleon Knockout Reactions

> We study the effect of wave function orthogonality in the relativistic treatment of the nucleon removal reactions (gamma, p) and (e, e' p). The continuum wave function describing the outgoing nucleon is made orthogonal to the relevant bound states using the Gram-Schmidt procedure. This procedure has the advantage of preserving the asymptotic character of the continuum wave function and hence the elastic observables are unaffected. The orthogonality effects are found to be negligible for (e, e' p) reactions for missing momenta up to 700 MeV/c. This holds true for both parallel and perpendicular kinematics. By contrast the orthogonalization of the wave functions appears to have a more pronounced effect in the case of (gamma, p) reactions. We find that the orthogonality effect can be significant in this case particularly for large angles. Polarization of the outgoing protons and photon asymmetry show more sensitivity than the cross sections. If the orthogonality condition is imposed solely on this one hole state the effects are usually smaller.

# Orthogonality Effects in Relativistic Models of Nucleon Knockout Reactions

## Key claim

The Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization procedure applied to continuum and bound nucleon wave functions in relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation (RDWIA) calculations shows that orthogonality effects are negligible for (e, e' p) reactions up to missing momenta of 700 MeV/c, but can be significant for (gamma, p) reactions, particularly at large angles and for spin observables. The authors note that if orthogonality is imposed solely on the one hole state involved in the reaction (minimal orthogonality), the effects are usually smaller than when all occupied states are included (full orthogonality).

## Method

The authors used the Gram-Schmidt procedure to orthogonalize the Dirac continuum wave function (generated from complex energy-dependent optical potentials) to the bound state wave functions (generated from real scalar and vector Woods-Saxon potentials) for nucleon knockout reactions. They applied this method to both (gamma, p) and (e, e' p) reactions on targets of 12C, 16O, 40Ca, and 208Pb, calculating cross sections, proton polarization, and photon asymmetry under two schemes: minimal orthogonality (only the specific hole state) and full orthogonality (all occupied states).

## Key numbers

- For (e, e' p) reactions, orthogonality effects are negligible for missing momenta up to 700 MeV/c, for both parallel and perpendicular kinematics.
- For (gamma, p) reactions, calculations were performed for photon energies from 60 to 500 MeV on four nuclei (12C, 16O, 40Ca, 208Pb).
- At low continuum nucleon energies (approximately 10 MeV), the magnitude of the overlap coefficients b_{L_B, J_B} can be close to 0.1.
- For 16O(gamma, p) at E_gamma = 100 MeV and 196 MeV, cross sections are affected only slightly at most angles but deviations can be large at extreme backward angles.
- For 40Ca(gamma, p) at theta_p = 90 degrees, effects on the direct knockout cross section are rather small up to photon energies of 300 MeV.
- For 40Ca(gamma, p) at theta_p = 135 degrees, larger effects on the cross section are found, particularly with full orthogonalization.
- For (e, e' p) on 16O in perpendicular kinematics with proton kinetic energy T_p = 35 MeV, orthogonality effects are small except at large missing momenta.
- For (e, e' p) on 40Ca in parallel kinematics with T_p = 100 MeV, orthogonality corrections are negligible.

## Context

This work extends earlier non-relativistic studies of orthogonality effects by Boffi et al. (1982, 1983) and Ciofi Degli Atti et al. (1983) into the relativistic framework, confirming their conclusion that (e, e' p) reactions are largely unaffected but finding that (gamma, p) reactions show non-negligible effects. The authors disagree with Boffi et al. (1982, 1983) regarding (gamma, p) cross sections, finding that even minimal orthogonalization produces effects due to spin-flip contributions not accounted for in the earlier work. The study enables more precise calculations for upcoming experiments at new electron beam facilities, particularly for spin-dependent observables in (gamma, p) reactions and for higher-order processes involving two-nucleon currents.
