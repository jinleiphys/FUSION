# A Lattice Study of the Glue in the Nucleon

> By introducing an additional operator into the action and using the Feynman-Hellmann theorem we describe a method to determine both the quark line connected and disconnected terms of matrix elements. As an illustration of the method we calculate the gluon contribution (chromo-electric and chromo-magnetic components) to the nucleon mass.

## Key claim
The paper presents a method using the Feynman-Hellmann theorem to extract the gluon momentum fraction in the nucleon from lattice QCD, demonstrating that this approach yields a cleaner signal than direct computation of the noisy disconnected quark-line diagrams. The method is illustrated in the quenched approximation, with the caveat that it requires dedicated simulations for each operator of interest.

## Method
The authors introduce an additional operator into the Wilson gluonic action, modifying the spatial and temporal plaquette couplings asymmetrically by a parameter λ. Using the Feynman-Hellmann theorem, the gradient of the nucleon mass with respect to λ at λ=0 is related to the gluon momentum fraction ⟨x⟩_g. The calculation is performed on a 24³×48 lattice at β=6.0 with quenched Wilson clover fermions at five quark masses (κ values from 0.1320 to 0.1342), using O(500) configurations per ensemble and data at λ = -0.03333, 0.0, and 0.03333.

## Key numbers
- Nucleon masses am_N at λ=0 range from 0.6799(62) (κ=0.1342) to 0.9772(33) (κ=0.1320)
- Pion masses am_π range from 0.31433(62) (κ=0.1342) to 0.55499(48) (κ=0.1320)
- Bare lattice gluon momentum fraction ⟨x⟩_g^lat ranges from 0.4826(456) (κ=0.1320) to 0.5893(1062) (κ=0.1342)
- Renormalization factor Z_g = 0.748 at β=6.0 from a Padé expression
- Final renormalized result: ⟨x⟩_g^{MSbar}(μ=2 GeV) = 0.43(7)(5), where the first error is from the lattice determination and the second from renormalization

## Context
This work extends previous lattice attempts to compute the gluon momentum fraction, which were plagued by noise from disconnected diagrams (Gockeler 1996, Meyer 2007, Liu 2012). The Feynman-Hellmann approach is a generalization of methods used to determine the sigma term (Horsley 2011), the β-function (Bali 1995), and singlet terms (Detmold 2004). The result improves upon the authors' previous quenched estimate of 0.53(23) from O(5000) configurations, and the method is noted as being applicable to dynamical fermion simulations in future work.
