# On the Cottingham formula and the electromagnetic contribution to the proton-neutron mass splitting

> The excess mass of the neutron over the proton arises from two sources within the Standard Model, electromagnetism and the splitting of the down and up quark masses. The Cottingham Formula provides a means of determining the QED corrections from the forward Compton Amplitude, but this is challenged by the need for a subtraction function and the mixing of the QED and QCD (electro-weak) effects. I review the present understanding of the Cottingham Formula, including a discussion on the development of the formula, its renormalization which induces the mixing of QED and QCD effects, and the necessary modeling of the subtraction function that must be done to arrive a numerical prediction. I summarize the Regge Model originally proposed by Gasser and Leutwyler and I also review the proposed model by Walker-Loud, Carlson and Miller, which is an interpolation function between the low and high $Q^2$ regimes, both of which are anchored by rigorous theoretical underpinnings, for which I argue a more reliable theoretical uncertainty estimate can be obtained.

# On the Cottingham formula and the electromagnetic contribution to the proton-neutron mass splitting

## Key claim
The Cottingham Formula provides a means of determining the QED corrections to the proton-neutron mass splitting from the forward Compton Amplitude, but a rigorous separation of QED and QCD (quark mass difference) contributions is not possible in a scheme-independent manner due to renormalization-induced mixing. A numerical prediction requires modeling the subtraction function, and the author argues that an interpolation function between low and high Q² regimes yields a more reliable theoretical uncertainty estimate than the Regge model originally proposed by Gasser and Leutwyler.

## Method
The paper reviews the derivation of the Cottingham Formula, which convolves a photon propagator with the forward Compton Amplitude and applies a dispersive parameterization of the structure functions. The renormalization procedure, first worked out by Collins (1978), cancels the UV divergence against a counterterm proportional to the isovector quark mass operator, inducing mixing between QED and QCD effects. Two models for the unknown subtraction function T₁(0,Q²) are compared: the Regge model of Gasser and Leutwyler (1975) and an interpolation function anchored by chiral effective field theory at low Q² and perturbative QCD at high Q², proposed by Walker-Loud, Carlson and Miller (2012).

## Key numbers
- The old Gasser-Leutwyler result for the QED correction to M_p - M_n: 0.76(30) MeV from 1975.
- The Walker-Loud, Carlson and Miller (2012) interpolation model result: δM^γ_{p-n} = 1.30(03)(47) MeV.
- The improved interpolation by Erben, Shanahan, Thomas and Young (2014): δM^γ_{p-n} = 1.04(35) MeV.
- The dipole scale used in the simplest interpolation model: m₀² ≈ 0.71 GeV².
- The residual finite correction from renormalization is estimated as ±(3α_fs)/(8πM) ln(Λ_UV²/Λ²) × (e_u² m_u - e_d² m_d)/δ × ⟨p|δ(ūu - d̄d)|p⟩.

## Context
This review extends the line of work on the Cottingham Formula originating with Cottingham (1963) and the renormalization by Collins (1978). It contrasts the Regge model of Gasser and Leutwyler (1975) and Gasser, Hoferichter, Leutwyler and Rusetsky (2015) with the interpolation model of Walker-Loud, Carlson and Miller (2012), which was subsequently refined by Erben, Shanahan, Thomas and Young (2014). The work is motivated by lattice QCD calculations that either compute the strong m_d - m_u correction and subtract it from experiment (Beane 2006, Walker-Loud 2009, de Divitiis 2011, Horsley 2012, Brantley 2016, Heffernan 2017) or incorporate QED directly (Blum 2010, Borsanyi 2013, Horsley 2015). The author identifies the isovector magnetic polarizability as the key experimental quantity needed to improve predictions, which would enable a rigorous test of low-energy QCD against lattice QCD results.
