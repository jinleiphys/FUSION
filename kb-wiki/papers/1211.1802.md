# Integral relations and the adiabatic expansion method for 1+2 reactions above the breakup threshold: Helium trimers with soft-core potentials

> The integral relations formalism introduced in \cite{bar09,rom11}, and designed to describe 1+$N$ reactions, is extended here to collision energies above the threshold for the target breakup. These two relations are completely general, and in this work they are used together with the adiabatic expansion method for the description of 1+2 reactions. The neutron-deuteron breakup, for which benchmark calculations are available, is taken as a test of the method. The s-wave collision between the $^4$He atom and $^4$He$_2$ dimer above the breakup threshold and the possibility of using soft-core two-body potentials plus a short-range three-body force will be investigated. Comparisons to previous calculations for the three-body recombination and collision dissociation rates will be shown.

## Key claim

The integral relations formalism, previously used for 1+2 reactions below the breakup threshold, is extended to collision energies above the target breakup threshold; the method works accurately only when the integral relations are used to extract the K-matrix from the internal part of the wave function, because extracting it from the asymptotic part leads to much slower convergence and errors up to 10% in the phase shift.

## Method

The authors generalize the integral relations of Barletta et al. (2009) and Romero-Redondo et al. (2011) to energies above the two-body breakup threshold by treating the infinitely many open breakup channels via hyperspherical adiabatic expansion, where each breakup channel asymptotically corresponds to a fixed grand-angular quantum number K. The trial three-body wave function is obtained by solving Faddeev equations with the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method, and the K-matrix elements are computed from the internal part of the wave function using the Kohn Variational Principle. The irregular Bessel functions in the integral relations are regularized by solving the radial Schrodinger equation for each adiabatic potential individually.

## Key numbers

- For neutron-deuteron scattering at 14.1 MeV laboratory energy, the inelasticity parameter |S_11| converges to 0.4645 (doublet) and 0.9782 (quartet) using integral relations, matching the benchmark values of 0.4649 and 0.9782 from Friar et al. (1995) to within 0.1%
- For neutron-deuteron scattering at 42.0 MeV, |S_11| converges to 0.5022 (doublet) and 0.9033 (quartet), matching the benchmark values of 0.5022 and 0.9033 from Friar et al. (1995)
- The real part of the phase shift Re(delta) for neutron-deuteron scattering converges to 105.53 degrees (doublet, 14.1 MeV) and 68.96 degrees (quartet, 14.1 MeV), compared to benchmark values of 105.50 and 68.96 degrees from Friar et al. (1995)
- Without integral relations, Re(delta) at 14.1 MeV for the doublet case converges to only 102.49 degrees (K_max=28), differing from the benchmark by about 3%
- The soft-core gaussian potential for He-He has the form V(r) = -1.227 exp(-r^2/10.03^2) with strength in K and range in atomic units

## Context

This work extends the integral relations formalism of Barletta et al. (2009) and Romero-Redondo et al. (2011), which was previously limited to energies below the breakup threshold, to describe breakup reactions. The method is tested against benchmark neutron-deuteron breakup calculations from Friar et al. (1990, 1995). The authors then apply the method to He-He2 collisions using soft-core potentials, following the approach of Kievsky et al. (2011) who showed that soft-core gaussian potentials plus a three-body force can reproduce the bound-state properties of hard-core potentials like LM2M2 from Aziz et al. (1991). This enables future studies of helium trimer breakup without the technical difficulties of hard-core repulsion.
