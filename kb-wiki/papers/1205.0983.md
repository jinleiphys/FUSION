# Phase transitions in dense matter and the maximum mass of neutron stars

> The recent precise measurement of the mass of pulsar PSR J1614$-$2230, as well as observational indications of even more massive neutron stars, has revived the question of the composition of matter at the high densities prevailing inside neutron-star cores. We study the impact on the maximum possible neutron-star mass of an "exotic" core consisting of non-nucleonic matter. For this purpose, we study the occurrence of a first-order phase transition in nucleonic matter. Given the current lack of knowledge of non-nucleonic matter, we consider the stiffest possible equation of state subject only to the constraints of causality and thermodynamic stability. The case of a hadron-quark phase transition is discussed separately. The purely nucleonic matter is described using a set of unified equations of state that have been recently developed to permit a consistent treatment of both homogeneous and inhomogeneous phases. We then compute the mass-radius relation of cold nonaccreting neutron stars with and without exotic cores from the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. We find that even if there is a significant softening of the equation of state associated with the actual transition to an exotic phase, there can still be a stiffening at higher densities closer to the center of the star that is sufficient to increase the maximum possible mass. However with quarks the maximum neutron-star mass is always reduced by assuming that the sound speed is limited by $c/\sqrt{3}$ as suggested by QCD calculations. In particular, by invoking such a phase transition, it becomes possible to support PSR J1614$-$2230 with a nucleonic equation of state that is soft enough to be compatible with the kaon and pion production in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

Even if a first-order phase transition to exotic non-nucleonic matter significantly softens the equation of state at intermediate densities, a subsequent stiffening at higher densities closer to the star's center can increase the maximum possible neutron-star mass, provided the exotic phase obeys only causality and thermodynamic stability. However, if the exotic phase consists of deconfined quarks with sound speed limited to \(c/\sqrt{3}\) as suggested by QCD calculations, the maximum neutron-star mass is always reduced.

## Method

The authors used three unified Brussels-Montreal equations of state (BSk19, BSk20, BSk21) for purely nucleonic matter, derived from nuclear energy-density functionals fitted to measured nuclear masses and to different stiffnesses of pure neutron matter. They modeled a first-order phase transition to an exotic phase at a baryon density \(n_{\rm N} = 0.2\) fm\(^{-3}\), constructing the stiffest possible equation of state for the exotic phase subject only to causality (sound speed \(\leq c\)) and thermodynamic stability, and separately considered a quark phase with sound speed limited to \(c/\sqrt{3}\). The mass-radius relation for cold nonaccreting neutron stars was computed by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations.

## Key numbers

- The maximum neutron-star mass for the softest nucleonic equation of state (BSk19) without exotic matter is too low to support PSR J1614\(-\)2230 (mass \(1.97 \pm 0.04 M_\odot\)), but with a causally limited exotic phase transition, BSk19 can support this pulsar.
- For the causally limited exotic phase, the maximum mass increases for all three functionals; the maximally stiff equation of state (causal limit at all densities above \(n_{\rm N}\)) yields a maximum mass of approximately \(3.7 M_\odot\), but such configurations are thermodynamically unstable.
- For the quark phase transition with sound speed limited to \(c/\sqrt{3}\), the maximum neutron-star mass is always reduced compared to the purely nucleonic case; effective bag constants for BSk19, BSk20, and BSk21 are 78.6, 65.5, and 56.7 MeV fm\(^{-3}\), respectively.
- The transition density \(n_{\rm N}\) is set to \(0.2\) fm\(^{-3}\), slightly above the highest density found in nuclei as predicted by Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations on more than 8000 nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous unified equations of state for purely nucleonic neutron stars (Chamel et al. 2011) by investigating how a first-order phase transition to exotic matter affects the maximum mass. It addresses the tension between the measured mass of PSR J1614\(-\)2230 (Demorest et al. 2010) and constraints from kaon production in heavy-ion collisions (Fuchs 2001, Sturm 2001, Hartnack 2006) and pion production ratios (Xiao 2009), which favor a soft nucleonic equation of state like BSk19. The study shows that a phase transition to a causally limited exotic phase can reconcile these observations without invoking a "fifth force" (Wen 2009) or variations of the gravitational constant (Wen 2012). The results also provide a lower bound on the maximum mass for first-order transitions, following the approach of Rhoades and Ruffini (1974), and rule out the identification of GRO J0422+32 as a neutron star if thermodynamic stability is required.
