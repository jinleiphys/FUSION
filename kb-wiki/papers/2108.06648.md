# Scaling behaviors of heavy flavor meson suppression and flow in different nuclear collision systems at the LHC

> We explore the system size dependence of heavy-quark-QGP interaction by studying the heavy flavor meson suppression and elliptic flow in Pb-Pb, Xe-Xe, Ar-Ar and O-O collisions at the LHC. The space-time evolution of the QGP is simulated using a (3+1)-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic model, while the heavy-quark-QGP interaction is described by an improved Langevin approach that includes both collisional and radiative energy loss inside a thermal medium. Within this framework, we provides a reasonable description of the $D$ meson suppression and flow coefficients in Pb-Pb collisions, as well as predictions for both $D$ and $B$ meson observables in other collision systems yet to be measured. We find a clear hierarchy for the heavy meson suppression with respect to the size of the colliding nuclei, while their elliptic flow coefficient relies on both the system size and the geometric anisotropy of the QGP. Sizable suppression and flow are predicted for both $D$ and $B$ mesons in O-O collisions, which serve as a crucial bridge of jet quenching between large and small collision systems. Scaling behaviors between different collision systems are shown for heavy meson suppression factor and the bulk-eccentricity-rescaled heavy meson elliptic flow as functions of the number of participant nucleons in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that both the nuclear modification factor ($R_\mathrm{AA}$) and the bulk-eccentricity-rescaled elliptic flow ($v_2/\varepsilon_2$) of heavy flavor mesons scale with the number of participant nucleons ($N_\mathrm{part}$) across different collision systems, but the abstract glosses over that the $v_2$ scaling only holds after rescaling by the bulk eccentricity, and that the $R_\mathrm{AA}$ scaling shows slight breaking due to different initial heavy quark spectra at different center-of-mass energies.

## Method

The space-time evolution of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) was simulated using the (3+1)-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic model CLVisc with initial conditions from the Trento model, using Woods-Saxon distributions for nuclear density profiles. Heavy quark propagation through the QGP was described by an improved Langevin approach that includes both collisional (drag and thermal random force) and radiative (medium-induced gluon bremsstrahlung) energy loss, with a fixed spatial diffusion coefficient $D_\mathrm{s}(2\pi T)=4$. Heavy quarks were converted to heavy flavor mesons via a hybrid coalescence-fragmentation model after the medium temperature dropped below $T_\mathrm{c}=160$ MeV.

## Key numbers

- Heavy quark diffusion coefficient set to $D_\mathrm{s}(2\pi T)=4$, adjusted to describe Pb-Pb data at $\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}}=5.02$ TeV.
- Specific shear viscosity of the QGP set to $\eta_v/s = 0.16$ in the hydrodynamic model.
- Initial proper time for hydrodynamic evolution: $\tau_0 = 0.6$ fm.
- Switching temperature for hadronization via Cooper-Frye formula: $T_{\rm sw}=137$ MeV.
- Lower cut-off for gluon energy in radiative energy loss: $\omega_0 = \pi T$.
- QGP critical temperature for heavy quark hadronization: $T_\mathrm{c}=160$ MeV.
- Center-of-mass energies: Pb-Pb at 5.02 TeV, Xe-Xe at 5.44 TeV, Ar-Ar at 5.85 TeV, O-O at 6.5 TeV.
- Woods-Saxon radius parameters: $^{208}$Pb $R_0=6.62$ fm, $^{129}$Xe $R_0=5.40$ fm, $^{40}$Ar $R_0=3.53$ fm, $^{16}$O $R_0=2.608$ fm.
- Number of Trento events averaged for smooth initial entropy distributions: 5000 per centrality interval.

## Context

This work extends the study of heavy quark energy loss and jet quenching from large systems (Pb-Pb) to intermediate and small systems (Xe-Xe, Ar-Ar, O-O), building on prior hydrodynamic and transport frameworks such as CLVisc (Pang 2018) and the improved Langevin approach (Cao 2013, Cao 2015). It contrasts with earlier studies that found no jet quenching in small systems like p-Pb (ALICE 2019) by predicting sizable suppression and flow in O-O collisions, which could serve as a bridge between large and small collision systems. The scaling behaviors identified enable future experimental tests at the LHC to disentangle the effects of medium size and geometric anisotropy on heavy flavor observables.
