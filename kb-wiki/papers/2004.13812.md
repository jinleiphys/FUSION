# Heavy-ion physics: freedom to do hot, dense, exciting QCD

> In these two lectures I review the basics of heavy-ion collisions at relativistic energies and the physics we can do with them. I aim to cover the basics on the kinematics and observables in heavy-ion collider experiments, the basics on the phenomenology of the nuclear matter phase diagram, some of the model building and simulations currently used in the heavy-ion physics community and a selected list of amazing phenomenological discoveries and predictions.

## Key claim

These two lectures review the basics of heavy-ion collisions at relativistic energies, covering kinematics, observables, the nuclear matter phase diagram, and model building, but the author explicitly frames the content as a pedagogical introduction that "will rely heavily on previous enlightening talks and short courses given at different venues" and on various textbooks, rather than presenting new original research results.

## Method

The lectures review the essential ideas of QCD as the theory of strong interactions, including the running coupling, confinement, and chiral symmetry, then discuss the phase diagram of nuclear matter in the temperature-baryon chemical potential plane. The author describes the four stages of a heavy-ion collision (Lorentz-contracted nuclei approaching, collision, QGP formation and expansion, hadronization) and explains how observables such as flow coefficients, centrality, and the Glauber model are used to characterize the collision and access different stages of the system's evolution.

## Key numbers

- At LHC with sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV, the average energy density is approximately 12 GeV/fm^3, which is about 20 times the energy density in a hadron
- Lattice QCD indicates that QCD matter in thermal equilibrium at T ~ 300 MeV has energy density epsilon ~ 12 T^4 = 12.7 GeV/fm^3
- The Lorentz contraction factor gamma is of order 100 at RHIC and of order 2500 at LHC
- The QCD scale Lambda_QCD is of order 200-300 MeV in the MSbar scheme for three active flavors
- The rho meson mass spectral distribution peaks at approximately 0.6 GeV^2 while the a1 axial-vector meson peaks at approximately 1.2 GeV^2
- The expected partons-to-hadrons phase transition occurs around T ~ Lambda_QCD ~ 200 MeV and n_B ~ Lambda_QCD^3 ~ 1 fm^{-3}

## Context

This paper is a lecture-style review that builds upon earlier pedagogical treatments (Talks2000) and textbooks such as Busza (2018), Wong (1994), Csernai (1994), Shuryak (2004), Vogt (2007), Florkowski (2010), and Sarkar (2010). It extends the discussion of QCD under extreme conditions by connecting the theoretical framework of chiral symmetry breaking and the QCD phase diagram to experimental programs at CERN-LHC, RHIC-BES, FAIR-SIS, and JINR-NICA, enabling students to understand how heavy-ion collisions probe the phase transition between hadron gas and quark-gluon plasma.
