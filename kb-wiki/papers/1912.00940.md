# Gravimeter search for compact dark matter objects moving in the Earth

> Dark matter could be composed of compact dark objects (CDOs). These objects may interact very weakly with normal matter and could move freely {\it inside} the Earth. A CDO moving in the inner core of the Earth will have an orbital period near 55 min and produce a time dependent signal in a gravimeter. Data from superconducting gravimeters rule out such objects moving inside the Earth unless their mass $m_D$ and or orbital radius $a$ are very small so that $m_D\, a < 1.2\times 10^{-13}M_\oplus R_\oplus$. Here $M_\oplus$ and $R_\oplus$ are the mass and radius of the Earth.

# Gravimeter search for compact dark matter objects moving in the Earth

## Key claim
Data from superconducting gravimeters rule out compact dark objects (CDOs) moving inside the Earth unless the product of their mass and orbital radius satisfies $m_D a < 1.2 \times 10^{-13} M_\oplus R_\oplus$, where $M_\oplus$ and $R_\oplus$ are the Earth's mass and radius. This limit depends on the unknown orbital inclination angle and assumes the CDO moves subsonically with negligible non-gravitational interactions.

## Method
The authors analyzed approximately 10 years of superconducting gravimeter data from the Black Forest Observatory (BFO) in Germany and the Canberra (CB) station in Australia, focusing on the frequency band 0.2-0.3 mHz where a CDO orbiting in the Earth's inner core would produce a periodic signal near 0.305 mHz (55 minute period). They corrected the raw gravity data for atmospheric pressure effects using empirically determined admittances (-3.0 nms$^{-2}$/hPa for BFO, -2.9 nms$^{-2}$/hPa for CB), subtracted synthetic tidal models, flagged and removed data segments contaminated by earthquakes and instrument artifacts, and computed Fourier amplitude spectra of the residual gravity signals. The detection limit was calibrated by injecting harmonic signals of known amplitude and varying phase into the data.

## Key numbers
- Orbital period for a CDO moving in the Earth's inner core: approximately 55 minutes, corresponding to frequency $\nu_0 = 0.305$ mHz
- Upper limit on gravity signal at 0.3 mHz: $\Delta g(0.3\ \mathrm{mHz}) < 3\ \mathrm{pm/s^2}$ from BFO data
- Final constraint on CDO parameters: $m_D a / (M_\oplus R_\oplus) < 1.2 \times 10^{-13}$
- For an orbital radius $a \approx 0.1 R_\oplus$, the mass limit is $m_D < 1.2 \times 10^{-12} M_\oplus = 4 \times 10^{-18} M_\odot = 7 \times 10^{12}$ kg
- Earth's central density: $\rho_c = 13.1$ g/cm$^3$; Earth's average density: $\bar\rho_\oplus = 5.51$ g/cm$^3$
- Empirical pressure admittance at BFO: -3.0 nms$^{-2}$/hPa; at CB: -2.9 nms$^{-2}$/hPa

## Context
This work extends the search for compact dark objects to masses between $10^{-19}$ and $10^{-11} M_\odot$, a range below the $10^{-11} M_\odot$ lower limit probed by microlensing surveys (Microlensing 2019). The authors previously searched for gravitational waves from CDOs orbiting inside the sun (Horowitz 2019), ruling out close binaries above $10^{-9} M_\odot$. The present analysis uses existing superconducting gravimeter data from the Global Geodynamics Project and International Geodynamics and Earth Tide Service networks, originally deployed for geophysical observations such as Earth tides and seismic free oscillations. The results enable future searches using lunar gravimeters or the LISA gravitational wave detector, and constrain the capture probability of CDOs by the Earth to less than $10^{-3}$ for $m_D = 10^{-18} M_\odot$ to less than $10^{-1}$ for $m_D = 10^{-16} M_\odot$.
