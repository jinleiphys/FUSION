# Space-time variation of the s and c quark masses

> Space-time variation of fundamental physical constants in expanding Universe is predicted by a number of popular models. The masses of second generation quarks are larger than first generation quark masses by several orders of magnitude, therefore space-time variation in quark masses may significantly vary between each generation. We evaluate limits on variation in the s and c quark masses from Big Bang nucleosynthesis, Oklo natural nuclear reactor, Yb+, Cs and Rb clock data. The construction of 229Th nuclear clock is expected to enhance these limits by several orders of magnitude. Furthermore, constraints are obtained on an oscillating scalar or pseudoscalar cold dark matter field, as interactions of the field with quarks produce variations in quark masses.

## Key claim

The paper derives new limits on the space-time variation of the strange (s) and charm (c) quark masses from multiple phenomena, and for the first time presents constraints on charm quark mass variation; the strongest limits come from the Oklo natural nuclear reactor, while a future 229Th nuclear clock is expected to improve these limits by several orders of magnitude, but the results depend on the assumption that the QCD scale ΛQCD does not vary and that other fundamental constants remain fixed.

## Method

The authors used the Walecka model to relate variations in s and c quark masses to changes in nucleon and meson masses via sigma terms from lattice QCD (σs = 53 MeV, σc = 78 MeV), then propagated these through sensitivity formulas for deuteron binding energy (Big Bang nucleosynthesis), the shift of the low-energy neutron resonance at the Oklo reactor, the proton-electron mass ratio from Yb+ and Cs clock data, and nuclear magnetic moment ratios from Rb/Cs atomic fountain clock data. For dark matter constraints, they considered both quadratic-in-φ and linear-in-φ interactions of an oscillating scalar or pseudoscalar cold dark matter field with quarks, using the same experimental limits to constrain the coupling scales Λ'f and Λf.

## Key numbers

- Strange quark mass: ms = 93 MeV; charm quark mass: mc = 1.3 GeV
- Nucleon mass sensitivity: δmN/mN = 0.056 δms/ms and 0.083 δmc/mc
- Limit on s quark mass variation from Big Bang nucleosynthesis: |δms/ms| < 0.0018
- Limit on c quark mass variation from Big Bang nucleosynthesis: |δmc/mc| < 0.025
- Limit on s quark mass variation from Oklo reactor: |δms/ms| < 1.7×10−10, corresponding to |(1/ms)(dms/dt)| < 9.4×10−20 yr−1
- Limit on c quark mass variation from Oklo reactor: |δmc/mc| < 1.2×10−9, corresponding to |(1/mc)(dmc/dt)| < 6.7×10−19 yr−1
- Limit on s quark mass variation from proton-electron mass ratio: |(1/ms)(dms/dt)| < 7.9×10−16 yr−1
- Limit on c quark mass variation from proton-electron mass ratio: |(1/mc)(dmc/dt)| < 5.3×10−16 yr−1
- Limit on s quark mass variation from nuclear magnetic moments (Rb/Cs): |(1/ms)(dms/dt)| < 9.8×10−15 yr−1
- Expected limit from 229Th nuclear clock: |(1/ms)(dms/dt)| ≲ 2×10−22 yr−1 and |(1/mc)(dmc/dt)| ≲ 1×10−22 yr−1

## Context

This work extends earlier constraints on strange quark mass variation from Shuryak (2003) and Tedesco (2006) by incorporating updated lattice QCD sigma terms and improved experimental limits, and presents the first constraints on charm quark mass variation. The results enable new searches for scalar and pseudoscalar dark matter interactions with second-generation quarks, complementing prior work by Stadnik (2015) on photons, electrons, and light quarks; the anticipated 229Th nuclear clock, as proposed by Flambaum (2006) and reviewed by Beeks (2021), is expected to provide the most sensitive tests of quark mass variation in the future.
