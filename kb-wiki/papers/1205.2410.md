# Monte Carlo approach for hadron azimuthal correlations in high energy proton and nuclear collisions

> We use a Monte Carlo approach to study hadron azimuthal angular correlations in high energy proton-proton and central nucleus-nucleus collisions at the BNL Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) energies at mid-rapidity. We build a hadron event generator that incorporates the production of $2\to 2$ and $2\to 3$ parton processes and their evolution into hadron states. For nucleus-nucleus collisions we include the effect of parton energy loss in the Quark-Gluon Plasma using a modified fragmentation function approach. In the presence of the medium, for the case when three partons are produced in the hard scattering, we analyze the Monte Carlo sample in parton and hadron momentum bins to reconstruct the angular correlations. We characterize this sample by the number of partons that are able to hadronize by fragmentation within the selected bins. In the nuclear environment the model allows hadronization by fragmentation only for partons with momentum above a threshold $p_T^{\tiny{thresh}}=2.4$ GeV. We argue that one should treat properly the effect of those partons with momentum below the threshold, since their interaction with the medium may lead to showers of low momentum hadrons along the direction of motion of the original partons as the medium becomes diluted.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Monte Carlo approach to study hadron azimuthal angular correlations in proton-proton and nucleus-nucleus collisions, arguing that the double-peak or broadening structure observed in the away-side correlations may be described by considering partons with momentum below a threshold of 2.4 GeV that cannot hadronize via fragmentation but instead interact with the bulk medium to produce low-momentum hadron showers.

## Method

The authors built a hadron event generator using MadGraph5 to produce 2-to-2 and 2-to-3 parton processes at mid-rapidity for RHIC energies, then evolved partons into hadrons via collinear fragmentation using KKP fragmentation functions. For nucleus-nucleus collisions, they incorporated parton energy loss in the Quark-Gluon Plasma using a modified fragmentation function approach, where the hard scattering is assigned a random radial position and direction to determine the average number of scatterings and energy loss for each parton. The model allows hadronization by fragmentation only for partons with momentum above a threshold of 2.4 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Parton momentum threshold for hadronization: p_T^thresh = 2.4 GeV, derived from the one-dimensional energy loss parametrization where the lower threshold is 1.6 x mu_0 with mu_0 = 1.5 GeV
- One-dimensional energy loss per unit length parameter: epsilon_0 = 2 GeV/fm for nucleus-nucleus collisions, with epsilon_0 x lambda_0 = 0.5 GeV relating to the mean free path
- Center-of-mass energy: sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV for RHIC collisions at mid-rapidity (|eta| < 0.5)
- Hadron momentum bins used for correlation analysis: leading hadron in 2.5 GeV <= p_T <= 4 GeV, away-side hadrons in 1 GeV <= p_T <= 2.5 GeV
- Parton momentum bins studied: 2-4, 6-8, 10-12, and 14-16 GeV
- The azimuthal correlation for 2-to-3 events shows peaks at 2pi/3 and 4pi/3 radians, with the correlation in data peaking at Delta phi = 2.2 radians for the selected momentum bins

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous approach (Ayala, 2012) that used leading-order QCD matrix elements for 2-to-3 parton processes but required angular cuts to avoid collinear singularities. The Monte Carlo implementation using MadGraph5 overcomes these limitations by incorporating built-in dipole subtraction methods to cancel collinear and soft divergences. The results are compared to preliminary data from the PHENIX Collaboration (Ajitanand, 2012) and to the earlier analytical approach, showing that the Monte Carlo method produces more defined correlation peaks. The paper identifies the need to study how partons below the 2.4 GeV threshold interact with the bulk medium, as their contribution may explain the observed away-side structures in heavy-ion collisions.
