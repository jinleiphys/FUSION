# Validation of the $^{10}\mathrm{Be}$ Ground-State Molecular Structure Using $^{10}\mathrm{Be}(p,pα)^{6}\mathrm{He}$ Triple Differential Reaction Cross-Section Measurements

> The cluster structure of the neutron-rich isotope $^{10}$Be has been probed via the $(p,pα)$ reaction at 150 MeV/nucleon in inverse kinematics and in quasifree conditions. The populated states of $^{6}$He residues were investigated through missing mass spectroscopy. The triple differential cross-section for the ground-state transition was extracted for quasifree angle pairs ($θ_{p}$, $θ_α$) and compared to distorted-wave impulse approximation reaction calculations performed in a microscopic framework using successively the Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Röpke product wave-function and the wave-function deduced from Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics calculations. The remarkable agreement between calculated and measured cross-sections in both shape and magnitude validates the molecular structure description of the $^{10}$Be ground-state, configured as an $α$-$α$ core with two valence neutrons occupying $π$-type molecular orbitals.

## Key claim
The measured triple differential cross-section for the ground-state transition in the \(^{10}\text{Be}(p,p\alpha)^{6}\text{He}\) reaction agrees remarkably well in both shape and magnitude with distorted-wave impulse approximation calculations using either the Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Röpke (THSR) product wave-function or the Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) wave-function, thereby validating the molecular structure description of the \(^{10}\text{Be}\) ground state as an \(\alpha\)-\(\alpha\) core with two valence neutrons in \(\pi\)-type molecular orbitals. The abstract’s claim of “validation” is conditioned on the specific agreement between the experimental and calculated cross-sections for the ground-state transition only, and on the use of microscopic reaction calculations that include the recoilless condition.

## Method
The experiment was performed at the RIKEN RIBF using a secondary \(^{10}\text{Be}\) beam at approximately 150 MeV/nucleon, produced by projectile fragmentation of a 230 MeV/nucleon \(^{18}\text{O}\) beam on a Be target. The \((p,p\alpha)\) reaction was measured in inverse kinematics with a pure solid hydrogen target, and the knocked-out \(\alpha\) clusters and recoil protons were detected in coincidence using the Recoil Proton Spectrometer and silicon-CsI telescopes, respectively. The triple differential cross-section for the ground-state transition was extracted for coplanar angle pairs \((\theta_p, \theta_\alpha)\) that include the recoilless condition, and compared to distorted-wave impulse approximation calculations using microscopic Reduced Width Amplitudes from the THSR and AMD models.

## Key numbers
- Incident beam energy: approximately 150 MeV/nucleon for the \(^{10}\text{Be}\) secondary beam.
- Missing mass resolution for the ground-state transition: \(\sigma = 1.06\) MeV, with the fitted peak centered at \((-0.02 \pm 0.03)\) MeV.
- Normalization factors between experimental and calculated triple differential cross-sections: \(1.04(7)\) for the THSR model and \(0.90(6)\) for the AMD model, both very close to unity.
- The \(^{10}\text{Be}\) beam intensity was approximately \(5 \times 10^5\) particles/second with a purity higher than 90\%.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of \(\alpha\)-cluster structure in neutron-rich Be isotopes, particularly the molecular orbital description of \(^{9}\text{Be}\) and excited states of \(^{10}\text{Be}\) by von Oertzen (1996, 1997) and Freer (2006), and the AMD calculations by Kanada-En’yo (2001, 2015). The theoretical prediction of the sensitivity of the triple differential cross-section to the \(\alpha\)-particle wave function in \(^{10}\text{Be}\) by Lyu (2018) motivated the present measurement. The results provide direct experimental evidence for the molecular structure of the \(^{10}\text{Be}\) ground state, and enable future studies of the evolution of \(\alpha\)-cluster structure in neutron-rich nuclei with increasing numbers of valence neutrons, as well as applications of knockout reactions to investigate other cluster types in unstable nuclei.
