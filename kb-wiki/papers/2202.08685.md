# Is first-order relativistic hydrodynamics in general frame stable and causal for arbitrary interaction?

> We derive a first-order, stable and causal, relativistic hydrodynamic theory from the microscopic kinetic equation using the gradient expansion technique in a general frame. The general frame is introduced from the arbitrary matching conditions for hydrodynamic fields. The interaction is introduced in the relativistic Boltzmann equation through the momentum-dependent relaxation time approximation (MDRTA) with the proposed collision operator that preserves the conservation laws. We demonstrate here for the first time that not only the general frame choice, but also the momentum dependence of microscopic interaction rate, captured through MDRTA, is imperative for producing the essential field corrections that give rise to a causal and stable first-order relativistic theory.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate for the first time that both a general hydrodynamic frame (beyond Landau-Lifshitz or Eckart) and the momentum dependence of the microscopic interaction rate (captured through a momentum-dependent relaxation time approximation) are jointly necessary to produce a causal and stable first-order relativistic hydrodynamic theory; the conventional momentum-independent relaxation time approximation leads to acausality even in a general frame.

## Method

The authors derive a first-order relativistic hydrodynamic theory from the relativistic Boltzmann equation using gradient expansion in a general frame, defined by arbitrary matching conditions (three integer indices i, j, k for scalar and vector constraints). They introduce a novel collision operator under the momentum-dependent relaxation time approximation (MDRTA) that preserves particle number and energy-momentum conservation irrespective of frame indices or the momentum dependence of the relaxation time. The out-of-equilibrium distribution function is constructed by separating homogeneous and inhomogeneous parts, with the homogeneous coefficients determined by the matching conditions and the inhomogeneous part solved from the transport equation using a polynomial basis. The resulting 14 field correction coefficients are then used to analyze the dispersion relations for shear and sound modes via linear perturbation analysis around hydrostatic equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- For the shear channel, the group velocity is given by \(v_g = \sqrt{\eta/\theta}\); causality requires \(v_g < 1\), which is satisfied for \(\Lambda > 1\) (where \(\Lambda\) is the power-law exponent of the momentum-dependent relaxation time \(\tau_R \propto \tilde{E}_p^\Lambda\)).
- The coefficient \(\theta_1\) (which controls shear channel stability) vanishes for both the Landau-Lifshitz frame (k=1) with any interaction and for \(\Lambda=0\) (momentum-independent RTA) in any general frame.
- For the longitudinal (sound) channel, the coefficient \(A_6\) becomes positive for \(\Lambda > 1\), but negative for \(0 < \Lambda < 1\) (excluding endpoints), with higher frame index values making the negative values larger.
- The coefficients \(\nu_1, \varepsilon_1\) vanish both for the Landau-Lifshitz/Eckart frame indices (i=1, j=2) for all \(\Lambda\) and at \(\Lambda=0\) for all frame choices; \(\nu_3, \varepsilon_3\) additionally vanish for \(\Lambda=1\) at all frames.
- Numerical parameters used: temperature \(T = 300\) MeV, mass \(m = 300\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the first-order stable and causal hydrodynamic framework developed by Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, and Kovtun (BDNK) (2017-2022) by deriving the field redefinition coefficients from microscopic kinetic theory rather than postulating constitutive relations. It contrasts with the earlier first-order Navier-Stokes formalism of Landau-Lifshitz (LL) and Eckart, which suffers from instability and superluminal signal propagation, and with second-order Muller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) theory (1967-1979) that was introduced to remedy those problems. The key advance is establishing a direct correlation between the momentum dependence of microscopic interactions (via MDRTA) and the causality/stability of the macroscopic hydrodynamic theory, enabling practical applications where the interaction dynamics must be carefully accounted for in first-order relativistic hydrodynamics.
