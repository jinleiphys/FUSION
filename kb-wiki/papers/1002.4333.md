# New method for calculating shell correction

> A new method is presented for calculation of the shell correction with the inclusion of the continuum part of the spectrum. The smoothing function used has a finite energy range in contrast to the Gaussian shape of the Strutinski method. The new method is specially useful for light nuclei where the generalized Strutinski procedure can not be applied.

## Key claim

The authors present a new method for calculating nuclear shell correction that uses a finite-range smoothing function instead of the infinite-range Gaussian used in the Strutinski method, which makes the procedure applicable to light nuclei where the generalized Strutinski procedure breaks down due to insufficient linear region in the smoothed level density.

## Method

The method replaces the Gaussian weight function of the standard Strutinski procedure with a finite-range weight function that is nonzero only for |x| < 1 and has continuous derivatives at the boundaries. The curvature correction polynomials are recalculated for this new weight function by solving the self-consistency condition, and the proper smoothing range is selected by requiring that the renormalized smoothing range Gamma_p = x_1^(p) * gamma_p exceeds the largest shell gap G by a factor F, with the first minimum of the plateau curve beyond this threshold being selected.

## Key numbers

- For the double magic nucleus 132Sn, the sigma variation of the shell correction is smaller than 200 keV and the deviation from the semi-classical value is less than 1 MeV.
- For 146Gd with the new method, shell correction values at the minima agree with the semi-classical result within 500 keV.
- The average difference from semi-classical results for neutrons is 0.6 MeV for the new procedure and 0.8 MeV for the generalized Strutinski procedure.
- The average difference from semi-classical results for protons is 0.4 MeV for the new procedure and 0.6 MeV for the generalized Strutinski procedure.
- For the new double magic nucleus 24O, the variation of delta E_n values is sigma approximately 0.5 MeV and the minima occur at Gamma_p approximately 8 MeV.
- The optimal factor F is 1.5-2 for light nuclei and 2.5-3.5 for heavier nuclei.
- The shell gap G is defined as the largest distance between consecutive occupied levels, replacing the harmonic oscillator estimate hbar Omega_0 = 41 A^{-1/3} MeV.

## Context

This work extends the generalized Strutinski procedure developed by Vertse et al. (1998, 2000), which introduced a modified plateau condition requiring linearity of the smoothed level density in a chosen energy interval. That procedure worked for medium and heavy nuclei but failed for light nuclei because the linear region becomes too short. The new finite-range smoothing method removes the dependence on the position of the linearity interval and enables shell correction calculations for the entire nuclear chart, including light nuclei near the drip lines such as the recently identified double magic nucleus 24O.
