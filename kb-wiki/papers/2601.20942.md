# Chiral interactions and superfluidity in the calcium isotopic chain

> We perform ab initio calculations of three-point mass differences in the odd- and even-mass $^{39-49}$Ca isotopes to probe nuclear superfluidity via empirical neutron pairing gaps. We also quantify the sensitivity of those gaps to the parameters of the interaction at mean-field level. Recent studies employing accurate chiral nuclear interactions have found these gaps to be too small. We show that experimental values can be reproduced at mean-field level by substantially increasing the attraction of the singlet $S$-wave two-nucleon contact interaction, but doing so induces an unphysical bound state of the di-neutron. The sensitivity of these predictions to the full calibration of the nuclear interaction is then studied by performing Bayesian posterior sampling in a delta-full chiral effective field theory at third chiral order. We find that pairing gaps remain largely unaffected, leaving the explanation of nuclear superfluidity as a future task for improved many-body modeling and refined interactions at higher chiral orders.

## Key claim

Experimental neutron pairing gaps in calcium isotopes can be reproduced at mean-field level by substantially increasing the attraction of the singlet S-wave two-nucleon contact interaction, but doing so induces an unphysical bound state of the di-neutron; when all 17 low-energy constants of the chiral interaction are varied simultaneously via Bayesian posterior sampling, pairing gaps remain largely unaffected, leaving the explanation of nuclear superfluidity as a future task for improved many-body modeling and refined interactions at higher chiral orders.

## Method

The authors performed spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (sHFB) calculations of two-neutron shell gaps and three-point mass differences for the calcium isotopic chain 39-49Ca using the delta-full chiral effective field theory interaction at next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO). They first isolated the impact of the subleading singlet-S contact low-energy constant C_{1S0} by varying it while keeping all other constants fixed, then performed a full Bayesian posterior sampling of all 17 low-energy constants using importance resampling from a prior set of 164 interactions filtered by history matching and likelihood weighting against calibration data including the two-neutron shell gaps of 40Ca and 48Ca.

## Key numbers

- The nominal value of the subleading singlet-S contact low-energy constant C_{1S0} is 2.505 x 10^4 GeV^{-4} for the delta-NNLO(394) interaction
- A 10% reduction in C_{1S0} (shift of -0.250 x 10^4 GeV^{-4}) brought sHFB results for the three-point mass difference closer to experimental data
- The overly strong NN attraction from this 10% reduction produced an unphysical bound di-neutron with a binding energy of 66 keV
- The ensemble of 164 interactions spans a C_{1S0} range of [2.18, 2.80] x 10^4 GeV^{-4}
- The effective number of samples after importance resampling was n_eff approximately 10
- Method error for sHFB calculations was estimated at 1 MeV and EFT truncation error at 0.5 MeV (one standard deviation each)
- Experimental three-point mass differences for calcium isotopes range from 0.61 MeV (49Ca) to 3.64 MeV (40Ca), while sHFB predictions with the nominal interaction range from 0.19 MeV (39Ca) to 2.88 MeV (40Ca)

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Soma et al. (2021) and Scalesi (thesis) which found that ab initio calculations using chiral interactions yield very little of the observed pairing correlations in calcium isotopes when the many-body expansion is truncated at low orders. The authors note that VS-IMSRG(2) calculations and angular-momentum-projected coupled-cluster calculations (PCC) by Sun et al. (private communication) improve agreement with experiment, indicating that non-trivial many-body correlations play an important role. The present study quantifies the role of chiral interaction uncertainties, concluding that parametric uncertainties at third chiral order cannot remedy the lack of pairing, and that future work should analyze many-body approximations and symmetry breaking/restoration, as well as compute BE(2) electromagnetic transitions which may correlate with nuclear superfluidity.
