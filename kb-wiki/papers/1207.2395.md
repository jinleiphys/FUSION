# Nuclear recollisions in laser-assisted $α$ decay

> Laser-induced nuclear recollisions following $α$ decay in the presence of an intense laser field are investigated theoretically. We show that while an intense optical laser does not influence notably the tunneling rate in $α$ decay, it can completely change the $α$ particle spectrum. For intensities of $10^{22}-10^{23}$ W/cm$^{2}$, the field is strong enough to induce recollisions between the emitted $α$ particle and the daughter nucleus. The energy gained by the $α$ particle in the field can reach 20 MeV and suffice to trigger several types of nuclear reactions on a femtosecond time scale. Similar conclusions can be drawn about laser-induced recollisions after proton emission. Prospects for the experimental realization of laser-induced nuclear recollisions are discussed.

## Key claim

While an intense optical laser does not significantly alter the tunneling rate in alpha decay, it can completely change the alpha particle spectrum by inducing recollisions between the emitted alpha particle and the daughter nucleus, but only for a very small fraction (approximately 10^{-9} to 10^{-12}) of emitted alpha particles that are emitted within a narrow angular tolerance and specific laser phase window.

## Method

The authors developed a one-dimensional theoretical formalism based on the Strong-Field Approximation and imaginary time method to describe laser-assisted tunneling of preformed alpha clusters through the Coulomb barrier of the nucleus, following the precluster model of Buck (1992). They calculated the transition amplitude and differential decay rate for alpha particles in a monochromatic laser field, then extended the analysis to finite laser pulses (500 fs duration, corresponding to approximately 100 laser cycles) to model recollision trajectories. The recollision spectrum was obtained by summing over both regular and recollision complex trajectories, with regularization parameters to handle classical cut-off divergencies.

## Key numbers

- Laser intensities of 10^{22} to 10^{23} W/cm^2 are required to induce recollisions between the emitted alpha particle and the daughter nucleus
- The energy gained by the alpha particle in the field can reach 20 MeV, sufficient to trigger nuclear reactions
- The relative modification of natural half-lives is extremely small, on the order of 10^{-7} to 10^{-8}, showing linear dependence on field intensity
- For 106Te alpha decay (half-life 7 x 10^{-5} s), the tolerance solid angle for recollisions is 10^{-8} sr at 10^{22} W/cm^2 and 5 x 10^{-8} sr at 10^{23} W/cm^2
- The recollision fraction is approximately 10^{-9} (geometric estimate) to 10^{-12} (quantum mechanical estimate) of all emitted alpha particles
- The maximum recollision energy is approximately 3.17 times the ponderomotive energy U_p
- The relativistic field strength parameter xi is approximately 0.05, confirming non-relativistic treatment is valid
- For 106Te at intensity 7.9 x 10^{22} W/cm^2, recollisions occur only for alpha particles emitted with laser phase phi_0 in the range [1.4 pi, 1.9 pi]
- The recollision plateau extends to approximately 6 U_p in the energy spectrum

## Context

This work extends the study of laser-driven recollisions from electrons (as in non-sequential double ionization and high-harmonic generation) and muons to alpha particles for the first time. It complements prior work on direct and indirect interaction of coherent light with nuclei, including coherent driving of nuclear transitions, electron bridge mechanisms in laser-assisted internal conversion, laser-assisted beta decay, and the nuclear photoeffect. The results enable probing short-lived excited nuclear states reached via alpha decay on a femtosecond time scale, such as the 2.6 MeV excited state of 208Pb (half-life 16.7 ps) from 212Po decay or the 59.5 keV (half-life 67 ns) and 102.9 keV (half-life 80 ps) states of 237Np from 241Am decay, and could allow triggering nuclear reactions like inelastic scattering, neutron emission, or fission on time scales not available with conventional ion beams.
