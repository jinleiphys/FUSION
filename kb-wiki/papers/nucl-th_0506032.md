# A New Application of the Gursey and Radicati Mass Formula

> We study the spin- and flavour- dependent SU(6) violations in the baryon spectrum by means of a Gürsey Radicati mass formula. The average energy of each SU(6)-multiplet is described using the SU(6) invariant interaction given by a hypercentral potential containing a linear and a hypercoulomb term. We show that the non strange and strange baryon masses are in general fairly well reproduced and moreover that the Gürsey Radicati formula holds in a satisfactory way also for the excited states up to 2 GeV. The coefficients of the Gürsey Radicati SU(6) breaking part obtained by the fit of the three-quark spectrum can be used to evaluate in first approximation the splitting within multiplets also for exotic baryon systems.

# A New Application of the Gursey and Radicati Mass Formula

## Key claim

The authors show that the Gursey-Radicati (GR) mass formula, originally applied only to ground-state baryons, can describe the masses of both non-strange and strange baryon excited states up to 2 GeV, provided the SU(6)-invariant multiplet energies are taken from a hypercentral constituent quark model with a linear-plus-hypercoulomb potential. The GR breaking coefficients obtained from fitting the three-quark spectrum can then be used as a first approximation for splittings within multiplets of exotic systems such as pentaquarks, but the model neglects spatial dependence in the SU(6)-breaking part and therefore cannot describe configuration mixing.

## Method

The authors construct a three-quark Hamiltonian whose SU(6)-invariant part is the hypercentral potential V(x) = -tau/x + alpha x, solved numerically for the unperturbed energies. The SU(6)-breaking part is the generalized Gursey-Radicati mass formula expressed as a linear combination of Casimir operators: A C2[SU_SF(6)] + B C2[SU_F(3)] + C C2[SU_S(2)] + D C1[U_Y(1)] + E (C2[SU_I(2)] - 1/4 (C1[U_Y(1)])^2). The parameters are determined in two ways: first analytically from mass differences of a few well-known resonances, then by a global fit to all 3- and 4-star baryon resonances. The constituent quark mass is taken equal for all flavours, so SU(3) breaking enters only through the spin-flavour dependent terms.

## Key numbers

* The hypercentral potential parameters from the analytical fit (column I) are tau = 4.59 and alpha = 1.61 (units not specified in text, but consistent with MeV/fm scale from prior hCQM work).
* The GR breaking coefficients from the analytical fit (column I) are: A = -12.9 MeV, B = 19.9 MeV, C = 38.5 MeV, D = -225.5 MeV, E = 59.7 MeV.
* The GR breaking coefficients from the global fit (column II) differ: A = -14.5 MeV, B = 21.3 MeV, C = 37.2 MeV, D = -228.0 MeV, E = 61.5 MeV.
* The mass difference (N(1650)S11 - N(1535)S11) = 3C = 115.5 MeV (from the analytical fixing equations).
* The mass difference (Delta(1232)P33 - N(938)P11) = 9B + 3C + 3E = 179.1 MeV (from the analytical fixing equations).
* The mass difference (Sigma(1193)P11 - N(938)P11) = (3/2)E - D = 225 MeV (from the analytical fixing equations).
* The mass difference (Lambda(1116)P01 - N(938)P11) = -D - (1/2)E = 178 MeV (from the analytical fixing equations).
* The Lambda(1405) and Lambda(1520) resonances come out degenerate and above experimental values; the Sigma(1670)D13 and Sigma(1775)D15 four-star resonances have predicted masses about 100 MeV above experimental values.

## Context

This work extends the original Gursey-Radicati mass formula (Gursey and Radicati, 1964) from ground-state baryons to excited states up to 2 GeV, using the hypercentral constituent quark model (hCQM) of Ferraris et al. (1995) and related works for the SU(6)-invariant energies. It builds on earlier studies of spin- and isospin-dependent SU(6) violations in the hCQM (Giannini et al., 2001) and on the algebraic approach to baryon spectrum (Bijnens et al., 1999) which used a similar formula. The model-independent nature of the GR breaking coefficients enables their application to pentaquark systems (Buccella et al., 2005), analogous to how the Gell-Mann-Okubo formula (Gell-Mann, 1961; Okubo, 1962) has been used across different models. The next step identified by the authors is to introduce an explicit SU(6)-breaking term with spatial dependence and a clear microscopic origin.
