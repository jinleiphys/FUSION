# Bayesian Quantification of Observability and Equation of State of Twin Stars

> The possibility of discovering twin stars, two neutron stars (NSs) with the same mass but different radii, is usually studied in forward modelings by using a restricted number of NS matter equation of state (EOS) encapsulating a first-order phase transition from hadronic to quark matter (QM). Informing our likelihood function with the NS radius data from GW170817 and using a meta-model with 9-parameters capable of mimicking most NS EOSs available in the literature, we conduct a Bayesian quantification of the observability and underlying EOSs of twin stars. Of the accepted EOSs, between 12-18\% yield twin stars, depending on the restrictions we place on the second branch. The possibility of twin stars remains robust even under recent observational constraints. We show that many of these twin star scenarios are observable with currently available levels of accuracy in measuring NS radii. We also present the marginalized posterior probability density functions (PDFs) of every EOS parameter for each of four mass-radius correlation topologies. We find that the inferred EOS depends sensitively on not only whether twin stars are present, but also the category of twin stars, indicating that the observation of twin stars would provide a strong constraint on the underlying EOS. In particular, for two coexisting hybrid stars having QM cores at different densities, the PDF for QM speed of sound squared $c_{\rm qm}^2$ has two peaks, one below and another above the conformal limit $c_{\rm qm}^2=1/3$ predicted by perturbative QCD.

## Key claim

Between 12% and 18% of accepted equations of state (EOSs) yield twin stars, depending on how strictly the second stable branch is defined, and many of these twin star scenarios are observable with currently available accuracy in measuring neutron star radii.

## Method

The authors constructed a 9-parameter meta-model for hybrid neutron star EOSs by coupling a hadronic matter EOS (parameterized via binding energy per nucleon and symmetry energy expansions) to a quark matter EOS using a constant sound speed model with a Maxwell construction for the first-order phase transition. They performed Bayesian inference using a Metropolis-Hastings MCMC sampler with uniform priors, informing the likelihood with the GW170817 radius measurement of R1.4 = 11.9 ± 0.875 km and a minimum mass constraint of 1.97 solar masses, then categorized the resulting mass-radius curves into four topologies (Absent, Both, Connected, Disconnected) following the scheme of Alford, Han, and Prakash (2013).

## Key numbers

- Between 12.01% and 17.62% of accepted EOSs yield twin stars, depending on the required length of the second stable branch (long vs. short criterion).
- For the short second-branch criterion, the mean transition density for Both-category EOSs is ρt/ρ0 = 3.80 ± 1.29, and for Disconnected-category EOSs is ρt/ρ0 = 1.66 ± 0.456.
- For Both-category EOSs under the short criterion, the mean mass range over which twin stars exist is ΔM = 0.192 ± 0.204 solar masses, and the mean radius difference is ΔR = 1.23 ± 2.75 km.
- For Disconnected-category EOSs under the short criterion, the mean mass range is ΔM = 0.0141 ± 0.0586 solar masses, and the mean radius difference is ΔR = 1.14 ± 0.965 km.
- The mean maximum mass (MTOV) for the Everything category under the short criterion is 2.22 ± 0.202 solar masses.

## Context

This work extends previous twin star studies that typically selected only a few representative EOSs or fixed the hadronic matter EOS while varying the quark matter EOS, such as those by Christian (2018, 2022, 2024) and Alford, Han, and Prakash (2013). By using a 9-dimensional meta-model capable of mimicking most existing EOSs and conducting a physics-informed Bayesian analysis (in contrast to agnostic Bayesian analyses like Brandes (2024)), the authors systematically quantify the relative frequency of twin star solutions and show that the inferred EOS depends sensitively on both the presence and category of twin stars. This enables future observational programs to use twin star detection as a strong constraint on the underlying EOS of supradense matter.
