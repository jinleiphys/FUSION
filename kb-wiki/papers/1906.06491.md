# Heavy hadron molecules in effective field theory: the emergence of exotic nuclear landscapes

> Heavy hadron molecules were first theorized from a crude analogy with the deuteron and the nuclear forces binding it, a conjecture which was proven to be on the right track after the discovery of the $X(3872)$. However, this analogy with nuclear physics has not been seriously exploited beyond a few calculations in the two- and three-body sectors, leaving a great number of possible theoretical consequences unexplored. Here we show that nuclear and heavy hadron effective field theories are formally identical: using a suitable notation, there is no formal difference between these two effective field theories. For this, instead of using the standard heavy superfield notation, we have written the heavy hadron interactions directly in terms of the light quark degrees of freedom. We give a few examples of how to exploit this analogy, e.g. the calculation of the two-pion exchange diagrams. Yet the most relevant application of the present idea is the conjecture of exotic nuclear landscapes, i.e. the possibility of few heavy hadron bound states with characteristics similar to those of the standard nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that effective field theories for heavy hadron molecules and nuclear physics are formally identical when written in a light-quark notation, but this equivalence is limited to the formal level because the coupling constants and symmetry requirements differ between the two systems.

## Method

The author rewrites heavy hadron interactions in terms of light quark degrees of freedom (the "brown muck") instead of the standard heavy superfield notation, showing that the resulting Lagrangians for contact-range interactions, one-pion exchange, and two-pion exchange have the same algebraic structure as those for nucleon-nucleon interactions. The translation rules between light-quark spin operators and heavy hadron spin operators are provided for heavy mesons (P, P*) and doubly heavy baryons (Xi_QQ, Xi_QQ*), allowing direct mapping of nuclear EFT results to heavy hadron systems.

## Key numbers

- The pion decay constant used is f_pi = 132 MeV
- The axial coupling for nucleons is g_A = 1.26, while for heavy mesons it is g_1 = 0.60 (deduced from D* to D pi decay width)
- The mass splitting between P* and P heavy mesons is Delta_Q, scaling as Lambda_QCD/m_Q, with the relation m(P*) - m(P) = (4/3)(m(Xi_QQ*) - m(Xi_QQ)) = Delta_Q
- The QCD scale Lambda_QCD is approximately 200-300 MeV
- For charmed mesons, the D*D* and DD pi pi intermediate states are roughly at the same energy level: m(D*D*) is approximately equal to m(DD pi pi)
- The energy gap for doubly charmed baryons is about 3/4 of that for charmed mesons
- In the bottom sector, energy gaps are about 1/3 of those in the charmed sector

## Context

This work extends the analogy between heavy hadron molecules and nuclear physics that began with the deuteron analogy by Voloshin (1976) and was validated by the discovery of the X(3872) by Belle (2003). The paper builds on previous EFT approaches to hadronic molecules including pionless EFT (Braaten 2003, Mehen 2011, Guo 2013), pionful EFT (Fleming 2007, Baru 2011, Valderrama 2012), and the one-boson exchange model (Liu 2008, Chen 2015). The formal equivalence enables direct translation of nuclear EFT results to heavy hadron systems, and the paper conjectures the possibility of "exotic nuclear landscapes" composed of doubly heavy baryons, though phenomenological arguments suggest that doubly charmed baryon systems are unlikely to form bound states beyond A=2,3,4 due to Coulomb repulsion.
