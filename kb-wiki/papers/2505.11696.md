# Unbound neutron $\nu0d_{3/2}$ strength in $^{17}$C and the N=16 shell gap

> Significant continuum strength has been observed to be populated in $^{17}$C produced in the d($^{16}$C,p) reaction at a beam energy of 17.2~MeV/nucleon. The strength appears at greater than $\sim$2~MeV above the single-neutron decay threshold and has been identified as arising from transfer into the neutron $0d_{3/2}$ orbital. Guided by shell model predictions the greater majority of the strength is associated with a 3/2$^+$ state at an excitation energy of 4.40$_{-0.14}^{+0.33}$ MeV and a much weaker 3/2$^+$ level at 5.60$_{-0.45}^{+1.35}$ MeV. The corresponding total widths were determined to be 3.45$_{-0.78}^{+1.82}$ and 1.6$_{-1.4}^{+4.6}$ MeV, respectively. From the backward angle proton differential cross sections and the branching ratios for neutron decay to the $^{16}$C(2$_{1}^{+}$) level, the corresponding spectroscopic factors to the ground state were deduced to be 0.47$\pm{10}$ and $<$0.09. Shell-model calculations employing the phenomenological SFO-tls interaction as well as Gamow Shell-Model calculations including continuum effects are in reasonable agreement with experiment, although the predicted strength lies at somewhat lower energy. The size of the N=16 shell gap ($\varepsilon_{ \nu0d_{3/2}}-\varepsilon _{ν1s_{1/2}}$) was estimated to be 5.08$_{-0.33}^{+0.43}$~MeV - some 1.3~MeV larger than found in the SFO-tls shell model calculation.

## Key claim
The paper reports that the unbound neutron 0d₃/₂ strength in ¹⁷C is primarily concentrated in a broad resonance at 4.40 MeV excitation energy, and the N=16 shell gap is estimated to be 5.08 MeV, at least 1.3 MeV larger than the SFO-tls shell model prediction of 3.74 MeV. The authors note that this experimental value is a lower limit because only about half of the expected spectroscopic strength for the ν0d₃/₂ orbital was located.

## Method
The experiment used the d(¹⁶C,p) reaction in inverse kinematics at a beam energy of 17.2 MeV/nucleon, with a radioactive ¹⁶C beam produced at GANIL. Protons were detected at backward angles (θ_lab = 147°–167°) using the TIARA Si-strip array, and the ¹⁷C excitation energy spectrum was reconstructed from proton energies and angles. The unbound strength was analyzed using single-level R-matrix lineshapes convolved with experimental resolution, with contributions from non-resonant breakup channels subtracted. Spectroscopic factors were deduced from backward-angle proton differential cross sections compared to Adiabatic Distorted Wave Approximation (ADWA) calculations, and from branching ratios for neutron decay to the ¹⁶C(2₁⁺) level measured via coincident γ-rays detected by EXOGAM.

## Key numbers
- Beam energy: 17.2 MeV/nucleon for the ¹⁶C beam
- Lower-lying 3/2⁺ resonance: excitation energy E_x = 4.40⁺⁰·³³₋₀·₁₄ MeV, total width Γ_tot = 3.45⁺¹·⁸²₋₀·₇₈ MeV
- Higher-lying 3/2⁺ resonance: excitation energy E_x = 5.60⁺¹·³⁵₋₀·₄₅ MeV, total width Γ_tot = 1.6⁺⁴·⁶₋₁·₄ MeV
- Spectroscopic factor for lower resonance to ground state: C²S₁(0⁺) = 0.47 ± 0.10 (from angular distribution) or 0.45⁺⁰·³²₋₀·₂₂ (from branching ratio and decay width)
- Spectroscopic factor for higher resonance to ground state: C²S₂(0⁺) < 0.09
- Branching ratio for lower resonance decay to ¹⁶C(2₁⁺): BR₁(2₁⁺) = 0.66 ± 0.15
- Branching ratio for higher resonance decay to ¹⁶C(2₁⁺): BR₂(2₁⁺) = 1 (with 1σ lower limit 0.72)
- N=16 shell gap: Δ = ε_ν0d₃/₂ − ε_ν1s₁/₂ = 5.08⁺⁰·⁴³₋₀·₃₃ MeV
- Effective single-particle energy for ν0d₃/₂: ε_ν0d₃/₂ = 3.40⁺⁰·³¹₋₀·₁₄ MeV
- Effective single-particle energy for ν1s₁/₂: ε_ν1s₁/₂ = −1.68 ± 0.30 MeV

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study of bound states in ¹⁷C (Pereira-López et al., 2021) by locating the unbound ν0d₃/₂ strength via the d(¹⁶C,p) reaction. The N=16 shell gap in ¹⁶C (5.08 MeV) is found to be comparable to that in ¹⁴C (4.78 MeV from Darden et al., 1973), contrary to the SFO-tls shell model prediction of a decrease. This result is relevant for understanding the persistence of the N=16 shell gap in more neutron-rich carbon isotopes, particularly ²²C, and calls into question shell model predictions as the neutron dripline is approached. The authors suggest that future measurements of (d,p) and (d,t) reactions on ¹⁸C would be a valuable next step.
