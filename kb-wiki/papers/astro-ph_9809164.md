# Prospects for Detecting Supernova Neutrino Flavor Oscillations

> The neutrinos from a Type II supernova provide perhaps our best opportunity to probe cosmologically interesting muon and/or tauon neutrino masses. This is because matter enhanced neutrino oscillations can lead to an anomalously hot nu_e spectrum, and thus to enhanced charged current cross sections in terrestrial detectors. Two recently proposed supernova neutrino observatories, OMNIS and LAND, will detect neutrons spalled from target nuclei by neutral and charged current neutrino interactions. As this signal is not flavor specific, it is not immediately clear whether a convincing neutrino oscillation signal can be extracted from such experiments. To address this issue we examine the responses of a series of possible light and heavy mass targets, 9Be, 23Na, 35Cl, and 208Pb. We find that strategies for detecting oscillations which use only neutron count rates are problematic at best, even if cross sections are determined by ancillary experiments. Plausible uncertainties in supernova neutrino spectra tend to obscure rate enhancements due to oscillations. However, in the case of 208Pb, a signal emerges that is largely flavor specific and extraordinarily sensitive to the nu_e temperature, the emission of two neutrons. This signal and its flavor specificity are associated with the strength and location of the first-forbidden responses for neutral and charge current reactions, aspects of the 208Pb neutrino cross section that have not been discussed previously. Hadronic spin transfer experiments might be helpful in confirming some of the nuclear structure physics underlying our conclusions.

# Prospects for Detecting Supernova Neutrino Flavor Oscillations

## Key claim
Matter-enhanced neutrino flavor oscillations in Type II supernovae can produce an anomalously hot electron neutrino spectrum, but detecting this signal through neutron spallation rates in OMNIS and LAND detectors is problematic because uncertainties in supernova neutrino spectra tend to obscure rate enhancements due to oscillations. However, the two-neutron spallation channel in 208Pb provides a largely flavor-specific signal that is extraordinarily sensitive to the electron neutrino temperature, associated with first-forbidden responses not previously discussed.

## Method
The authors calculated neutrino-nucleus cross sections for neutron spallation on four target materials (9Be, 23Na, 35Cl, and 208Pb) using allowed Gamow-Teller and Fermi transitions combined with first-forbidden contributions estimated via the Goldhaber-Teller model. Neutron emission probabilities were determined through Hauser-Feshbach calculations, and flux-averaged cross sections were evaluated using modified Fermi-Dirac spectra with varying temperatures and degeneracy parameters to represent uncertainties in supernova neutrino transport codes.

## Key numbers
- For 208Pb, the isobaric analog state in 208Bi is located at an excitation energy of 15.16 MeV, approximately 0.2 MeV above the two-neutron breakup threshold
- The Gamow-Teller resonance in 208Pb(νe,e-)208Bi has centroid at approximately 17.9 MeV with width Δ ≈ 2.4 MeV, carrying strength equivalent to 46% of the Ikeda sum rule (with ±50% uncertainty)
- The isovector M1 strength in 208Pb totals (8.8 + 6.8 + 0.6) μN², with approximately 55% of the neutral current allowed distribution lying below the neutron breakup threshold at 7.368 MeV
- Heavy-flavor neutrino average energies considered: 20, 25, and 30 MeV (ηeff = 0) and 25 MeV (ηeff = 3.0)
- Electron antineutrino average energies considered: 12.8, 16, and 19.2 MeV (ηeff = 3.0)
- Electron neutrino average energies considered: 8.8, 11, and 13.2 MeV (ηeff = 3.0)
- For light nuclei, the ratio of charged current to neutral current cross sections (per flavor) for the highest energy spectra is in the range of 3 to 5

## Context
This work extends previous studies of supernova neutrino detection by examining neutron spallation detectors OMNIS (proposed by Cline et al., 1991, 1994) and LAND (proposed by Hargrove), which unlike water Cherenkov detectors like SuperKamiokande and SNO, record neutrons without flavor specificity. The paper builds on earlier calculations of neutrino-nucleus cross sections by Qian et al. (1993) and Wilson and Mayle transport calculations, while introducing first-forbidden response contributions previously neglected. The results enable future experimental work on hadronic spin transfer to characterize the 208Pb response, and suggest that two-neutron spallation in lead provides a unique thermometer for electron neutrino temperature that could test oscillation scenarios involving cosmologically interesting tau neutrino masses (1-100 eV range from the seesaw mechanism).
