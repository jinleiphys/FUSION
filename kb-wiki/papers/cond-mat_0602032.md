# Fermi surfaces and Luttinger's theorem in paired fermion systems

> We discuss ground state properties of a mixture of two fermion species which can bind to form a molecular boson. When the densities of the fermions are unbalanced, one or more Fermi surfaces can appear: we describe the constraints placed by Luttinger's theorem on the volumes enclosed by these surfaces in such Bose-Fermi mixtures. We also discuss the nature of the quantum phase transitions involving changes in the number of Fermi surfaces.

## Key claim

Luttinger's theorem imposes exact constraints on Fermi surface volumes in Bose-Fermi mixtures of paired fermions, but the specific form of the constraint depends on whether a Bose condensate is present: without a condensate, each fermion species individually conserves its Fermi surface volume (including boson number), while with a condensate only the difference of the two Fermi surface volumes is conserved and determined by the fermion number difference.

## Method

The authors derive Luttinger's theorem for a Hamiltonian describing two fermion species that can bind into a bosonic molecule, using a 2x2 fermion Green's function matrix that includes anomalous (pairing) terms. They employ a Luttinger-Ward functional approach, exploiting gauge invariance under a transformation that rotates the two fermion species oppositely, to show that the self-energy contribution to the particle number vanishes. The resulting constraint relates the fermion number difference to the difference of the two Fermi surface volumes in all phases, with additional individual constraints derived for phases without a Bose condensate. The analysis is extended to one-dimensional systems using twist operators, and to quantum phase transitions between phases with different numbers of Fermi surfaces.

## Key numbers

- The fermion number difference \(\Delta N = N_\uparrow - N_\downarrow\) is related to Fermi surface volumes by \(\Delta N = \frac{A}{(2\pi)^d} (\Omega_\uparrow - \Omega_\downarrow)\) (Eq. lutt1), where \(A\) is system volume and \(d\) is dimensionality.
- In phases without a Bose condensate, individual constraints are \(N_\uparrow + N_B = \frac{A}{(2\pi)^d} \Omega_\uparrow\) and \(N_\downarrow + N_B = \frac{A}{(2\pi)^d} \Omega_\downarrow\) (Eq. lutt2fs), where \(N_B\) is the boson number.
- In one dimension, the spin twist operator gives momentum \(k_s = (2\pi/L)(N_\uparrow - N_\downarrow)\), and the charge twist operator gives \(k_c = (2\pi/L)(N_\uparrow + N_\downarrow + 2N_B)\) (Eqs. spin, charge).
- The Fermi wavevectors in 1D without a bosonic gapless mode are \(2k_{F\sigma} = (k_c + \sigma k_s)/2 = (\pi/L)(N_c + \sigma N_s)\) for \(\sigma = \pm 1\).
- In 1D with a bosonic gapless mode, the constraint reduces to \(2(k_{F\uparrow} - k_{F\downarrow}) = k_s = (2\pi/L)N_s\).

## Context

This work extends Luttinger's theorem to the experimentally relevant case of two fermions binding into a bosonic molecule, as studied in ultracold atom experiments on \(^6\)Li (Martin 2005, Rand 2005) and \(^{40}\)K (Regal 2004). It builds on prior work by Powell (2005) and Coleman (2005) who studied Bose-Fermi mixtures where a boson and fermion bind to form a fermionic molecule, and uses non-perturbative arguments similar to Yamanaka, Oshikawa and Affleck (1997) for one-dimensional systems. The results enable experimental detection of Fermi surface volumes from momentum distributions in trapped atomic gases, and provide exact constraints that apply even in strongly interacting regimes where fermions fluctuate into molecular states.
