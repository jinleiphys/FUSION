# Lattice methods and the nuclear few- and many-body problem

> We begin with a brief overview of lattice calculations using chiral effective field theory and some recent applications. We then describe several methods for computing scattering on the lattice. After that we focus on the main goal, explaining the theory and algorithms relevant to lattice simulations of nuclear few- and many-body systems. We discuss the exact equivalence of four different lattice formalisms, the Grassmann path integral, transfer matrix operator, Grassmann path integral with auxiliary fields, and transfer matrix operator with auxiliary fields. Along with our analysis we include several coding examples and a number of exercises for the calculations of few- and many-body systems at leading order in chiral effective field theory.

## Key claim

Lattice effective field theory provides a framework for computing nuclear few- and many-body observables where all systematic errors are introduced when defining the low-energy effective theory rather than in the computational scheme, but the method shows a tendency toward overbinding with increasing nucleon number that appears related to nuclear forces residing near a quantum phase transition between a Bose-condensed alpha-particle gas and a nuclear liquid.

## Method

The author presents four equivalent lattice formalisms for nuclear systems: the Grassmann path integral, transfer matrix operator, Grassmann path integral with auxiliary fields, and transfer matrix operator with auxiliary fields. For scattering calculations, the spherical wall method imposes a hard spherical boundary on the relative separation between particles to extract phase shifts from standing wave solutions, while the adiabatic projection method produces dressed cluster states by applying powers of the transfer matrix to initial cluster states and then computing a radial adiabatic transfer matrix from which scattering phase shifts are determined.

## Context

This chapter builds upon lattice EFT methods developed for infinite nuclear matter by Muller (1999) and Lee (2004-2005), and extends earlier work on light nuclei in pionless EFT by Borasoy (2005) and chiral EFT by Borasoy (2006). The adiabatic projection method for scattering, first applied to alpha-alpha scattering by Elhatisari (2015), scales roughly quadratically with nucleon number and opens a gateway to scattering and reactions involving heavier nuclei, including the triple-alpha reaction and alpha capture on carbon that determine the carbon-to-oxygen ratio during stellar helium burning.
