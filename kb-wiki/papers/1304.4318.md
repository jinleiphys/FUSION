# Nuclear "pasta" structures in low-density nuclear matter and neutron star crust

> In neutron star crust, non-uniform structure of nuclear matter is expected, which is called the "pasta" structure. From the recent studies of giant flares in magnetars, these structures might be related to some observables and physical quantities of the neutron star crust. To investigate the above quantities, we numerically explore the pasta structures with a fully threedimensional geometry and study the properties of low-density nuclear matter, based on the relativistic mean-field model and the Thomas-Fermi approximation. We observe typical pasta structures for fixed proton number-fraction and two of them for cold catalyzed matter. We also discuss the crystalline configuration of "pasta".

## Key claim

The authors find that, contrary to previous expectations from compressible liquid-drop models (CLDM) that a body-centered cubic (bcc) lattice is energetically favored, a face-centered cubic (fcc) lattice of droplets emerges as the ground state in some density regions for fixed proton number-fraction, and also near the transition density from the droplet phase in cold catalyzed matter, though the energy difference between bcc and fcc is very small.

## Method

The authors performed fully three-dimensional, periodic-boundary-condition calculations of nuclear pasta structures using a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model under the Thomas-Fermi approximation, without assuming any geometrical symmetry. They solved the coupled field equations for sigma, omega, and rho mesons together with the Coulomb potential on a cubic grid with grid width 0.3 fm, using a cell large enough to include several units of the structure, and searched for the ground state by iteratively adjusting local chemical potentials to achieve uniformity.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter: 0.153 fm^-3, with energy per nucleon -16.4 MeV, incompressibility 240 MeV, symmetry energy 33.4 MeV, and slope parameter L = 84 MeV.
- For symmetric matter (Y_p=0.5), typical pasta phases appear at specific baryon densities: spherical droplets (fcc lattice) at 0.01 fm^-3, cylindrical rods (honeycomb lattice) at 0.024 fm^-3, slabs at 0.05 fm^-3, cylindrical tubes (honeycomb lattice) at 0.08 fm^-3, and spherical bubbles (fcc lattice) at 0.09 fm^-3.
- The energy per baryon in non-uniform structures is lower than uniform matter by up to about 15 MeV/A, and the baryon partial pressure is higher by up to about 0.5 MeV/A.
- The Coulomb energy difference between bcc and fcc droplet lattices amounts to about 0.2-0.8 MeV, with the ratio of Coulomb energy to total energy difference about 20%.
- Droplet radii for fcc and bcc lattices at fixed Y_p=0.5 range from 6.86 fm (fcc) and 6.99 fm (bcc) at 0.012 fm^-3 to 7.79 fm (fcc) and 7.92 fm (bcc) at 0.020 fm^-3.
- For cold catalyzed matter, the ground state shows bcc droplets at 0.01 fm^-3, fcc droplets at 0.03 fm^-3, and honeycomb rods at 0.056 fm^-3.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous three-dimensional RMF Thomas-Fermi study (Okamoto, 2013) to include cold catalyzed matter at beta-equilibrium, and goes beyond earlier Wigner-Seitz approximation calculations (Ravenhall, 1983; Hashimoto, 1984) and CLDM studies (Oyamatsu, 1993) that assumed specific geometries. The finding of fcc droplet lattices contradicts the CLDM expectation of bcc being favored (Oyamatsu, 1993) and also differs from QMD calculations (Watanabe, 2003) that found bcc lattices, possibly due to different treatment of electron distributions. The results enable more detailed studies of shear moduli and breaking strains of neutron star crust, which are relevant to interpreting quasi-periodic oscillations in magnetar giant flares (Sotani, 2007) and gravitational wave emission from neutron star mountains.
