# Dynamics of dinuclear system formation and its decay in heavy ion collisions

> A variety of phenomena connected with the formation of a dinuclear complex is observed in the heavy ion collisions at low energies. The dinuclear system model allows us to analyze the experimental data and to interpret them by comparison of the partial capture, fusion and evaporation residue cross sections measured for the different reactions leading to the same compound nucleus. The comparison of theoretical and experimental values of the mass and angular distributions of the reaction products gives us a detailed information about reaction mechanism forming the observed yields. The observed very small cross sections of the evaporation residues may be explained by the strong fusion hindrance and/or instability of the heated and rotating compound nucleus and smallness of its survival probability. The fusion hindrance arises due to competition between complete fusion and quasifission while the smallness of survival probability is connected with the decrease of the fission barrier at large excitation energy and angular momentum of compound nucleus.

## Key claim

The dinuclear system (DNS) model can explain the very small evaporation residue cross sections observed in superheavy element synthesis by two distinct mechanisms: strong fusion hindrance due to competition between complete fusion and quasifission, and/or the small survival probability of the heated and rotating compound nucleus due to decreased fission barrier at high excitation energy and angular momentum.

## Method

The authors solve dynamical equations of motion for the relative radial distance, angular momentum, and surface vibrations of the colliding nuclei, using a nucleus-nucleus potential calculated by the double folding procedure. They compute the quasifission decay time of the dinuclear system using the one-dimensional Kramers rate formula, with the quasifission barrier and harmonic oscillator frequencies obtained from the shape of the nucleus-nucleus interaction potential. The partial fusion cross section is calculated as the product of the capture cross section and the fusion probability, which accounts for competition between complete fusion and quasifission.

## Key numbers

- For the $^{96}$Zr+$^{124}$Sn reaction, the intrinsic fusion barrier $B^*_{\rm fus}(Z_P)$ = 10.2 MeV, and the quasifission barrier $B_{\rm qf}$ for charge asymmetry $Z_P=40$ is 5.68 MeV.
- Calculated values of $\hbar\omega_{\rm m}$ = 46.52 MeV and $\hbar\omega_{\rm qf}$ = 22.37 MeV for the harmonic oscillator approximations to the nucleus-nucleus potential.
- The friction coefficient $\gamma$ used in calculations is $8 \times 10^{-22}$ MeV fm$^{-2}$s.
- For the "cold fusion" reaction $^{64}$Ni+$^{208}$Pb, the fusion probability $P_{\rm CN} = 1.4 \times 10^{-7}$; for $^{70}$Zn+$^{208}$Pb, $P_{\rm CN} = 2.5 \times 10^{-9}$; for $^{76}$Ge+$^{208}$Pb, $P_{\rm CN} = 1.2 \times 10^{-10}$.
- For the "hot fusion" reaction $^{48}$Ca+$^{244}$Pu, $P_{\rm CN} = 4.96 \times 10^{-2}$; for $^{48}$Ca+$^{248}$Cm, $P_{\rm CN} = 1.13 \times 10^{-2}$; for $^{48}$Ca+$^{249}$Cf, $P_{\rm CN} = 7.14 \times 10^{-3}$.
- Evaporation residue cross sections for superheavy element synthesis are near or less than 1 pb, decreasing from 3-4 pb for Z=114 to 0.5 pb for Z=118.
- In "cold fusion" reactions the compound nucleus excitation energy $E^*_{\rm CN}$ is less than 20 MeV; in "hot fusion" reactions $E^*_{\rm CN}$ is more than 25 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the dinuclear system model developed by Antonenko and collaborators to analyze fusion hindrance and quasifission in heavy ion collisions, building on earlier studies of deep inelastic collisions by Wilpert and of nucleon exchange mechanisms by Isotope and Sharing. The authors compare their theoretical results with experimental data from Oganessian (2006) for hot fusion synthesis of elements Z=114-118, Hofmann (2001) and Morita (2004) for cold fusion synthesis of elements Z=111-113, and Keller (1987) for the $^{32}$S+$^{182}$W reaction. The model enables quantitative prediction of evaporation residue cross sections for superheavy element synthesis and provides a framework for separating quasifission from fusion-fission products in experimental mass-angle distributions.
