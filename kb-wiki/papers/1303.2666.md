# Constraining the astrophysical origin of the p-nuclei through nuclear physics and meteoritic data

> A small number of naturally occurring, proton-rich nuclides (the p-nuclei) cannot be made in the s- and r-process. Their origin is not well understood. Massive stars can produce p-nuclei through photodisintegration of pre-existing intermediate and heavy nuclei. This so-called gamma-process requires high stellar plasma temperatures and occurs mainly in explosive O/Ne burning during a core-collapse supernova. Although the gamma-process in massive stars has been successful in producing a large range of p-nuclei, significant deficiences remain. An increasing number of processes and sites has been studied in recent years in search of viable alternatives replacing or supplementing the massive star models. A large number of unstable nuclei, however, with only theoretically predicted reaction rates are included in the reaction network and thus the nuclear input may also bear considerable uncertainties. The current status of astrophysical models, nuclear input, and observational constraints is reviewed. After an overview of currently discussed models, the focus is on the possibility to better constrain those models through different means. Meteoritic data not only provide the actual isotopic abundances of the p-nuclei but can also put constraints on the possible contribution of proton-rich nucleosynthesis. The main part of the review focusses on the nuclear uncertainties involved in the determination of the astrophysical reaction rates required for the extended reaction networks used in nucleosynthesis studies. Experimental approaches are discussed together with their necessary connection to theory, which is especially pronounced for reactions with intermediate and heavy nuclei in explosive nuclear burning, even close to stability.

## Key claim
The paper reviews the current understanding of p-nuclei production, concluding that no single astrophysical process can reproduce the solar abundances of all 30 remaining p-isotopes, and that the longstanding deficiencies in the gamma-process models for light p-nuclei (A < 124) and those in the mass range 150 ≤ A ≤ 165 may require alternative sites or improved nuclear reaction rates, but the nuclear input itself bears considerable uncertainties due to many reaction rates being only theoretically predicted.

## Method
The authors review the current status of astrophysical models for p-nuclei production, including the gamma-process in core-collapse supernovae, thermonuclear supernovae (type Ia and sub-Chandrasekhar He-detonation models), the rp-process on neutron stars, the nu-p-process in neutrino-driven winds, and accretion disk outflows. They examine how meteoritic data, including solar p-abundances, extinct radionuclides, and isotopic anomalies, can constrain these models. The main focus is on nuclear uncertainties in astrophysical reaction rates for intermediate and heavy nuclei, discussing experimental approaches (photodisintegration, charged-particle induced reactions, elastic scattering, neutron-induced reactions) and their necessary connection to theoretical nuclear models.

## Key numbers
- 35 p-nuclides were historically identified, but after accounting for s-process contributions to 164Er, 152Gd, 180Ta, 113In, and 115Sn, only 30 p-isotopes remain to be explained by other processes.
- The gamma-process in massive stars produces p-nuclei in mass ranges 124 ≤ A ≤ 150 and 168 ≤ A ≤ 200 within about a factor of 2 relative to 16O, while p-isotopes below A < 124 and between 150 ≤ A ≤ 165 are severely underproduced.
- The gamma-process requires temperatures of 2 ≤ T ≤ 3.5 GK, with light p-nuclei requiring 2.5-3.5 GK and heavier ones requiring T < 2.5 GK to avoid complete destruction.
- The p-isotope 92Mo has an isotopic contribution of 14.53% with solar abundance 3.86×10^-1 (relative to Si=10^6), while 94Mo has 9.15% with abundance 2.41×10^-1.
- The p-isotope 180Ta^m has the lowest isotopic contribution at 0.01201% with solar abundance 2.58×10^-6.
- Modern composition uncertainties for p-isotopes are below 1% for recent measurements (e.g., Os), while general uncertainties are below 5%.

## Context
This review extends the work of B2FH (1957) and subsequent studies on the p-process, building on gamma-process models in massive stars by Woosley and Howard (1978), Arnould (1976), Rayet et al. (1990, 1995), and Rauscher et al. (2002). It addresses the longstanding underproduction problems for light p-nuclei (92,94Mo and 96,98Ru) identified by Woosley and Howard (1978) and Howard and Meyer (1993), and discusses alternative sites including the pn-process in sub-Chandrasekhar white dwarf explosions (Goriely et al., 2002), the nu-p-process in core-collapse supernova neutrino-driven winds (Frohlich et al., 2006), and the rp-process on neutron stars (Schatz et al., 1998). The review enables future work by identifying key nuclear uncertainties that must be resolved through experiments, particularly for reactions involving unstable nuclei, and by showing how meteoritic data on extinct radionuclides like 92Nb can rule out certain production scenarios.
