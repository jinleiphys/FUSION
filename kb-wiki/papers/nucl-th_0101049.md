# SU(3) density matrix theory

> The irreducible representations of the Lie algebra ${\frak su}$(3) describe rotational bands in the context of the nuclear shell and interacting boson models. The density matrices associated with ${\frak su}$(3) provide an alternative theoretical framework for obtaining these bands. The ${\frak su}$(3) density matrix formulation is mathematically simpler than representation theory, yet it yields similar results. Bands are solutions to a system of polynomial equations defined by the quadratic and cubic ${\frak su}$(3) Casimirs. Analytic solutions are found in many physically important cases including rotation about principal axes and spheroids. Numerical solutions are reported in other cases including tilted rotors. The physics of rotational bands is more transparent in the presented formalism. In representation theory bands terminate because the space is finite-dimensional. In ${\frak su}$(3) density matrix theory bands terminate when faster rotation produces a spheroid rotating around its symmetry axis.

# SU(3) density matrix theory

## Key claim

The paper claims that rotational bands in nuclear physics, which are traditionally described by irreducible representations of the SU(3) Lie algebra in the shell model and interacting boson model, can be obtained from a density matrix formulation that is mathematically simpler and physically more transparent. The hedge is that the density matrix theory yields "similar results" to representation theory, not identical ones, and that quantum fluctuations are not included in the density matrix approach, leading to discrepancies such as the angular momentum being I^2 rather than I(I+1).

## Method

The authors construct a mean-field theory for the SU(3) dynamical symmetry by mapping quantum states to density matrices in the dual space of the su(3) Lie algebra, where admissible densities lie on level surfaces defined by constant values of the quadratic and cubic Casimir invariants. Rotational bands are obtained as solutions to a system of polynomial equations that constrain the quadrupole deformation parameters (beta, gamma) and the body-fixed angular momentum components (I_1, I_2, I_3) for a given total angular momentum I. Analytic solutions are derived for principal axis rotations and spheroidal shapes, while numerical solutions are reported for tilted rotors.

## Key numbers

- The quadratic Casimir for a highest weight state with labels (lambda, mu) is C_2 = (2/3)(lambda^2 + lambda*mu + mu^2)
- The cubic Casimir for a highest weight state with labels (lambda, mu) is C_3 = (1/9)(2*lambda^3 + 3*lambda^2*mu - 3*lambda*mu^2 - 2*mu^3)
- The maximum allowed angular momentum is I_max = lambda + mu, which matches the upper bound from representation theory
- For nonrotating states (I=0), the maximum deformation is beta_max = 2*sqrt(lambda^2 + lambda*mu + mu^2)/3
- The minimum deformation at maximum angular momentum is beta_min = |lambda - mu|/3
- For rotation about the short principal axis, the angular momentum range is 0 <= I <= lambda
- For rotation about the long principal axis, the angular momentum range is 0 <= I <= mu
- For rotation about the middle principal axis, the angular momentum range is 0 <= I <= lambda + mu
- For a prolate spheroid rotating perpendicular to its symmetry axis (K=0), the solution gives I^2 = 4*lambda*mu and q = -(lambda - mu)/3
- For noncollective rotation about the symmetry axis (K=I), the solutions give I = lambda (oblate), I = mu (prolate), and I = lambda + mu

## Context

This work extends the mean-field density matrix approach previously developed for the unitary group U(n) by Rosensteel and collaborators (Rosensteel and Rowe, 1981; Rosensteel, 1981) to the specific case of SU(3), which is the simplest nontrivial dynamical symmetry in nuclear structure physics. The paper builds on the Elliott SU(3) model of rotational bands in the nuclear shell model (Elliott, 1958) and the interacting boson model of Arima and Iachello (1978). The density matrix formulation provides a bridge between the full shell model and Hartree-Fock mean field theory, enabling a clearer physical interpretation of band termination: in representation theory bands terminate because the space is finite-dimensional, while in density matrix theory bands terminate when faster rotation produces a spheroid rotating around its symmetry axis. The approach enables future work on energy functionals to determine the dynamics of rotational bands beyond the kinematical constraints derived here.
