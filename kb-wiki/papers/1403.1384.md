# Mapping from quasi-elastic scattering to fusion reactions

> The fusion barrier distribution has provided a nice representation for the channel coupling effects on heavy-ion fusion reactions at energies around the Coulomb barrier. Here we discuss how one can extract the same representation using the so called sum-of-differences (SOD) method with quasi-elastic scattering cross sections. In contrast to the conventional quasi-elastic barrier distribution, the SOD barrier distribution has an advantage in that it can be applied both to non-symmetric and symmetric systems. It is also the case that the correspondence to the fusion barrier distribution is much better than the quasi-elastic barrier distribution. We demonstrate its usefulness by studying $^{16}$O+$^{144}$Sm, $^{58}$Ni+$^{58}$Ni, and $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C systems.

## Key claim

The sum-of-differences (SOD) method, which extracts fusion cross sections from quasi-elastic scattering cross sections via an angular integral, produces a barrier distribution that corresponds much better to the true fusion barrier distribution than the conventional quasi-elastic barrier distribution, and it can be applied to symmetric systems where the conventional method fails due to divergence at 180 degrees.

## Method

The authors define the SOD barrier distribution by first computing fusion cross sections from quasi-elastic cross sections using the angular integral σ_fus ≈ 2π ∫(dσ_Ruth/dΩ − dσ_qel/dΩ) sinθ dθ for non-symmetric systems, or the analogous expression with Mott cross sections for symmetric systems. They then take the second derivative of Eσ_fus with respect to energy to obtain the barrier distribution. The method is tested by performing coupled-channels calculations for ¹⁶O+¹⁴⁴Sm, ⁵⁸Ni+⁵⁸Ni, and ¹²C+¹²C systems, comparing the SOD-derived barrier distributions to exact fusion barrier distributions and to conventional quasi-elastic barrier distributions.

## Key numbers

- For the ¹⁶O+¹⁴⁴Sm system, the SOD method with θ_min = 20° yields fusion cross sections "almost indistinguishable" from exact coupled-channels results; the SOD barrier distribution is normalized between E_c.m. = 55 and 70 MeV.
- For the ⁵⁸Ni+⁵⁸Ni symmetric system, averaging the SOD cross sections over θ_max between 176.5° and 179.5° reproduces the exact fusion cross sections "almost perfectly."
- For the ¹²C+¹²C system with single-channel calculations (diffuseness a = 0.8 fm, depth V₀ = −8028.5 MeV), the SOD method reproduces exact fusion cross sections including oscillatory structure.
- A potential model fit to ¹²C+¹²C experimental data reproduces cross sections up to E_c.m. ≈ 25 MeV; above that energy, the calculation overestimates data unless the penetrability for l = 14 is reduced by a factor of 2 and all higher partial waves are set to zero.

## Context

This work extends the sum-of-differences method originally proposed by Holdeman and Thaler (1965) for reaction cross sections, applying it to obtain fusion barrier distributions from quasi-elastic scattering. It addresses two drawbacks of the conventional quasi-elastic barrier distribution defined by Timmers et al. (1994): the smearing due to nuclear distortion effects (which the authors show for the ¹⁶O+¹⁴⁴Sm system) and the inapplicability to symmetric systems. The method enables experimental determination of fusion cross sections for light symmetric systems like ¹²C+¹²C and ¹⁶O+¹⁶O, where direct evaporation residue measurements show large discrepancies among data sets (Kovar 1979, Sperr 1976).
