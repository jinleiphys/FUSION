# Spin-up of Solitary Pulsars: Signal of Deconfinement

## Key claim
A phase transition in the core of a neutron star, such as quark deconfinement or Bose condensation, can cause spontaneous spin-up of a solitary millisecond pulsar, but only if the critical density for the phase transition falls in the density range spanned by neutron stars and the transition occurs near or at the limiting mass star. The spin-up epoch lasts for 2×10^7 years, which is 1/50 of the spin-down time for the model pulsar.

## Method
The author solved the general relativistic deceleration equation for a rotating neutron star using an equation of state that describes a first-order deconfinement phase transition from charge-neutral nuclear matter to quark matter. Nuclear matter was described in a relativistically covariant theory (Garpman, Glendenning and Karant 1979; Glendenning 1985; Glendenning and Moszkowski 1991) and quark matter in the MIT bag model (Chodos et al. 1974). The moment of inertia was computed using the expression from Glendenning and Weber (1992a, 1992b), which incorporates changes in composition, centrifugal stretching, and frame dragging, rather than the Hartle expression which neglects these effects.

## Key numbers
- Spin-up epoch duration: 2×10^7 years
- Spin-up epoch relative to spin-down time: 1/50 of the spin-down time
- Anomalous braking index values endure for 10^8 years, corresponding to the slow spin-down and gradual envelopment of a growing central region by the new phase
- The phase transition is expected to occur in very hot matter at T∼200 MeV or in cold but dense matter

## Context
This work extends the prediction of "backbending" in rotating nuclei, first predicted by Mottelson and Valatin (1960) and discovered by Johnson et al. (1972) and Stephens and Simon (1972), to neutron star physics. The phenomenon is analogous to an ice skater pulling in their arms to increase spin rate. The calculation builds on the author's prior work with Pei and Weber (1997) on spin-up signals from phase transitions. The paper notes that if no pulsar is observed to produce this signal, little is learned about whether the deconfined phase exists, similar to the situation in nuclear collisions.
