# Coordinate versus momentum cuts and effects of collective flow on critical fluctuations

> We analyze particle number fluctuations in the crossover region near the critical endpoint of a first-order phase transition by utilizing molecular dynamics simulations of the classical Lennard-Jones fluid. We extend our previous study [V.A. Kuznietsov et al., Phys. Rev. C 105, 044903 (2022)] by incorporating longitudinal collective flow. The scaled variance of particle number distribution inside different coordinate and momentum space acceptances is computed through ensemble averaging and found to agree with earlier results obtained using time averaging, validating the ergodic hypothesis for fluctuation observables. Presence of a sizable collective flow is found to be essential for observing large fluctuations from the critical point in momentum space acceptances. We discuss our findings in the context of heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that large critical fluctuations observed in coordinate space can survive in momentum space acceptances typical of heavy-ion collisions, but only when a sizable longitudinal collective flow is present; without flow, the critical signal is completely washed out in momentum cuts.

## Method

The authors performed molecular dynamics simulations of a classical Lennard-Jones fluid near its critical point (3D-Ising universality class) at fixed temperature \(\tilde T = 1.4 \simeq 1.06 \tilde T_c\) and three densities: dilute (\(\tilde n = 0.1 \simeq 0.32 \tilde n_c\)), near-critical (\(\tilde n = 0.3 \simeq 0.95 \tilde n_c\)), and dense (\(\tilde n = 0.6 \simeq 1.90 \tilde n_c\)). For each density, approximately 32000 independent simulations with random initial conditions were run to compute ensemble-averaged scaled variances of particle number distributions inside coordinate and momentum space acceptances. Longitudinal collective flow was incorporated by mapping the Lennard-Jones longitudinal coordinate \(\tilde z^{\rm LJ}\) to space-time rapidity \(\eta_s\) via a linear transformation based on the beam rapidity \(y_{\rm cm}^{\rm beam}(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}})\), and adding a thermal rapidity component derived from the simulated thermal velocities.

## Key numbers

* The critical point of the Lennard-Jones fluid is located at \(\tilde T_c = 1.321 \pm 0.007\) and \(\tilde n_c = 0.316 \pm 0.005\).
* At near-critical density (\(n = 0.95 n_c\)) with \(\alpha = 0.5\) acceptance fraction, the equilibrium corrected scaled variance in coordinate space is \(\tilde w_{\alpha}^{\rm eq} = 1.882 \pm 0.001\).
* The equilibration time at near-critical density is \(\tilde \tau_\alpha = 4.055 \pm 0.231\) in dimensionless units, compared to \(\tilde \tau_\alpha = 0.710 \pm 0.160\) at dense conditions (\(n = 1.9 n_c\)).
* The conversion factor from dimensionless Lennard-Jones time to physical time is estimated as \(\sqrt{(m \sigma^2) / \varepsilon} \sim 1.8-2.4\) fm/\(c\), using \(\varepsilon \sim 107\) MeV and \(\sigma \sim 0.6-0.8\) fm.
* For a fixed acceptance fraction \(\alpha = 0.5\) at near-critical density, the corrected scaled variance in rapidity acceptance increases monotonically with beam rapidity and saturates at the coordinate space value.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous molecular dynamics study of the Lennard-Jones fluid (Kuznietsov et al., Phys. Rev. C 105, 044903, 2022) by incorporating longitudinal collective flow and replacing time averaging with ensemble averaging. The earlier study found that critical fluctuations were completely lost when coordinate cuts were replaced by momentum cuts in a static box. The present results validate the ergodic hypothesis for critical fluctuations and show that collective flow can restore the critical signal in momentum space acceptances, which is essential for interpreting proton number fluctuation measurements from the STAR Collaboration (STAR, 2020; STAR, 2021) and future experiments at RHIC BES-II, FAIR CBM, and HADES. The findings enable more realistic comparisons between theoretical critical point predictions and heavy-ion collision data by demonstrating that rapidity acceptances can capture coordinate-space critical fluctuations when flow is present.
