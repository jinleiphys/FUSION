# Heavy-quark spin and flavour symmetry partners of the X(3872) revisited: what can we learn from the one boson exchange model?

> Heavy-quark symmetry as applied to heavy hadron systems implies that their interactions are independent of their heavy-quark spin (heavy-quark spin symmetry) and heavy flavour contents (heavy flavour symmetry). In the molecular hypothesis the $X(3872)$ resonance is a $1^{++}$ $D^*\bar{D}$ bound state. If this is the case, the application of heavy-quark symmetry to a molecular $X(3872)$ suggests the existence of a series of partner states, the most obvious of which is a possible $2^{++}$ $D^*\bar{D}^*$ bound state for which the two-body potential is identical to that of the $1^{++}$ $D^*\bar{D}$ system, the reason being that these two heavy hadron-antihadron states have identical light-spin content. As already discussed in the literature this leads to the prediction of a partner state at $4012\,{\rm MeV}$, at least in the absence of other dynamical effects which might affect the location of this molecule. However the prediction of further heavy-quark symmetry partners cannot be done solely on the basis of symmetry and requires additional information. We propose to use the one boson exchange model to fill this gap, in which case we will be able to predict or discard the existence of other partner states. Besides the isoscalar $2^{++}$ $D^*\bar{D}^*$ bound state, we correctly reproduce the location and quantum numbers of the isovector hidden-bottom $Z_b(10610)$ and $Z_b(10650)$ molecular candidates. We also predict the hidden-bottom $1^{++}$ $B^*\bar{B}^*$ and $2^{++}$ $B^*\bar{B}^*$ partners of the $X(3872)$, in agreement with previous theoretical speculations, plus a series of other states. The isoscalar, doubly charmed $1^+$ $D D^*$ and $D^* D^*$ molecules and their doubly bottomed counterparts are likely to bind, providing a few instances of explicitly exotic systems.

## Key claim

The one boson exchange (OBE) model, with its cutoff fixed by reproducing the X(3872) as a molecular state, can predict the full spectrum of heavy meson-antimeson molecules beyond what heavy-quark symmetry alone provides, but the predictions depend on the specific choice of form factor and cutoff regularization.

## Method

The authors constructed an OBE potential for heavy meson-antimeson systems using exchange of π, σ, ρ, and ω mesons, with coupling constants determined from chiral perturbation theory, quark model relations, and vector meson dominance. They fixed the monopolar form factor cutoff Λ = 1.01 GeV by requiring the OBE potential to reproduce the X(3872) as an isoscalar 1⁺⁺ D*D̅ bound state with approximately 4 MeV binding energy. Using this cutoff, they solved the Schrödinger equation for all S-wave heavy meson-antimeson configurations in both the charmed and bottom sectors.

## Key numbers

- The X(3872) is reproduced as a bound state with binding energy of about 4 MeV relative to the isospin-averaged D D* threshold, using cutoff Λ = 1.01 GeV
- The predicted 2⁺⁺ D*D̅* partner state (X(4012)) lies at 4012 MeV, as previously discussed in the literature
- The isovector hidden-bottom Zb(10610) and Zb(10650) molecular candidates are correctly reproduced in location and quantum numbers
- For the deuteron (neutron-proton bound state with B₂ = 2.2 MeV), the OBE model yields Λ_d = 0.86 GeV for comparison
- The pion axial coupling used is g = 0.60, compatible with the experimental determination g₁ = 0.59 ± 0.01 ± 0.07 from D* → Dπ decay
- The σ meson coupling is g_σ = 3.4, derived from the quark model relation g_σ = (1/3) g_σNN
- The ρ and ω electric-type couplings are g_ρ = g_ω = 2.6, with magnetic-type couplings κ_ρ = κ_ω = 4.5

## Context

This work extends the heavy-quark symmetry analysis of AlFiky (2005), Valderrama (2012), and Nieves (2012), who showed that if the X(3872) is a 1⁺⁺ D*D̅ molecule, heavy-quark spin symmetry forces a 2⁺⁺ D*D̅* partner state with identical binding energy. The OBE model provides the dynamical input needed to predict additional partner states beyond those constrained by symmetry alone, including hidden-bottom partners of the X(3872) previously speculated by Törnqvist (1993). The approach enables systematic exploration of the full molecular spectrum in both charmed and bottom sectors, including doubly heavy exotic systems.
