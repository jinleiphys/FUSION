# Pushing forward jet substructure measurements in heavy-ion collisions

> Energetic jets that traverse the quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collisions serve as excellent probes to study this new state of deconfined QCD matter. Presently, however, our ability to achieve a crisp theoretical interpretation of the crescent number of jet observables measured in experiments is hampered by the presence of selection biases. The aim of this work is to minimise those selection biases associated to the modification of the quark- vs. gluon-initiated jet fraction in order to assess the presence of other medium-induced effects, namely color decoherence, by exploring the rapidity dependence of jet substructure observables. So far, all jet substructure measurements at mid-rapidity have shown that heavy-ion jets are narrower than vacuum jets. We show both analytically and with Monte Carlo simulations that if the narrowing effect persists at forward rapidities, where the quark-initiated jet fraction is greatly increased, this could serve as an unambiguous experimental observation of color decoherence dynamics in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that measuring jet substructure observables (specifically the leading-$k_t$ distribution) as a function of jet rapidity can experimentally distinguish between two competing explanations for the observed narrowing of jets in heavy-ion collisions: the "modified quark/gluon fraction" model versus the "color decoherence" model. The authors show that if the narrowing effect persists at forward rapidities (where quark-initiated jets dominate), this would serve as an unambiguous experimental observation of color decoherence dynamics, but only if the narrowing is not simply due to enhanced quark fractions.

## Method
The authors performed analytic calculations at double-logarithmic accuracy and Monte Carlo simulations using Pythia for vacuum baselines, then implemented two semi-analytic medium models: a modified quark/gluon fraction model based on single-parton quenching weights with Casimir scaling, and a color decoherence model incorporating a critical resolution angle theta_c = 2/sqrt(hat_q L^3) that separates resolved from unresolved emissions. They also used the Hybrid Strong/Weak Coupling Monte Carlo model to study the rapidity dependence of jet suppression and the leading-k_t distribution, considering both current LHC capabilities (|y| < 2.5) and future HL-LHC capabilities (|y| up to 4.5).

## Key numbers
- The critical resolution angle for color decoherence is defined as theta_c = 2/sqrt(hat_q L^3), where hat_q is the quenching parameter and L the medium length
- For the color decoherence model, the medium is modeled as a brick of length L = 4 fm with transport coefficient hat_q = 0.3 GeV^3 and coupling alpha_s^med = 0.24, yielding R_AA ~ 0.47 at p_t = 120 GeV for R = 0.4
- For the modified q/g fraction model, matching the same R_AA = 0.47 at p_t = 120 GeV requires L = 4 fm, hat_q = 0.7 GeV^3, and alpha_s^med = 0.28
- The quark-initiated jet fraction is studied for jets with p_t from 20-300 GeV across rapidity bins up to |y| = 4.5
- For the HL-LHC, an integrated luminosity of L = 10 nb^-1 in Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s) = 5.5 TeV per nucleon pair is assumed, with N_coll ~ 2000 for central collisions
- Estimated number of jets at HL-LHC ranges from 1.5 x 10^3 (for high p_t 225-300 GeV with q-fraction ~0.9) to 3.2 x 10^8 (for low p_t 20-80 GeV with q-fraction ~0.3)

## Context
This work extends the theoretical debate between the "modified q/g fraction" model introduced by Spousta (2015) and Ringer (2019), which attributes jet narrowing to enhanced quark fractions in Pb+Pb, and the color decoherence model developed by Mehtar-Tani (2010-2012) and implemented in Monte Carlos like JetMed (Caucal 2018, 2019) and the Hybrid Strong/Weak Coupling Model (Casalderrey-Solana 2014, 2019). The paper builds on previous rapidity-dependent measurements by ATLAS (2018) of the inclusive jet spectrum up to |y| < 2.8 and fragmentation functions up to |y| < 2.1, and proposes a strategy for future measurements at the HL-LHC with detectors like ALICE3 (designed for 8 units in rapidity) and upgraded ATLAS/CMS detectors.
