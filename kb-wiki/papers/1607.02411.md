# Has the QCD critical point been observed at RHIC? - A Rebuttal

> This note rebuts an old, but recurring claim by Antoniou, Davis and Diakonos [1] that the critical point and associated critical exponents reported in Ref. [2], is based on an erroneous treatment of scaling relations near the critical point.

## Key claim

The claim by Antoniou, Davis and Diakonos that the critical point and critical exponents reported in Lacey (2014) are based on an erroneous treatment of scaling relations near the QCD critical point is rebutted; the Finite Size Scaling analysis is valid because it is performed for fixed values of temperature and baryon chemical potential at each collision energy, making the relevant critical exponent gamma, not alpha.

## Method

The analysis uses the phenomenology of thermal models to establish the chemical freeze-out region, associating combinations of freeze-out temperature and baryon chemical potential with values of collision energy per nucleon pair. Collision energy is used as a control parameter to measure the distance to the critical endpoint via the variable tau_s = (sqrt(s_NN) - sqrt(s_NN^cep))/sqrt(s_NN^cep), and a Finite Size Scaling analysis is performed for fixed T and mu_B at each collision energy, validated by recent extractions of T and mu_B as a function of collision centrality or system size.

## Key numbers

- The critical endpoint location (T^cep and mu_B^cep) and critical exponents were estimated in Lacey (2014) and subsequently validated in Lacey (2016) with a different set of observables.
- The chemical freeze-out temperature and baryon chemical potential are extracted as a function of collision centrality or system size (see Fig. 1 of the paper, based on Das (2014)).

## Context

This rebuttal addresses a recurring claim by Antoniou, Davis and Diakonos (2016) that the scaling analysis in Lacey (2014) is erroneous. The work extends the Finite Size Scaling technique used in Lacey (2014) and is validated by a closure test showing the full data set collapses onto a single scaling function. The analysis is further supported by recent validation in Lacey (2016) using different observables, and relies on thermal model extractions of freeze-out parameters from Cleymans (2006), Becattini (2012), and Andronic (2009).
