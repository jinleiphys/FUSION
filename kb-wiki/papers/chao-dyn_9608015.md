# Partial Dynamical Symmetry and Mixed Dynamics

> Partial dynamical symmetry describes a situation in which some eigenstates have a symmetry which the quantum Hamiltonian does not share. This property is shown to have a classical analogue in which some tori in phase space are associated with a symmetry which the classical Hamiltonian does not share. A local analysis in the vicinity of these special tori reveals a neighbourhood of phase space foliated by tori. This clarifies the suppression of classical chaos associated with partial dynamical symmetry. The results are used to divide the states of a mixed system into ``chaotic'' and ``regular'' classes.

## Key claim

Partial dynamical symmetry (PDS) in a quantum Hamiltonian, where only a subset of eigenstates respect a symmetry the Hamiltonian does not share, has a classical analogue in which a special torus (or periodic orbit) in phase space is associated with a symmetry the classical Hamiltonian does not share; the presence of this special torus induces a finite neighborhood of phase space foliated by KAM tori, which explains the suppression of classical chaos even though the fraction of special quantum states vanishes as \(\hbar^2\) in the classical limit.

## Method

The authors constructed a bosonic \(U(3)\) algebraic model with two independent degrees of freedom, starting from a Hamiltonian \(H_0\) with a \(U(2)\) dynamical symmetry and adding a term \(H_1\) that breaks the symmetry but is annihilated by states with \(n_b=0\), creating a partial dynamical symmetry. They then added a third term \(H_2\) to break the PDS completely, and studied both the quantum spectrum (via Brody fits to level spacing distributions) and classical phase space (via Poincaré sections and perturbation theory) as a function of the coupling parameters \(B\) and \(C\).

## Key numbers

- The fraction of special solvable states (with \(n_b=0\)) is \(2/(N+1)\), which approaches zero in the classical limit; for \(N=60\) this fraction is \(2/61 \approx 0.0328\).
- The classical limit corresponds to \(\hbar = 1/N\); calculations were performed with \(N=60\) and \(A=0.8642\).
- The total number of quantum states is \((N+1)(N+2)/2\); for \(N=60\) this is \(61 \times 62 / 2 = 1891\).
- The phase space volume is \(2\pi^2\); the classical Hamiltonian per boson is \(\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{H}_0 + \bar{B}\mathcal{H}_1 + C\mathcal{H}_2\) with \(\bar{B}=NB\).
- For \(\bar{B}=0.5\) and varying \(C\), both the classical chaotic volume fraction \(\mu\) and the Brody parameter \(\omega\) (ranging from 0 for integrable to 1 for chaotic) show a suppression of chaos near \(C=0\).
- The first-order classical perturbation calculation is valid for \(\bar{B} I_b^{3/2} \ll 1\); the classical variances scale as \(\sigma^2_{a,cl} = 2\bar{B}^2 I_b^3 I_a / (\Delta\omega)^2\) and \(\sigma^2_{b,cl} = 2\bar{B}^2 I_b^3 [I_a/(\Delta\omega)^2 + I_c/(\omega_b)^2]\).

## Context

This work extends the concept of partial dynamical symmetry (PDS), previously introduced in algebraic nuclear models by Leviatan (1996) and applied to a five-dimensional quadrupole model by Whelan (1995), to a simpler \(U(3)\) model with two degrees of freedom to better understand the classical suppression of chaos. It builds on studies of discrete symmetries in mixed-phase-space systems by Bohigas, Tomsovic, and Ullmo (1990) and on the breaking of discrete symmetries in chaotic Hamiltonians by Li, Kaplan, and Cederbaum (1994). The results enable a classification of quantum states into "special," "almost special" (accessible by perturbation theory), and "mixed" classes, and suggest that the suppression of chaos due to PDS should persist in higher-dimensional systems.
