# HBT pion interferometry with phenomenological mean field interaction

> To extract information on hadron production dynamics in the ultrarelativistic heavy ion collision, the space-time structure of the hadron source has been measured using Hanbury Brown and Twiss interferometry. We study the distortion of the source images due to the effect of a final state interaction. We describe the interaction, taking place during penetrating through a cloud formed by evaporating particles, in terms of a one-body mean field potential localized in the vicinity of the source region. By adopting the semiclassical method, the modification of the propagation of an emitted particle is examined. In analogy to the optical model applied to nuclear reactions, our phenomenological model has an imaginary part of the potential, which describes the absorption in the cloud. In this work, we focus on the pion interferometry and mean field interaction obtained using a phenomenological $ππ$ forward scattering amplitude in the elastic channels. The p-wave scattering with $ρ$ meson resonance leads to an attractive mean field interaction, and the presence of the absorptive part is mainly attributed to the formation of this resonance. We also incorporate a simple time dependence of the potential reflecting the dynamics of the evaporating source. Using the obtained potential, we examine how and to what extent the so-called HBT Gaussian radius is varied by the modification of the propagation.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether a final-state mean field interaction, modeled via a phenomenological optical potential with an attractive real part and an absorptive imaginary part derived from ππ forward scattering, can distort HBT Gaussian radii enough to alleviate the "RHIC HBT puzzle," but finds that the effect after the sudden freeze-out assumed in hydrodynamical models is not strong enough to resolve the discrepancy by itself.

## Method

The author constructs a one-body mean field potential for pions using the phenomenological elastic forward ππ scattering amplitude, focusing on the p-wave ρ meson resonance which yields an attractive real part and an absorptive imaginary part. A semiclassical stationary-phase approximation to the path integral is used to compute the modification of pion propagation through this time-dependent potential localized near the freeze-out hypersurface, and the resulting distortion of the HBT Gaussian radius parameters (Rout, Rside, Rlong) is evaluated.

## Key numbers

* The ρ meson resonance mass is approximately 770 MeV, and the attractive regime of the ππ scattering amplitude below this mass leads to an attractive mean field interaction for pions with momenta in "some hundreds of MeV."
* The pion mass used in the nonrelativistic Hamiltonian is m ≃ 140 MeV, and the formalism is extended to relativistic kinematics for pions carrying momenta in the hundreds of MeV range.
* The sideward radius Rside and its ratio to the outward radius Rout/Rside are found to be improved by the attractive mean field interaction in the low-momentum regime, but the magnitude of the effect is "not strong enough to resolve the problem by the sole effect of the mean field interaction after the 'freeze-out'."
* The magnitude of the effect from this attractive interaction is stated to be "on the same order of the individual effect obtained by some upgrades of the hydrodynamical modeling" (citing Pratt 2009).

## Context

This work extends the author's previous schematic model with a static potential (Hattori and Monnai, cited as HM) by incorporating a time-dependent potential and relativistic kinematics, and it builds on independent examinations by Cramer et al. (cited as CMWY) and Pratt (2006) of mean-field-induced distortions of HBT images. The study is motivated by the "RHIC HBT puzzle," where hydrodynamical simulations (e.g., those of Rischke and Gyulassy, 1996; Socolowski et al., cited as SBD; Hirano and Tsuda, 2002; Kolb and Heinz, 2003) predicted Rout/Rside ≃ 2, but measurements from STAR and PHENIX showed systematically smaller ratios. The results suggest that a consistent description incorporating the mean field interaction via a microscopic transport approach (e.g., with a Vlasov term) should be examined, as the effect after freeze-out alone is insufficient.
