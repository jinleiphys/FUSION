# Use of the double dispersion relation in QCD sum rules with external fields

> In QCD sum rules with external fields, the double dispersion relation is often used to represent the correlation function. In this work, we point out that the double spectral density, when it is determined by successive applications of the Borel transformation, contains the spurious terms which should be kept in the subtraction terms in the double dispersion relation. They are zero under the Borel transformation but, if the dispersion integral is restricted with QCD duality, they contribute to the continuum. For the simple case with zero external momentum, it is shown that subtracting out the spurious terms is equivalent to the QCD sum rules represented by the single dispersion relation.

# Use of the double dispersion relation in QCD sum rules with external fields

## Key claim

The double spectral density obtained by successive applications of the Borel transformation contains spurious subtraction terms that vanish under the Borel transformation when the dispersion integral runs from zero to infinity, but these terms contribute nonzero continuum when the dispersion integral is restricted by QCD duality below a continuum threshold. For the case of zero external momentum, subtracting these spurious terms yields a sum rule equivalent to that obtained from the single dispersion relation.

## Method

The author examines the standard procedure for constructing QCD sum rules with external fields, where a double-variable dispersion relation represents the correlation function. The double spectral density is determined by applying successive Borel transformations to the operator product expansion (OPE). The author then tests whether this spectral density, when inserted back into the double dispersion integral with the dispersion integral restricted by QCD duality below a continuum threshold S0, reproduces the original OPE without contamination from subtraction terms. Two simple OPE examples are analyzed: a logarithmic term and a single-pole term, both using the asymptotic form of the twist-3 pion wave function. The zero-external-momentum case is treated separately, where the double dispersion relation reduces to a single-variable form with a squared denominator.

## Key numbers

* For the OPE example Π₁ = ∫ du φ_p(u) ln[-(q-up_π)²], the double spectral density is ρ₁(s₁,s₂) = -s₁ δ(s₁-s₂); the spurious continuum from the subtraction term under double Borel transformation becomes S₀ M² exp(-S₀/M²) where 1/M² = 1/M₁² + 1/M₂².
* For the OPE example Π₂ = ∫ du φ_p(u) / [-up₂²-(1-u)p₁²], the spectral density is ρ₂(s₁,s₂) = δ(s₁-s₂) θ(s₁); the spurious contribution arises from boundary terms when the lower limit of the dispersion integral changes from 0⁻ to S₀.
* For the zero-external-momentum case with OPE ln(-p²), the spectral density is ρ(s) = -s; the spurious term in the dispersion integral takes the form p²/(s-p²) evaluated at the boundaries.
* Subtracting the spurious continuum replaces the continuum factor E_n(x) with E_{n-1}(x) for n ≥ 1, where x ≡ S₀/M² and E_n(x) = 1 - (1 + x + ... + xⁿ/n!) e^{-x}.
* In the πNN coupling sum rule for the iγ₅ p_μ γ^μ Dirac structure, the Borel curve with spurious continuum varies within 11.5-11.2, while without it the variation becomes 15-30, showing substantial effects.

## Context

This work extends the methodology of QCD sum rules with external fields as developed by Ioffe (1995) and applied to pion-nucleon couplings by Shiomi and Hatsuda (1995) and Kim, Lee, and Oka (1999). The double dispersion relation approach was previously used by Beilin and Radyushkin (1985) and Ball and Braun (1994). The paper identifies a systematic inconsistency in how the double spectral density is combined with QCD duality, which had not been previously recognized. This finding enables a correction to continuum contributions in light-cone QCD sum rules, where the spurious terms can lead to significant numerical differences, as demonstrated in the πNN coupling calculations of Kim, Lee, and Oka (1999). The work also relates to the debate between Krippa and Birse (2000) and Kim (2000) regarding whether certain terms should be kept in QCD sum rules with external fields.
