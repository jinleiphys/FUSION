# Stochastic variational method with noncentral forces

> This paper extends the application of the stochastic variational method to noncentral interactions. Several examples are presented for three- and four-nucleon systems with realistic nuclear forces. The correlated Gaussians easily cope with the strong short range repulsion of the potential and the stochastic variational method efficiently selects the most important spin, isospin and orbital angular momentum components of the wave function. The examples confirm the usefulness and accuracy of the method.

# Stochastic variational method with noncentral forces

## Key claim

The stochastic variational method (SVM) with correlated Gaussian basis functions can be extended to noncentral nuclear interactions and yields ground-state energies for three- and four-nucleon systems that agree with the best available calculations (Green's Function Monte Carlo, Faddeev, Correlated Hyperspherical Harmonics) to within a few hundred keV, provided the nonlinear variational parameters and spin-isospin-orbital channels are selected stochastically rather than by exhaustive enumeration.

## Method

The trial wave function is a linear combination of antisymmetrized correlated Gaussians, each with a diagonal matrix of nonlinear parameters in a chosen Jacobi coordinate system, multiplied by vector-coupled products of spherical harmonics for the orbital part and successively coupled spin and isospin functions. Basis states are built incrementally: at each step, a fixed number of randomly generated candidates (each with randomly chosen spin, isospin, and partial-wave channel, plus random nonlinear parameters) are tested, and the one that lowers the ground-state energy the most is added to the basis. After reaching a target dimension, the nonlinear parameters of existing basis states are refined by further random trials until the energy gain falls below 0.005 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Triton ground-state energy with AV6 potential: -7.15 MeV (SVM), compared to -7.22(0.12) MeV (GFMC) and -7.15 MeV (Faddeev)
- Triton ground-state energy with AV8 potential: -7.79 MeV (SVM), matching -7.79 MeV (Faddeev) and -7.79 MeV (CHH)
- Triton ground-state energy with RV8 potential: -7.59 MeV (SVM), compared to -7.54(0.10) MeV (GFMC) and -7.59 MeV (Faddeev)
- Alpha particle ground-state energy with AV6 potential: -25.25 MeV (SVM), compared to -24.79(0.20) MeV (GFMC)
- Alpha particle ground-state energy with AV8 potential: -25.62 MeV (SVM), compared to -25.75(0.02) MeV (GFMC) and -25.60 MeV (CHH)
- Alpha particle ground-state energy with RV8 potential: -24.49 MeV (SVM), compared to -24.55(0.13) MeV (GFMC)
- Triton D-state probability: 8.7% (AV6), 8.9% (AV8), 9.7% (RV8)
- Alpha particle D-state probability: 15.1% (AV6), 14.2% (AV8), 15.5% (RV8)
- Basis size required: about 50 states for triton, about 200 states for alpha particle to obtain fairly good binding energies
- Energy of alpha particle with 100 basis states is within 0.5 MeV of its final value

## Context

This work extends the SVM, previously applied only to central interactions (Varga and Suzuki, 1995), to realistic nuclear forces including spin-orbit, tensor, and other noncentral components. It provides an alternative to methods such as Green's Function Monte Carlo (Carlson, 1987, 1988), Faddeev calculations (Glöckle and Kamada, 1993), Correlated Hyperspherical Harmonics (Viviani, Kievsky and Rosati, 1995), and the Gaussian Variational Method (Kameyama, Kamimura and Fukushima, 1989), which for A=4 nuclei agree only within a few hundred keV. The low basis dimensions required for A=3 and A=4 systems suggest that the SVM can be extended to A=5, 6, and 7 nuclei with realistic forces, enabling studies of systems such as the neutron halo of 6He where the two-neutron separation energy is about 1 MeV.
