# Tidal waves as yrast states in transitional nuclei

> The yrast states of transitional nuclei are described as quadrupole waves running over the nuclear surface, which we call tidal waves. In contrast to a rotor, which generates angular momentum by increasing the angular velocity at approximately constant deformation, a tidal wave generates angular momentum by increasing the deformation at approximately constant angular velocity. The properties of the tidal waves are calculated by means of the cranking model in a microscopic way. The calculated energies and E2 transition probabilities of the yrast states in the transitional nuclides with $Z$= 44, 46, 48 and $N=56, 58, ..., 66$ reproduce the experiment in detail. The nonlinear response of the nucleonic orbitals results in a strong coupling between shape and single particle degrees of freedom.

## Key claim

The yrast states of transitional nuclei can be described as quadrupole surface waves ("tidal waves") that generate angular momentum primarily by increasing deformation at approximately constant angular velocity, in contrast to rigid rotors which increase angular velocity at constant deformation; however, the authors note that real nuclei lie between these idealized cases and that the simple tidal wave picture does not account for the full complexity when single-particle degrees of freedom also contribute angular momentum.

## Method

The authors used the Shell Correction version of the Tilted Axis Cranking model (SCTAC) to calculate yrast state properties for even-even nuclei with Z=44, 46, 48 and N=56, 58, 60, 62, 64, 66. They solved the cranking problem numerically on a grid in the ε₂-γ deformation plane without the small-deformation approximation, using a diabatic tracing technique to follow configurations through crossings of h₁₁/₂ routhians, and minimized the interpolated energy function E(ε₂,γ,J) with respect to deformation for fixed angular momentum.

## Key numbers

- Ground state deformation energies were calculated using the modified oscillator potential with parameters from Ref. [qptac]; a small adjustment kε₂² was added with k values of 8.5, 7.9, 8.5, 9.6, 11.9, and 14.5 MeV for N=56, 58, 60, 62, 64, and 66 respectively
- For ¹⁵⁴Gd, the phenomenological model parameters were Θ₀=21.5 MeV⁻¹ and Θ₁=1.18 MeV⁻¹; experimental transition quadrupole moments Qₜ,ₑ ranged from 6.2 eb at I=2 to 6.60±0.16 eb at I=8
- For ¹¹⁰Cd, experimental rotational frequencies ℏωₑ for the ground state band ranged from 0.328 MeV at I=2 to 0.168 MeV at I=10
- The calculated energy of the 0⁺₂ state in ¹¹⁰Cd was 3.51 MeV, compared to the experimental value of 1.47 MeV
- Pairing parameters were kept fixed at Δₙ=Δₚ=1.2 MeV
- A correction of 100 MeV⁻¹ ε₂² sin²(γ-π/3) was added to the angular momentum, contributing less than 0.5 ℏ

## Context

This work extends the cranking model approach beyond its traditional application to well-deformed rotors, building on Marshalek's (1977) demonstration that RPA equations for harmonic quadrupole vibrations are a solution to the selfconsistent cranking model in the small-deformation limit. The paper contrasts its microscopic approach with phenomenological models based on the Bohr Hamiltonian (Bohr and Mottelson 1975) and the Interacting Boson Model (Iachello and Arima 1987), as well as with the Generator Coordinate Method and mean-field-plus-cranking-mass-parameter approaches, noting that these adiabatic methods often unjustifiably separate collective quadrupole excitations from quasiparticle excitations. The results enable microscopic calculation of yrast states in transitional and vibrational nuclei up to spin 16, and the method can be extended to odd-A and odd-odd nuclei.
