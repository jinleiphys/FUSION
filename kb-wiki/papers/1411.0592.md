# Two-band description of resonant superfluidity in atomic Fermi gases

> Fermionic superfluidity in atomic Fermi gases across a Feshbach resonance is normally described by the atom-molecule theory, which treats the closed channel as a noninteracting point boson. In this work we present a theoretical description of the resonant superfluidity in analogy to the two-band superconductors. We employ the underlying two-channel scattering model of Feshbach resonance where the closed channel is treated as a composite boson with binding energy $\varepsilon_0$ and the resonance is triggered by the microscopic interchannel coupling $U_{12}$. The binding energy $\varepsilon_0$ naturally serves as an energy scale of the system, which has been sent to infinity in the atom-molecule theory. We show that the atom-molecule theory can be viewed as a leading-order low-energy effective theory of the underlying fermionic theory in the limit $\varepsilon_0\rightarrow\infty$ and $U_{12}\rightarrow0$, while keeping the phenomenological atom-molecule coupling finite. The resulting two-band description of the superfluid state is in analogy to the BCS theory of two-band superconductors. In the dilute limit $\varepsilon_0\rightarrow\infty$, the two-band description recovers precisely the atom-molecule theory. The two-band theory provides a natural approach to study the corrections because of a finite binding energy $\varepsilon_0$ in realistic experimental systems. For broad and moderate resonances, the correction is not important for current experimental densities. However, for extremely narrow resonance, we find that the correction becomes significant. The finite binding energy correction could be important for the stability of homogeneous polarized superfluid against phase separation in imbalanced Fermi gases across a narrow Feshbach resonance.

## Key claim

The atom-molecule theory of resonant superfluidity is a leading-order low-energy effective theory of an underlying two-channel fermionic model in the limit where the closed-channel binding energy \(\varepsilon_0 \to \infty\) and the interchannel coupling \(U_{12} \to 0\), while keeping the phenomenological atom-molecule coupling \(g\) finite; for finite \(\varepsilon_0\) the two-band description yields corrections that become significant for extremely narrow resonances.

## Method

The authors start from a microscopic two-channel Hamiltonian treating both open and closed channels as fermions with a contact interaction, renormalize the coupling matrix to remove cutoff dependence, and derive the low-energy scattering amplitude. They then perform a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to obtain a mean-field description of the superfluid state, treating the closed-channel binding energy \(\varepsilon_0\) as a finite band offset. The atom-molecule model is recovered by taking the limit \(\varepsilon_0 \to \infty\) and \(U_{12} \to 0\) while keeping \(g = U_{12} \sqrt{(M\varepsilon_0)^{3/2}/(2\pi)}\) finite, and the next-to-leading-order correction from finite \(\varepsilon_0\) is computed.

## Key numbers

- The effective range is given by \(r_{\rm eff} = -16\pi^2 / [M^2 U_{12}^2 (M\varepsilon_0)^{3/2}]\), which is always negative.
- The atom-molecule coupling is related to microscopic parameters by \(g = U_{12} \sqrt{(M\varepsilon_0)^{3/2}/(2\pi)}\).
- The closed-channel molecule scattering length at next-to-leading order is \(a_{\rm m} \simeq 2/\sqrt{M\varepsilon_0}\).
- For broad and moderate resonances, the correction from finite \(\varepsilon_0\) is not important at current experimental densities; for extremely narrow resonances the correction becomes significant.

## Context

This work extends the standard atom-molecule theory of Feshbach resonances (developed by multiple groups in the early 2000s) by returning to the underlying two-channel fermionic model of Bruun (2004) and treating it as a two-band superfluid analogous to two-band BCS superconductors. The two-band description provides a natural framework to compute finite-binding-energy corrections that are negligible for broad resonances but become important for narrow resonances, such as the \(^6\)Li resonance at \(B \simeq 543.3\) G studied experimentally by the MIT group. This approach enables systematic study of corrections to universal behavior and may affect the stability of polarized superfluids against phase separation in imbalanced Fermi gases across narrow Feshbach resonances.
