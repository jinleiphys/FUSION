# Extension of Quantum Molecular Dynamics and its Application to Heavy-Ion Collisions

> In order to treat low-energy heavy-ion reactions, we make an extension of quantum molecular dynamics method. A phenomenological Pauli potential is introduced into effective interactions to approximate the nature of the Fermion many-body system. We treat the widths of nucleon wave-packets as time-dependent dynamical variables. With these modifications, our model can well describe the ground-state properties in wide mass range. Improvements due to the extension are also obtained in the nucleus-nucleus collision calculations.

## Key claim

The extended quantum molecular dynamics (EQMD) model, which introduces a phenomenological Pauli potential and treats nucleon wave-packet widths as time-dependent dynamical variables, can describe ground-state properties across a wide mass range and improve nucleus-nucleus collision calculations, but the model still produces somewhat higher matter density near the center and smaller root mean square radii than experimental values.

## Method

The authors extended the standard quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) approach by adding a phenomenological Pauli potential to the effective interactions (Skyrme, Coulomb, and symmetry terms) to approximate fermionic behavior, and by treating the complex Gaussian width of each nucleon wave-packet as a time-dependent dynamical variable. The equations of motion for the 8A parameters (position, momentum, and complex width for each nucleon) were derived from the time-dependent variational principle, and ground states were obtained by solving damped equations of motion to reach energy-minimum configurations. For collision simulations, the boosted nuclei were evolved with the EQMD equations plus a stochastic two-body collision term, followed by statistical decay calculations for the produced fragments.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per nucleon for finite nuclei: parameter set 1 reproduces the systematic trend reasonably; parameter set 2 reproduces binding energies "almost perfectly" across the mass range.
- For $^{12}$C ground state with parameter set 2: binding energy 92.7 MeV (experimental 92.2 MeV) and rms radius 2.31 fm (experimental 2.46 fm).
- For $^{16}$O ground state: binding energy 130.8 MeV and rms radius 2.5 fm, which is about 92% of the experimental value 2.73 fm.
- Fusion cross section for $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O: EQMD reproduces about 85% of experimental data, while standard QMD gives almost zero fusion cross section under the same fusion criterion (no nucleon/fragment emission within 450 fm/c after contact).
- Pauli potential parameters: set 1 uses $c_P = 15$ MeV, $f_0 = 1.05$, $\mu = 2.0$; set 2 uses $c_P = 15$ MeV, $f_0 = 1.0$, $\mu = 1.3$.
- Two-body collision parameters: $d_{\rm coll} = 2.0$ fm; nucleon-nucleon cross section $\sigma_{\rm NN} = 100/(1+\epsilon/200\ {\rm MeV})$ mb.

## Context

This work extends the standard QMD approach of Aichelin and Stocker (1986) and Aichelin (1991) to address its inability to produce stable ground states for low-energy heavy-ion reactions, a problem noted in earlier QMD fusion studies by Maruyama, Ohnishi, and Horiuchi (1990). The EQMD method provides an alternative to fully antisymmetrized approaches like FMD (Feldmeier, 1990) and AMD (Ono et al., 1992), which are computationally expensive for heavy systems due to CPU time scaling approximately as the fourth power of particle number. The model enables systematic studies of low-energy reactions between heavy nuclei, where no other many-body model has been successfully applied, and shows improvements in describing alpha-clustering structures and fusion cross sections that were previously only accessible via AMD calculations.
