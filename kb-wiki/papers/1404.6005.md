# Quark-Gluon Plasma as the Possible Source of Cosmological Dark Radiation

> The effective number of neutrinos, $N_{\text{eff}}$, obtained from CMB fluctuations accounts for all effectively massless degrees of freedom present in the Universe, including but not limited to the three known neutrinos. Using a lattice-QCD derived QGP equation of state, we constrain the observed range of $N_{\text{eff}}$ in terms of the the freeze-out of unknown degrees of freedom near to quark-gluon hadronization. We explore limits on the coupling of these particles, applying methods of kinetic theory. We present bounds on the coupling of such particles and discuss the implications of a connection between $N_{\text{eff}}$ and the QGP transformation for laboratory studies of QGP.

## Key claim

The observed range of the effective number of neutrinos, \(N_{\text{eff}}\), from CMB data can be explained by the freeze-out of unknown light sterile particles (SPs) at or near the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) hadronization temperature, provided the number of such degrees of freedom is approximately \(3\pm 2\) and their coupling is either very weak (cosmological) or strong but confined to the deconfined phase (laboratory).

## Method

The authors used a lattice-QCD derived equation of state for the QGP (from Borsanyi et al. (2013)) to compute the effective number of entropy degrees of freedom, \(g_*^S\), as a function of temperature. They then applied entropy conservation to relate the decoupling temperature of hypothetical sterile particles (SPs) to their contribution to \(\delta N_{\text{eff}} = N_{\text{eff}} - 3.046\). Kinetic theory, via Boltzmann two-body scattering operators, was used to estimate the minimum coupling strength (parameterized as a modified Fermi constant \(G_{SP} = C G_F\)) required for SPs to remain in chemical equilibrium down to the QGP hadronization temperature, both in a cosmological setting and in laboratory heavy-ion collisions.

## Key numbers

- The Planck satellite analysis gives \(N_{\text{eff}} = 3.36 \pm 0.34\) (CMB only) and \(N_{\text{eff}} = 3.62 \pm 0.25\) (CMB+\(H_0\)) at 68% confidence.
- The QGP transformation temperature range is reported as \(T_c = 142 - 163\) MeV.
- For a cosmological setting, the lower bound on the coupling strength is \(C \gtrsim 2 \times 10^{-3}\), corresponding to \(G_{SP}^{-1/2} \lesssim 6.5\) TeV.
- For a laboratory QGP setting (with a simple temperature profile \(T\tau = T_0\tau_0\), \(\tau_0 = 8 \times 10^{-24}\) s, cooling from \(T_0 = 600\) MeV to \(T = 150\) MeV in \(2.4 \times 10^{-23}\) s), the required coupling is \(C \gtrsim 2 \times 10^6\), corresponding to \(G_{SP}^{-1/2} \lesssim 200\) MeV.
- The best fit for the number of SP degrees of freedom at QGP hadronization is \(3 \pm 2\), implying that approximately \(12 \pm 8\%\) of the entropy content of the QGP is carried by escaping SPs.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Anchordoqui et al. (2011, 2012) that proposed sterile neutrinos as a source of dark radiation, but uses a more recent lattice-QCD equation of state (Borsanyi et al., 2013) instead of the older lattice data (Bazavov et al., 2009) or ideal gas approximations. It also contrasts with a model by Weinberg (2013) that proposed Goldstone bosons from a new symmetry breaking. The results motivate further experimental searches for missing energy in laboratory QGP experiments (e.g., in heavy-ion collisions) and suggest that SPs could restore a pressure discontinuity at the QGP surface, affecting flow observables such as \(v_2\).
