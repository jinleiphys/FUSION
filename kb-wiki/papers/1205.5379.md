# Projected Quasi-particle Perturbation theory

> The BCS and/or HFB theories are extended by treating the effect of four quasi-particle states perturbatively. The approach is tested on the pairing hamiltonian, showing that it combines the advantage of standard perturbation theory valid at low pairing strength and of non-perturbative approaches breaking particle number valid at higher pairing strength. Including the restoration of particle number, further improves the description of pairing correlation. In the presented test, the agreement between the exact solution and the combined perturbative + projection is almost perfect. The proposed method scales friendly when the number of particles increases and provides a simple alternative to other more complicated approaches.

# Projected Quasi-particle Perturbation theory

## Key claim

The paper claims that combining BCS/HFB quasi-particle theory with second-order perturbation theory and subsequent particle-number projection yields nearly exact agreement with the exact pairing Hamiltonian solution across all coupling strengths, while remaining computationally tractable for large particle numbers. The hedge is that the method is tested only on the constant-pairing equidistant-level model Hamiltonian, and the near-perfect agreement is demonstrated for systems up to N=14 where exact diagonalization is available.

## Method

The authors start from a BCS/HFB ground state written in the canonical basis and treat the residual four-quasi-particle (4QP) coupling as a perturbation to the quasi-particle Hamiltonian H0. They compute the second-order energy correction from 4QP states using standard perturbation theory, then apply particle-number projection (via the Fomenko discretization of the gauge-angle integral with 199 discretization points) to the perturbed wavefunction truncated at second order. The projected expectation value of the Hamiltonian is evaluated numerically using analytic expressions for the overlaps and operator matrix elements between projected BCS-type states.

## Key numbers

- For N=8 particles with Ω=8 equidistant levels (Δε=1 MeV) and constant pairing strength g/Δε=0.8, the correlation energy per particle from QP³T matches the exact solution within the line thickness of the plot, while BCS and PAV show systematic deviations.
- The BCS threshold for the N=8 system occurs at g/Δε ≈ 0.3; below this threshold standard perturbation theory is used instead of QP²T.
- For the N=2, Ω=2 system, the QP³T correlation energy curve is shown to be nearly indistinguishable from the exact solution across all coupling strengths.
- The projection integral uses 199 discretization points, but the authors note the number can be reduced to 5 without changing the result.
- For N=100 particles at g/Δε=0.8, the QP³T calculation remains feasible, whereas exact diagonalization becomes impractical above N=14.
- The 4QP Hilbert space size is Ω(Ω-1)/2, compared to the full diagonalization space size of Ω!/(N_pair!(Ω-N_pair)!).

## Context

This work extends the standard many-body perturbation theory approach to pairing, which was known to work only in the weak-coupling regime (Bishari 1971, Sandulescu and Bertsch 2008), by building on top of the BCS/HFB quasi-particle vacuum rather than the normal Slater determinant. It provides a simpler alternative to more computationally demanding methods such as exact Richardson-Gaudin diagonalization (Richardson 1964, Dukelsky 2004), Variation After Projection (Rodriguez and Egido 2007, Hupin and Lacroix 2012), and Quantum Monte-Carlo techniques (Mukherjee, Alhassid, and Bertsch 2011). The method can be directly implemented on existing BCS/HFB codes (Bonche, Flocard, and Heenen 2005; Dobaczewski and Olbratowski 2004) and suggests that projection may be needed in the Gorkov-Green function approach to ab-initio nuclear calculations (Soma, Duguet, and Barbieri 2011).
