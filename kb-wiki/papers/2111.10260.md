# The slope, the hill, the drop, and the swoosh: Learning about the nuclear matter equation of state from the binary Love relations

> Analyses that connect astrophysical observations of neutron stars with nuclear matter properties sometimes rely on equation-of-state insensitive relations. We show that the slope of the binary Love relations (i.e.~between the tidal deformabilities of binary neutron stars) encodes the rate of change of the nuclear matter speed of sound below three times nuclear saturation density. Twin stars lead to relations that present a signature ''hill'', ''drop'', and ''swoosh'' due to the second (mass-radius) stable branch, requiring a new description of the binary love relations.

## Key claim

The slope of the binary Love relations (BLRs) between the tidal deformabilities of binary neutron stars encodes the number density at which structure in the speed of sound first appears, but is independent of whether that structure is a bump or a plateau; twin stars from first-order phase transitions produce additional "hill," "drop," and "swoosh" features in the BLRs that depend on whether the stars lie in the first or second stable branch of the mass-radius curve.

## Method

The authors model the nuclear equation of state (EoS) using a crust (SLy EoS up to ~1.5 nuclear saturation density) plus structure functions that introduce bumps or plateaus in the speed of sound \(c_s^2\) to mimic higher-order phase transitions, and transition functions for smooth connections. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations for non-rotating neutron star masses and radii, then solve the linearized Einstein equations to compute the dimensionless tidal deformability \(\Lambda\) for each star, constructing binary Love relations \(\Lambda_a(\Lambda_s)\) (where \(\Lambda_{s,a} = (\Lambda_1 \pm \Lambda_2)/2\)) for fixed mass ratios.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: \(\rho_{sat} \equiv m_N n_{\rm sat} \approx 2.7 \times 10^{14}~{\rm g~cm}^{-3}\), with baryon number density \(n_{\rm sat} \equiv 0.16~{\rm fm}^{-3}\)
- For EoSs with structure in \(c_s^2\) below ~3 \(n_{\rm sat}\), the linear regression slope of \(\Lambda_a/\Lambda_s\) is approximately 0.57 (EoSs 1-3) versus 0.65 (EoS 4, structure at higher density); the leading-order Newtonian approximation gives 0.56 and 0.64 respectively
- The approximation in Eq. (4) matches fully-numerical BLRs to better than \(\lesssim 15\%\) for \(\Lambda_s > 300\)
- For stars in the second stable branch (twin stars), \(\Lambda_{1,2} \propto \mathcal{C}_{1,2}^{-9}\) (instead of \(\propto \mathcal{C}^{-5}\) for low compactness), with proportionality constant \(b \approx 0.35\)
- The swoosh feature appears at \(\Lambda_a \in (1,8)\) for type C binaries (both stars in the second branch)
- During LIGO O5, expected tidal deformability measurement uncertainties are \(\delta \Lambda_{1,2} \approx 50\)–\(100\); 3G detectors may achieve \(\delta \Lambda_{1,2} \approx 5\)–\(10\); detecting the drop or swoosh would require \(\delta \Lambda_{1,2} \approx 1\) and \(\approx 10\) respectively

## Context

This work extends the universal binary Love relations originally derived by Yagi (2013, 2015) using EoSs without phase transition features, showing that structure in the speed of sound (bumps or plateaus) and first-order phase transitions producing mass twins imprint observable signatures on the BLRs. The authors use the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model and the NL3 model (Dexheimer 2018, Pais 2016) to demonstrate how specific vector interactions change the slope of the mass-radius curve and thus the BLR slope. The results enable future gravitational wave observations (LIGO O5 and 3G detectors) to potentially identify the density at which the speed of sound rises rapidly and to detect first-order phase transitions through the hill, drop, and swoosh features, though the latter two require precision beyond current detector capabilities.
