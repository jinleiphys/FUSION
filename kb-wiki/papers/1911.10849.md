# Low-density in-medium effects on light clusters from heavy-ion data

> The modification of the ground state properties of light atomic nuclei in the nuclear and stellar medium is addressed, using chemical equilibrium constants evaluated from a new analysis of the intermediate energy heavy-ion (Xe$+$Sn) collision data measured by the INDRA collaboration. Three different reactions are considered, mainly differing by the isotopic content of the emission source. The thermodynamic conditions of the data samples are extracted from the measured multiplicities allowing for a parametrization of the in-medium modification, determined with the single hypothesis that the different nuclear species in a given sample correspond to a unique common value for the density of the expanding source. We show that this correction, which was not considered in previous analyses of chemical constants from heavy ion collisions, is necessary, since the observables of the analyzed systems show strong deviations from the expected results for an ideal gas of free clusters. This data set is further compared to a relativistic mean-field model, and seen to be reasonably compatible with a universal correction of the attractive $σ$-meson coupling.

## Key claim

In-medium modifications of light cluster properties are necessary to explain heavy-ion collision data, but the suppression effect is smaller than previously estimated when the thermodynamic parameters (density, temperature) are extracted consistently from the data rather than assuming an ideal gas of clusters. The correction corresponds to a universal reduction of the attractive scalar meson coupling to nucleons bound in clusters, with a coupling parameter \(x_s = 0.92 \pm 0.02\) rather than the earlier value of \(x_s = 0.85 \pm 0.05\).

## Method

Chemical equilibrium constants for light clusters (deuteron, triton, helium-3, helium-4) were extracted from INDRA data on three different Xe+Sn collision systems at intermediate energies, using the measured cluster multiplicities as a function of surface velocity to select statistical ensembles at different emission times. The baryon density was determined from the free volume, which was estimated from the differential cluster spectra using a Bayesian technique that imposed the condition that all cluster species in a given sample correspond to the same source volume, rather than assuming ideal-gas expressions. The resulting chemical constants were compared to a relativistic mean-field model (Pais et al., 2018, 2019) that includes a phenomenological scalar coupling \(x_s\) for nucleons bound in clusters.

## Key numbers

- The optimal scalar coupling parameter extracted from the consistent analysis is \(x_s = 0.92 \pm 0.02\), compared to the earlier value of \(x_s = 0.85 \pm 0.05\) from comparison with NIMROD data (Qin et al., 2012).
- For the \(\alpha\)-particle at density \(\rho = 0.003\) fm\(^{-3}\) and temperature \(T = 5.1\) MeV, the chemical equilibrium constant is \(0.36 \times 10^{10}\) fm\(^9\) with \(x_s = 0.92\), versus \(0.22 \times 10^{10}\) fm\(^9\) with \(x_s = 0.85\).
- At the highest density point \(\rho = 0.026\) fm\(^{-3}\) and \(T = 10.4\) MeV, the \(\alpha\)-particle chemical constant is \(0.20 \times 10^7\) fm\(^9\) with \(x_s = 0.92\), versus \(0.23 \times 10^6\) fm\(^9\) with \(x_s = 0.85\), a factor of approximately 8.7 increase.
- The baryonic density increases by up to a factor of two when in-medium corrections are included compared to the ideal-gas assumption.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of INDRA data presented by Bougault et al. (2020) by resolving a methodological inconsistency: previous analyses (Qin et al., 2012; Bougault et al., 2020) used ideal-gas expressions to extract thermodynamic parameters while simultaneously trying to measure deviations from ideal-gas behavior. The new analysis provides a consistent determination of in-medium corrections and revises the scalar coupling parameter in the relativistic mean-field model of Pais et al. (2018, 2019). The results enable more reliable predictions of light cluster abundances in core-collapse supernova and neutron star merger simulations, where these clusters affect weak interaction rates and r-process nucleosynthesis.
