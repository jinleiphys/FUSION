# Dispersive effects in neutron matter superfluidity

> The explicit energy dependence of the single particle self-energy (dispersive effects), due to short range correlations, is included in the treatment of neutron matter superfluidity. The method can be applied in general to strong interacting fermion systems, and it is expected to be valid whenever the pairing gap is substantially smaller than the Fermi kinetic energy. The results for neutron matter show that dispersive effects are strong in the density region near the gap closure.

# Dispersive effects in neutron matter superfluidity

## Key claim

Dispersive effects from the energy dependence of the single-particle self-energy can strongly reduce the pairing gap in neutron matter, but the method is only valid when the pairing gap is substantially smaller than the Fermi kinetic energy, and the results show the reduction is most pronounced near the density region where the gap closes.

## Method

The authors developed a generalized gap equation that includes the full energy dependence of the single-particle self-energy (dispersive effects) while assuming an energy-independent irreducible interaction. They solved this equation for neutron matter using the bare Argonne v14 potential as the pairing interaction and the Brueckner approximation for the self-energy, then compared results with and without the self-energy contributions to isolate the dispersive effects.

- At Fermi momentum k_F = 0.9 fm^{-1}, the imaginary part of the neutron self-energy at the quasi-particle pole is small compared to the real part of the quasi-particle energy, justifying the pole approximation.

## Context

This work extends earlier microscopic approaches to neutron matter superfluidity that used the effective mass approximation or normal state calculations for the single-particle spectrum, such as the variational Jastrow method (Clark, 1979), the Babu-Brown approach (Wambach, 1993), and BCS calculations with realistic bare interactions (Baldo, 1990). The method is simpler than the fully self-consistent scheme recently developed by Bozek (2000) for a schematic interaction, and it enables systematic inclusion of dispersive effects in realistic calculations of neutron matter pairing, though the authors note that correlation effects on the irreducible interaction must still be included consistently before drawing final conclusions on pairing strength in neutron stars.
