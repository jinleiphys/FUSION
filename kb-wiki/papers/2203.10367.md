# Modeling Heavy-Ion Fusion Cross Section Data via a Novel Artificial Intelligence Approach

> We perform a comprehensive analysis of complete fusion cross section data with the aim to derive, in a completely data-driven way, a model suitable to predict the integrated cross section of the fusion between light to medium mass nuclei at above barrier energies. To this end, we adopted a novel artificial intelligence approach, based on a hybridization of genetic programming and artificial neural networks, capable to derive an analytical model for the description of experimental data. The approach enables, for the first time, to perform a global search for computationally simple models over several variables and a considerable body of nuclear data. The derived phenomenological formula can serve to reproduce the trend of fusion cross section for a large variety of light to intermediate mass collision systems in an energy domain ranging approximately from the Coulomb barrier to the onset of multi-fragmentation phenomena.

## Key claim

The authors derive a fully data-driven phenomenological model for complete fusion cross sections of light-to-medium mass nuclei at above-barrier energies, using a hybrid artificial intelligence approach that combines genetic programming and neural networks. The key hedge is that the model is derived from a dataset limited to systems with \(Z_p \ge 6\), \(Z_t \ge 6\), and \(A_{cn} \le 130\), with bombarding energies below 5 MeV/nucleon, and its predictions are validated only within this restricted domain.

## Method

The authors collected approximately 4500 experimental fusion cross section points from the Nuclear Reaction Video Project database for 124 light-to-medium mass systems, then applied a seven-step preprocessing protocol including pattern weighting to balance collision systems, enrichment of the high-energy region III using predictions from Eudes et al. (2014), and addition of regularization points to enforce physical boundaries (zero cross section at very low and very high energies). They then used the Brain Project algorithm, a hybridization of genetic programming and artificial neural networks, to perform symbolic regression over 25 input features (including charges, masses, spins, parities, separation energies, and Q-values) and derived analytical formulas by optimizing a fitness function that balances prediction error against model complexity (number of nodes and features).

## Key numbers

- The simplest model uses only 3 features (\(1/E_{cm}\), \(Z_1\), \(Z_2\)) and 10 nodes, with the formula \(\sigma_{fus}^{(n_{tgt}=10)}(E_{cm}) = 1102 \cdot \exp\left[-\left(1.39 - 0.469 \cdot Z_2 \cdot Z_1 \cdot \frac{1}{E_{cm}}\right)^2\right]\) in mb.
- The more accurate model uses 20 nodes and has the formula \(\sigma_{fus}^{(n_{tgt}=20)}(E_{cm}) = 144.5 \cdot \erf\left\{\exp\left[-\left(0.129 \cdot \frac{1}{E_{cm}} \cdot Z\right)\right]\right\}\) (truncated in the provided text).
- The dataset ultimately contained about 4500 experimental points from 124 light-to-medium mass systems, with an additional 63 medium-mass systems (\(Z_p \cdot Z_t > 250\)) used only for extrapolation testing.
- Optimal error values were found in the range \(n_{tgt} \simeq 10-20\), with saturation effects starting from \(n_{tgt} = 16\).

## Context

This work extends phenomenological modeling of heavy-ion fusion cross sections, which previously relied on semi-classical models such as critical distance models (Lee 1980, Matsuse 1982) and compound nucleus limitation models (Bass 1977), as well as earlier phenomenological formulas (Porto 1984, Giordano 1990, Eudes 2014) that typically described only limited energy regions. The novel AI approach enables, for the first time, a global search over 25 variables and a large dataset to derive computationally simple analytical models that satisfy physical boundary conditions (vanishing cross section at very low and very high energies), and it demonstrates generalization to collision systems not included in the learning set. This enables future work to incorporate additional nuclear structure variables or extend the approach to heavier systems where fusion-fission competes.
