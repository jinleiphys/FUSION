# Shell model studies of the $^{130}Te$ neutrinoless double-beta decay

> Most uncertainties regarding the theoretical study of the neutrinoless double-beta decay are related to the accuracy of the nuclear matrix elements that appear in the expressions of the lifetimes. We calculate the nuclear matrix elements for the $0νββ$ decay of $^{130}Te$ in a shell model approach, using a recently proposed effective Hamiltonian. To ensure the reliability of the results, we investigate this Hamiltonian by performing calculations of spectroscopic quantities and comparing them to the latest experimental data available, and we analyze the $2νββ$ and the $0νββ$ decay nuclear matrix elements of $^{136}$Xe. Finally, we report new nuclear matrix for the $^{130}$Te considering the light neutrino exchange and heavy neutrino exchange mechanisms, alongside with an overview of some recent values reported in the literature.

# Shell model studies of the $^{130}$Te neutrinoless double-beta decay

## Key claim

The authors calculate nuclear matrix elements (NME) for the $0\nu\beta\beta$ decay of $^{130}$Te using a shell model approach with the SVD effective Hamiltonian in the reduced $jj55$ model space, but acknowledge that the larger $jj77$ model space (which includes spin-orbit partner orbitals) is not yet computationally feasible for $^{130}$Te, and that the missing orbitals cause the Ikeda sum rule to be unsatisfied, resulting in about half of the GT sum-rule being lost.

## Method

The authors perform shell model calculations using the SVD effective Hamiltonian (derived from the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential with core-polarization effects) in the $jj55$ model space (orbitals $0g_{7/2}, 1d_{5/2}, 1d_{3/2}, 2s_{1/2}, 0h_{11/2}$) for nucleons between the $Z,N \in [50,82]$ shell closures. They validate the Hamiltonian by comparing calculated spectroscopic quantities (energy spectra, $B(E2)\uparrow$ transition probabilities, occupation probabilities, and Gamow-Teller strengths) against experimental data for $^{130}$Te, $^{130}$Xe, $^{136}$Xe, and $^{136}$Ba, and by re-analyzing the $2\nu\beta\beta$ and $0\nu\beta\beta$ decay NME of $^{136}$Xe. The NME calculations include short range correlations (SRC), finite nucleon size effects (FNS), and higher order corrections of the nucleon current (HOC), using both Argonne-V18 and CD-Bonn SRC parameterizations.

## Key numbers

- For $^{130}$Te light neutrino exchange: total NME $M^{0\nu}_{\nu} = 1.80$ (AV18 SRC) and $1.94$ (CD-Bonn SRC), with $g_A=1.254$ and optimal closure energy $\left<E\right>=3.5$ MeV
- For $^{130}$Te heavy neutrino exchange: total NME $M^{0\nu}_{N} = 94.60$ (AV18 SRC) and $136.08$ (CD-Bonn SRC)
- For $^{136}$Xe light neutrino exchange: total NME $M^{0\nu}_{\nu} = 1.63$ (AV18 SRC) and $1.76$ (CD-Bonn SRC)
- For $^{136}$Xe heavy neutrino exchange: total NME $M^{0\nu}_{N} = 85.43$ (AV18 SRC) and $122.59$ (CD-Bonn SRC)
- Calculated $2\nu\beta\beta$ decay NME using quenching factor 0.74: $M^{2\nu\beta\beta}_{^{130}Te} = 0.0238$ MeV$^{-1}$ and $M^{2\nu\beta\beta}_{^{136}Xe} = 0.0256$ MeV$^{-1}$
- Experimental $2\nu\beta\beta$ NME require slightly smaller quenching factors: $qf_{^{130}Te}=0.59$ giving $M^{2\nu\beta\beta}_{^{130}Te} = 0.0175^{+0.0016}_{-0.0014}$ MeV$^{-1}$, and $qf_{^{136}Xe}=0.71$ giving $M^{2\nu\beta\beta}_{^{136}Xe} = 0.0218 \pm 0.0003$ MeV$^{-1}$
- Calculated $B(E2)\uparrow$ values: $^{130}$Te = 0.153 (adopted 0.297), $^{130}$Xe = 0.502 (adopted 0.634), $^{136}$Xe = 0.215 (adopted 0.217), $^{136}$Ba = 0.479 (adopted 0.413), all in units not specified but consistent with standard $e^2b^2$
- The effect of different SRC parameterizations on NME is about 10% for light neutrino exchange and about 30% for heavy neutrino exchange

## Context

This work extends the shell model approach to $0\nu\beta\beta$ decay NME calculations for $^{130}$Te, motivated by the CUORE experiment's interest in this isotope. The authors use the SVD Hamiltonian (reported in SVD (2014) for Sn isotopes) and compare their results to NME from other methods including ISM (Strasbourg-Madrid group, published in NPA818), QRPA (Tuebingen group, QRPA-Tu 2014; Jyvaskyla group, QRPA-Jy 2010; University of North Carolina group, QRPA-EN 2013), and IBM-2 (Yale group, IBA-PRC2013). The work enables comparison of $^{130}$Te NME across different nuclear structure methods and provides input for extracting neutrino physics parameters from future experimental half-life measurements. The authors note that their ISM light neutrino results differ from other methods by up to a factor of two, while for heavy neutrino exchange their results are closer to IBM-2 values but still differ from QRPA calculations by a factor of two.
