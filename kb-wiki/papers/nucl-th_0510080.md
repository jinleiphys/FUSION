# Modern optical potentials and the role of nuclear structure

> Microscopic descriptions of exotic nuclei are the subject of much experimental and theoretical effort. Not only are such important in their own right but are also necessary for applications in nuclear astrophysics. Evaluations of model wave functions may be done with analyses of elastic and inelastic scattering from hydrogen. Those require a realistic model of nucleon-nucleus scattering as scattering from hydrogen translates to proton scattering in the inverse kinematics. The Melbourne g-folding model for intermediate energy is presented along with various examples. Implications for existing and future experimental and theoretical work are discussed.

## Key claim

The Melbourne g-folding model provides a predictive, parameter-free description of nucleon-nucleus scattering at intermediate energies, but its success depends critically on using a credible nuclear structure model (shell model, Skyrme-Hartree-Fock, or RPA) that specifies the one-body density matrix elements, not just the density itself, to preserve the nonlocality of the exchange terms.

## Method

The Melbourne g-folding model constructs the nucleon-nucleus optical potential by first solving the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone equation in infinite nuclear matter to obtain g matrices from the bare NN interaction (BonnB), then mapping these to finite nuclei via a double Bessel transform and folding with the target's one-body density matrix elements. Elastic scattering observables are calculated in a single-shot distorted-wave Born approximation (DWBA) with no fitting to data, using the DWBA98 code. Inelastic scattering is treated in the distorted-wave approximation with the same effective g matrices as transition operators.

## Key numbers

- Elastic scattering of 65 MeV protons from nuclei up to mass-64: differential cross sections and analysing powers are well reproduced across the mass range, with excellent reproduction of the momentum-transfer dependence as mass increases.
- Elastic scattering of 41A MeV 6He ions from hydrogen: using Woods-Saxon wave functions (with halo neutron binding set to the separation energy) gives better agreement with data than harmonic oscillator functions; the halo effect appears as a decrease in the elastic differential cross section at large angles.
- Inelastic scattering of 41A MeV 6He to the 2+ state: the halo produces an enhancement in the cross section around 30 degrees.
- Reaction cross sections for S, Ar, and Ca isotopes at 65 and 200 MeV calculated with Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SkX* force) show good agreement with Carlson's estimates and exhibit a Coulomb shift more pronounced at 65 MeV.
- Elastic proton scattering from 208Pb at 121 MeV: RPA and Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SkM* force) both give reasonable ground-state descriptions; inelastic scattering at 135 MeV to the 2+ and 3- states is described self-consistently using RPA transition densities.
- Differential cross sections and polarisations for 40 MeV proton scattering from a sample of Sn isotopes (100Sn to 168Sn) show reasonable agreement with data and natural variation with mass.

## Context

This work extends the Melbourne g-folding model (Amos et al., 2000) to systematically test nuclear structure models including the shell model, Skyrme-Hartree-Fock, and RPA across stable and exotic nuclei. It builds on earlier comparisons of electron and proton scattering from 12C and 6,7Li (Karataglidis et al., 1995, 1997) and addresses problems found in few-body descriptions of halo nuclei (Crespo et al., 1996, 1999) where core density uncertainties and the neglect of medium modifications in multiple-scattering expansions led to significant changes in predictions. The model enables future analyses of scattering data from radioactive beam facilities, including low-energy experiments via the Multi-Channel Algebraic Scattering theory (Amos et al., 2005, this conference).
