# General properties of astrophysical reaction rates in explosive nucleosynthesis

> Fundamental differences in the prediction of reaction rates with intermediate and heavy target nuclei compared to the ones with light nuclei are discussed, with special emphasis on stellar modifications of the rates. Ground and excited state contributions to the stellar rates are quantified, deriving a linear weighting of excited state contributions despite of a Boltzmann population of the nuclear states. A Coulomb suppression effect of the excited state contributions is identified, acting against the usual Q-value rule in some reactions. The proper inclusion of experimental data in revised stellar rates is shown, containing revised uncertainties. An application to the s-process shows that the actual uncertainties in the neutron capture rates are larger than would be expected from the experimental errors alone. Sensitivities of reaction rates and cross sections are defined and their application in reaction studies is discussed. The conclusion provides a guide to experiment as well as theory on how to best improve the rates used in astrophysical simulations and how to assess their uncertainties.

# General properties of astrophysical reaction rates in explosive nucleosynthesis

## Key claim

The paper argues that stellar reaction rates for intermediate and heavy target nuclei are fundamentally different from those for light nuclei, with excited state contributions often dominating the stellar rate even at relatively low temperatures due to a linear energy weighting that counteracts the exponential Boltzmann suppression. The author shows that the commonly used stellar enhancement factor (SEF) is an unsuitable measure for connecting laboratory measurements to stellar reactivities, and that the actual uncertainties in s-process neutron capture rates are larger than would be expected from experimental errors alone.

## Method

The author derives the formal definition of stellar reaction rates including thermal population of excited nuclear states, showing that the effective cross section involves a linear weighting factor W_i = (E - E_i)/E for excited state contributions. The relative ground state contribution X_0 is quantified for neutron capture and photodisintegration reactions across the nuclear chart, and a reciprocity relation between forward and reverse stellar reactivities is established. The paper introduces a renormalization procedure for incorporating experimental data into stellar rates using X_0, and defines relative sensitivities s(Ω,q) to quantify how variations in nuclear properties (nucleon widths, alpha widths, gamma widths, level densities) affect cross sections and reactivities.

## Key numbers

- Ground state contributions X_0 for (n,γ) rates at kT = 30 keV show considerable excited state contributions (X_0 << 1) for a surprisingly large number of target nuclei near stability, with the smallest values in deformed nuclei
- At T = 2.5 GK (typical for γ-process), X_0 values for selected (γ,n) reactions include: ⁸⁶Sr = 0.00059, ⁹⁰Zr = 0.00034, ¹⁴²Nd = 0.0028, ¹⁸⁶W = 0.00049, ¹⁹⁰Pt = 0.000069, ¹⁹⁷Au = 0.00035, ²⁰⁴Pb = 0.0059
- The approximation replacing the Planck distribution denominator exp(E/(kT))-1 with exp(E/(kT)) introduces errors of less than a few percent for astrophysically relevant temperatures, with the largest error found between 50% and 100% for a few heavy nuclei at the driplines for proton or alpha capture at T < 0.3 GK
- For neutron captures, errors from this approximation were never larger than 10% at any investigated temperature, even at the driplines
- More than 1200 cases of Coulomb suppression were found in a large-scale study of about 60000 reactions, where excited state contributions are smaller than naively expected from the Q-value rule
- The renormalization factor for incorporating experimental data is f* = 1 + X_0(R_0^exp/R_0 - 1), and the new uncertainty factor is U*_new = U_exp + (U* - U_exp)(1 - X_0)

## Context

This work extends the foundational formalism of stellar reaction rates from Fowler (1975) and Holmes (1976) by quantifying the linear weighting of excited state contributions and identifying the Coulomb suppression effect (Rauscher 2011, 2012) that acts against the usual Q-value rule. The paper corrects the previous misuse of the stellar enhancement factor in compilations such as Bao and Kaeppeler (1987), Bao et al. (2000), and all KaDoNiS versions up to v0.3, superseding those stellar rates with the revised table in Rauscher (2012). The sensitivity analysis framework enables future studies to identify which nuclear properties most need improved theoretical description or experimental measurement, and the equilibrium considerations (NSE, QSE, steady flow) guide researchers on when individual reaction rates are or are not required for nucleosynthesis modeling.
