# Two-Body T-Matrices without Angular Momentum Decomposition: Energy and Momentum Dependencies

> The two-body t-matrix is calculated directly as function of two vector momenta for different Malfliet-Tjon type potentials. At a few hundred MeV projectile energy the total amplitude is quite a smooth function showing only a strong peak in forward direction. In contrast the corresponding partial wave contributions, whose number increases with increasing energy, become more and more oscillatory with increasing energy. The angular and momentum dependence of the full amplitude is studied and displayed on as well as off the energy shell as function of positive and negative energies. The behavior of the t-matrix in the vicinity of bound state poles and resonance poles in the second energy sheet is studied. It is found that the angular dependence of T exhibits a very characteristic behavior in the vicinity of those poles, which is given by the Legendre function corresponding to the quantum number either of the bound state or the resonance (or virtual) state. This behavior is illustrated with numerical examples.

# Two-Body T-Matrices without Angular Momentum Decomposition: Energy and Momentum Dependencies

## Key claim

The two-body t-matrix can be calculated directly as a function of two vector momenta without angular momentum decomposition, yielding a smooth total amplitude even at intermediate energies where partial wave contributions become highly oscillatory. The angular dependence of the t-matrix in the vicinity of bound state poles and resonance poles in the second energy sheet is given by the Legendre function corresponding to the quantum number of that state, a behavior the authors illustrate numerically but do not claim as a universal theorem.

## Method

The authors solve the two-body Lippmann-Schwinger equation directly in momentum space as a two-dimensional integral equation in the off-shell momenta and the cosine of the scattering angle, avoiding partial wave decomposition entirely. They use Malfliet-Tjon type potentials (a superposition of attractive and repulsive Yukawa interactions) and treat the Cauchy singularity by separating principal value and delta-function parts, with the principal value handled by subtraction. For the second energy sheet, the Lippmann-Schwinger equation is analytically continued by deforming the integration path, and the homogeneous eigenvalue problem is solved using the power method to locate resonance and virtual state poles.

## Key numbers

- At 300 MeV and 800 MeV laboratory energies, the on-shell amplitude shows a strong forward peak requiring high orders of Legendre polynomials for correct description in partial wave representation.
- For potential V(I), the bound state energy is E = -2.23 MeV.
- For potential V(II), s-wave bound state at E_s = -190.16 MeV and p-wave bound state at E_p = -14.629 MeV.
- With 40 q-points and 32 x-points, the pole positions from the two-dimensional equation agree with bound state energies: E_pol(l=0) = -190.164 MeV versus E_b(l=0) = -190.162 MeV; E_pol(l=1) = -14.6296 MeV versus E_b(l=1) = -14.6296 MeV.
- For the s-wave virtual state from potential V(V), the scattering length a_s = -23.5818 fm and effective range r_s = 2.8789 fm, with the virtual state pole at E_p(l=0) = -0.06663 MeV.
- For the p-wave resonance from potential V(IV), the pole is at E_res(l=1) = (4.3177 - i 2.2386) MeV.
- The off-shell unitarity relation was fulfilled with accuracy below 0.001% using 24 or 32 q-points and 24 x-points.

## Context

This work extends earlier investigations of NN scattering without angular momentum decomposition by Holz, Rice, Hiltrop, and Huber (no specific years given in text), and provides a systematic study of the full angular and momentum dependence of the t-matrix on and off the energy shell that the authors state is not available in the literature. The approach enables more economical treatment of three- and many-body scattering at intermediate energies using Faddeev-Yakubovsky equations, where two-body t-matrices at negative energies and off-shell momenta are required as dynamical input. The authors note that partial wave projected amplitudes are usually displayed and discussed in works such as Redish (no year given), whereas their direct vector-momentum method avoids the increasingly oscillatory partial wave contributions that appear at higher energies.
