# How to model BEC numrically?

> The new method of numerical modelling of Bose-Einstein correlations observed in all kinds of multiparticle production processes is proposed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new Monte Carlo method for numerically modeling Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) that directly generates particles according to Bose-Einstein statistics, rather than using an afterburner approach to enhance correlations in existing event generators. The method is presented as a first proof of principle, with the caveat that it is still essentially one-dimensional, includes only pions, and omits resonances and final-state interactions.

## Method

The method constructs a Monte Carlo event generator that selects pions from an energy pool \(W\) according to a thermal distribution \(f(E) = \exp(-E/T)\) and allocates them to elementary emitting cells (EECs) defined by their four-momentum. New particles of the same charge and energy are added to a cell with weights \(P = P_0 \exp(-E/T)\) until the first failure, which yields a geometrical distribution of particles per cell and a Bose-Einstein character for cell occupancy. The process repeats with new EECs until the initial energy \(W\) is exhausted, followed by corrections for energy-momentum and charge conservation; a Gaussian smearing of momenta within each EEC (width \(\sigma\)) is then applied to reproduce the observed correlation function \(C_2(Q)\).

## Key numbers

- The thermal distribution for selecting particles is \(f(E) = \exp(-E/T)\).
- The cell occupancy follows \(\langle n \rangle = P/(1-P) = 1/[\exp((E+\mu)/T - 1]\) with \(\mu = \ln P_0\).
- The multiplicity distribution resulting from the method is Negative Binomial (NB) in form.
- The fit to \(e^+e^-\) annihilation data (Fig. 2) uses a Gaussian smearing width \(\sigma\) to define the average EEC size, though no numerical value for \(\sigma\) is given in the text.

## Context

This work extends earlier attempts to model BEC numerically, which relied on afterburner methods that enhance pairs of identical bosons with small momentum differences in existing Monte Carlo event generators (Weiner, year not given). The approach is contrasted with the dominant space-time descriptions that symmetrize wave functions over an assumed source function (Weiner, year not given; KZ, year not given). The method enables future development of a full, multi-dimensional event generator that includes resonances, transverse momentum effects, and final-state interactions, which the authors state are necessary before serious description of experimental data across all reaction types.
