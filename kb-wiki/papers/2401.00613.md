# Extracting spectra in the shell model Monte Carlo method using imaginary-time correlation matrices

> Conventional diagonalization methods to calculate nuclear energy levels in the framework of the configuration-interaction (CI) shell model approach are prohibited in very large model spaces. The shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) is a powerful technique for calculating thermal and ground-state observables of nuclei in very large model spaces, but it is challenging to extract nuclear spectra in this approach. We present a novel method to extract low-lying energy levels for given values of a set of good quantum numbers such as spin and parity. The method is based on imaginary-time one-body density correlation matrices that satisfy asymptotically a generalized eigenvalue problem. We validate the method in a light nucleus that allows comparison with exact diagonalization results of the CI shell model Hamiltonian. The method is applicable to other finite-size quantum many-body systems that can be described within a CI shell model approach.

# Extracting spectra in the shell model Monte Carlo method using imaginary-time correlation matrices

## Key claim
The method can extract low-lying excitation energies from shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) calculations using imaginary-time correlation matrices that satisfy a generalized eigenvalue problem, but only when the imaginary-time separation is sufficiently large and the statistical noise is small enough that the weight matrix remains positive definite; the method is validated for a light nucleus (²⁰Ne) where exact diagonalization is still possible, and higher-lying states become inaccessible due to contamination from other states and statistical noise.

## Method
The authors compute imaginary-time correlation matrices (ITCM) of angular-momentum-coupled one-body densities for fixed spin and parity in the SMMC framework, using the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to represent the Gibbs operator as a path integral over auxiliary fields. These ITCM are shown to satisfy a generalized eigenvalue problem (GEVP) at zero temperature, where the generalized eigenvalues are exponentials of the excitation energies. The method is applied to ²⁰Ne in the sd-shell model space using an attractive quadrupole-quadrupole interaction, with SMMC calculations performed at inverse temperature β = 10 MeV⁻¹ and compared to exact results from the CI shell model code BIGSTICK.

## Key numbers
- SMMC calculations for ²⁰Ne were performed at β = 10 MeV⁻¹ with time-slice extrapolations from Δβ = 1/32 and 1/64 MeV⁻¹.
- The ITCM for K^π = 2⁺ had dimension 16 with two zero eigenvalues (within SMMC resolution) at τ₀ = 3/16 MeV⁻¹, leading to truncation to dimension 14.
- Extracted excitation energies for the first 2⁺ state: SMMC gives 1.12(1) MeV vs. exact 1.2 MeV.
- Extracted excitation energies for the second 2⁺ state: SMMC gives 5.25(1) MeV vs. exact 5.3 MeV.
- Extracted excitation energies for the third 2⁺ state: SMMC gives 5.43(2) MeV vs. exact 5.5 MeV.
- Extracted excitation energies for the first 0⁺ excited state: SMMC gives 7.22(3) MeV vs. exact 7.1 MeV.
- Extracted excitation energies for the first 4⁺ state: SMMC gives 7.09(3) MeV vs. exact 7.0 MeV.
- Extracted excitation energies for the first 1⁺ state: SMMC gives 5.61(1) MeV vs. exact 5.7 MeV.
- Extracted excitation energies for the first 3⁺ state: SMMC gives 5.84(2) MeV vs. exact 5.9 MeV.
- Only eight 2⁺ excitations were accessible in the SMMC calculation due to statistical noise and contamination from higher states.

## Context
This work extends the SMMC method, previously limited to thermal and ground-state observables (Alhassid 2001, Alhassid 2017), to extract spectroscopic information about individual excited levels. The use of a generalized eigenvalue problem for imaginary-time correlation matrices was inspired by similar techniques in lattice QCD for studying excited baryon states (Luscher 1990, Edwards 2013). The method enables CI shell model calculations of low-lying spectra in model spaces many orders of magnitude larger than those accessible to conventional diagonalization methods such as BIGSTICK (Johnson 2018), and is applicable to other finite-size quantum many-body systems described within a CI shell model approach.
