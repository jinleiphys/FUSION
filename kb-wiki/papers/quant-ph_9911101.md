# Quantum Coins, Dice and Children: Probability and Quantum Statistics

> We discuss counterintuitive aspects of probabilities for systems of identical particles obeying quantum statistics. Quantum coins and children (two level systems) and quantum dice (many level systems) are used as examples. It is emphasized that, even in the absence of interactions, (anti)symmetrizations of multi-particle wavefunctions destroy statistical independences and often lead to dramatic departures from our intuitive expectations.

## Key claim

Even in the absence of interactions, the (anti)symmetrization of multi-particle wavefunctions for identical particles destroys statistical independence, leading to counterintuitive probability results that differ dramatically from classical expectations; these effects are most striking in systems with a finite number of accessible quantum states.

## Method

The authors analyze probability problems for systems of identical particles obeying Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac statistics, using pedagogical analogies: quantum coins (two-level systems), quantum children (two-level systems for conditional probabilities), and quantum dice (many-level systems). They enumerate all distinct allowable multi-particle states under each statistics, assuming all such states are a priori equally likely, and compute unconditional and conditional probabilities both by direct listing and by density matrix formalism with annihilation operators.

## Key numbers

- For two quantum coins (two-level bosons), the probability of both being "heads" is 1/3, compared to 1/4 for distinguishable particles.
- For two quantum coins (two-level fermions), the probability of both being "heads" is 0.
- For two bosonic children, conditional probability that both are boys given at least one is a boy is 1/2 (versus 1/3 for distinguishable children).
- For two bosonic children, conditional probability that the other is a boy given one randomly selected child is a boy is 2/3 (versus 1/2 for distinguishable children).
- For n bosonic children (n large), the unconditional probability distribution of the fraction R of boys is uniform: f_0(R) = 1, with expectation value ⟨R⟩_0 = 1/2.
- For n bosonic children (n large), after one randomly selected child is found to be a boy, the conditional probability distribution of R for the remaining children is f_1(R) = 2R, with conditional expectation ⟨R⟩_1 = 2/3.
- For n bosonic dice with k states, after removing n' dice with counts N_1, N_2, ..., N_k, the conditional expectation of the fraction in state 1 is ⟨R⟩ = ν_1/(ν_1+ν_2+...+ν_k), where ν_j = N_j + 1.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of quantum coin tossing found in Dirac's "The Principles of Quantum Mechanics" and shares philosophy with discussions in Griffiths and Stowe, but uniquely focuses on conditional probabilities which give the clearest and most counterintuitive manifestations of differences between classical and quantum statistics. The authors note that these subtleties are rarely discussed in undergraduate textbooks because they require systems with a finite number of accessible levels, a condition rarely met in important physical systems. The results are potentially testable in Bose-Einstein condensates of F-spin-1 atoms, where the three spin states (F_z = 1, 0, -1) provide a natural realization of quantum dice with k=3.
