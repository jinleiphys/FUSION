# Deviation of the Nucleon Shape From Spherical Symmetry: Experimental Status

> In this brief pedagogical overview the physical basis of the deviation of the nucleon shape from spherical symmetry will be presented along with the experimental methods used to determine it by the gamma* p -> Delta reaction.The fact that significant non-spherical electric(E2) and Coulomb quadrupole(C2) amplitudes have been observed will be demonstrated. These multipoles for the N,Delta system as a function of Q^2 from the photon point through 4 GeV^2 have been measured with modest precision. Their precise magnitude remains model dependent due to the contributions of the background amplitudes, although rapid progress is being made to reduce these uncertainties. A discussion of what is required to perform a model independent analysis is presented. All of the data to date are consistent with a prolate shape for the proton (larger at the poles) and an oblate shape(flatter at the poles) for the Delta. It is suggested here that the fundamental reason for this lies in the spontaneous breaking of chiral symmetry in QCD and the resulting, long range(low Q^2), effects of the pion cloud. This verification of this suggestion, as well as a more accurate measurement of the deviation from spherical symmetry, requires further experimental and theoretical effort.

## Key claim

All data to date are consistent with a prolate shape for the proton and an oblate shape for the Delta, but the precise magnitude of the non-spherical electric (E2) and Coulomb quadrupole (C2) amplitudes remains model dependent due to background amplitude contributions, and the suggestion that the fundamental reason lies in spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking and pion cloud effects requires further experimental and theoretical work.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental determination of nucleon deformation via the gamma* p -> Delta reaction, using both photoproduction with polarized photon beams and electroproduction with out-of-plane spectrometer systems. Electroproduction experiments measure the five-fold differential cross section to extract the transverse (sigma_T), longitudinal (sigma_L), transverse-longitudinal interference (sigma_TL), transverse-transverse interference (sigma_TT), and the fifth structure function (sigma_TL') which are then analyzed with reaction models (MAID, Sato-Lee, dispersion relations, SAID) to extract the resonant M1, E2, and C2 multipole amplitudes. The Bates out-of-plane spectrometer system (OOPS) simultaneously measures sigma_0, sigma_TT, and sigma_TL by deploying multiple spectrometers at different azimuthal angles phi.

## Key numbers

- E2/M1 ratio at the photon point: -2.5 +/- 0.5% (from Mainz and Brookhaven polarized photon experiments)
- Experimental M1 matrix element for gamma N -> Delta: 288 +/- 8 (units 10^{-3} GeV^{-1/2})
- Experimental E2 matrix element for gamma N -> Delta: -7.2 +/- 0.5 (units 10^{-3} GeV^{-1/2})
- Quark model (Capstick) M1 prediction: 196 (units 10^{-3} GeV^{-1/2}), E2 prediction: -0.1 (units 10^{-3} GeV^{-1/2})
- Sato-Lee dressed model M1: 258 (units 10^{-3} GeV^{-1/2}), E2: -4.6 +/- 2.3 (units 10^{-3} GeV^{-1/2})
- Bates electroproduction data taken at Q^2 = 0.127 GeV^2 over a range of CM energies W including the Delta resonance at W = 1232 MeV
- EMR and CMR ratios measured as a function of Q^2 from the photon point through 4 GeV^2
- Two lattice QCD calculations at Q^2 = 0 and 0.52 GeV^2 show non-zero quadrupole transition amplitudes

## Context

This pedagogical review extends the line of work on nucleon deformation originally raised by Glashow and developed in quark models by Isgur and Capstick, which predicted E2 amplitudes an order of magnitude too small. The paper contrasts quark model predictions with the dynamical Sato-Lee meson exchange model (1996) that includes pion cloud effects, and with chiral perturbation theory calculations by Hemmert that incorporate the Delta as a dynamical degree of freedom. The Bates OOPS data at low Q^2 (0.127 GeV^2) disagree with the Sato-Lee model predictions, while JLab CLAS data at higher Q^2 (0.4 to 1.8 GeV^2) show better agreement, indicating the need for improved treatment of pion cloud dynamics. The work enables future model-independent multipole analyses and planned experiments at Mainz to probe the low Q^2 region where pion cloud effects are predicted to be largest.
