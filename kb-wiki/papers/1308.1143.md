# Pseudospin symmetry in supersymmetric quantum mechanics: II. Spin-orbit effects

> Following a previous paper [Haozhao Liang \textit{et al.}, Phys. Rev. C \textbf{87}, 014334 (2013)], we discuss the spin-orbit effects on the pseudospin symmetry (PSS) within the framework of supersymmetric quantum mechanics. By using the perturbation theory, we demonstrate that the perturbative nature of PSS maintains when a substantial spin-orbit potential is included. With the explicit PSS-breaking potential, the spin-orbit effects on the pseudospin-orbit splittings are investigated in a quantitative way.

## Key claim

The spin-orbit term does not change the perturbative nature of pseudospin symmetry; the pseudospin-orbit splittings can still be excellently reproduced by first-order perturbation theory even when a substantial spin-orbit potential is included.

## Method

The authors combined supersymmetric quantum mechanics with perturbation theory to analyze the spin-orbit effects on pseudospin symmetry. They solved the first-order differential equations for reduced superpotentials \(q_\kappa(r)\) using a Woods-Saxon central potential and a corresponding spin-orbit potential, then constructed the SUSY partner Hamiltonian and decomposed it into pseudospin-conserving and pseudospin-breaking parts. Rayleigh-Schrodinger perturbation calculations were performed based on the pseudospin-conserving Hamiltonian with the pseudospin-breaking perturbation.

## Key numbers

* The spin-orbit splittings for \(3p\) and \(2f\) spin doublets obtained from the model were 0.613 MeV and 1.723 MeV, respectively, compared to experimental data of 0.509 MeV and 2.005 MeV.
* The pseudospin-orbit splitting of the \(1\tilde{f}\) doublet was 2.381 MeV when the spin-orbit term was included.
* The pseudospin-orbit splittings of \(2\tilde{p}\) and \(1\tilde{f}\) doublets were reduced from 1.552 MeV and 4.364 MeV (without spin-orbit) to 0.840 MeV and 2.381 MeV (with spin-orbit), respectively.
* The spin-orbit term reduces the reduced pseudospin-orbit splittings systematically by 0.15 to 0.3 MeV across all bound pseudospin doublets.

## Context

This work extends the previous investigation by Liang et al. (2013) which combined similarity renormalization group technique, supersymmetric quantum mechanics, and perturbation theory to study pseudospin symmetry without the spin-orbit term. The present paper adds the spin-orbit term, which is a crucial feature of nuclear shell structure first identified by Haxel (1949) and Goeppert-Mayer (1949). The quantitative framework developed here enables future studies of the Darwin term and effective mass effects, and helps explain why pseudospin symmetry is better conserved than spin symmetry in realistic nuclei.
