# What Can We Learn from Reaction Zone in Relativistic Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions?

> We apply the ``zone of reactions'' as a tool in studying the interacting system formed in a collision of relativistic nuclei. With the use of the intensity of collisions of particles (the number of collisions in unit volume per unit time), we study the space-time structure of a fireball. In this approach, three basic regions for the evolution of a system are separated by the scale of the intensity of collisions: the zone of a hot fireball, the zone of a cold fireball, and the zone of residual interaction. It is shown that the conception of a zone of reactions can be used for the determination of the hypersurfaces of a chemical freeze-out and a sharp kinetic freeze-out.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the space-time region where 99% of all hadronic collisions occur (the combined "hot" and "cold" fireball zones) can be identified with the sharp kinetic freeze-out hypersurface, and the boundary of the "hot" fireball zone (containing all inelastic reactions) can be associated with the chemical freeze-out hypersurface, but the specific percentages (90%, 9%, 0.9%) used to define these zones are acknowledged as conventional and replaceable.

## Method

The authors use the UrQMD v2.3 transport model to calculate the collision intensity (number of two-particle collisions and resonance decays per unit four-volume) at each space-time point for central Au+Au collisions at 10.8A GeV (AGS) and central Pb+Pb collisions at 158A GeV (SPS). They divide a 4-cube of reactions (200 fm on each edge) into equal four-volume pixels, sort the pixels by the number of reactions they contain, and then sum pixels from highest to lowest reaction count to define zones containing 90%, an additional 9%, and a further 0.9% of all hadronic reactions, which they label as the "hot fireball," "cold fireball," and "fireball halo," respectively.

## Key numbers

*   For AGS (Au+Au at 10.8A GeV), the hot fireball zone decays completely within approximately 16 fm/c; the cold fireball zone lives for 32-34 fm/c.
*   For SPS (Pb+Pb at 158A GeV), the hot fireball zone decays completely within approximately 50 fm/c; the cold fireball zone lives for 90-100 fm/c.
*   The hot fireball zone contains 98% of all inelastic reactions for AGS and 99% for SPS.
*   The upper boundary of the combined hot and cold fireball zones (the proposed sharp kinetic freeze-out hypersurface) is a space-like hyperbola of the form \(t = A\sqrt{\tau^2 + z^2}\), with \(A = 0.75\) and \(\tau = 64\) fm/c for AGS, and \(A = 0.95\) and \(\tau = 38\) fm/c for SPS.
*   The lower time-like boundary of the cold fireball zone is a straight line \(t = t_0 + (1/v)z\) with \(v = 0.8\) for AGS and \(v = 0.98\) for SPS.
*   The maximum intervals of coordinate longitudinal rapidity \(\Delta\eta\) for the reaction zones are \(\Delta y = 3.2\) for AGS and \(\Delta y = 5.8\) for SPS.
*   The size of the hot fireball zone near \(\eta = 0\) along the proper-time axis is \(\Delta\tau_{\rm hot} = 17\) fm/c for AGS and \(\Delta\tau_{\rm hot} = 19\) fm/c for SPS.

## Context

This work extends the use of collision intensity as a tool to define the space-time structure of the fireball, building on prior approaches that used energy density (Russkikh and Ivanov, 2006; Sollfrank et al., 1998), temperature (McLerran, 1986; Huovinen, 2007), or particle density (CERES collaboration) as the critical parameter for defining freeze-out hypersurfaces. The method enables a direct, model-based identification of chemical and kinetic freeze-out hypersurfaces from transport model output, which can be compared with the hypersurfaces assumed in hydrodynamic models (Stoecker and Greiner, 1986; Rischke, 1998; Huovinen, 2006; Romatschke, 2009) and kinetic models (Molnar and Gyulassy, 2000; Kisiel and Florkowski, 2006; Magas and Csernai, 2008).
