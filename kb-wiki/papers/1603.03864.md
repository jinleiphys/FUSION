# The Cosmological Lithium Problem Revisited

> After a brief review of the cosmological lithium problem, we report a few recent attempts to find theoretical solutions by our group at Texas A&M University (Commerce & College Station). We will discuss our studies on the theoretical description of electron screening, the possible existence of parallel universes of dark matter, and the use of non-extensive statistics during the Big Bang nucleosynthesis epoch. Last but not least, we discuss possible solutions within nuclear physics realm. The impact of recent measurements of relevant nuclear reaction cross sections for the Big Bang nucleosynthesis based on indirect methods is also assessed. Although our attempts may not able to explain the observed discrepancies between theory and observations, they suggest theoretical developments that can be useful also for stellar nucleosynthesis.

# The Cosmological Lithium Problem Revisited

## Key claim

The authors report that none of their group's proposed theoretical solutions (electron screening modifications, non-extensive statistics, parallel dark matter universes, or nuclear cross section uncertainties) can fully resolve the discrepancy between Big Bang nucleosynthesis predictions and observed lithium abundances, although improved nuclear reaction data may reduce the tension for the isotopic ratio problem.

## Method

The group performed BBN abundance calculations using standard codes modified to include: (1) an artificial scaling factor w applied to the Debye-Huckel electron screening enhancement formula; (2) Tsallis non-extensive statistics with parameter q replacing the Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution for reaction rates; (3) a parallel-universe dark matter model with N_Q = 6 sectors (one visible, five dark) coupled by a hypothetical Weakly Interacting Massive Gauge Boson; and (4) R-matrix fits to recent direct and indirect nuclear reaction measurements including Trojan Horse Method data.

## Key numbers

- Observed primordial 7Li/H = 1.58 (+0.35, -0.28) x 10^-10 from metal-poor halo stars (Sbo10), compared to BBN prediction of 4.46 x 10^-10 (Piz14)
- BBN calculation using best fits to recent experimental data yields 7Li/H = 4.683 (+0.335, -0.292) x 10^-10, still discrepant with observation 1.58 +/- 0.31 x 10^-10
- For non-extensive statistics, chi-square fit constrains q = 1.00 (+0.05, -0.02); q=0.5 gives 7Li/H = 6.89 x 10^-10 and q=2 gives 356 x 10^-10, both worsening the discrepancy
- Electron screening enhancement factor in BBN is f = exp(4.49 x 10^-8 zeta Z1 Z2) for T9 less than about 1, too small to affect abundances even with scaling factor w up to 10^4
- For the parallel universe model with N_DM = 5 dark sectors, the temperature ratio constraint is T'/T < 0.78 / N^(1/4)_DM = 0.52, and the dark sector baryon-to-photon ratio is eta'_b about 7 eta_b
- Re-analysis of 4He(alpha,gamma)6Li yields 6Li/7Li = (1.5 +/- 0.3) x 10^-5, compared to BBN prediction of about 10^-5 and some observations of about 5 x 10^-2
- BBN calculation yields D/H = 2.692 (+0.177, -0.070) x 10^-5, consistent with observed mean 2.82 +/- 0.26 x 10^-5
- BBN calculation yields 4He mass fraction Y_p = 0.2485 (+0.001, -0.002), compared to observed 0.2565 +/- 0.006

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Wan11, BFH13, Oli11, BFF12) on alternative BBN scenarios and responds to the persistent lithium problem first identified by Spite and Spite (1982). The paper reviews attempts to modify standard BBN through electron screening (Debye-Huckel approximation challenged by Shaviv (1996, 2000) and others), non-extensive statistics (Tsallis 1988), and parallel dark matter sectors (Berezhiani 1996). The nuclear physics approach builds on R-matrix fits by Piz14 and recent Trojan Horse Method measurements, and the re-analysis of 6Li production by MSB16 finds agreement with LUNA collaboration data. The work enables future investigations into whether the lithium problem requires new physics or will be resolved by improved nuclear reaction data and stellar modeling of lithium depletion in metal-poor stars.
