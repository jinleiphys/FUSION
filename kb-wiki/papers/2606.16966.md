# Spin-Selective Hadron Spectroscopy via Azimuthal Anisotropies from Entanglement-Enabled Spin Interference

> The $π^+π^-$ invariant mass spectrum above the $ρ^0(770)$ is rich with broad, overlapping resonances. Disentangling them, whether in photoproduction, ultra-peripheral heavy-ion collisions, or electroproduction, is a longstanding challenge for conventional partial-wave analysis. We show that the recently observed entanglement-enabled spin-interference effect in ultra-peripheral collisions provides a quantum-mechanical filter that resolves this ambiguity: the angular harmonics $A_n$ of the $\cos(nΔϕ)$ asymmetry, which are governed by selection rules in the spin of the interfering states. Specifically, overlap between two distinct spin-1 amplitudes leads to interference that populate $A_2$ alone, while overlap of a spin-1 amplitude with a spin-2 one generates $A_1$ and $A_3$. Utilizing ALICE data in the $1.0$--$1.4\,\mathrm{GeV} \; c^{-2}$ region, we demonstrate that two physically distinct hypotheses -- an additional spin-1 $ρ'(1450)$ (produced via photonuclear interactions) versus a spin-2 (photon-photon) $f_2(1270)$ state -- fit the invariant mass spectrum equally well but predict different $A_n$: identically zero $A_1$ and $A_3$ in the spin-1 case, versus pronounced peaks in the spin-2 case. This selection rule provides a new tool for hadronic spectroscopy in ultra-peripheral collisions and the first viable route to isolating the $γγ\toπ^+π^-$ continuum from the dominant photonuclear background, revealing a clean low-energy probe of non-perturbative QCD.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the entanglement-enabled spin-interference (EESI) effect in ultra-peripheral collisions provides a quantum-mechanical filter that resolves the longstanding ambiguity between overlapping spin-1 and spin-2 resonances in the dipion invariant mass spectrum above 1 GeV/c², specifically distinguishing the photonuclear spin-1 ρ'(1450) hypothesis from the photon-photon spin-2 f₂(1270) hypothesis. The key hedge is that this distinction works only because the two hypotheses fit the invariant mass spectrum equally well, but produce qualitatively different azimuthal anisotropy harmonics A₁ and A₃.

## Method

The authors fit the π⁺π⁻ invariant mass spectrum from ALICE PbPb ultra-peripheral collisions at √sNN = 5.02 TeV with two competing models: model A includes ρ⁰(770), ω(782), a spin-1 ρ'(1450) resonance, and a photonuclear continuum; model B replaces the spin-1 resonance with a spin-2 f₂(1270) and adds a γγ continuum term. Using the factorization of the cross section into resonant Breit-Wigner structure and angular dependence, they extract the resonance parameters from the mass spectrum and then compute predictions for the azimuthal anisotropy harmonics A₁, A₂, and A₃ using theoretical values for the angular coefficients aₙ obtained from existing STAR and ALICE measurements (for a₂) and from theoretical calculations by Hagiwara et al. (for a₁ and a₃).

## Key numbers

- The analysis uses ALICE data in the invariant mass region 1.0–1.4 GeV/c² from PbPb UPCs at √sNN = 5.02 TeV
- The angular coefficients extracted are: a₁ = 0.148, a₂ = 0.228, and a₃ = 0.022, averaged over the pair transverse momentum range 0.0–0.1 GeV/c
- The predicted peak amplitude for model B (spin-2 f₂(1270)) is A₁ ∼ 3A₃ ∼ a few percent at the resonance mass
- Event yields are of order 10⁷ exclusive dipions per experiment, sufficient for high-significance detection of non-zero odd harmonics
- The ω(782) contribution to A₂ is currently uncertain at the level of ∼10%

## Context

This work extends the recent observation of entanglement-enabled spin interference (EESI) by the STAR and ALICE collaborations (STAR 2022, ALICE 2024) from a qualitative effect into a quantitative spectroscopic tool. It addresses a longstanding problem in hadron spectroscopy where standard partial-wave analysis cannot cleanly disentangle the spin-2 f₂(1270) from the spin-1 ρ'(1450) due to overlapping broad spin-1 states, as noted by Hammoud (2020). The technique enables the first viable route to isolating the γγ→π⁺π⁻ continuum from the dominant photonuclear background, providing direct empirical input to dispersive analyses and chiral perturbation theory, and is applicable to future measurements at RHIC, the LHC, and the EIC.
