# Mass and Flavor Mixing Schemes of Quarks and Leptons

> We give an overview of recent progress in the study of fermion mass and flavor mixing phenomena. The hints exhibited by the quark and lepton mass spectra towards possible underlying flavor symmetries, from which realistic models of mass generation could be built, are emphasized. A variety of schemes of quark mass matrices at low and superhigh energy scales are described, and their consequences on flavor mixing and CP violation are discussed. Instructive patterns of lepton mass matrices, which can naturally lead to large flavor mixing angles, are explored to interpret current data on atmospheric and solar neutrino oscillations. We expect that B-meson factories and long-baseline neutrino experiments will soon shed more light on the dynamics of fermion masses, flavor mixing and CP violation.

## Key claim

This review argues that phenomenological approaches to fermion mass matrices, particularly those based on texture zeros and flavor symmetries, remain crucial for interpreting experimental data on quark mixing, CP violation, and neutrino oscillations, even though no fundamental theory of fermion masses yet exists. The authors emphasize that many theoretical models merely transfer the mass hierarchy problem from one place to another without producing testable predictions.

## Method

The paper surveys and classifies various schemes of quark and lepton mass matrices, including Hermitian textures with four or five texture zeros, flavor democracy patterns, non-Hermitian textures (nearest-neighbor mixing, triangular, and pure phase patterns), and seesaw-invariant lepton mass matrices. For each scheme, the authors derive consequences for flavor mixing angles and CP-violating parameters, and examine the running of these parameters from superhigh energy scales to the weak scale using the minimal supersymmetric standard model framework.

## Context

This review extends the authors' previous work on flavor symmetries and mass matrix textures, particularly their studies of democratic mass matrices and nearly degenerate neutrino masses (Fritzsch and Xing, 1996, 1998). It surveys the landscape of quark and lepton mass models developed since the 1970s, including the texture zero approach pioneered by Fritzsch (1977, 1978), family symmetry models by Harari (1978) and Froggatt and Nielsen (1979), and the seesaw mechanism. The paper enables future work by providing a comprehensive framework for interpreting data from B-meson factories and long-baseline neutrino experiments that were expected to constrain these models.
