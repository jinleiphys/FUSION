# Romanovski polynomials in selected physics problems

> We briefly review the five possible real polynomial solutions of hypergeometric differential equations. Three of them are the well known classical orthogonal polynomials, but the other two are different with respect to their orthogonality properties. We then focus on the family of polynomials which exhibits a finite orthogonality. This family, to be referred to as the Romanovski polynomials, is required in exact solutions of several physics problems ranging from quantum mechanics and quark physics to random matrix theory. It appears timely to draw attention to it by the present study. Our survey also includes several new observations on the orthogonality properties of the Romanovski polynomials and new developments from their Rodrigues formula.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Romanovski polynomials, which arise as polynomial solutions of hypergeometric differential equations with complex-conjugate roots in the coefficient \(s(x)\), deserve wider recognition alongside the classical orthogonal polynomials; however, their orthogonality is only finite, meaning that only a limited subset of polynomials within a given family satisfy orthogonality relations, and only those with degree \(n < -\beta\) are normalizable.

## Method

The authors classify all five real polynomial solutions of hypergeometric differential equations according to the roots of the polynomial \(s(x)\) (constant, first-degree, second-degree with two distinct real roots, one double real root, or two complex-conjugate roots). They then focus on the fifth class, defining Romanovski polynomials \(R_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)\) via the Rodrigues formula with weight function \(w^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x) = (1+x^2)^{\beta-1} e^{-\alpha \cot^{-1} x}\) and \(s(x)=1+x^2\). They derive properties including a differential recursion relation, a generating function in closed form, and the finite orthogonality condition, and they introduce an alternative family \(\mathcal{Q}^{(\alpha,\beta)}\) defined by derivatives of the weight function.

## Key numbers

* The orthogonality condition for Romanovski polynomials within a family \(\mathcal{R}^{(\alpha,\beta)}\) holds if and only if \(m+n < 1-2\beta\) (where \(m\) and \(n\) are the polynomial degrees).
* Polynomials \(R_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)\) are normalizable only when \(n < -\beta\).
* The eigenvalue \(\lambda_n\) for the Romanovski differential equation is \(\lambda_n = -n(2\beta + n - 1)\).
* The coefficient \(a_n\) of the highest-degree term is given by \(a_n = \frac{1}{n!} \prod_{k=0}^{n-1} [2\beta (n-k) + n(n-1) - k(k-1)]\) for \(n \geq 1\), and does not depend on \(\alpha\).
* The first three Romanovski polynomials are: \(R_0^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)=1\), \(R_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)=2\beta x + \alpha\), and \(R_2^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x) = (2\beta+1)(2\beta+2)x^2 + 2(2\beta+1)\alpha x + (2\beta + \alpha^2 + 2)\).

## Context

This work extends the classification of hypergeometric polynomial solutions by Nikiforov and Uvarov (1988) and Lesky (1962), placing Romanovski polynomials on equal footing with the classical Hermite, Laguerre, and Jacobi families. The paper clarifies that Romanovski polynomials are distinct from a mere complexification of Jacobi polynomials, and it provides new results on their finite orthogonality and Rodrigues formula. These polynomials have been used in exact solutions of the Schrodinger equation with hyperbolic Scarf and trigonometric Rosen-Morse potentials (Alvarez-Castillo 2006, Compean 2006), the Klein-Gordon equation with equal vector and scalar potentials (Wen-Chao), and non-central potential problems (Dutt), and the present survey enables their further application in quantum mechanics, quark physics, and random matrix theory.
