# Application of the Pauli principle in many-body scattering

> A new development in the antisymmetrization of the first-order nucleon-nucleus elastic microscopic optical potential is presented which systematically includes the many-body character of the nucleus within the two-body scattering operators. The results reduce the overall strength of the nucleon-nucleus potential and require the inclusion of historically excluded channels from the nucleon-nucleon potential input. Calculations produced improve the match with neutron-nucleus total cross section, elastic proton-nucleus differential cross section, and spin observable data. A comparison is also done using different nucleon-nucleon potentials from the past twenty years.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard Watson approximation, which only antisymmetrizes the two active nucleons in nucleon-nucleus scattering, is insufficient; instead, the many-body character of the nucleus requires introducing a new quantum number that distinguishes the projectile (which does not interact with the spectator core) from target nucleons (which do), thereby reducing the overall strength of the optical potential and requiring historically excluded nucleon-nucleon channels to be included.

## Method

The author develops a modified antisymmetrization scheme for the first-order microscopic optical potential by introducing an additional SU(2) quantum number "a" that labels whether a nucleon interacts with the spectator core (m_a = -1/2 for target nucleons) or not (m_a = +1/2 for the projectile). This many-body basis is applied within both the folding t-operator approach and the folding g-operator approach, and the resulting optical potentials are compared with experimental data for neutron-nucleus total cross sections, elastic proton-nucleus differential cross sections, and spin observables using different nucleon-nucleon potentials from the past twenty years.

## Key numbers

- For an N=Z nucleus, the average target nucleon is "half proton and half neutron," which motivates halving the traditional proton-proton or neutron-neutron two-body amplitudes when summing the many-body optical potential
- The factor of 0.5 appears in the new quantum number formalism because the new quantum number "a" divides the original space into symmetric (a=1) and antisymmetric (a=0) parts
- The medium effects from the nuclear environment are shown to be important notably below 200 MeV laboratory energy for the incoming nucleon

## Context

This work extends the pioneering antisymmetrization work of Watson and collaborators (1950s-1960s) and the more rigorous many-body scattering theories of Kowalski and collaborators (late 1970s-early 1980s), which were mathematically sophisticated but had limited practical application. The paper also builds upon the folding t-operator approach of Bolle and collaborators and the folding g-operator approach originating from Brueckner, providing a simple modification that is practical for all microscopic optical potential calculations. The new quantum number formalism enables improved agreement with experimental data while maintaining tractability for calculations on medium-mass and heavy nuclei, unlike the exact Faddeev methods that were limited to the lightest nuclei.
