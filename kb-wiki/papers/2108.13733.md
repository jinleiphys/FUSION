# Structure of ultra-magnetised neutron stars

> In this review we discuss self-consistent methods to calculate the global structure of strongly magnetised neutron stars within the general-relativistic framework. We outline why solutions in spherical symmetry cannot be applied to strongly magnetised compact stars, and elaborate on a consistent formalism to compute rotating magnetised neutron star models. We also discuss an application of the above full numerical solution for studying the influence of strong magnetic fields on the radius and crust thickness of magnetars. The above technique is also applied to construct a "universal" magnetic field profile inside the neutron star, that may be useful for studies in nuclear physics. The methodology developed here is particularly useful to interpret multi-messenger astrophysical data of strongly magnetised neutron stars.

## Key claim

This review argues that self-consistent general-relativistic models of strongly magnetised neutron stars must solve the coupled Einstein-Maxwell equations with a magnetic-field-dependent equation of state, because spherical Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations cannot correctly describe magnetar structure when magnetic fields are strong enough to break spherical symmetry. The authors caution that below approximately 10^18 G the magnetic field has almost no influence on the equation of state, so the most dramatic structural effects arise from the anisotropy of the energy-momentum tensor rather than from changes to the microphysics.

## Method

The authors review the formalism for computing stationary, axisymmetric neutron star models in general relativity, starting from the energy-momentum tensor that includes contributions from a perfect fluid, magnetisation, and the electromagnetic field. They describe how the Einstein-Maxwell equations are solved numerically for a purely poloidal magnetic field configuration, using the assumption of circular spacetime and rigid rotation. The equilibrium condition is obtained from the vanishing divergence of the total energy-momentum tensor, yielding a first integral that balances pressure gradients, gravitational forces, and the Lorentz force from free currents.

## Key numbers

- Surface magnetic fields of magnetars are estimated at 10^15–10^16 G from pulsar period and period-derivative measurements assuming a dipole model
- The Virial Theorem gives a maximum interior magnetic field strength of approximately 10^18 G
- The critical magnetic field for electrons is b^e_c = 4.4 × 10^13 G
- For crust magnetic fields above roughly 1300 b^e_c (approximately 5.7 × 10^16 G), the field becomes strongly quantising for electrons
- At b = 10^18 G the hadronic equation of state is still almost indistinguishable from the unmagnetised case, while at b = 10^19 G the impact becomes non-negligible
- For quark matter, the effect of magnetic fields starts to become evident only above 10^18 G, with de Haas-van Alphen oscillations clearly visible at b = 10^19 G

## Context

This review extends earlier work on magnetised neutron star structure by Bocquet et al. (1995), who first solved the coupled Einstein-Maxwell equations for rotating models, and by subsequent groups such as Cardall (2001), Kiuchi et al. (2008), and Bucciantini et al. (2011) who used perfect-fluid or polytropic equations of state without magnetic field modifications to the microphysics. The authors contrast their consistent approach with perturbative methods by Konno et al. (1999) and with attempts to apply spherical TOV equations by authors such as Strickland et al. (2012), Ferrer et al. (2010), and Dexheimer et al. (2014), which they argue are invalid for strongly magnetised stars. The methodology enables construction of a "universal" magnetic field profile inside neutron stars that can be used for nuclear physics studies and for interpreting multi-messenger astrophysical data from magnetars.
