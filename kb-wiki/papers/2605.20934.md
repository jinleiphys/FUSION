# Superfluid fraction in the crystalline crust of a neutron star: role of quantum zero-point motion of ions

> The suppression of the neutron superfluid fraction in the inner crust of a cold neutron star is mitigated by the quantum zero-point motion of ions about their equilibrium position. In turn, the crustal dynamics is altered by the presence of the neutron superfluid. These effects are studied self-consistently to assess the validity of the usual assumption of a perfect rigid lattice. To this end, fully three-dimensional band-structure calculations of the superfluid fraction are carried out in the weak-coupling approximation, considering body- and face-centered cubic lattices. In both cases, the superfluid fraction is still found to be strongly suppressed in the intermediate region of the inner crust. In turn, the effective mass of the ions is dramatically increased, thus further damping the ion fluctuations. These results are of relevance for the rotational and thermal evolutions of neutron stars.

# Superfluid fraction in the crystalline crust of a neutron star: role of quantum zero-point motion of ions

## Key claim
The quantum zero-point motion of ions about their equilibrium positions slightly mitigates the suppression of the neutron superfluid fraction in the inner crust of a neutron star, but the effect is very small (within numerical errors) and the rigid-lattice approximation remains valid for the intermediate crust region at average baryon density 0.03 fm⁻³, because the superfluid itself dramatically increases the effective ion mass and thereby damps the ion fluctuations.

## Method
Fully three-dimensional band-structure calculations of the neutron superfluid fraction were performed in the weak-coupling approximation using Skyrme-Hartree-Fock equations with Bloch wave functions, incorporating the Debye-Waller factor to account for quantum zero-point motion of ions. The calculations were carried out self-consistently for both body-centered cubic (bcc) and face-centered cubic (fcc) lattices at an average baryon density of 0.03 fm⁻³, iterating between the superfluid fraction, the effective ion mass, and the mean-square ion displacement until convergence to three significant digits.

## Key numbers
- Neutron superfluid fraction for bcc lattice without Debye-Waller factor: ρ_{n,s}/ρ_{n,f} = 0.079136; with self-consistent Debye-Waller factor: ρ_{n,s}/ρ_{n,f} = 0.079521 (converged value)
- Neutron superfluid fraction for fcc lattice without Debye-Waller factor: ρ_{n,s}/ρ_{n,f} = 0.068406; with self-consistent Debye-Waller factor: ρ_{n,s}/ρ_{n,f} = 0.069490 (converged value)
- Effective ion mass ratio for bcc lattice: m*_I/m_I = 8.1867 (converged, with Debye-Waller); for fcc lattice: m*_I/m_I = 8.2650 (converged, with Debye-Waller)
- Root mean square ion displacement relative to lattice spacing for bcc: 0.020607 (converged); for fcc: 0.016317 (converged)
- Ion plasma temperature (initial, bare mass): T_I ≈ 4.13 × 10⁹ K; neutron superfluid critical temperature: T_cn ≈ 1.05 × 10¹⁰ K (from BCS relation with pairing gap Δ = 1.59 MeV)
- Wigner-Seitz cell radius: R_cell = 23.30 fm; total nucleons per cell: A_cell = 1590; protons per cell: Z = 40; bound nucleons per ion: A ≈ 181; free neutron density: n_{n,f} ≈ 2.66 × 10⁻² fm⁻³

## Context
This work extends previous band-structure calculations of the neutron superfluid fraction in neutron star crusts (Chamel 2004, 2005; Chamel and Carter 2005; Kashiwaba and Sekizawa 2019; Sekizawa 2022; Minami 2022; Almirante 2024; Chamel 2025) by relaxing the assumption of a perfectly rigid lattice. Kobyakov and Pethick (2013) had speculated that ion fluctuations could affect superfluid dynamics, but no detailed calculations were performed. The results show that the rigid-lattice approximation is very good for the intermediate crust, but the author notes that deeper regions (the nuclear pasta mantle) may require further investigation, and the findings challenge standard interpretations of pulsar glitches (Andersson 2012; Chamel 2013; Delsate 2016) and may affect neutron star cooling models (Chamel and Page 2013).
