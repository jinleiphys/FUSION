# Verification of the Surrogate Ratio Method

> Effects of difference in the spin and parity distributions for the surrogate and neutron-induced reactions are investigated. Without assuming specific (schematic) spin-parity distributions, it was found that the surrogate ratio method can be employed to determine neutron fission and capture cross sections if 1) weak Weisskopf-Ewing condition (defined in this paper) is satisfied, 2) there exist two surrogate reactions whose spin-parity distributions of the decaying nuclei are almost equivalent, and 3) difference of the representative spin values between the neutron-induced and surrogate reactions is no much larger than 10 $\hbar$. If these conditions are satisfied, we need not to know the spin-parity distributions populated by the surrogate method. Instead, we should just select a pair of surrogate reactions which will populate the similar spin-parity distributions, using targets having similar structure and reactions having the similar reaction mechanisms. Achievable accuracy is estimated to be around 5 and 10 % for fission and capture channels, respectively, for nuclei of the Uranium region. The surrogate absolute method, on the contrary, can be marginally applicable to determination of fission cross sections. However, there will be little hope to apply this method for capture cross section measurements unless the spin-parity distributions in the neutron-induced and surrogate reactions are fairly close to each other or the difference can be corrected theoretically. The surrogate ratio method was shown also to be a robust method in the presence of breakup reactions, again, without assuming specific breakup reaction mechanisms.

## Key claim

The surrogate ratio method can determine neutron fission and capture cross sections with accuracies of approximately 5% and 10% respectively for nuclei in the Uranium region, provided three conditions are met: the weak Weisskopf-Ewing condition (defined in the paper) is satisfied, two surrogate reactions have nearly equivalent spin-parity distributions, and the difference in representative spin values between neutron-induced and surrogate reactions does not exceed 10 ℏ. The surrogate absolute method, by contrast, is only marginally applicable for fission and hopeless for capture unless spin-parity distributions are very close or theoretically correctable.

## Method

The authors used the Hauser-Feshbach theory implemented in the CCONE code system to calculate decay branching ratios to fission and capture channels for various spin-parity states of 239U, representing the compound nucleus produced by n+238U reactions and corresponding surrogate reactions such as 237U(t,p)239U. They compared the ratios of branching ratios between pairs of nuclei (239U and 237U, and 197Au and 193Ir) for different spin-parity values to test convergence to the neutron-induced reaction ratios. The calculations used parameter values from the JENDL Actinoid File 2008 for discrete level structures, transmission coefficients, level density, fission barrier, and giant dipole resonance parameters.

## Key numbers

- Fission branching ratios vary by about 15% at 5 MeV and about 50% at 1.5 MeV depending on spin-parity state for 239U
- Capture branching ratios scatter by a factor of about 10 at 5 MeV, with much larger variation at lower energies
- Ratios of fission probabilities between 239U and 237U converge to the neutron fission ratio with deviation of only about 3% at 5 MeV
- Ratios of capture branching ratios between the two nuclei scatter by about ±5% at 5 MeV around the neutron capture ratio
- At energies from 2.5 to 4 MeV, surrogate capture ratios deviate from the neutron ratio by only 10%
- Achievable accuracy estimated at 3-5% for fission and 10% for capture cross sections for Uranium region nuclei at 2.5 to 5 MeV
- The condition requires difference of representative spin values between neutron-induced and surrogate reactions no larger than 10 ℏ

## Context

This work extends the surrogate method literature by providing a formal verification of the ratio method without assuming schematic spin-parity distributions, unlike prior approaches that relied on the Weisskopf-Ewing condition (Weisskopf and Ewing) or arbitrary spin-parity assumptions. The paper defines the "weak Weisskopf-Ewing condition" as the convergence of branching ratio ratios between two nuclei to the neutron reaction ratio on a spin-by-spin basis. The results explain why the ratio method succeeded in measurements by Lyles et al. (2007) for 236U(n,f) cross sections at energies above several MeV where second and third chance fission occur, and enable future determination of neutron capture cross sections by combining surrogate ratio measurements with Hauser-Feshbach calculations to normalize giant dipole resonance parameters.
