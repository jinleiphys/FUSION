# Novel approaches in Hadron Spectroscopy

> The last two decades have witnessed the discovery of a myriad of new and unexpected hadrons. The future holds more surprises for us, thanks to new-generation experiments. Understanding the signals and determining the properties of the states requires a parallel theoretical effort. To make full use of available and forthcoming data, a careful amplitude modeling is required, together with a sound treatment of the statistical uncertainties, and a systematic survey of the model dependencies. We review the contributions made by the Joint Physics Analysis Center to the field of hadron spectroscopy.

# Novel approaches in Hadron Spectroscopy

## Key claim

The Joint Physics Analysis Center (JPAC) argues that to fully exploit present and future high-statistics datasets in hadron spectroscopy, one must combine reaction theory, hadron phenomenology, and data analysis with careful amplitude modeling and systematic treatment of model dependencies, rather than relying on any single theoretical approach. The review summarizes JPAC's contributions since 2013 but does not present a single new experimental result; instead it advocates for bottom-up amplitude analyses that respect fundamental principles as fit to data.

## Method

JPAC developed amplitude analysis tools combining S-matrix principles, Regge theory, and three-body scattering formalisms, applied to both light and heavy hadron sectors. The collaboration implemented statistical clustering methods to separate physical resonances from parametrization artifacts, machine learning techniques for pattern recognition in amplitude spaces, and finite-energy sum rules to impose duality constraints. These tools were applied to experimental data from LHCb, COMPASS, BESIII, GlueX, CLAS, BaBar, and Belle, with publicly available implementations.

## Key numbers

- The paper shows differential cross section dσ/dt for π⁻ p and π⁺ p scattering at energies including E = 3.675 GeV, E = 4.125 GeV, and E = 5.025 GeV, with values ranging from approximately 10⁻⁵ to 10 μb/GeV² over −t from 0 to 3.5 GeV².
- The review covers the Zc(3900) state and Pc(4312) pentaquark, but no new numerical results for these states are presented in the truncated text.
- The paper references JPAC's work on η → 3π decays and three-body scattering, but specific numerical values from those analyses are not included in the available text.

## Context

This review summarizes the work of the Joint Physics Analysis Center (JPAC), founded in 2013 at the initiative of Mike Pennington, originally to support spectroscopy analyses at Jefferson Lab. It extends the program of amplitude analysis developed by previous generations of hadron spectroscopists, including the S-matrix approaches of the 1960s-70s and the quark model work of Godfrey and Isgur (1985) and Capstick and Isgur (1985). The review enables future work by providing publicly available analysis tools and establishing best practices for combining theoretical amplitude modeling with experimental data from current facilities (LHCb, COMPASS, BESIII, GlueX, CLAS) and forthcoming experiments such as Belle II.
