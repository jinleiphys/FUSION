# Boltzmann Equation Solver Adapted to Emergent Chemical Non-equilibrium

> We present a novel method to solve the spatially homogeneous and isotropic relativistic Boltzmann equation. We employ a basis set of orthogonal polynomials dynamically adapted to allow for emergence of chemical non-equilibrium. Two time dependent parameters characterize the set of orthogonal polynomials, the effective temperature $T(t)$ and phase space occupation factor $Υ(t)$. In this first paper we address (effectively) massless fermions and derive dynamical equations for $T(t)$ and $Υ(t)$ such that the zeroth order term of the basis alone captures the particle number density and energy density of each particle distribution. We validate our method and illustrate the reduced computational cost and the ability to easily represent final state chemical non-equilibrium by studying a model problem that is motivated by the physics of the neutrino freeze-out processes in the early Universe, where the essential physical characteristics include reheating from another disappearing particle component ($e^\pm$-annihilation).

## Key claim

The paper presents a spectral method for solving the relativistic Boltzmann equation that uses a dynamically adapted basis of orthogonal polynomials, where two time-dependent parameters, effective temperature \(T(t)\) and phase space occupation factor \(\Upsilon(t)\), are evolved such that the zeroth-order basis term alone captures the exact particle number density and energy density, enabling efficient representation of emergent chemical non-equilibrium even when the system is far from chemical equilibrium.

## Method

The authors solve the spatially homogeneous and isotropic relativistic Boltzmann equation for effectively massless fermions by expanding the distribution function in a basis of orthogonal polynomials generated by the weight function \(w(z) = z^2 / (\Upsilon^{-1} e^z + 1)\) on \([0,\infty)\), where \(z = p/T(t)\). They impose conditions \(b^0(t) \hat{\psi}_0(t) = 1\) and \(b^1(t) = 0\) on the expansion coefficients, which fixes the dynamics of \(T(t)\) and \(\Upsilon(t)\) such that the zeroth-order term alone captures the particle number density and energy density. The remaining modes \(b^2, \ldots, b^N\) describe non-thermal distortions, and the Boltzmann equation is projected onto the finite-dimensional subspace to obtain coupled ordinary differential equations for all unknowns.

## Key numbers

- The particle number density is given by \(n = \frac{g_p T^3}{2\pi^2} \int_0^\infty f_\Upsilon z^2 dz\) and the energy density by \(\rho = \frac{g_p T^4}{2\pi^2} \int_0^\infty f_\Upsilon z^3 dz\), where \(f_\Upsilon = 1/(\Upsilon^{-1} e^z + 1)\) and \(g_p\) is the degeneracy factor.
- The chemical equilibrium method requires at least four modes to capture both particle number and energy flow, while the chemical non-equilibrium method captures both exactly with the zeroth-order term alone.
- For the chemical equilibrium basis, the perturbation \(\chi = f_\Upsilon / f_{ch}\) is not square-integrable with respect to the weight \(f_{ch} dy\) when the reheating ratio \(R = aT \geq 2\), causing the expansion to fail with large spurious oscillations even with 20 modes.
- The error in approximating a chemical non-equilibrium distribution with the chemical equilibrium basis is shown for \(\Upsilon = 0.5\) and \(\Upsilon = 1.5\) as a function of number of modes and reheating ratio \(R\).

## Context

This work extends spectral methods for the relativistic Boltzmann equation previously applied to neutrino freeze-out by Esposito et al. (2000) and Mangano et al. (2002), which used a fixed chemical equilibrium basis and dilution temperature scaling \(T \propto 1/a\). The new method adapts to emergent chemical non-equilibrium, motivated by recent PLANCK CMB results suggesting neutrinos may have experienced greater reheating than previously believed, as discussed by Birrell et al. (2014). The approach also connects to chemical non-equilibrium analysis in quark-gluon plasma physics, where the method of Rafelski (1991) and subsequent work by Petran et al. (2013) has been the only successful statistical hadronization model for experimental results. The present paper treats only the massless case; a method for massive particles will be presented in future publications.
