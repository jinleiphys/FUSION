# Is the concept of the non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian relevant in the case of potential scattering?

> We examine the notion and properties of the non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian of an unstable system using as an example potential resonance scattering with a fixed angular momentum. We present a consistent self-adjoint formulation of the problem of scattering on a finite-range potential, which is based on separation of the configuration space on two, internal and external, segments. The scattering amplitude is expressed in terms of the resolvent of a non-Hermitian operator H. The explicit form of this operator depends both on the radius of separation and the boundary conditions at this place which can be chosen in many different ways. We discuss this freedom and show explicitly that the physical scattering amplitude is, nevertheless, unique though not all choices are equally adequate from the physical point of view. The energy-dependent operator H should not be confused with the non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian H_{eff} exploited usually to describe interference of overlapping resonances. We apply the developed formalism to a chain of L delta-barriers whose solution is also found independently in a closed form. For a fixed band of L overlapping resonances, the smooth energy dependence of H can be ignored so that complex eigenvalues of the LxL submatrix H_{eff} define the energies and widths of the resonances.We construct H_{eff} for the two commonly considered types of the boundary conditions (Neumann and Dirichlet) for the internal motion. Formation in the outer well of a short-lived doorway state is explicitly demonstrated together with the appearance of L-1 long-lived states trapped in the inner part of the chain.

## Key claim

The non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian concept is relevant for potential scattering only when the energy spectrum has a band structure with overlapping resonances, such as in a chain of connected cavities; in single two-dimensional cavities, the simple Breit-Wigner approximation is usually sufficient and no nontrivial effective Hamiltonian appears.

## Method

The authors separate the configuration space into internal and external segments at a chosen radius \(a\), using Bloch's method to define Hermitian internal and external operators with specific boundary conditions (Neumann or Dirichlet). They express the scattering amplitude in terms of the resolvent of an energy-dependent non-Hermitian operator \(\mathcal{H}\), then apply the formalism to an exactly solvable chain of \(L\) delta-barriers (the open Kronig-Penney model), deriving closed-form expressions for the \(S\)-matrix and comparing the exact poles with those obtained from truncated effective Hamiltonians.

## Key numbers

- For a chain of \(L\) delta-barriers, the \(S\)-matrix poles come in pairs \(z_n\) and \(z_{-n} \equiv -z_n^*\) symmetric about the imaginary axis, or lie on the imaginary axis as \(z_q = iy_q\); all poles of the first type are in the lower half-plane.
- The resonance energy and width are given by \(E_n = |z_n|^2\) and \(\Gamma_n = 4|\Re(z_n) \Im(z_n)|\).
- For the free-particle case (\(U(r) \equiv 0\)) with Neumann boundary conditions, the matrix elements of \(\mathcal{H}\) are \(\mathcal{H}^{\text{(n)}}_{mn} = \frac{1}{a^2} \left[ \left(m+\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \pi^2 \delta_{mn} - i k a (-1)^{(m+n)} \right]\).
- For the free-particle case with Dirichlet boundary conditions, the matrix elements are \(\mathcal{H}^{\text{(d)}}_{mn} = \frac{1}{a^2} \left[ m^2 \pi^2 \delta_{mn} - i \pi^2 \frac{mn}{ka} (-1)^{(m+n)} \right]\).
- The external wave function for Dirichlet boundary conditions is \(\phi_k^0(r) = \sqrt{2/\pi} \sin[k(r-a)]\) with \(S^{\text{(d)}}_0(k) = e^{-2ika}\); for Neumann boundary conditions, \(S^{\text{(n)}}_0(k) = -S^{\text{(d)}}_0(k) = e^{-2i(ka+\pi/2)}\).

## Context

This work extends the Feshbach (1958, 1962) and Kapur-Peierls (1938) and Wigner-Eisenbud (1947) formalisms for resonance reactions to potential scattering, clarifying when the non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian concept is valid. It directly addresses numerical difficulties found by Pichugin (2001) in computing poles of the \(S\)-matrix for cavity-lead systems, showing that poor choice of boundary conditions at the separation surface leads to slow convergence and spurious poles. The results enable reliable construction of effective Hamiltonians for open chains of cavities, relevant to chaotic scattering studies in microwave cavities (Stoeckmann, 1999) and quantum transport in mesoscopic systems.
