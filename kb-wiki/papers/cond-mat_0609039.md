# New Trends in Density Matrix Renormalization

> The Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) has become a powerful numerical method that can be applied to low-dimensional strongly correlated fermionic and bosonic systems. It allows for a very precise calculation of static, dynamic and thermodynamic properties. Its field of applicability has now extended beyond Condensed Matter, and it is now successfully used in Quantum Chemistry, Statistical Mechanics, Quantum Information Theory, Nuclear and High Energy Physics as well. In this article, we briefly review the main aspects of the method and present some of the most relevant applications so as to give an overview on the scope and possibilities of DMRG. We focus on the most important extensions of the method such as the calculation of dynamical properties, the application to classical systems, finite temperature simulations, phonons and disorder, field theory, time-dependent properties and the ab initio calculation of electronic states in molecules. The recent quantum information interpretation, the development of highly accurate time-dependent algorithms and the possibility of using the DMRG as the impurity-solver of the Dynamical Mean Field Method (DMFT) give new insights into its present and potential uses. We review the numerous very recent applications of these techniques where the DMRG has shown to be one of the most reliable and versatile methods in modern computational physics.

## Key claim

The Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) has become a powerful numerical method for low-dimensional strongly correlated systems, but its performance depends critically on the entanglement entropy of the system: it works excellently for gapped and one-dimensional systems where the density matrix eigenvalue spectrum decays exponentially or follows a logarithmic scaling, but becomes unreliable for dimensions higher than one where the eigenvalue decay is extremely slow.

## Method

The DMRG method systematically truncates the Hilbert space by keeping the most probable states of a wave function (typically the ground state) rather than the lowest energy states. It starts with a small system and gradually increases its size, constructing the Hamiltonian for a "universe" divided into system and environment parts, diagonalizing to obtain target states, and constructing the reduced density matrix for the system block. The eigenstates of this density matrix with the highest eigenvalues are kept up to a cutoff, with the truncation error defined as the sum of discarded density matrix eigenvalues. A finite-system algorithm fixes the lattice size and iterates back and forth until convergence, achieving higher precision than the infinite-system algorithm.

## Key numbers

- For the spin-1 Heisenberg chain, a precision of \(10^{-10}\) for the ground state energy can be achieved for systems of hundreds of sites
- The spin gap in the \(S=1\) Heisenberg chain was calculated as \(\Delta=0.41050 J\) with unprecedented accuracy
- The spin correlation length for the \(S=1\) Heisenberg chain was obtained as 6.03 lattice spacings
- For the \(S=2\) antiferromagnetic Heisenberg open chain, the spin gap was calculated as \(\Delta=0.085 J\) for systems up to 350 sites, with a spin correlation length of 49 lattice spacings
- For the \(S=1/2\) isotropic Heisenberg model with \(N=100\) sites, an error of \(10^{-5}\) was achieved keeping \(m=150\) states
- For the 1D Hubbard model with 16 sites keeping \(m\simeq 1000\) states, an error of 5-10% was obtained using standard RG procedures
- The von Neumann entropy for gapless critical 1D systems scales as \(S_N = c/3 \ln(N) + \lambda_1\) where \(c\) is the central charge of the underlying conformal field theory
- For (d+1)-dimensional field theory, the entropy scales as \(S_N \propto (N/\lambda)^{d-1}\) where \(\lambda\) is an ultraviolet cutoff

## Context

This review extends the foundational DMRG work of White (1992) and the comprehensive book and review by Schollwöck, covering developments through 2006. It describes how DMRG has expanded beyond condensed matter into quantum chemistry, statistical mechanics, quantum information theory, nuclear physics, and high energy physics, with new applications including time-dependent algorithms, finite-temperature simulations, and use as an impurity-solver for Dynamical Mean Field Theory (DMFT). The quantum information interpretation, particularly the connection between entanglement entropy and DMRG performance as analyzed by Vidal (2002), Latorre, and others, provides new insights into the method's limitations in higher dimensions and its optimal application to one-dimensional and gapped systems.
