# Correlated two-particle scattering on finite cavities

> The correlated two-particle problem is solved analytically in the presence of a finite cavity. The method is demonstrated here in terms of exactly solvable models for both the cavity as well as the two-particle correlation where the two-particle potential is chosen in separable form. The two-particle phase shift is calculated and compared to the single-particle one. The two-particle bound state behavior is discussed and the influence of the cavity on the binding properties is calculated.

# Correlated two-particle scattering on finite cavities

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two-particle correlations in the presence of a finite cavity lead to interference effects that can suppress or enhance bound state formation, but only under the approximation that the center-of-mass momentum of the two particles is approximately conserved during scattering. The exact analytical solution is restricted to cavities with only discrete states; for cavities with continuum states, the authors present an approximate diagonal solution valid when the change of center-of-mass momenta is negligible.

## Method

The authors employ the Gell-Mann and Goldberger two-potential formula to separate the one-particle scattering channel (an opaque delta-shell potential at radius R) from the two-particle correlation channel (a separable potential of rank one). The one-particle problem is solved analytically for a delta-shell cavity potential, yielding explicit wave functions and phase shifts. The two-particle T-matrix is then solved using a separable potential approximation, with the cavity effects entering through modified form factors constructed from the one-particle wave functions. For cavities with continuum states, the authors approximate the solution by assuming diagonal center-of-mass momentum conservation, reducing the integral equation to an algebraic form.

## Key numbers

- The opaque wall potential is given by V_a(r) = (1/2m)(Ω/R)δ(r-R), where Ω is the wall strength parameter and R is the cavity radius.
- The single-particle cross section σ(k) = (4π/k²)/(1+cot²φ) has zeros at x_n = nπ (where x = kR) and maxima determined by -2x_μ/Ω = sin(2x_μ) and -2x_μ/Ω = tan(2x_μ).
- For contact potential with zero center-of-mass momentum, the two-particle phase shift is tan φ(p) = (pR A_{pR}²)/(F_Ω(pR) + R/a₀), where a₀ is the two-particle scattering length and A_{pR} is the cavity amplitude.
- Bound states exist when -R/a₀ ≥ F_Ω(0); for Ω=10, F_Ω(0)=10/11, giving a critical scattering length a₀ = -1.1R.
- For the finite-range separable potential with exponential form factor, the dimensionless coupling constant ξ = mλ/(4π²β³) = -1/(√(2π) + π/(βa₀)), and the potential range r₀ = (1/β)(2√(2/π) + 1/(a₀β)).

## Context

This work extends coupled-channel scattering theory, specifically the Gell-Mann and Goldberger (1953) two-potential formalism, to treat correlated two-particle scattering on finite quantum structures such as quantum dots. It contrasts with the Faddeev technique used by Beregi et al. (1970, 1971, 1972) by adopting a simpler separable potential approach. The method enables future investigation of two-particle correlation effects in realistic nanostructures, provided the one-particle wave functions are available from standard ab-initio methods, and suggests new types of experiments on quantum cavities where two-particle correlations beyond single-particle transport properties can be observed.
