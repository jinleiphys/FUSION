# Particle physics origin of the 5 MeV bump in the reactor antineutrino spectrum?

> One of the most puzzling questions in neutrino physics is the origin of the excess at 5 MeV in the reactor antineutrino spectrum. In this paper, we explore the excess via the reaction $^{13}$C$(\overlineν, \overlineν^\prime n)^{12}$C$^*$ in organic scintillator detectors. The de-excitation of $^{12}$C$^*$ yields a prompt $4.4$ MeV photon, while the thermalization of the product neutron causes proton recoils, which in turn yield an additional prompt energy contribution with finite width. Together, these effects can mimic an inverse beta decay event with around 5 MeV energy. We consider several non-standard neutrino interactions to produce such a process and find that the parameter space preferred by Daya Bay is disfavored by measurements of neutrino-induced deuteron disintegration and coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering. While non-minimal particle physics scenarios may be viable, a nuclear physics solution to this anomaly appears more appealing.

## Key claim
The 5 MeV bump in the reactor antineutrino spectrum could be explained by neutrino-induced neutron knockout from carbon-13 in organic scintillator, producing a prompt 4.4 MeV gamma plus proton recoil energy that mimics an inverse beta decay event. However, the authors find that the particle physics parameter space required to produce this process at the observed rate is disfavored by measurements of neutrino-induced deuteron disintegration and coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering, making a nuclear physics explanation more appealing.

## Method
The authors estimated the cross section for the reaction 13C(anti-nu, anti-nu' n)12C* by rescaling measured 13C(e,e'n)12C* cross sections using an effective coupling zeta-squared, assuming a new vector interaction mediated by a boson X with mass M_X. They convolved this cross section with reactor antineutrino fluxes (Huber-Mueller fluxes augmented by a power-law component above 8 MeV) and simulated neutron thermalization and scintillation quenching using Birks' law to produce a prompt energy spectrum. The resulting spectrum was compared to Daya Bay and NEOS data using a chi-squared test, and the preferred parameter space was confronted with constraints from COHERENT (coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering) and neutrino-induced deuteron disintegration measurements.

## Key numbers
- The best-fit point for transitions only to the first excited state of 12C gives chi-squared_min = 10.3 for 21 degrees of freedom (p = 0.97), with parameters {M_X = 1 keV, log10(zeta) = -6.075, power-law index I = 13}
- Without any new physics, the fit to Daya Bay data gives chi-squared = 35.1 for 24 degrees of freedom (p = 0.07)
- Including ground-state transitions increases chi-squared_min to 49.1 for 21 degrees of freedom (p = 4.9 x 10^-4)
- For NEOS data alone, chi-squared_min/d.o.f. = 14.2/58 with new physics versus 66.7/60 without new physics
- A combined Daya Bay and NEOS fit gives chi-squared_min/d.o.f. = 24.0/82 with new physics versus 101.8/84 without new physics
- The parameter goodness-of-fit test between Daya Bay and NEOS yields chi-squared_PG = 0.66 with 2 degrees of freedom (p = 0.72)
- The benchmark oscillation parameters used are Delta m^2_41 = 0.4 eV^2 and sin^2 2theta_ee = 0.04

## Context
This work extends the search for an explanation of the 5 MeV reactor bump (first identified by Huber in 2016) beyond nuclear physics into beyond-Standard-Model particle physics. Previous attempts to explain the bump via miscalculated uranium-238 fluxes (Hayes 2015, Huber 2016) are noted as not mutually exclusive with the proposed mechanism. The authors find that minimal particle physics models are ruled out by existing data from COHERENT (Akimov 2017) and deuterium scattering experiments (Reines 1980), suggesting that a nuclear physics solution remains more promising. The analysis enables future work to focus on non-minimal particle physics scenarios or to pursue the nuclear physics explanation more thoroughly.
