# Extracting a model quark propagator's spectral density

> We propose a practical procedure to extrapolate the space-like quark propagator onto the complex plane, which follows the Schlessinger Point Method and the spectral representation of the propagator. As a feasible example, we employ quark propagators for different flavors, obtained from the solutions of the corresponding Dyson-Schwinger equation (DSE). Two different truncations are employed. Thus, the analytical structure of the quark propagator is studied, capitalizing on the current-quark mass dependence of the observed features.

# Extracting a model quark propagator's spectral density

## Key claim
The authors propose a practical procedure using the Schlessinger Point Method (SPM) combined with a spectral representation to extrapolate space-like quark propagators into the complex plane, but the reliability of the extrapolation depends on careful selection of the number and distribution of input points and the choice of a sqrt(p^2) mapping to introduce a branch cut.

## Method
The quark propagator is obtained by solving the Dyson-Schwinger equation (gap equation) for different quark flavors (u/d, s, c, b) in Euclidean space, using two truncations: the Rainbow-Ladder (RL) approximation and a beyond-Rainbow-Ladder (BYRL) truncation that includes an Anomalous Chromomagnetic Moment term. The space-like propagator solutions are then extrapolated into the complex plane using the SPM with a continued fraction representation, after mapping the training set from p^2 to sqrt(p^2) to introduce a branch cut. The spectral density is computed from the Cauchy integral along the branch cut, and a single pair of complex conjugate poles is extracted via minimization to represent the propagator.

## Context
This work extends prior Dyson-Schwinger equation studies of quark propagator analytic structure (Jarecke 2002, Bhagwat 2002, Chang 2013, Raya 2016) by providing a systematic SPM-based method to extract spectral densities and complex conjugate poles from space-like propagator solutions. The approach is applicable to lattice QCD propagators and enables more efficient computation of hadron form factors and meson excited states, which previously required two pairs of complex conjugate poles (Chang 2013, Raya 2015, Ding 2018). The BYRL truncation uses the process-independent strong running coupling from Cui (2019), derived from first-principles lattice QCD, contrasting with the phenomenological RL interaction model of Qin (2011).
