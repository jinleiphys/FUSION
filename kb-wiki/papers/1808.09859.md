# The isospin and neutron-to-proton excess dependence of short-range correlations

> We provide a systematic study of the isospin composition and neutron-to-proton $\left( \frac{N}{Z} \right)$ ratio dependence of nuclear short-range correlations (SRC) across the nuclear mass table. We use the low-order correlation operator approximation (LCA) to compute the SRC contribution to the single-nucleon momentum distributions for 14 different nuclei from $A=4$ to $A=208$. Ten asymmetric nuclei are included for which the neutrons outnumber the protons by a factor of up to 1.54. The computed momentum distributions are used to extract the pair composition of the SRC. We find that there is a comprehensive picture for the isospin composition of SRC and their evolution with nucleon momentum. We also compute the non-relativistic kinetic energy of neutrons and protons and its evolution with nuclear mass $A$ and $\frac{N}{Z}$. Confirming the conclusions from alternate studies it is shown that the minority species (protons) become increasingly more short-range correlated as the neutron-to-proton ratio increases. We forge connections between measured nucleon-knockout quantities sensitive to SRC and single-nucleon momentum distributions. It is shown that the LCA can account for the observed trends in the data, like the fact that in neutron-rich nuclei the protons are responsible for an unexpectedly large fraction of the high-momentum components.

## Key claim

The low-order correlation operator approximation (LCA) can account for the observed trends in nucleon-knockout data, including the finding that in neutron-rich nuclei the protons are responsible for an unexpectedly large fraction of the high-momentum components, confirming that the minority species (protons) become increasingly more short-range correlated as the neutron-to-proton ratio increases.

## Method

The authors used the low-order correlation operator approximation (LCA) to compute the short-range correlation (SRC) contribution to single-nucleon momentum distributions for 14 nuclei from A=4 to A=208, including ten asymmetric nuclei with neutron-to-proton ratios up to 1.54. The LCA truncates the SRC operator to retain terms linear and quadratic in the correlation functions (central Jastrow, tensor, and spin-isospin terms), and uses harmonic oscillator single-particle states with a global parameterization to separate pair relative and center-of-mass motion. The computed momentum distributions were then decomposed into pair contributions (pp, pn, np, nn) and used to extract the isospin composition of SRC and the non-relativistic kinetic energies of neutrons and protons.

## Key numbers

- 14 nuclei were studied, from A=4 to A=208, including ten asymmetric nuclei with neutron-to-proton ratios up to 1.54 (for 208Pb)
- At nucleon momenta p > 2 fm^-1, the momentum distribution tails are universal across all nuclei (scaled versions of one another)
- In the momentum range 2.0 to 2.75 fm^-1, pn pairs account for 80-90% of the single-nucleon momentum distribution, while pp and nn pairs each account for about 5-10%
- For 40Ca, the LCA with "hard" gc gives average kinetic energy per nucleon of 27.75 MeV, compared to cluster variational Monte Carlo results of 30.34 +/- 1.60 MeV (AV18+UIX) and 32.54 +/- 1.94 MeV (AV18)
- SRC accounts for about 40% of the average nucleon kinetic energy across the nuclear mass table
- The ratio of neutron to proton kinetic energy after SRC corrections gives alpha approximately -0.08 (negative), compared to the IPM result of alpha approximately 0.3 and the Fermi-gas prediction of alpha = 2/3
- For lead, about 80% of nucleons have momentum lower than the Fermi momentum (p_F = 1.25 fm^-1)

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of short-range correlations across the nuclear mass table using the LCA method developed by Vanhalst et al. (2014), and connects to experimental results from the CLAS collaboration at Jefferson Lab for 12C, 27Al, 56Fe, and 208Pb (Hen et al., 2014; Duer et al., 2018). The LCA results are compared with ab-initio Quantum Monte Carlo calculations by the Argonne group (Wiringa et al., 2013; Lonardoni et al., 2017) for light nuclei up to 40Ca, and the approach enables systematic studies of SRC properties for heavier nuclei where full Monte Carlo calculations are computationally prohibitive. The findings enable quantitative connections between measured nucleon-knockout quantities and single-nucleon momentum distributions, and provide a framework for interpreting the neutron-to-proton asymmetry dependence of SRC observed in recent experiments.
