# The Physics of Jet Quenching in Perturbative QCD

> Hard processes in collider experiments typically produce QCD jets, which have long served as precision tests of QCD in the vacuum. More recently, heavy-ion programs at RHIC and the LHC have offered a novel perspective on jets, establishing them as unique probes of strongly interacting matter. Experimental observations, including the suppression of high-$p_T$ hadrons and jets, provide compelling evidence for the formation of a new state of matter and its strong coupling to energetic partons. These advances have motivated new theoretical approaches to jet quenching that go beyond standard perturbative techniques, aiming to elucidate the mechanisms of energy dissipation and thermalization of energetic partons in the quark-gluon plasma. This review highlights recent progress, beginning with a unified description of medium-induced radiation across the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal regime and its role in turbulent gluon cascades. We then examine radiative corrections that renormalize the transport coefficient $\hat q$, the mechanism of color decoherence in multi-parton systems, and nonlinear QCD evolution equations for jet energy loss. Finally, we confront this framework with experimental measurements, underscoring the need for precision phenomenology to fully exploit the rich data sets from RHIC and the LHC.

# The Physics of Jet Quenching in Perturbative QCD

## Key claim

The review argues that medium-induced gluon cascades in the quark-gluon plasma can be described as a probabilistic, quasi-instantaneous branching process when the medium length is much larger than the formation time, but this picture relies on the assumption that corrections from overlapping formation times are numerically negligible except for large logarithms that can be absorbed into renormalization of the transport coefficient.

## Method

The author develops a perturbative QCD framework for jet quenching by constructing a generating functional formalism for medium-induced gluon cascades, where the building blocks are the broadening probability and the branching rate derived from the three-point function of scalar propagators. The evolution of the single-inclusive gluon distribution is governed by a rate equation that combines elastic broadening and collinear branching terms, with the branching kernel simplified using the harmonic oscillator approximation. The analysis employs an improved opacity expansion (IOE) technique that treats multiple soft gluon exchanges via the harmonic oscillator approximation and accounts for rare hard interactions perturbatively.

## Key numbers

- The branching probability scales as \(P \sim \bar\alpha L/t_f(\omega)\), where \(\bar\alpha\) is the coupling and \(t_f(\omega)\) is the formation time
- The characteristic stopping time is \(t_\ast(E) = (1/\bar\alpha)\sqrt{E/\hat q}\), after which most initial energy is radiated into soft gluons
- The reduced branching kernel is \(\mathcal{K}(z) = [1-z(1-z)]^{5/2} / [z^{3/2}(1-z)^{3/2}]\)
- The energy fraction remaining in the spectrum after time \(\tau = L/t_\ast\) is \(\mathcal{E}(0,\tau) = e^{-\pi\tau^2}\), with about 60% of initial energy flowing into the condensate when \(\tau \sim 1/\sqrt{\pi}\)
- The energy flux to \(x=0\) is \(\mathcal{F}(0,\tau) = 2\pi\tau e^{-\pi\tau^2}\)
- The condensate fraction is \(n_c(\tau) = 1 - e^{-\pi\tau^2}\)
- The critical angle for color decoherence \(\theta_c\) is typically smaller than the jet cone size

## Context

This review extends the foundational BDMPS-Z framework (Baier, Dokshitzer, Mueller, Peigné, Schiff) for medium-induced radiation and the work of Blaizot et al. (2012-2015) on gluon cascades and wave turbulence. It synthesizes recent developments including the improved opacity expansion (Mehtar-Tani 2019, Barata 2021) that unifies the ASW (Salgado 2003, Armesto 2004), Gyulassy-Levai-Vitev (2000), and higher-twist (Wang 2001) approximations. The framework enables precision phenomenology for jet observables at RHIC and the LHC, with particular relevance for understanding the \(R\)-dependence of the jet nuclear modification factor measured by ALICE, and provides a foundation for computing substructure observables such as angularities, energy-energy correlators, and groomed jet observables.
