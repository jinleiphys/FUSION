# Equilibrium in Heavy Ion Collisions

> We discuss the question of equilibriation in heavy ion collisions and how it can be addressed in experiment

## Key claim

The success of thermal models in reproducing particle ratios is a necessary but not sufficient condition for the existence of matter close to equilibrium; multi-particle correlations, specifically of multiply strange hadrons like the Omega baryon, are required to determine the volume over which strangeness is equilibrated, and two-particle observables alone cannot distinguish between a single equilibrated system and a collection of independent nucleon-nucleon collisions.

## Method

The authors model a heavy ion collision as a total volume \(V_f\) divided into \(p\) domains, each with the same temperature (assumed to be the universal freeze-out temperature of 170 MeV) and each conserving strangeness explicitly. They calculate the abundance and probability distributions of strange particles, particularly the Omega baryon, as a function of domain volume \(V_p = V_f/p\), and compare these to the grand-canonical limit and to Poisson distributions.

## Key numbers

- For domain volumes \(V_p > 20 \, V_{nuc}\) (where \(V_{nuc}\) is the volume of a nucleon-nucleon collision), the effect of strangeness conservation on average particle number is small.
- At SPS energies, NA57 measures a strong centrality dependence of the Omega-baryon yield compatible with a strangeness equilibration volume \(V \leq 20 \, V_{nuc}\) even for the most central collisions.
- For \(N_{\Omega} \geq 5\), the probability distribution of Omega baryons shows substantial sensitivity to the number of domains, with probabilities \(P > 10^{-6}\) accessible in experiments collecting of the order of \(10^6\) events.
- For \(N_{\Omega} \leq 5\), the Omega probability distribution is essentially Poissonian, and the variance equals the mean, mimicking the grand-canonical limit.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of equilibration in heavy ion collisions beyond the thermal model approach, which was reviewed by Braun-Munzinger et al. (2003) and is based on the statistical model of Fermi (1950). It builds on the canonical treatment of strangeness conservation by Redlich and collaborators (2000) and addresses the experimental observations of the NA57 collaboration (Manzari, 2003) at SPS energies. The paper enables a dedicated experimental search for multi-Omega final states to determine the strangeness equilibration volume, providing a lower bound on the overall equilibrium volume created in heavy ion collisions.
