# Proton polarizability contribution to hydrogen Lamb shift

> The correction to the hydrogen Lamb shift due to the proton electric and magnetic polarizabilities is expressed analytically through their static values, which are known from experiment. The numerical value of the correction is $ - 71 \pm 11 \pm 7$ Hz.

## Key claim

The proton electric and magnetic polarizability correction to the hydrogen Lamb shift is expressed analytically through static polarizability values, yielding a numerical result of -71 Hz, but this value is derived within a logarithmic approximation and carries a 15% theoretical uncertainty from that approximation.

## Method

The authors compute the electron-proton forward scattering amplitude using the gauge A₀=0 for virtual photons, then perform a Wick rotation and integrate over Euclidean frequency with logarithmic accuracy. The nuclear-spin independent Compton forward scattering amplitude is expressed in terms of the electric and magnetic polarizabilities, which are taken at zero momentum transfer within the logarithmic approximation. The final integration yields an effective delta-function potential proportional to 5α(0)-β(0) times the logarithm of the ratio of the typical proton excitation energy to the electron mass.

## Key numbers

- Proton polarizability sum: α_p(0) + β_p(0) = (14.2 ± 0.5) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Proton polarizability difference: α_p(0) - β_p(0) = (10.0 ± 1.8) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Combined coefficient: 5α_p(0) - β_p(0) = (58.4 ± 5.3) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Proton polarizability correction to the hydrogen 1S Lamb shift: -71 ± 11 ± 7 Hz, where the first error (11 Hz) is the 15% logarithmic approximation uncertainty and the second error (7 Hz) comes from the polarizability measurements
- Typical proton excitation energy: ~300 MeV

## Context

This work extends the approach of Khriplovich (1997) for nuclear polarizability corrections to the Lamb shift, and differs from an earlier estimate by Pachucki (1996) which used a factor of 2 instead of 5 for the electric polarizability and omitted the magnetic polarizability entirely. The result improves upon the logarithmic-accuracy formula of Ericson (1966) for arbitrary nuclei by including the magnetic polarizability contribution, and is relevant for interpreting the upcoming high-precision measurements of the hydrogen-deuterium 1S-2S isotope shift.
