# Temperature of projectile like fragments in heavy ion collisions

> A model in which a projectile like fragment can be simply regarded as a remnant after removal of some part of the projectile leads to an excited fragment. This excitation energy can be calculated with a Hamiltonian that gives correct nuclear matter binding, compressibility and density distribution in finite nuclei. In heavy ion collisions the model produces a dependence of excitation energy on impact parameter which appears to be correct but the magnitude of the excitation energy falls short. It is argued that dynamic effects left out in the model will increase this magnitude. The model can be directly extended to include dynamics but at the expense of increased computation. For many calculations for observables, a temperature is an easier tool to use rather than an excitation energy. Hence temperature dependences on impact parameter in heavy ion collisions are displayed.

# Temperature of projectile like fragments in heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The excitation energy of projectile-like fragments (PLFs) can be estimated from a static, semi-classical model using Thomas-Fermi ground states and a sudden-approximation abrasion geometry, but the resulting temperatures systematically underestimate those inferred from experimental data because dynamic effects (energy flow from participants to spectators) are omitted.

## Method

Thomas-Fermi solutions for projectile ground states are generated using a Hamiltonian with zero-range Skyrme interactions plus a finite-range Yukawa potential that reproduces nuclear matter binding, compressibility, and realistic density distributions. Test particles (100 per nucleon) sample the phase-space distribution; a PLF is formed by removing test particles whose positions lie within the target's geometric cross-section at a given impact parameter, using straight-line trajectories. The excitation energy of the resulting "crooked" PLF is computed as the difference between its total energy (kinetic plus potential, evaluated on a cubic lattice with the Lenk-Pandharipande smoothing prescription) and the ground-state energy of a nucleus of the same mass number, calculated with the same Hamiltonian and test-particle method. Temperature is then extracted from excitation energy using the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM).

## Key numbers

- For Sn on Sn, the previous empirical parametrization was $T(b) = 7.5\ \mathrm{MeV} - [A_s(b)/A_0] \times 4.5\ \mathrm{MeV}$, valid for beam energies between 140 MeV/nucleon and 1 GeV/nucleon.
- The finite-range Yukawa potential parameters used are $V_0 = -668.65\ \mathrm{MeV}$ and $a = 0.45979\ \mathrm{fm}$; the Skyrme parameters are $A = -1533.6\ \mathrm{MeV\ fm^3}$, $B = 2805.3\ \mathrm{MeV\ fm^{7/2}}$, and $\sigma = 7/6$.
- The lattice spacing for density evaluation is $l = 1\ \mathrm{fm}$ with $n = 1$ in the Lenk-Pandharipande form factor.
- The model is applied to systems including $^{124}\mathrm{Sn}$ on $^{119}\mathrm{Sn}$, $^{136}\mathrm{Xe}$ on $^{208}\mathrm{Pb}$, $^{58}\mathrm{Ni}$ on $^{9}\mathrm{Be}$, and $^{58}\mathrm{Ni}$ on $^{181}\mathrm{Ta}$.
- The lowest beam energy for which the model is considered valid is 140 MeV/nucleon (with Ni as projectile); the model is developed for cases like Sn on Sn at 600 MeV/nucleon and Xe on Pb at 1 GeV/nucleon.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier projectile fragmentation model (Mallik et al., 2012-2013) by replacing the previously fitted temperature with a microscopically calculated excitation energy, using a Hamiltonian that reproduces ground-state properties. The static calculation underestimates temperatures compared to the "experimental" curve derived from intermediate-mass-fragment yields in Sn-on-Sn collisions (Mallik et al., 2013), confirming the need for dynamical effects. The approach contrasts with the shell-model-based method of Grimard and Schmidt (1994), which estimated 13.3 MeV per hole as average excitation energy; the present model is more basic in starting from a microscopic interaction and can be seamlessly extended to include dynamics via Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport calculations.
