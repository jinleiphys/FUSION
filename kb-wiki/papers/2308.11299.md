# Nuclear $β$ spectrum from projected shell model (I): allowed one-to-one transition

> Nuclear $β$ spectrum and the corresponding (anti-)neutrino spectrum play important roles in many aspects of nuclear astrophysics, particle physics, nuclear industry and nuclear data. In this work we propose a projected shell model (PSM) to calculate the level energies as well as the reduced one-body transition density (ROBTD) by the Pfaffian algorithm for nuclear $β$ decays. The calculated level energies and ROBTD are inputed to the Beta Spectrum Generator (BSG) code to study the high precision $β$ spectrum of allowed one-to-one transitions. When experimental level energies are adopted, the calculated $β$ spectrum by ROBTD of the PSM deviates from the one by the extreme simple particle evaluation of the BSG by up to $10\%$, reflecting the importance of nuclear many-body correlations. When calculated level energies are adopted, the calculated $β$ spectrum shows sensitive dependence on the reliability of calculated level energies. The developed method for ROBTD by the PSM will also be useful for study of the first-forbidden transitions, the isovector spin monopole resonance etc. in a straightforward way.

## Key claim
The paper proposes a projected shell model (PSM) method for calculating reduced one-body transition density (ROBTD) via a Pfaffian algorithm, and shows that when experimental level energies are used, the resulting beta spectrum deviates from the simple single-particle evaluation by up to 10%, demonstrating the importance of nuclear many-body correlations. The authors note that when calculated level energies are used instead, the beta spectrum shows sensitive dependence on the reliability of those calculated energies.

## Method
The authors combine the projected shell model (PSM) with the Beta Spectrum Generator (BSG) code to compute high-precision beta spectra for allowed one-to-one transitions. The PSM calculates level energies and ROBTD using a Pfaffian algorithm for angular-momentum-projected matrix elements, starting from Nilsson+BCS mean-field calculations with configuration mixing. The ROBTD and level energies are then input into the BSG code, which incorporates corrections including the Fermi function, shape factor, radiative correction, atomic exchange, and atomic mismatch effects.

## Key numbers
- The beta spectrum calculated with PSM ROBTD and experimental level energies deviates from the extreme simple particle (ESP) evaluation by up to 10% for the 0+ to 1+ transition from 162Gd to 162Tb.
- The experimental Logft value for the 162Gd (0+) to 162Tb (1+) transition is 4.46, while the PSM calculation gives 4.10.
- The experimental Logft value for the 103Ru (3/2+) to 103Rh (5/2+) transition is 5.72, while the PSM calculation gives 5.74.
- The 1+ state of 162Tb calculated by PSM has a main 2-qp configuration (ν 5/2-[523] ⊗ π 7/2-[523]) with about 8% mixing from 4-qp configurations.

## Context
This work extends the projected shell model framework previously applied to nuclear levels and weak-interaction processes (LJWang 2018, LJWang 2020, LJWang 2021) to enable calculation of ROBTD for beta decay spectra. The method builds on the BSG code developed by Hayen (2019) for high-precision beta spectra, replacing the simple single-particle evaluation of nuclear matrix elements with a many-body treatment. The developed ROBTD formalism paves the way for future studies of first-forbidden transitions, isovector spin monopole resonance, and potentially neutrinoless double beta decay nuclear matrix elements.
