# Multipole excitations in hot nuclei within the finite temperature quasiparticle random phase approximation framework

> The effect of temperature on the evolution of the isovector dipole and isoscalar quadrupole excitations in $^{68}$Ni and $^{120}$Sn nuclei is studied within the fully self-consistent finite temperature quasiparticle random phase approximation framework, based on the Skyrme-type SLy5 energy density functional. The new low-energy excitations emerge due to the transitions from thermally occupied states to the discretized continuum at finite temperatures, whereas the isovector giant dipole resonance is not strongly impacted by the increase of temperature. The radiative dipole strength at low-energies is also investigated for the $^{122}$Sn nucleus, becoming compatible with the available experimental data when the temperature is included. In addition, both the isoscalar giant quadrupole resonance and low-energy quadrupole states are sensitive to the temperature effect: while the centroid energies decrease in the case of the isoscalar giant quadrupole resonance, the collectivity of the first $2^{+}$ state is quenched and the opening of new excitation channels fragments the low-energy strength at finite temperatures.

## Key claim

The paper claims that finite temperature induces new low-energy dipole and quadrupole excitations in open-shell nuclei due to transitions from thermally occupied states to the discretized continuum, while the isovector giant dipole resonance remains largely unaffected; however, the authors caution that the continuum is treated via discretization rather than exactly, so the quantitative predictions for the very-low-energy region should be considered qualitative.

## Method

The authors performed fully self-consistent finite temperature quasiparticle random phase approximation (FT-QRPA) calculations based on the Skyrme-type SLy5 energy density functional, using a spherical coordinate-space Hartree-Fock-BCS (HFBCS) ground state at finite temperature. The FT-QRPA matrix includes both two-quasiparticle creation/annihilation operators and one-quasiparticle creation plus one-quasiparticle annihilation operators, with temperature-dependent occupation factors from the Fermi-Dirac distribution. The continuum was discretized inside a spherical box of 20 fm with 0.1 fm mesh size, and calculations were performed up to T=2 MeV to avoid unphysical neutron vapor.

## Key numbers

* For $^{68}$Ni at T=0 MeV, the isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR) centroid energy is 16.8 MeV; at T=2 MeV it shifts to 16.5 MeV.
* The pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) in $^{68}$Ni at T=0 MeV exhausts 1.2% of the energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR); at T=2 MeV the PDR region fragments into states at 9.62 MeV (0.32% EWSR) and 10.3 MeV (0.31% EWSR).
* For $^{120}$Sn at T=0 MeV, the IVGDR centroid energy is 15.0 MeV; at T=2 MeV it is 14.95 MeV.
* The low-energy dipole state in $^{120}$Sn at T=0 MeV appears at 8.9 MeV exhausting 0.88% of the EWSR; at T=2 MeV the two main peaks are at 8.4 MeV (0.29% EWSR) and 9.8 MeV (0.38% EWSR).
* The critical temperatures for the superfluid-to-normal phase transition are T$_c$=0.96 MeV for $^{68}$Ni and T$_c$=0.84 MeV for $^{120}$Sn, following the empirical rule T$_c\approx$0.57$\Delta_{T=0}$.
* For $^{122}$Sn, the $\gamma$-ray strength function at T=1.02 and 1.17 MeV becomes compatible with experimental data above E>3 MeV, while below 3 MeV the FT-QRPA overestimates the strength for T$\geq$1.17 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the finite temperature RPA framework of Civitarese (1984), Vautherin (1984), and Bes (1984) to the fully self-consistent QRPA level for open-shell nuclei, going beyond the schematic models of Sommermann (1983) and the non-self-consistent continuum-QRPA of Khan (2004) which used the Landau approximation. The results enable future studies of hot nuclei in astrophysical contexts, particularly the r-process nucleosynthesis, and provide a microscopic baseline for understanding the low-energy enhancement of radiative dipole strength observed experimentally by Guttormsen (2005), Larsen (2010), and Tofte-Petersen (2010, 2011).
