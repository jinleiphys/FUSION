# Anomalies in Particle Physics

> The currently accepted mathematical description of the fundamental constituents and interactions of matter is the Standard Model of particle physics. Its last missing particle, the famous Higgs boson, was observed at the Large Hadron Collider at CERN in 2012. However, it is clear that the Standard Model cannot be the ultimate theory of Nature, and e.g. cannot account for Dark Matter or non-vanishing neutrino masses (and does not include gravity). In fact, searches for physics beyond the SM have been intensified since the Higgs boson discovery. In this article, we review the hints for new physics, called ``anomalies'', obtained in particle physics experiments within the last years. We consider both direct high-energy searches for new resonances at the LHC and indirect low-energy precision experiments. These anomalies range from the nuclear scale (approximately the mass of the proton) to the electroweak scale (i.e. the mass of the Higgs boson) to the TeV scale (the highest scale directly accessible at the LHC), therefore spanning over four orders of magnitude. After discussing the experimental and theoretical status of the anomalies, we summarize possible explanations in terms of new particles and new interactions. In particular, new Higgs bosons and leptoquarks are promising candidates. Discovery prospects and implications for future colliders are discussed.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that multiple experimental deviations from Standard Model predictions, spanning energy scales from MeV to TeV, collectively suggest the need for new physics, but emphasizes that no single anomaly has yet reached the discovery threshold of 5σ with full systematic control, and that the statistical significance of several anomalies depends critically on theoretical uncertainties and the choice of Standard Model predictions.

## Method

The authors survey experimental anomalies in particle physics, applying selection criteria that require a combined global statistical significance of at least 3σ after trial factors, experimental signatures in more than one channel or measured by more than one independent experiment, and compatibility with a theoretically robust model that does not contradict existing constraints. They categorize anomalies by energy scale and assess possible explanations in terms of specific beyond-Standard-Model particles including leptoquarks, di-quarks, Z' bosons, W' bosons, vector-like quarks, vector-like leptons, new scalars, and heavy gluons.

## Key numbers

- The anomalous magnetic moment of the muon shows a deviation from the Standard Model prediction of 5.1σ based on the Brookhaven E821 and Fermilab g-2 experiments, but the authors note this significance is unreliable due to tensions between different Standard Model predictions
- The X17 anomaly in excited nuclear decays (⁷Li(p,e⁺e⁻)⁸Be, ⁴He, ¹²C) has a statistical significance exceeding 6σ, consistent with a new particle of mass ≈17 MeV
- The MiniBooNE neutrino excess in the energy range 200 < E_ν < 1250 MeV has a significance of 4.8σ, which reduces to 4.3σ (3.4σ) when MicroBooNE exclusive (inclusive) electron-neutrino data are added
- The Gallium anomaly in radioactive source experiments (⁵¹Cr, ³⁷Ar) shows a deficit of ⁷¹Ge production with significance about 5σ
- The difference of CP asymmetries in D meson decays measured by LHCb is ΔA_CP = (-15.4 ± 2.9) × 10⁻⁴, compared to a Standard Model estimate of |ΔA_CP| < 3.6 × 10⁻⁴
- The combined significance of deviations in total branching ratios for B̅ → D(*)K and B̅_s → D(*)ₛπ is 5.6σ
- The neutron lifetime discrepancy between beam experiments (τ_n = 888.2 ± 2.0 s) and bottle experiments (τ_n = 877.75 ± 0.28_stat +0.22/-0.16_syst s) is about 4σ

## Context

This review extends the ongoing discussion of anomalies in particle physics following the 2012 Higgs boson discovery at the Large Hadron Collider, covering results from experiments including LHCb, Belle, MiniBooNE, MicroBooNE, the g-2 collaboration at Fermilab, and the Atomki laboratory. The paper situates these anomalies within the broader search for physics beyond the Standard Model, noting that supersymmetry, compositeness, and extra-dimensional models have not received experimental confirmation, and advocates for a data-driven bottom-up approach. The anomalies point toward possible explanations involving new Higgs bosons, leptoquarks, Z' bosons, and vector-like fermions, with future prospects depending on data from the LHC Run 3, the high-luminosity LHC, and proposed colliders such as FCC-ee, ILC, CEPC, and CLIC, as well as dedicated experiments like MUonE, PADME, Mu3e, and PIONEER.
