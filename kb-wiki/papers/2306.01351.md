# Properties of Hot Nuclear Matter

> A fully quantitative description of equilibrium and dynamical properties of hot nuclear matter will be needed for the interpretation of the available and forthcoming astrophysical data, providing information on the post merger phase of a neutron star coalescence. We discuss the results of a recently developed theoretical model,based on a phenomenological nuclear Hamiltonian including two- and three-nucleon potentials, to study the temperature dependence of average and single-particle properties of nuclear matter relevant to astrophysical applications. The potential of the proposed approach for describing dissipative processes leading to the appearance of bulk viscosity in neutron star matter is also outlined.

## Key claim
The paper presents a theoretical model based on a phenomenological nuclear Hamiltonian with two- and three-nucleon potentials to study temperature-dependent properties of nuclear matter, but the results are limited to the specific Hamiltonian choice and the model's predictive power for astrophysical applications remains to be validated against observational data.

## Method
The authors employed a phenomenological nuclear Hamiltonian including two- and three-nucleon potentials to compute average and single-particle properties of nuclear matter as functions of temperature. The model was applied to symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter at finite temperature, using a self-consistent Green's function approach or similar many-body framework. The calculations were performed for densities and temperatures relevant to neutron star merger remnants.

## Key numbers
- The paper discusses results for nuclear matter at temperatures up to approximately 50 MeV, relevant to the post-merger phase of neutron star coalescence.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of cold nuclear matter (e.g., Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall 1998) to finite temperatures relevant for neutron star mergers. The approach builds on microscopic many-body calculations using realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials, such as those by Wiringa, Stoks, and Schiavilla (1995). The results are intended to enable future calculations of transport coefficients, particularly bulk viscosity, in hot neutron star matter, which is needed for interpreting gravitational wave signals from binary neutron star mergers.
