# Time-dependent Hartree-Fock plus Langevin approach for hot fusion reactions to synthesize the $Z=120$ superheavy element

> We develop a novel approach to fusion reactions for syntheses of superheavy elements, which combines the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method with a dynamical diffusion model based on the Langevin equation. In this approach, the distance of the closest approach for the capture process is estimated within the TDHF approach, which is then plugged into the dynamical diffusion model as an initial condition. We apply this approach to hot fusion reactions leading to formation of the element $Z=120$, that is, the $^{48}$Ca+$^{254,257}$Fm, $^{51}$V+$^{249}$Bk, and $^{54}$Cr+$^{248}$Cm reactions. Our calculations indicate that the distances of the closest approach for these systems are similar to each other and thus the difference in the probabilities of evaporation residue formation among those reaction systems originates mainly from the evaporation process, which is sensitive to the fission barrier height and the excitation energy of a compound nucleus.

## Key claim

The magicity of the \({}^{48}\)Ca projectile plays a minor role in the entrance-channel dynamics of hot fusion reactions leading to element \(Z=120\); instead, the main effect of using a \({}^{48}\)Ca projectile is to lower the excitation energy of the compound nucleus, which enhances the survival probability against fission, provided the fission barrier height of the specific compound nucleus is sufficiently high.

## Method

The authors developed a hybrid approach that combines time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations with a fusion-by-diffusion model based on the Langevin equation. For each reaction system, they performed TDHF calculations for head-on collisions (\(b=0\)) with a side-collision geometry to extract the distance of closest approach \(R_{\rm min}\), which was then used as the injection point in the fusion-by-diffusion model to compute the diffusion probability \(P_{\rm CN}\) and the survival probability \(W_{\rm sur}\) for \(s\)-wave scattering at the Coulomb barrier energy.

## Key numbers

* For the \({}^{48}\)Ca+\({}^{254}\)Fm system (leading to \({}^{302}\)120), the distance of closest approach at \(E=V_b\) is \(R_{\rm min}=12.93\) fm; the product \(P_{\rm CN}W_{\rm sur}=302\times 10^{-13}\).
* For the \({}^{54}\)Cr+\({}^{248}\)Cm system (leading to \({}^{302}\)120), the distance of closest approach at \(E=V_b\) is \(R_{\rm min}=13.09\) fm; the product \(P_{\rm CN}W_{\rm sur}=2.47\times 10^{-13}\).
* For the \({}^{51}\)V+\({}^{249}\)Bk system (leading to \({}^{300}\)120), the distance of closest approach at \(E=V_b\) is \(R_{\rm min}=12.94\) fm; the product \(P_{\rm CN}W_{\rm sur}=0.461\times 10^{-13}\).
* For the \({}^{48}\)Ca+\({}^{257}\)Fm system (leading to \({}^{305}\)120), the distance of closest approach at \(E=V_b\) is \(R_{\rm min}=12.94\) fm; the product \(P_{\rm CN}W_{\rm sur}=1.82\times 10^{-13}\).
* The Coulomb barrier heights (frozen Hartree-Fock) are \(V_b=212.8\) MeV for \({}^{48}\)Ca+\({}^{254}\)Fm, \(243.2\) MeV for \({}^{54}\)Cr+\({}^{248}\)Cm, \(237.0\) MeV for \({}^{51}\)V+\({}^{249}\)Bk, and \(212.4\) MeV for \({}^{48}\)Ca+\({}^{257}\)Fm.
* The fission barrier heights for \(Z=120\) compound nuclei are 5.04 MeV (\(A=300\)), 4.66 MeV (\(A=302\)), and 3.54 MeV (\(A=305\), averaged from \(A=304\) and 306).

## Context

This work extends the fusion-by-diffusion model of Abe (2002) and the fusion-by-diffusion model of Siwek-Wilczynska and Wilczynski (2003) by replacing the empirical injection point with a microscopically calculated distance of closest approach from TDHF. The results contradict the expectation from earlier experiments (Satou, 2006) and calculations (Moller, 1997) that magicity strongly influences entrance-channel dynamics; instead, for superheavy element synthesis the magicity of \({}^{48}\)Ca mainly affects the compound-nucleus excitation energy. The approach enables systematic predictions for the synthesis of element 120 using experimentally feasible projectile-target combinations, and can be extended to cold fusion reactions with \({}^{208}\)Pb.
