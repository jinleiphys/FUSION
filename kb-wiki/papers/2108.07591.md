# Physics Opportunities with Meson Beams for EIC

> Over the past two decades, meson photo- and electroproduction data of unprecedented quality and quantity have been measured at electromagnetic facilities worldwide. By contrast, the meson-beam data for the same hadronic final states are mostly outdated and largely of poor quality, or even non-existent, and thus provide inadequate input to help interpret, analyze, and exploit the full potential of the new electromagnetic data. To reap the full benefit of the high-precision electromagnetic data, new high-statistics data from measurements with meson beams, with good angle and energy coverage for a wide range of reactions, are critically needed to advance our knowledge in baryon and meson spectroscopy and other related areas of hadron physics. To address this situation, a state-of-the-art meson-beam facility needs to be constructed. The present letter summarizes unresolved issues in hadron physics and outlines the vast opportunities and advances that only become possible with such a facility.

## Key claim

New high-statistics meson-beam data are critically needed to complement the high-precision electromagnetic data from facilities like JLab, because the existing meson-beam data for the same hadronic final states are mostly outdated, of poor quality, or non-existent, and without such data the full potential of the electromagnetic program cannot be realized.

## Method

The authors review the current state of meson photo- and electroproduction data and contrast it with the poor quality of existing meson-beam data for baryon and meson spectroscopy. They propose adding a secondary meson-beam facility to the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) by using the EIC booster to produce proton beams that generate secondary pion and kaon beams. They estimate achievable particle fluxes using the Sanford-Wang parametrizations combined with the Yamamoto method for secondary beamline design, considering a specific stretcher ring configuration to achieve a continuous-wave beam with good duty factor.

## Key numbers

- The EIC booster design intensity is \(1 \times 10^{12}\) protons per spill (160 nC), corresponding to about 0.6 kW average beam power, which is 2 orders of magnitude below the BNL AGS when it served kaon beamlines and 3 orders of magnitude less than J-PARC.
- With a stretcher ring, a time-average of \(5 \times 10^{11}\) protons/s could be slow-extracted from the stretcher ring into a production target for secondary particles.
- For forward-angle production at 3 GeV/c, pion fluxes of about 10 MHz are possible at the end of a 30 m channel with 3.125 msr solid angle and 2% momentum bite.
- K\(^+\) fluxes are an order of magnitude less than pion fluxes, and K\(^-\) fluxes are another order of magnitude below that.
- The nominal 8 GeV/c booster corresponds to a magnetic rigidity of 26.67 T-m.
- The 2 GeV/c pion beam at J-PARC will allow baryon spectroscopy measurements up to center-of-mass energy \(W \approx 2150\) MeV.

## Context

This paper extends the arguments made in the White Paper by Briscoe et al. (2015) and aligns with the Long Range Plan for Nuclear Science (Geesaman et al., 2015), which recommends a high-energy high-luminosity polarized EIC as the highest priority for new facility construction. The work addresses the "missing resonances" problem identified by Koniuk and Isgur (1979), where quark models predict far more baryon states than are observed experimentally, likely because many resonances couple weakly to the \(\pi N\) channel that supplies most of the current data. A meson-beam facility at the EIC would enable complementary measurements in reactions that do not involve \(\pi N\) in initial or final states, and would allow determination of hyperon resonance pole positions and decays, which is currently impossible with existing data.
