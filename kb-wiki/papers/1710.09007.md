# QCD phase diagram from chiral symmetry restoration: analytic approach at high and low temperature using the Linear Sigma Model with Quarks

> We use the linear sigma model with quarks to study the QCD phase diagram from the point of view of chiral symmetry restoration. We compute the leading order effective potential for high and low temperatures and finite quark chemical potential, up to the contribution of the ring diagrams to account for the plasma screening effects. We fix the values of the model couplings using physical values for the input parameters such as the vacuum pion and sigma masses, the critical temperature at vanishing quark chemical potential and the conjectured end point value of the baryon chemical potential of the transition line at vanishing temperature. We also make the analysis for the same input parameters but with vanishing pion mass. We find that the critical end point (CEP) is located at low temperatures and high quark chemical potentials $(315<μ^{\text{CEP}}<349\ MeV,18< T^{\text{CEP}}<45\ MeV)$.

## Key claim

Using the linear sigma model with quarks and including plasma screening effects via ring diagrams, the critical end point (CEP) in the QCD phase diagram is located at low temperatures and high quark chemical potentials, specifically in the range \(315 < \mu^{\text{CEP}} < 349\ \text{MeV}\) and \(18 < T^{\text{CEP}} < 45\ \text{MeV}\), with the exact location depending on the chosen input parameters (such as the critical temperature at zero chemical potential and the conjectured endpoint baryon chemical potential at zero temperature).

## Method

The authors computed the leading-order effective potential for the linear sigma model coupled to quarks at both high and low temperatures and finite quark chemical potential, including ring diagram contributions to account for plasma screening effects. The model couplings \(\lambda\) and \(g\) were fixed using physical input parameters: the vacuum pion and sigma masses, the critical temperature at vanishing quark chemical potential (taken as 165 or 175 MeV for two light flavors), and the conjectured endpoint value of the baryon chemical potential at vanishing temperature (taken as 330 or 350 MeV for the quark chemical potential). The analysis was performed for both physical pion mass and the chiral limit (vanishing pion mass), and the transition curves were determined by requiring a second-order transition at \(\mu_q=0\) and a first-order transition at \(T=0\).

## Key numbers

*   The critical end point (CEP) is located in the range \(315 < \mu^{\text{CEP}} < 349\ \text{MeV}\) for the quark chemical potential and \(18 < T^{\text{CEP}} < 45\ \text{MeV}\) for the temperature, using physical vacuum pion mass and with \(\mu_q = \mu_b\).
*   For the analysis with physical pion mass and \(\mu_q = \mu_b\), the coupling constants are in the range \(0.77 < \lambda < 0.86\) and \(1.53 < g < 1.63\), with the upper band computed using \(T^c_0(\mu_q=0) = 175\ \text{MeV}\) and \(\mu^c_q(T=0) = 350\ \text{MeV}\) and the lower band using \(T^c_0(\mu_q=0) = 165\ \text{MeV}\) and \(\mu^c_q(T=0) = 330\ \text{MeV}\).
*   For the chiral limit (\(m_\pi=0\)) with \(\mu_q = \mu_b\), the coupling constants are in the range \(1.02 < \lambda < 1.13\) and \(1.78 < g < 1.89\), using the same temperature and chemical potential endpoints for the bands.

## Context

This work extends previous analyses using the linear sigma model with quarks (Ayala et al., 2017) by including plasma screening effects via ring diagrams and providing an analytic approximation for the effective potential at both high and low temperatures. The results are consistent with lattice QCD findings that the transition at zero chemical potential is a crossover (occurring at \(T^c_0 \simeq 170\) MeV for two light flavors) and with effective model studies that predict a first-order transition at high baryon chemical potential. The approach enables an analytic determination of the CEP location, which can be compared with results from Schwinger-Dyson equations, functional renormalization methods, and holography, and provides a framework for future studies where couplings are allowed to depend on temperature and chemical potential.
