# Extraction of the Heavy-Quark Potential from Bottomonium Observables in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The in-medium color potential is a fundamental quantity for understanding the properties of the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma (sQGP). Open and hidden heavy-flavor (HF) production in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions (URHICs) has been found to be a sensitive probe of this potential. Here we utilize a previously developed quarkonium transport approach in combination with insights from open HF diffusion to extract the color-singlet potential from experimental results on $Υ$ production in URHICs. Starting from a parameterized trial potential, we evaluate the $Υ$ transport parameters and conduct systematic fits to available data for the centrality dependence of ground and excited states at RHIC and the LHC. The best fits and their statistical significance are converted into a temperature-dependent potential. Including nonperturbative effects in the dissociation rate guided from open HF phenomenology, we extract a rather strongly coupled potential with substantial remnants of the long-range confining force in the QGP.

## Key claim

The authors extract a temperature-dependent heavy-quark potential from bottomonium suppression data at RHIC and the LHC, finding that a strongly coupled potential with substantial remnants of the confining force in the quark-gluon plasma is required, but only when the heavy-light interaction strength (K factor) is constrained by open heavy-flavor phenomenology; without that constraint, the extracted potential is much weaker.

## Method

The authors use a semi-classical Boltzmann/rate equation approach for bottomonium transport, where the in-medium dissociation energies of the Upsilon states are computed from a screened Cornell-type potential with a Debye-screened color-Coulomb term and a string term with a string-breaking cutoff. The potential is parameterized by four parameters controlling the temperature dependence of the Debye mass and the string screening mass, and the reaction rates include a K-factor enhancement of the quasifree dissociation rate to match open heavy-flavor diffusion constraints. A chi-squared fit to 53 data points for Upsilon(1S,2S,3S) nuclear modification factors from STAR, CMS, and ALICE at RHIC and LHC energies is performed, and the 95% confidence region in parameter space is mapped to extract the potential.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit chi-squared values of approximately 46 are obtained for K=1, 5, and 10 scenarios, with 49 degrees of freedom (95% confidence level corresponds to chi-squared below 66.3).
- The vacuum potential parameters are fixed to alpha_s = 0.298, string tension sigma = 0.220 GeV^2, and bare bottom mass m_b^0 = 4.719 GeV, yielding a good fit to vacuum masses of Upsilon(1S), Upsilon(2S), chi_b(1P), and chi_b(2P).
- The onset temperature for screening is T_o = 0.15 GeV, with a check at T_o = 0.13 GeV showing no significant differences.
- For the K=5 scenario, the extracted potential at T = 194 MeV remains close to the vacuum potential, while at T = 400 MeV it is substantially stronger than the K=1 scenario, closely matching the "strong-binding scenario" from T-matrix calculations of Liu (2017).
- The Upsilon(2S) suppression reaches 90% or more in semi/central collisions at the LHC, with regeneration starting at T less than about 250 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the quarkonium transport approach of Du (2017) by incorporating constraints from open heavy-flavor phenomenology, which requires large enhancements (K factors of 5-10) over perturbative QCD rates for heavy-quark diffusion as established by Liu (2018) and Rapp (2018). The extracted potential for K=5 closely matches the "strong-binding scenario" from T-matrix calculations of Liu (2017) that were constrained only by lattice QCD data, not by heavy-ion phenomenology. The results enable combined analyses of open and hidden heavy-flavor probes and support earlier findings by Riek (2010) and Liu (2017) that remnants of the confining force above the pseudo-critical temperature are essential for the strongly-coupled QGP.
