# Diffuse Gamma Rays from the Galactic Plane: Probing the "GeV Excess" and Identifying the "TeV Excess"

> Pion decay gamma rays have long been recognized as a unique signature of hadronic cosmic rays and their interactions with the interstellar medium. We present a model-independent way of constraining this signal with observations of the Galactic Plane in diffuse gamma rays. We combine detections by the EGRET instrument at GeV energies and the Milagro Cherenkov detector at TeV energies with upper limits from KASCADE and CASA-MIA ground arrays at PeV energies. Such a long "lever arm", spanning at least six orders of magnitude in energy, reveals a "TeV excess" in the diffuse Galactic Plane gamma-ray spectrum. While the origin of this excess is unknown, it likely implies also enhanced TeV neutrino fluxes, significantly improving the prospects for their detection. We show that unresolved point sources are a possible source of the TeV excess. In fact, the spectra of the unidentified EGRET sources in the Milagro region must break between ~10 GeV and ~1 TeV to avoid strongly overshooting the Milagro measurement; this may have important implications for cosmic-ray acceleration. Finally, we use our approach to examine the recent suggestion that dark-matter annihilation may account for the observed excess in diffuse Galactic gamma-rays detected by EGRET at energies above 1 GeV. Within our model-independent approach, current data cannot rule this possibility in or out; however we point out how a long "lever arm" can be used to constrain the pionic gamma-ray component and in turn limit the "GeV excess" and its possible sources. Experiments such as HESS and MAGIC, and the upcoming VERITAS and GLAST, should be able to finally disentangle the main sources of the Galactic gamma rays.

## Key claim

The Milagro detection of diffuse gamma-ray emission from the Galactic Plane at TeV energies is too large to be purely pionic in origin when using a realistic cosmic-ray spectral index and normalization, revealing a "TeV excess" that is distinct from the well-known "GeV excess" seen by EGRET; however, unresolved point sources could plausibly account for this excess if their spectra break between approximately 10 GeV and 1 TeV.

## Method

The authors combined EGRET detections at GeV energies (0.03-10 GeV) with the Milagro detection at 3.5 TeV and upper limits from KASCADE and CASA-MIA at PeV energies, all for the Galactic Plane region. They compared these data to the expected pionic gamma-ray flux from hadronic cosmic-ray interactions, using the characteristic spectral shape of pion decay (symmetric about the pion bump at m_pi/2 ~ 0.07 GeV) and two different assumptions for the cosmic-ray spectral index (alpha = 2.61 and alpha = 2.75). They also extrapolated the spectra of ten unidentified EGRET point sources in the Milagro region to TeV energies to evaluate whether unresolved sources could explain the excess.

## Key numbers

- Milagro integral flux: phi_gamma (>3.5 TeV) = (6.8 +/- 1.5 +/- 2.2) x 10^-11 photons cm^-2 s^-1 sr^-1 in the Galactic Plane region l in (40 deg, 100 deg), |b| < 5 deg
- For cosmic-ray spectral index alpha = 2.75 (locally observed), the maximal allowed pionic flux at 3.5 TeV is 5 times smaller than the Milagro measurement
- For the best-fit EGRET/Milagro index alpha = 2.61, the maximal pionic flux at 3.5 TeV is 66% of the observed Milagro result
- The combined extrapolated flux of ten unidentified EGRET sources at 3.5 TeV is approximately 2.5 x 10^-13 photons cm^-2 s^-1 GeV^-1 sr^-1, about a factor of 10 larger than the total diffuse emission measured by Milagro (approximately 3.0 x 10^-14 in the same units)
- The pionic component of diffuse Galactic gamma-ray emission is at most ~50% of the total, based on the lack of a strong pion bump feature at m_pi/2 ~ 0.07 GeV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the "GeV excess" first identified by Hunter (1997) in EGRET data and the detailed Galactic emission modeling by Strong, Moskalenko, and Reimer (2000, 2004). The authors show that the Milagro detection (Milagro Collaboration, 2005) cannot be explained by pionic emission from cosmic rays with the locally measured spectrum (JACEE, 1998), and that the dark-matter annihilation interpretation of the GeV excess proposed by de Boer (2005) would actually worsen the TeV excess. The results motivate targeted TeV observations of the ten unidentified EGRET sources in the Milagro region by HESS, MAGIC, VERITAS, and GLAST to determine whether the TeV excess arises from truly diffuse emission or unresolved point sources.
