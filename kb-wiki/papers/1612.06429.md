# Probing Extreme-Density Matter with Gravitational Wave Observations of Binary Neutron Star Merger Remnants

> We present a proof-of-concept study, based on numerical-relativity simulations, of how gravitational waves (GWs) from neutron star merger remnants can probe the nature of matter at extreme densities. Phase transitions and extra degrees of freedom can emerge at densities beyond those reached during the inspiral, and typically result in a softening of the equation of state (EOS). We show that such physical effects change the qualitative dynamics of the remnant evolution, but they are not identifiable as a signature in the GW frequency, with the exception of possible black-hole formation effects. The EOS softening is, instead, encoded in the GW luminosity and phase and is in principle detectable up to distances of the order of several Mpcs with advanced detectors and up to hundreds of Mpcs with third generation detectors. Probing extreme-density matter will require going beyond the current paradigm and developing a more holistic strategy for modeling and analyzing postmerger GW signals.

## Key claim

Gravitational waves from binary neutron star merger remnants can probe the equation of state at densities beyond those reached during inspiral, but this requires analyzing the GW luminosity and phase rather than the peak frequency alone, and the effect is only detectable up to approximately 20 Mpc with advanced detectors and approximately 200 Mpc with third-generation detectors.

## Method

The authors performed fully general-relativistic numerical simulations of seven neutron star binaries with total masses between 2.5 and 3.0 solar masses, using two equations of state: the nucleonic DD2 and the hyperonic BHB-Lambda-phi that includes Lambda-hyperons. They evolved the last approximately three orbits before merger and up to 21 ms after merger using the WhiskyTHC code with a resolution of approximately 185 m in the finest grid, including neutrino cooling. They then computed optimal signal-to-noise ratios and fitting factors for Advanced LIGO and the Einstein Telescope to quantify distinguishability between the two equations of state.

## Key numbers

- DD2 equation of state predicts maximum neutron star mass of 2.42 solar masses and R_1.6 = 13.17 km; BHB-Lambda-phi predicts maximum mass of 2.11 solar masses and R_1.6 = 13.27 km
- Nuclear saturation density taken as n_nuc = 0.16 fm^-3 (rho_nuc approximately 2.7 x 10^14 g cm^-3)
- For the most massive hyperonic binary (1.5+1.5 solar masses), maximum density increases from approximately 2 n_nuc to approximately 4.5 n_nuc immediately at merger
- Black hole formation occurs at 12.0 ms and 2.3 ms after merger for the most massive hyperonic models (1.4+1.4 and 1.5+1.5 solar masses, respectively)
- Peak frequency differences between equations of state are below approximately 50 Hz for most binaries, except the 1.4+1.4 binary with Delta f_2 approximately 250 Hz
- Advanced LIGO could distinguish between the two equations of state at distances up to approximately 20 Mpc; the Einstein Telescope up to approximately 200 Mpc
- For the 1.4+1.4 binary, the numerical resolution mismatch is a factor approximately 2 smaller than the mismatch due to hyperons

## Context

This work extends previous studies of postmerger GW signals by Bauswein (2011), Hotokezaka (2013), and Bernuzzi (2015) that established empirical relations between the postmerger peak frequency f_2 and neutron star properties, but found that f_2 is primarily sensitive to inspiral-phase densities. The results contradict the suggestion by Sekiguchi (2011) that hyperon production might be encoded in the frequency evolution of the GW signal. The paper enables a new Bayesian approach to postmerger GW data analysis using full waveform templates, which will require large banks of numerical waveforms or reduced-order models to probe the equation of state at the highest densities.
