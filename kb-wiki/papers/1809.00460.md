# Stability of alpha-chain States against Break-up and Binary Disintegrations

> We focus upon the raison d'etre of the alpha-chain states on the basis of the fully microscopic framework, where the Pauli principle among all the nucleons is fully taken into account. Our purpose is to find out the limiting number of alpha clusters, on which the linear alpha-cluster state can stably exist. How many alpha-clusters can stably make an alpha-chain state? We examine the properties of equally separated alpha clusters on a straight line and compare its stability with that on a circle. We also confirm its stability in terms of break-up and binary disintegrations including alpha-decay and fission modes. For the effective nucleon-nucleon interaction, we employ the F1 force, which has finite-range three-body terms and guarantees overall saturation properties of nuclei. This interaction also gives reasonable binding energy and size of the alpha particle and the alpha-alpha scattering phase shift. The result astonishes us because we can point out a possible existence of alpha-chain states with vast numbers of alpha clusters.

## Key claim

The paper claims that linear alpha-chain states can exist stably for vast numbers of alpha clusters (up to 60 studied, with the trend suggesting stability continues beyond), but this stability is conditional on the use of the F1 effective nucleon-nucleon interaction and the fully microscopic framework that respects the Pauli principle; the fission mode becomes more fragile as the number of clusters increases, making the chain susceptible to external perturbations.

## Method

The authors used the Brink-Bloch parameter space to construct fully antisymmetrized wave functions for n equally spaced alpha clusters in linear and annular configurations. They calculated adiabatic energy surfaces as functions of the nearest-neighbor distance between alpha clusters using the F1 effective interaction, which includes finite-range two- and three-body forces. They then examined stability against complete break-up into n alpha particles and against binary disintegrations into two blocks of m and (n-m) alpha clusters, including alpha-decay (m=1) and symmetric fission (m=n/2) modes.

## Key numbers

- For the linear chain, the energy pocket depth (E_max - E_min) per n converges to 4.2 MeV for large n (e.g., 4.884 MeV for n=20, 4.224 MeV for n=60).
- For the annular chain, the energy pocket depth per n converges to 3.9 MeV for large n (e.g., 4.854 MeV for n=20, 3.923 MeV for n=60).
- The optimal nearest-neighbor distance d_min for the linear chain is about 3.3 fm for all n (3.30 fm for n=20, 3.36 fm for n=60).
- The linear configuration becomes more stable than the annular one beyond n=12, as shown by the difference E_min(l) - E_min(a) becoming negative (-0.280 MeV for n=15, -0.785 MeV for n=60).
- For binary disintegrations of the linear chain, the minimum energy E_min is slightly negative for all n (e.g., -0.063 MeV for n=20, -0.135 MeV for n=60), indicating stability against splitting.
- The barrier height for the alpha-decay mode (m=1) remains almost constant across all n, while the fission mode barrier height decreases exponentially with n.
- The lowest allowed harmonic oscillator quanta N_min for the linear chain increases with n (180 for n=10, 420 for n=15, 7080 for n=60).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing study of alpha-chain states, which began with Morinaga's suggestion of the second 0+ state of 12C as a candidate, now understood as the Hoyle state described by the Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Roepke (THSR) wave function. Previous theoretical work by groups such as Maruhn (2010) and others showed that adding angular momentum or neutrons stabilizes linear-chain configurations against bending. The present paper goes beyond these studies by examining chains with up to 60 alpha clusters using the F1 force, which was developed by one of the authors (Tohsaki, 1994) and satisfies saturation properties and alpha-alpha scattering data without adjustable parameters. The results suggest that linear alpha-chain states could exist in astrophysical environments, though the fission mode's fragility means external forces could disrupt them.
