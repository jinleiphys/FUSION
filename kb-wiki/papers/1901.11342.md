# Possibilities of future double beta decay experiments to investigate inverted and normal ordering region of neutrino mass

> An overview of modern experiments on the search for neutrinoless double decay is presented. The obtained limits on the effective mass of the Majorana neutrino $\langle m_ν \rangle$ are discussed taking into account the uncertainties in the value of the nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) and the value of the axial-vector constant $g_A$. Predictions for the values of $\langle m_ν \rangle$ from the results of oscillation experiments and modern cosmological data are presented. The possibilities of the next generation experiments with sensitivity to $\langle m_ν \rangle$ at the level of $\sim$ 10-50 meV (studying mainly the inverted ordering (IO) region) are discussed. %Description of the most developed and promising projects is presented. The prospects for studying the normal ordering (NO) region are discussed too. It is shown that the possibilities of studying the NO depend on the mass of the lightest neutrino m$_0$. In the limiting case of small mass (m$_0$ $\le$ 0.1 meV), the values of $\langle m_ν \rangle$ $\approx$ 1-4 meV are predicted, which makes the study of this region inaccessible by the next generation experiments. But there is an allowed region of m$_0$ (7-30 meV) in the framework of NO, where the predicted values for $\langle m_ν \rangle$ could be $\sim$ 10-30 meV and that is quite achievable for the next generation experiments. The possibility to rich in the future sensitivity to $\langle m_ν \rangle$ at the level of $\sim$ 1-10 meV is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that next-generation experiments with sensitivity to the effective Majorana neutrino mass ⟨m_ν⟩ at the level of 10–50 meV can probe the inverted ordering region, but the prospects for studying the normal ordering region depend critically on the mass of the lightest neutrino m₀; if m₀ is in the range 7–30 meV, ⟨m_ν⟩ could be 10–30 meV and accessible, whereas if m₀ ≤ 0.1 meV, ⟨m_ν⟩ ≈ 1–4 meV would be inaccessible to next-generation experiments.

## Method

The author reviews current limits on ⟨m_ν⟩ from neutrinoless double beta decay experiments on ¹³⁶Xe, ⁷⁶Ge, ¹³⁰Te, ¹⁰⁰Mo, and ⁸²Se, incorporating uncertainties in nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) and the axial-vector constant g_A. Predictions for ⟨m_ν⟩ are derived from neutrino oscillation data (using 2σ and 3σ ranges from Capozzi et al. (2017)) and cosmological constraints on the sum of neutrino masses Σm_ν from the PLANCK collaboration (Σm_ν < 0.12 eV). The author then evaluates the sensitivity of planned experiments (CUPID, nEXO, LEGEND-1000) against the predicted ⟨m_ν⟩ ranges for both inverted and normal mass orderings.

## Key numbers

- Present conservative limit on ⟨m_ν⟩ from double beta decay experiments: 0.23 eV at 90% C.L.
- Predicted ⟨m_ν⟩ range for inverted ordering: 14–50 meV for all values of m₀
- For normal ordering with m₀ = 7–30 meV, predicted ⟨m_ν⟩: ∼10–30 meV
- For normal ordering with m₀ ≤ 0.1 meV (limiting case), predicted ⟨m_ν⟩: ∼1–4 meV
- PLANCK cosmological limit: Σm_ν < 0.12 eV, leading to m₀ < 30 meV (normal ordering) and m₀ < 16 meV (inverted ordering)
- Sensitivity of next-generation experiments (CUPID, nEXO, LEGEND-1000) at 90% C.L. is quoted as covering the IO range, but to observe the effect at 3σ level, sensitivity must be ∼1.9 times better than the 90% C.L. values shown in Table 3
- For ⟨m_ν⟩ = 3–10 meV, detectors containing ∼1–10 tons of ββ isotope are required, with sensitivity to T₁/₂ ∼ 10²⁸–10²⁹ yr
- For ⟨m_ν⟩ = 1–3 meV, detectors containing ∼10–100 tons of ββ isotope are required, with sensitivity to T₁/₂ ∼ 10²⁹–10³⁰ yr

## Context

This review extends the discussion of neutrino mass ordering from oscillation experiments (Capozzi et al. 2017) and cosmological data (PLANCK collaboration) to the experimental reach of future double beta decay searches. It builds on the standard formalism linking the half-life of 0νββ decay to ⟨m_ν⟩ via NMEs and g_A, as discussed in reviews by Vergados et al. (2016) and Suhonen (2017). The paper enables a roadmap for next-generation experiments: if the inverted ordering is realized, CUPID, nEXO, and LEGEND-1000 have the best chance of detection within 5–15 years; if the normal ordering is realized, the feasibility depends on m₀, with the range 10–30 meV accessible to those same experiments but lower values requiring ton-scale detectors as analyzed in Barabash (2018).
