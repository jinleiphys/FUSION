# Tasting Nuclear Pasta Made with Classical Molecular Dynamics Simulations

> Nuclear clusters or voids in the inner crust of neutron stars were predicted to have various shapes collectively nicknamed nuclear pasta. The recent review in Ref. \cite{Lopez1} by López, Dorso and Frank summarized their systematic investigations into properties especially the morphological and thermodynamical phase transitions of the nuclear pasta within a Classical Molecular Dynamics model, providing further stimuli to find more observational evidences of the predicted nuclear pasta in neutron stars.

# Tasting Nuclear Pasta Made with Classical Molecular Dynamics Simulations

## Key claim

The review by López, Dorso and Frank summarizes their systematic investigations into morphological and thermodynamical phase transitions of nuclear pasta using a Classical Molecular Dynamics model, but the author notes that the results are subject to possible model dependence, effects of poorly known nuclear physics inputs, finite-size effects in simulating infinite matter with periodic boundary conditions, and the lack of quantum effects.

## Method

López, Dorso and Frank used a Classical Molecular Dynamics model to simulate nucleonic matter and neutron star matter, varying temperature, density, and neutron-to-proton ratio, both with and without including electrons. They applied multiple morphological and thermodynamic tools to characterize the emerging structures in the neutron star crust and constructed phase diagrams for both nucleonic matter and neutron star matter. They also investigated the isospin dependence of critical points, morphologic phase transitions, and the symmetry energy of clustered matter.

## Key numbers

- The second component of GW190814 has a mass of (2.5–2.67) M$_{\odot}$, as discovered by LIGO/VIRGO.
- The exact transition density between the uniform core and inhomogeneous crust depends sensitively on the characteristics of nuclear symmetry energy, as noted in prior work by Li (2019), J. Xu, and Newton (2012).

## Context

This work extends the pioneering studies of nuclear pasta shapes by Ravenhall et al. and Hashimoto et al., as well as subsequent predictions of additional shapes such as gyroid, double-diamond, sponge-like, and parking-garage-like structures by various groups. The review by López, Dorso and Frank provides further stimuli to find observational evidence of nuclear pasta in neutron stars, which is important for understanding supernova explosions, protoneutron star cooling, neutrino transport, pulsar glitches, gravitational wave emission, and torsional oscillations of neutron stars. The main challenge remains finding unique signatures of nuclear pasta, as most astrophysical phenomena mentioned have alternative explanations without considering nuclear pasta in the inner crust.
