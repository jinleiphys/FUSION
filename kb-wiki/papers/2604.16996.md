# Machine Learning Insights into Discrepancies Between Theoretical and Experimental Fission Barrier Heights

> Accurate determination of nuclear fission barrier heights is essential for understanding nuclear stability, fission dynamics, and nucleosynthesis. However, theoretical models such as the Extended Thomas-Fermi plus Strutinsky Integral (ETFSI) approach and the macroscopic-microscopic calculations of Möller et al. exhibit systematic deviations from experiment, especially in regions of strong deformation and pronounced shell effects. In this work, machine learning is used as a diagnostic tool to analyze these discrepancies. Using the Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) algorithm within a residual-learning framework, the model learns corrections to ETFSI predictions from physically motivated nuclear features, including proton and neutron numbers, binding energies, separation energies, and pairing-related quantities. The model reproduces experimental barrier heights with root-mean-squared errors of about 0.3-1.2 MeV across training, test, and cross-validation datasets. Feature-importance analysis shows that inner barriers depend on binding-energy trends, mass and neutron-number effects, and pairing contributions, whereas outer barriers are governed more strongly by macroscopic quantities, particularly proton number, consistent with the dominant role of Coulomb repulsion and fissility at large deformation. These results show that machine learning can improve predictive accuracy while providing physically interpretable insight into the limitations of theoretical nuclear models.

## Key claim

Machine learning can serve as a diagnostic tool to analyze systematic discrepancies between theoretical and experimental fission barrier heights, revealing that inner and outer barriers are governed by different physical mechanisms: inner barriers depend on binding-energy trends, mass and neutron-number effects, and pairing contributions, while outer barriers are dominated by macroscopic quantities, particularly proton number and Coulomb repulsion.

## Method

The authors used the Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) algorithm within a residual-learning framework to learn corrections to Extended Thomas-Fermi plus Strutinsky Integral (ETFSI) predictions for inner and outer fission barrier heights. The model was trained on a hybrid dataset combining experimental data from the RIPL-2 database (45 inner barrier points from thorium to curium, 77 outer barrier points from mercury to curium) with ETFSI theoretical values (501 nuclei for inner barriers, 238 for outer barriers), using 14 physically motivated nuclear features including proton and neutron numbers, binding energies, separation energies, pairing-related quantities, and fissility terms. Shallow regression trees (maximum depth of two) were employed to enforce smooth, physically consistent corrections, with experimental data weighted ten times higher than theoretical data during training.

## Key numbers

- Training RMSE for inner barrier heights: 0.30 MeV with R² = 0.990
- Test RMSE for inner barrier heights: 0.62 MeV with R² = 0.969
- Five-fold cross-validation RMSE for inner barriers: 0.57 ± 0.05 MeV with R² = 0.963 ± 0.007
- Training RMSE for outer barrier heights: 0.48 MeV with R² = 0.994
- Test RMSE for outer barrier heights: 1.15 MeV with R² = 0.973
- Five-fold cross-validation RMSE for outer barriers: 1.23 ± 0.16 MeV with R² = 0.960 ± 0.009
- Experimental inner barrier dataset: 45 data points spanning isotopes from thorium (Z=90) to curium (Z=96)
- Experimental outer barrier dataset: 77 data points covering nuclei from mercury (Z=80) to curium (Z=96)
- ETFSI inner barrier dataset: 2299 nuclei from platinum (Z=78) to Z=120
- ETFSI outer barrier dataset: 1011 nuclei
- Final inner barrier dataset after merging: 501 nuclei
- Final outer barrier dataset after merging: 238 nuclei
- XGBoost learning rate: η = 0.3
- Number of regression trees: K = 100
- Maximum tree depth: 2 (terminal leaves ≤ 4)
- Sample weights: 1.0 for experimental data, 0.1 for theoretical data

## Context

This work extends the use of machine learning in nuclear physics beyond purely predictive tasks toward probing the limitations of theoretical models, building on prior applications including nuclear mass predictions by Utama (2016), beta-decay half-lives by Niu (2018), and fission fragment distribution modeling by McDonnell (2024). The study directly addresses systematic discrepancies between ETFSI calculations by Pearson (1996) and macroscopic-microscopic calculations by Moller et al., which can reach several MeV in regions of strong shell effects and deformation. The feature-importance analysis quantitatively clarifies the relative contributions of nuclear properties to inner and outer barrier discrepancies, enabling future refinement of theoretical models by identifying which physical mechanisms (pairing correlations, shell effects, Coulomb treatment) are most responsible for deviations from experiment.
