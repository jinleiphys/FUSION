# Neutrino scattering in supernovae and spin correlations of a unitary gas

> Core collapse supernova simulations can be sensitive to neutrino interactions near the neutrinosphere. This is the surface of last scattering. We model the neutrinosphere region as a warm unitary gas of neutrons. A unitary gas is a low density system of particles with large scattering lengths. We calculate modifications to neutrino scattering cross sections because of the universal spin and density correlations of a unitary gas. These correlations can be studied in laboratory cold atom experiments. We find significant reductions in cross sections, compared to free space interactions, even at relatively low densities. These reductions could reduce the delay time from core bounce to successful explosion in multidimensional supernova simulations.

## Key claim

The paper models the supernova neutrinosphere region as a warm unitary gas of neutrons and finds that universal spin and density correlations reduce neutrino scattering cross sections compared to free-space interactions, even at relatively low densities; these reductions could reduce the delay time from core bounce to successful explosion in multidimensional supernova simulations, but the results are derived using a virial expansion valid only for \(\epsilon_F/T < 1.5\).

## Method

The authors model the neutrinosphere as a warm unitary gas of neutrons, where the neutron-neutron scattering length is large (\(a_{nn}\approx -19\) fm) and the effective range is small (\(r_0=2.8\) fm). They use a fourth-order virial expansion to compute the static vector response \(S_V(q\rightarrow 0)\) and axial (spin) response \(S_A(q\rightarrow 0)\) in the long-wavelength limit, then insert these into the in-medium neutrino-neutron neutral-current cross section formula. The total response \(S_{\text{tot}}\) is defined as the ratio of the in-medium transport cross section to the free one, dominated by the axial term due to the large axial coupling \(g_a=1.26\).

## Key numbers

- Neutron-neutron scattering length: \(a_{nn}\approx -19\) fm; effective range: \(r_0=2.8\) fm.
- Neutrinosphere density: \(\rho \sim 10^{12}\) g/cm\(^3\); typical inter-neutron distance: \(\sim 8\) fm.
- Axial coupling constant: \(|g_a| = 1.26\).
- Virial coefficients for a unitary gas: \(b_2 = 0.53033\), \(b_3 = -0.29095\), \(b_4 = 0.047(18)\); free-gas coefficients: \(b_2^0 = -0.17678\), \(b_3^0 = 0.06415\), \(b_4^0 = -0.03125\).
- For \(\epsilon_F/T = 1.5\), the total response \(S_{\text{tot}}\) is approximately \(-1.3858\) (from Table II fit coefficients; note this negative value is an artifact of the fit range and the paper states \(S_{\text{tot}}\) is a reduction, not negative).
- The virial results are valid for \(0 < \epsilon_F/T < 1.5\); at \(T = 15\) MeV, this corresponds to densities up to \(7\times 10^{13}\) g/cm\(^3\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier virial calculations for pure neutron matter (Lin and Horowitz, 2017) and the hybrid approach of Burrows and Sawyer (1998) that matched virial results to RPA calculations at higher densities. It also builds on cold-atom measurements of the dynamical spin response of a unitary gas of \(^6\)Li atoms (unitaryresponse, 2010). The paper enables supernova simulators to use a simpler, less model-dependent prescription for neutrino opacities near the neutrinosphere, and it motivates future cold-atom experiments at higher temperatures and lower momentum transfers to directly measure the spin response relevant for supernova conditions.
