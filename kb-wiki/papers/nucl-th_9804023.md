# Charge-Asymmetry of the Nucleon-Nucleon Interaction

> Based upon the Bonn meson-exchange model for the nucleon-nucleon ($NN$) interaction, we study systematically the charge-symmetry-breaking (CSB) of the $NN$ interaction due to nucleon mass splitting. Particular attention is payed to CSB generated by the $2π$-exchange contribution to the $NN$ interaction, $πρ$ diagrams, and other multi-meson-exchanges. We calculate the CSB differences in the $^1S_0$ effective range parameters as well as phase shift differences in $S$, $P$ and higher partial waves up to 300 MeV lab. energy. We find a total CSB difference in the singlet scattering length of 1.6 fm which explains the empirical value accurately. The corresponding CSB phase-shift differences are appreciable at low energy in the $^1S_0$ state. In the other partial waves, the CSB splitting of the phase shifts is small and increases with energy, with typical values in the order of 0.1 deg at 300 MeV in $P$ and $D$ waves.

# Charge-Asymmetry of the Nucleon-Nucleon Interaction

## Key claim
The empirical charge-symmetry-breaking (CSB) difference in the singlet scattering length (1.5 ± 0.5 fm) can be explained entirely by nucleon mass splitting alone, without invoking additional mechanisms such as rho-omega mixing. The total CSB difference calculated from the Bonn meson-exchange model is 1.58 fm, which agrees with the empirical value, but this result depends on the assumed value of the pion-nucleon coupling constant.

## Method
The authors used the Bonn full meson-exchange model for the nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction, evaluating irreducible diagrams up to fourth order using time-ordered perturbation theory. They systematically calculated the charge-symmetry-breaking effects arising from the neutron-proton mass difference (1.2933 MeV) by switching step-by-step from proton-proton to neutron-neutron scattering, accounting for the exact proton mass (938.2723 MeV) and neutron mass (939.5656 MeV) in all diagrams. The CSB contributions were computed for kinetic energy effects, one-boson-exchange diagrams, two-pion exchange with NN, NΔ, and ΔΔ intermediate states, and πρ-exchange diagrams with NN, NΔ, and ΔΔ intermediate states, as well as further 3π and 4π contributions.

## Key numbers
- Total CSB difference in the singlet scattering length: 1.58 fm, compared to the empirical value of 1.5 ± 0.5 fm
- Proton mass: 938.2723 MeV; neutron mass: 939.5656 MeV; mass difference: 1.2933 MeV
- Kinetic energy contribution to the singlet scattering length difference: 0.25 fm (for nn vs. pp)
- Largest total CSB phase shift difference: 1.8 degrees in the ¹S₀ state at 1 MeV laboratory energy
- ¹S₀ phase shift difference at 300 MeV: 0.15 degrees
- Typical CSB phase shift differences in P and D waves at 300 MeV: approximately 0.1 degrees
- CSB phase shift differences for partial waves with L ≥ 3: less than 0.02 degrees at 300 MeV, 0.01 degrees or less at 200 MeV
- Using a smaller pion-nucleon coupling constant (g²π/4π = 13.6 instead of 14.4) reduces the total Δa_CSB by about 15%

## Context
This work extends previous studies of charge-symmetry-breaking in the nuclear force by providing a systematic calculation of all contributions from nucleon mass splitting within a consistent meson-exchange framework. The authors compare their results to Coon and Niskanen (1996), who obtained a similar total Δa_CSB of 1.56 fm but with very different individual contributions from 2πNN (1.28 fm vs. 0.37 fm in this work) and 2πNΔ (0.24 fm vs. 1.85 fm in this work). The finding that nucleon mass splitting alone can explain the empirical CSB fits with recent doubts about the traditional paradigm that rho-omega mixing is the dominant source of CSB, as discussed by Goldman, Henderson, and Thomas (1992), Piekarewicz and Williams (1993), and Krein, Thomas, and Williams (1993). The results enable future studies of CSB from delta-isobar mass splitting, which the authors note is not yet well known experimentally.
