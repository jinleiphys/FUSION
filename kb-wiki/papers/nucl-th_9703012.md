# Liquid-Gas Phase Transition in Nuclear Equation of State

> A canonical ensemble model is used to describe a caloric curve of nuclear liquid-gas phase transition. Allowing a discontinuity in the freeze out density from one spinodal density to another for a given initial temperature, the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition can be described as first order. Averaging over various freeze out densities of all the possible initial temperatures for a given total reaction energy, the first order characteristics of liquid-gas phase transition is smeared out to a smooth transition. Two experiments, one at low beam energy and one at high beam energy show different caloric behaviors and are discussed.

# Liquid-Gas Phase Transition in Nuclear Equation of State

## Key claim
The nuclear liquid-gas phase transition can appear as a sharp first-order transition when the freeze-out density jumps discontinuously between spinodal densities at a fixed initial temperature, but averaging over multiple initial temperatures for a given total reaction energy smears this into a smooth caloric curve. The different caloric behaviors observed in low-beam-energy versus high-beam-energy experiments arise from this averaging effect rather than from fundamentally different phase transition mechanisms.

## Method
The authors used a canonical ensemble model of nuclear multifragmentation where cluster distributions are weighted by free energies, with the freeze-out density as a free parameter. They calculated the excitation energy per nucleon as a function of temperature using Eq. (4), then allowed the freeze-out density to jump between the liquid-side and gas-side spinodal densities at a given initial temperature to produce a first-order transition. For comparison with experimental data, they averaged the caloric curves over a range of initial temperatures (0-2 MeV for low energy, 0-8 MeV for high energy) with different binding energy parameters.

## Key numbers
- For nuclear matter with a_B = 15.77 MeV: T_z = 15.16 MeV and E*_z = 17.7 MeV (the temperature and excitation energy where the zero-pressure state enters the spinodal instability region)
- The liquid-side spinodal energy E*_l = 2.1 MeV with T_l ≈ 5 MeV for T_i = 0 (the break-up temperature where a nuclear system fragments into two clusters on average)
- The gas-side spinodal energy E*_g = 16.15 MeV for zero temperature
- Low-energy data (600 MeV/A Au + Au) fitted with a_B = ε_0 = 10 MeV, spinodal densities 0.677 ρ_0 (liquid) and 0.015 ρ_0 (gas), averaging over T_i = 0-2 MeV
- High-energy data (1 GeV Au + C) fitted with a_B = ε_0 = 6 MeV, averaging over T_i = 0-8 MeV, with T_i = 8.7 MeV at critical multiplicity

## Context
This work extends the canonical ensemble model of nuclear multifragmentation developed in prior work (Lee and Mekjian, 1995) to explain the different caloric curves observed in low-energy experiments (Pochodzalla et al., 1995) showing indications of a sharp first-order phase transition, versus higher-energy experiments (Haettner et al., 1994) showing a much smoother transition. The analysis enables interpretation of heavy-ion collision data in terms of equilibrium thermodynamics and identifies that low-energy reactions with larger fragment sizes are better suited for extracting infinite nuclear matter properties, while high-energy collisions with T_max above the critical temperature are required for studying critical behavior.
