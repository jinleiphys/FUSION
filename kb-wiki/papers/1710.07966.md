# Short range correlations and the isospin dependence of nuclear correlation functions

> Pair densities and associated correlation functions provide a critical tool for introducing many-body correlations into a wide-range of effective theories. Ab initio calculations show that two-nucleon pair-densities exhibit strong spin and isospin dependence. However, such calculations are not available for all nuclei of current interest. We therefore provide a simple model, which involves combining the short and long separation distance behavior using a single blending function, to accurately describe the two-nucleon correlations inherent in existing ab initio calculations. We show that the salient features of the correlation function arise from the features of the two-body short-range nuclear interaction, and that the suppression of the pp and nn pair-densities caused by the Pauli principle is important. Our procedure for obtaining pair-density functions and correlation functions can be applied to heavy nuclei which lack ab initio calculations.

# Short range correlations and the isospin dependence of nuclear correlation functions

## Key claim
The authors provide a simple model that blends short-distance behavior from the contact formalism with long-distance uncorrelated behavior using a single blending function, and show that this model can reproduce ab initio Cluster Variational Monte Carlo (CVMC) two-body densities for both pp and pn pairs in 16O and 40Ca to within ±10%, with the caveat that the model requires contact coefficients that may be determined either from ab initio calculations or from experimental data.

## Method
The authors construct a two-body density model by combining a short-range contact formalism density (using zero-energy two-body wave functions from the AV18 potential) with a long-range uncorrelated density derived from one-body densities, joined by a blending function g(r) that equals 1 for r ≤ 0.9 fm and decays exponentially for larger separations. They then compute correlation functions as the ratio of the full two-body density to an uncorrelated density that includes Pauli exchange effects via a local-density approximation with a Fermi momentum of 200 MeV/c, and compare their results to CVMC calculations for 16O and 40Ca.

## Key numbers
- The blending function parameter a = 1.518 ± 0.001 fm when κ = 2, with the same function describing all pair types in both 16O and 40Ca.
- The peak value of the product C_A^{np,s=1} |φ_{np,s=1}(r)|^2 is four times larger than for any other spin-isospin combination.
- The model reproduces CVMC two-body densities for both pp and pn pairs in 16O and 40Ca to within ±10%.
- The Fermi momentum used in the Pauli exchange approximation is k̅_F = 200 MeV/c.
- For the parameterized correlation function F(r), the pp/nn parameters include α = 3.17 fm⁻², γ = 0.995, β₁ = 1.81 fm⁻², β₂ = 5.90 fm⁻³, β₃ = -9.87 fm⁻⁴; the pn parameters include α = 1.08 fm⁻², γ = 0.985, β₁ = -0.432 fm⁻², β₂ = -3.30 fm⁻³, β₃ = 2.01 fm⁻⁴.

## Context
This work extends the contact formalism of Weiss (2015, 2016) to construct correlation functions for nuclei too heavy for ab initio calculations, and provides a simpler alternative to the full CVMC approach of Pieper (1992) and Lonardoni (2017). The resulting correlation functions are compared to earlier models by Miller and Spencer (1975), Simkovic (2009), Alvioli (2005), Benhar (2014), and the UCOM calculation by Roth (2005); the authors find their results are close to Simkovic (2009) and Alvioli (2005) for 16O but differ from Benhar (2014) and Miller and Spencer (1975). The procedure enables predictions of two-body densities and correlation functions for heavy nuclei where ab initio calculations do not exist, with potential applications to neutrinoless double beta decay matrix elements.
