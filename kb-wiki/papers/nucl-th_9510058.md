# Rare-Earth Nuclei: Radii, Isotope-Shifts and Deformation Properties in the Relativistic Mean Field Theory

> A systematic study of the ground-state properties of even-even rare earth nuclei has been performed in the framework of the Relativistic Mean-Field (RMF) theory using the parameter set NL-SH. Nuclear radii, isotope shifts and deformation properties of the heavier rare-earth nuclei have been obtained, which encompass atomic numbers ranging from Z=60 to Z=70 and include a large range of isospin. It is shown that RMF theory is able to provide a good and comprehensive description of the empirical binding energies of the isotopic chains. At the same time the quadrupole deformations $β_{2}$ obtained in the RMF theory are found to be in good agreement with the available empirical values. The theory predicts a shape transition from prolate to oblate for nuclei at neutron number N=78 in all the chains. A further addition of neutrons up to the magic number 82 brings about the spherical shape. For nuclei above N=82, the RMF theory predicts the well-known onset of prolate deformation at about N=88, which saturates at about N=102. The deformation properties display an identical behaviour for all the nuclear chains. A good description of the above deformation transitions in the RMF theory in all the isotopic chains leads to a successful reproduction of the anomalous behaviour of the empirical isotopic shifts of the rare-earth nuclei. The RMF theory exhibits a remarkable success in providing a unified and microscopic description of various empirical data.

# Rare-Earth Nuclei: Radii, Isotope-Shifts and Deformation Properties in the Relativistic Mean Field Theory

## Key claim
The Relativistic Mean-Field (RMF) theory with the parameter set NL-SH provides a good and comprehensive description of binding energies, charge radii, isotope shifts, and quadrupole deformations for even-even rare-earth nuclei with atomic numbers Z=60 to Z=70, but the agreement is limited to the pure mean-field level without angular momentum or particle number projection, and deviations from empirical binding energies are at most 0.3%.

## Method
The authors performed relativistic Hartree calculations for six isotopic chains (Nd, Sm, Gd, Dy, Er, Yb) using the RMF theory with the NL-SH parameter set, solving the Dirac equation via oscillator expansion in an axially symmetric harmonic-oscillator basis with 12 shells. Pairing was included using the BCS formalism with constant pairing gaps taken from empirical separation energies or extrapolated as Δ = 11.2/√N(Z) MeV, and charge radii were obtained by folding proton point densities with a Gaussian proton form factor (rms radius 0.8 fm).

## Key numbers
- Binding energies: RMF values deviate from empirical data by at most 0.3%; rms deviation of about 2 MeV across all isotopic chains
- Quadrupole deformation β₂ for ¹⁶⁶Er: 0.34 in the ground state
- Shape transition from prolate to oblate predicted at neutron number N=78 in all chains
- Onset of prolate deformation above N=82 at about N=88, saturating at about N=102
- Saturation quadrupole deformation values: β₂ = 0.30–0.35 for most chains
- Oscillator parameter b₀ = 41 A⁻¹/³ used for basis definition
- Charge radius formula: r_c = √(r_p² + 0.64) with proton rms radius 0.8 fm
- Pairing gap formula: Δ = 11.2/√N(Z) MeV for extrapolated cases
- Compressibility modulus K ≈ 355 MeV for NL-SH and SIII forces

## Context
This work extends previous RMF studies of Pb isotope shifts (Lalazissis, Sharma, Ring 1993) and systematic studies of Kr, Sr, and Zr isotopes (Lalazissis and Sharma 1995) into the rare-earth region. It provides a systematic alternative to earlier non-relativistic calculations using Skyrme forces (Vautherin 1973, Quentin and Flocard 1978) and Gogny forces (Gogny 1975), as well as earlier relativistic calculations with the NL1 force (Gambhir, Ring, Thimet 1990) that were limited to a few representative nuclei. The results enable comparison with experimental isotope shift measurements by Neugart et al. (1982, 1983, 1985) and the Leningrad-Troitz collaboration (Alkhazov et al. 1987, 1988), and with mass model predictions from the Finite Range Droplet Model (Moller, Nix, Myers 1995) and Extended Thomas-Fermi plus Strutinsky Integral (Aboussir, Pearson, Dutta, Tondeur 1992).
