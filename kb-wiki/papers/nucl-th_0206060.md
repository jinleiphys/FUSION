# Tracking the phase-transition energy in disassembly of hot nuclei

> In efforts to determine phase transitions in the disintegration of highly excited heavy nuclei, a popular practice is to parametrise the yields of isotopes as a function of temperature in the form $Y(z)=z^{-τ}f(z^σ(T-T_0))$, where $Y(z)$'s are the measured yields and $τ, σ$ and $T_0$ are fitted to the yields. Here $T_0$ would be interpreted as the phase transition temperature. For finite systems such as those obtained in nuclear collisions, this parametrisation is only approximate and hence allows for extraction of $T_0$ in more than one way. In this work we look in detail at how values of $T_0$ differ, depending on methods of extraction. It should be mentioned that for finite systems, this approximate parametrisation works not only at the critical point, but also for first order phase transitions (at least in some models). Thus the approximate fit is no guarantee that one is seeing a critical phenomenon. A different but more conventional search for the nuclear phase transition would look for a maximum in the specific heat as a function of temperature $T_2$. In this case $T_2$ is interpreted as the phase transition temperature. Ideally $T_0$ and $T_2$ would coincide. We invesigate this possibility, both in theory and from the ISiS data, performing both canonical ($T$) and microcanonical ($e=E^*/A$) calculations. Although more than one value of $T_0$ can be extracted from the approximate parmetrisation, the work here points to the best value from among the choices. Several interesting results, seen in theoretical calculations, are borne out in experiment.

# Tracking the phase-transition energy in disassembly of hot nuclei

## Key claim

The popular parametrization of isotope yields as $Y(z)=z^{-\tau}f(z^{\sigma}(T-T_0))$ is only approximate for finite nuclear systems and works not only at the critical point but also for first-order phase transitions, meaning a good fit does not guarantee critical behavior. The Coulomb force causes different extraction methods for the phase-transition energy to diverge, and the specific-heat maximum ($e_2$) or the minimum of the power-law deviation ($e_1'$) provide better measures of the phase-transition energy than the scaling-based extraction ($e_1$).

## Method

The authors performed microcanonical lattice gas model (LGM) simulations and canonical thermodynamic model calculations for a $^{197}$Au system ($Z=79$, $N=118$) at a freeze-out density of $0.27\rho_0$, both with and without Coulomb interactions. They extracted three candidate phase-transition energies: $e_1$ from the best scaling collapse of yields, $e_1'$ from the minimum of $\chi^2$ in power-law fits, and $e_2$ from the maximum of the specific heat $C_V$. The theoretical results were compared to experimental data from 8 GeV/c $\pi^-$ on gold collisions from the ISiS collaboration.

## Key numbers

- LGM without Coulomb: $e_1 = 11.66$ MeV, $e_2 = 12.61$ MeV, $e_1' = 13.88$ MeV (all close, within about 2 MeV)
- LGM with Coulomb: $e_1 = 3.60$ MeV, $e_2 = 8.38$ MeV, $e_1' = 10.5$ MeV (large splitting, $e_1$ drops well below $e_2$)
- Thermodynamic model without Coulomb: $e_1 = 6.77$ MeV, $e_2 = 8.01$ MeV, $e_1' = 7.54$ MeV ($\Delta e = e_2 - e_1 = 1.24$ MeV)
- Thermodynamic model with Coulomb: $e_1 = 0.75$ MeV, $e_2 = 6.03$ MeV, $e_1' = 4.94$ MeV ($\Delta e = e_2 - e_1 = 5.28$ MeV)
- ISiS experimental data: $e_1' \approx 4$ MeV, $e_2 \approx 4$ MeV, with $\tau \approx 2.1$ and $\sigma \approx 0.53$
- Thermodynamic model with Coulomb for $^{197}$Au: $e_2 = 6.03$ MeV, $\sigma = 0.96$ (higher than experimental values)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of phase transitions in nuclear fragmentation by comparing the yield-parametrization method (used by Scharenberg (2001) and Gulminelli and Chomaz (1999)) with the specific-heat method (following the caloric curve measurements of Pochodzalla (1995)). The authors show that the Coulomb force, often neglected in scaling analyses, causes the scaling-based extraction $e_1$ to diverge from the other measures, resolving ambiguities in prior work. The results enable a consistent interpretation of the ISiS data, where $e_1'$ and $e_2$ coincide at about 4 MeV, in agreement with the critical excitation energies found by Elliott (2002) using Fisher droplet model analysis and by Berkenbusch (2002) using percolation theory, while the thermodynamic model suggests a first-order phase transition with the boiling point at a similar energy.
