# The Glauber model and the heavy ion reaction cross section

> We reexamine the Glauber model and calculate the total reaction cross section as a function of energy in the low and intermediate energy range, where many of the corrections in the model, are effective. The most significant effect in this energy range is by the modification of the trajectory due to the Coulomb field. The modification in the trajectory due to nuclear field is also taken into account in a self consistent way. The energy ranges in which particular corrections are effective, are quantified and it is found that when the center of mass energy of the system becomes 30 times the Coulomb barrier, none of the trajectory modification to the Glauber model is really required. The reaction cross sections for light and heavy systems, right from near coulomb barrier to intermediate energies have been calculated. The exact nuclear densities and free nucleon-nucleon (NN) cross sections have been used in the calculations. The center of mass correction which is important for light systems, has also been taken into account. There is an excellent agreement between the calculations with the modified Glauber model and the experimental data. This suggests that the heavy ion reactions in this energy range can be explained by the Glauber model in terms of free NN cross sections without incorporating any medium modification.

## Key claim

The paper reexamines the Glauber model for heavy-ion total reaction cross sections and finds that, when the center-of-mass energy of the system reaches 30 times the Coulomb barrier, none of the trajectory modifications (Coulomb or nuclear) to the standard Glauber model are required. The study claims that heavy-ion reactions in the low-to-intermediate energy range can be explained using free nucleon-nucleon cross sections without any medium modification, contrary to some prior work.

## Method

The author calculates total reaction cross sections using the Glauber model with three levels of trajectory modification: the standard straight-line Glauber model (GM), the Coulomb-modified Glauber model (CMGM) which replaces the impact parameter with the distance of closest approach under the Coulomb field, and the Coulomb-plus-nuclear modified Glauber model (CNMGM) which self-consistently includes both Coulomb and nuclear potentials in the trajectory. Exact two-parameter Fermi or harmonic oscillator nuclear densities are used, along with parameterized free nucleon-nucleon cross sections, and center-of-mass corrections are applied for light systems. Calculations are performed for systems including 12C and 16O projectiles on various targets from 12C to 208Pb, for energies up to 50 times the Coulomb barrier.

## Key numbers

* For the 12C + 12C system at 9.33 MeV/nucleon, the measured reaction cross section is 1444 mb; at 35.00 MeV/nucleon it is 1259 mb.
* For the 16O + 16O system at 2.0 MeV/nucleon, the reaction cross section is 73.86 mb; at 30.00 MeV/nucleon it is 1655 mb.
* For the 16O + 208Pb system at 5.00 MeV/nucleon, the reaction cross section is 124.1 mb; at 94.00 MeV/nucleon it is 3600 mb.
* The Coulomb barrier for each system is calculated as V_C = Z_P Z_T * 1.44 / (1.5 (A^{1/3} + B^{1/3})) MeV; for example, 12C + 12C has V_C/M = 1.258 and 12C + 208Pb has V_C/M = 5.068.
* When the center-of-mass energy reaches 30 times the Coulomb barrier, the CNMGM and GM calculations agree within 2 to 3%.
* The range parameter r_0 for the NN profile function is taken as 0.6 fm for energies from 2 to 200 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the Coulomb-modified Glauber model (CMGM) developed by earlier authors (Charagi and Gupta, 1990) and the Coulomb-plus-nuclear modified Glauber model (CNMGM) introduced by Shukla (2001). It directly contradicts the findings of Cai and Feng (1999) and P.N.U. (2000), who argued that medium modifications to nucleon-nucleon cross sections are necessary; the present study shows that free NN cross sections suffice when exact densities and proper trajectory corrections are used. The results enable reliable extraction of nuclear sizes from total reaction cross section measurements across a wide energy range, from near the Coulomb barrier to intermediate energies, without requiring density-dependent NN cross sections.
