# Freeze-out conditions for production of resonances, hadronic molecules, and light nuclei

> We investigate the freeze-out conditions of a particle in an expanding system of interacting particles in order to understand the productions of resonances, hadronic molecules and light nuclei in heavy ion collisions. Applying the kinetic freeze-out condition with explicit hydrodynamic calculations for the expanding hadronic phase to the daughter particles of $K^*$ mesons, we find that the larger suppression of the yield ratio of $K^*/K$ at LHC than at RHIC compared to the expectations from the statistical hadronization model based on chemical freeze-out parameters reflects the lower kinetic freeze-out temperature at LHC than at RHIC. Furthermore, we point out that for the light nuclei or hadronic molecules that are bound, the yields are affected by the freeze-out condition of the respective particle in the hadronic matter, which leads to the observation that the deuteron production yields are independent of the size of deuteron, and depend only on the number of ground state constituents.

# Freeze-out conditions for production of resonances, hadronic molecules, and light nuclei

## Key claim

The larger suppression of the $K^*/K$ yield ratio at LHC compared to RHIC, relative to statistical hadronization model predictions based on chemical freeze-out parameters, is explained by the lower kinetic freeze-out temperature at LHC (about 120–125 MeV) than at RHIC (about 130–135 MeV), as demonstrated by applying the kinetic freeze-out condition to daughter particles in explicit hydrodynamic calculations. For bound states like deuterons, the yields depend primarily on the number of ground-state constituents rather than the size of the bound state, because the freeze-out condition relates cross section to freeze-out radius.

## Method

The authors applied the kinetic freeze-out condition $\tau_{exp} = \tau_{scatt}^i$ to pions in an expanding hadronic system, using hydrodynamic simulations with boost-invariance and symmetric transverse expansion for central collisions at RHIC and LHC. They solved entropy-conservation equations with a lattice equation of state, assuming an initial thermalization time of 0.5 fm/c, and compared the expansion time $\tau_{exp} = V/(dV/dt)$ to two estimates of the pion scattering time: one using $\sigma_{\pi}=40$ mb, $\langle v\rangle=0.7c$, and thermal pion densities, and another using a parameterized scattering time $1/\tau_{scatt}^{\pi} = (59.5\ \text{fm}^{-1})(T/1\ \text{GeV})^{3.45}$ multiplied by a phenomenological factor $\xi=0.295$. For $K^*$ mesons, they considered simplified rate equations for $K^*$ and $K$ abundances during the hadronic stage, with scattering times involving interactions with pions and $\rho$ mesons.

## Key numbers

- Kinetic freeze-out temperature at RHIC: about 130 MeV (from $\tau_{scatt}^{\pi,a}$) or about 135 MeV (from $\tau_{scatt}^{\pi,b}$)
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature at LHC: about 120 MeV (from $\tau_{scatt}^{\pi,a}$) or about 125 MeV (from $\tau_{scatt}^{\pi,b}$)
- Pion total cross section in hadronic medium taken as 40 mb
- Average relative velocity $\langle v \rangle = 0.7c$ used in scattering time estimate
- Parameterized pion scattering time: $1/\tau_{scatt}^{\pi} = (59.5\ \text{fm}^{-1})(T/1\ \text{GeV})^{3.45}$, multiplied by $\xi=0.295$
- Initial thermalization time for hydrodynamic simulations: 0.5 fm/c

## Context

This work extends the statistical hadronization model framework of Braun-Munzinger et al. (1994–2001) by incorporating kinetic freeze-out conditions to resolve discrepancies between statistical model predictions and experimental $K^*/K$ ratios measured at RHIC (Adams et al., 2004) and LHC (Abelev et al., 2014). The authors build on their own earlier rate-equation analysis (Cho et al., 2015) and on hydrodynamic modeling approaches (Kolb et al., 2000; Schenke et al., 2010; Song et al., 2010; Borsanyi et al., 2010). The results enable a unified understanding of resonance, hadronic molecule, and light nucleus production in heavy ion collisions, suggesting that measurements of these yields can probe the duration and temperature of the hadronic stage.
