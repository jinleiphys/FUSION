# Compact Ultradense Objects in the Solar System

> We describe properties and gravitational interactions of meteor-mass and greater compact ultra dense objects with nuclear density or greater (CUDO s). We discuss possible enclosure of CUDO s in comets, stability of these objects on impact with the Earth and Sun and show that the hypothesis of a CUDO core helps resolve issues challenging the understanding of a few selected cometary impacts.

# Compact Ultradense Objects in the Solar System

## Key claim

Compact ultradense objects (CUDOs) with masses below the microlensing exclusion limit and above a lower mass threshold set by tidal disruption could explain anomalous cometary impacts and cratering events where the recovered impactor mass is far smaller than required by conventional models, though the authors acknowledge that each individual example alone would not create a case for CUDOs.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for gravitationally self-bound objects composed of either fermionic dark matter particles supported by Fermi-degeneracy pressure or massless particles confined by a vacuum (bag) pressure, analogous to neutron stars and quark stars respectively. They derive mass-radius relations and identify an upper mass limit from gravitational collapse instability and a lower mass limit from tidal disruption by a planetary target body, then apply these constraints to historical impact events including the Barringer Meteor Crater, the Tunguska event, and the AD 536 event.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass for a Fermi-degeneracy-supported CUDO: \( M_{\rm max} = \frac{0.209}{(g/2)^{1/2}} \left(\frac{1\:{\rm TeV}}{m_{\chi}}\right)^{2} M_{\oplus} \), with radius \( R = \frac{0.809}{(g/2)^{1/2}} \left(\frac{1\:{\rm TeV}}{m_{\chi}}\right)^{2} \) cm, where \( m_{\chi} \) is the dark matter particle mass and \( g \) its degeneracy.
- Maximum mass for a vacuum-pressure-supported CUDO: \( M_{\rm max} = \frac{0.014}{(g/2)^{1/2}} \left(\frac{1\:{\rm TeV}}{B^{1/4}}\right)^{2} M_{\oplus} \), with radius \( R = \frac{0.023}{(g/2)^{1/2}} \left(\frac{1\:{\rm TeV}}{B^{1/4}}\right)^{2} \) cm, where \( B^{1/4} \) is the confining scale.
- The stability domain extends down to masses as low as \( 10^{-19} M_{\oplus} = 6 \times 10^{5} \) kg for vacuum-pressure-supported CUDOs, with corresponding radius \( 10^{-17} R_{\oplus} = 0.6 \) Angstrom.
- Microlensing surveys exclude MACHOs with masses above \( M > 2 \times 10^{-2} M_{\oplus} \) as more than 20% of the Milky Way halo dark matter.
- The largest recovered meteorite from the Barringer Meteor Crater is 639 kg, far less than the required impactor mass.
- Comet C/2011 W3 (Lovejoy) had a residual nucleus mass estimated at approximately \( 10^{12} \) g (a sphere about 150-200 m across) after passing through the solar corona.

## Context

This work extends the study of compact dark matter objects from prior TOV equation solutions by Narain (2006) and Dietl (2011) to consider their interactions with solar system bodies, adding for the first time an effective lower mass limit from tidal disruption. The CUDO hypothesis is presented as an alternative to conventional impactor models that have struggled to explain events such as the Barringer Meteor Crater, where modeling by Artemieva (2009) could not achieve a consistent description within known impactor structure, and the survival of Comet Lovejoy, which Sekanina (2012) found at odds with the rubble-pile comet model. The work enables future searches for CUDOs through anomalous high-density measurements in solar system bodies and potential detection by gravitometer satellites such as LISA-Pathfinder.
