# On the equilibrium of self-gravitating neutrons, protons and electrons in β-equilibrium

> We have recently proved the impossibility of imposing the condition of local charge neutrality in a self-gravitating system of degenerate neutrons, protons and electrons in $β$-equilibrium. The coupled system of the general relativistic Thomas-Fermi equations and the Einstein-Maxwell equations have been shown to supersede the traditional Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. Here we present the Newtonian limit of the new equilibrium equations. We also extend the treatment to the case of finite temperatures and finally we give the explicit demonstration of the constancy of the Klein potentials in the case of finite temperatures generalizing the condition of constancy of the general relativistic Fermi energies in the case of zero temperatures.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the condition of local charge neutrality is inconsistent with the Einstein-Maxwell equations and microphysical equilibrium conditions for self-gravitating degenerate neutrons, protons, and electrons in beta-equilibrium; the new equilibrium equations produce a gravito-electrodynamic structure that persists in both the Newtonian and general relativistic regimes, and the constancy of the Klein potentials holds for finite temperatures as well as zero temperature.

## Method

The authors solve the coupled system of general relativistic Thomas-Fermi equations and Einstein-Maxwell equations for a spherically symmetric, non-rotating configuration of degenerate neutrons, protons, and electrons in beta-equilibrium, superseding the traditional Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. They derive the Newtonian limit by taking the weak-field, non-relativistic (c to infinity) limit of the general relativistic equations. For finite temperatures, they replace the zero-temperature equation of state with Fermi-Dirac distributions and use the Tolman isothermal condition to demonstrate the constancy of the Klein potentials, then integrate the resulting system numerically for given temperatures.

## Key numbers

- Central density of the example configuration: rho(0) = 3.94 rho_nuc, where rho_nuc = 2.7 x 10^14 g cm^-3 (nuclear density).
- Neutron radius R_n = 12.735 km, proton radius R_p = 12.863 km, electron radius R_e = R_p + 10^3 lambda_e, where lambda_e = hbar/(m_e c) is the electron Compton wavelength.
- Fermi temperatures for typical neutron star central densities: electrons ~10^12 K, protons ~10^11 K, neutrons ~10^13 K.
- Thermal energy for T ~10^8 K: E_th ~10^38 erg; Coulomb energy from internal electric field: E_C ~10^16 erg.
- Finite temperature test case: T_infinity = 2.3 x 10^5 K, with results qualitatively and quantitatively similar to the degenerate case.
- For distances r << R_n at densities > rho_nuc, electrodynamical properties remain unperturbed even for T_infinity ~10^11 K.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous general relativistic treatment (Rotondo et al., 2011; Rotondo et al., 2011) which proved the impossibility of local charge neutrality in such systems, and generalizes the Feynman-Metropolis-Teller treatment of compressed atoms to relativistic regimes. The Newtonian limit shows the gravito-electrodynamic structure is not purely relativistic, but general relativity remains mandatory for accurate neutron star properties. The finite-temperature extension demonstrates that thermal effects do not alter the electromagnetic structure, and the constancy of Klein potentials generalizes the zero-temperature Fermi energy condition. This enables future work including additional particle species and strong interactions in neutron star models.
