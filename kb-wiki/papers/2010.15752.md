# "Tiny galaxies" from "little bang"

> We show, by solving the linearized Vlasov equations, that in the presence of an attractive density-dependent vector, or tensor, mean field potential, under a portion of the spinodal unstable phase region where the attractions among the quarks are more dominant, the transverse modes, i.e., the fluctuations of the current densities perpendicular to the wave vector, are activated due to relativity. In particular, the current densities in both the transverse directions oscillate with the same frequency, indicating the existence of the spontaneous rotations of the dense clusters. These rotating clusters would certainly create a local vortical flow field which, according to chiral (or spin) vortical effect, leads to the macroscopic separation of the axial charges or spins due to the spin-orbital interactions. Hence, for the first time, the connection between the axial charge or spin in-homogeneity and the first-order phase transition is proposed.

## Key claim

By solving the linearized Vlasov equations, the authors show that transverse current-density fluctuations (perpendicular to the wave vector) are activated due to relativity in the presence of an attractive density-dependent vector or tensor mean-field potential, but only under a portion of the spinodal unstable phase region where quark attractions are sufficiently strong; the resulting rotating dense clusters would create local vortical flow that, via the chiral or spin vortical effect, could produce macroscopic separation of axial charges or spins.

## Method

The authors solved the linearized Vlasov equations for a quark system with a density-dependent vector mean-field potential, expanding the phase-space distribution function around equilibrium (Fermi-Dirac) and keeping only leading-order fluctuations. By Fourier transforming the fluctuations and integrating over momentum space, they derived homogeneous linear equations for the density and current-density fluctuations, separating longitudinal (sound) modes from transverse modes. The eigen-frequencies of the transverse modes were obtained from the condition \(U'(\rho^*)\chi_T(\omega,k)=1\), and the response function \(\chi_T\) was evaluated for both purely real and purely imaginary frequencies to determine the phase regions where transverse modes are activated or produce spontaneous rotation.

## Key numbers

* At \(T=50\) MeV and net quark density \(n=0.45\) fm\(^{-3}\), the transverse response function \(\chi_T(\omega/k)\) achieves its minimum value \(\chi_{T\rm{min}}\) at approximately \(\omega/k \approx 0.94\) for purely real frequencies.
* The mean-field potential used in the example is \(U(\rho^*)=A(\rho^*/\rho_0)+B(\rho^*/\rho_0)^\sigma\) with parameters \(A=-700\) MeV, \(B=100\) MeV, \(\sigma=2.6\), \(\rho_0=0.5\) fm\(^{-3}\), and quark mass \(m=6\) MeV.
* The spinodal unstable phase region is bounded by the isothermal spinodal criterion \((\partial p/\partial \rho^*)_T < 0\), which is equivalent to \(U'(\rho^*) < \chi_L^{*-1}\) where \(\chi_L^* = -(\partial \rho^*/\partial \mu^*)_T\).
* The criterion for spontaneous cluster rotation is \(\chi_T^{*-1} < U'(\rho^*) < \chi_{T\rm{min}}^{-1}\), where \(\chi_T^*\) is the asymptotic value of \(\chi_T\) as \(\omega/k \to \infty\).

## Context

This work extends the conventional understanding of spinodal instability in first-order phase transitions of quark matter, which was previously characterized only by longitudinal (sound) modes with imaginary speed of sound (Randrup 2010, Steinheimer 2012, Li 2015). The authors show that transverse modes can be activated when attractions are stronger than those needed for spinodal instability alone, and they connect this to the chiral vortical effect (Vilenkin 1979, Son 2009, Kharzeev 2010) to propose a new link between first-order phase transitions and axial charge or spin inhomogeneity observed at RHIC. The work enables future transport simulations based on the covariant equations of motion and the anomalous Lorentz transformation for massive particles (Li 2020) to visualize cluster rotation and investigate spin inhomogeneity from the spin vortical effect.
