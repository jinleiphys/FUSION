# Cold atoms beyond atomic physics

> In the last 25 years, much progress has been made producing and controlling Bose-Einstein condensates (BECs) and degenerate Fermi gases. The advances in trapping, cooling and tuning the interparticle interactions in these cold atom systems lead to an unprecedented amount of control that one can exert over them. This work aims to show that knowledge acquired studying cold atom systems can be applied to other fields that share similarities and analogies with them, provided that the differences are also known and taken into account. We focus on two specific fields, nuclear physics and statistical optics. The nuclear physics discussion occurs with the BCS-BEC crossover in mind, in which we compare cold Fermi gases with nuclear and neutron matter and nuclei. We connect BECs and atom lasers through both systems' matter-wave character for the analogy with statistical optics. Finally, we present some challenges that, if solved, would increase our understanding of cold atom systems and, thus, the related areas.

## Key claim

The paper argues that knowledge from cold atom systems can be applied to nuclear physics and statistical optics through analogies, but only when the differences between the systems are also understood and accounted for; the BCS-BEC crossover framework is particularly useful for comparing cold Fermi gases with nuclear and neutron matter, while the matter-wave character of BECs enables analogies with statistical optics.

## Method

The authors review the BCS-BEC crossover in two-component atomic Fermi gases, describing how Feshbach resonances allow tuning of the scattering length to realize the crossover from BCS superfluidity to molecular BEC. They then compare this framework to nuclear physics systems including symmetric nuclear matter (deuteron pairing), asymmetric nuclear matter, neutron-neutron pairing, alpha particle condensation, and nuclei, using quantum Monte Carlo methods and BCS gap equation solutions from the literature. For the statistical optics analogy, they connect ground-state BECs and turbulent BECs to optical Gaussian beams and elliptical speckle light maps based on the work of Tavares (2017).

## Key numbers

- The typical Fermi temperature in cold atom experiments is approximately 1 microkelvin, with experiments reaching hundreds of nanokelvin temperatures
- Number density in cold atom systems is close to 10^13 cm^-3, giving a typical interparticle spacing close to 500 nm
- The nn s-wave scattering length is a_nn = -18.5 fm, with effective range r^e_nn = 2.7 fm
- The deuteron binding energy is approximately 2.23 MeV, only 0.1% of its rest mass
- The deuteron rms radius is 2.8 fm, close to the value for 20Ne
- The Bertsch parameter xi = 0.372(5) from quantum Monte Carlo results at unitarity
- The maximum ratio of the pairing gap to Fermi energy in neutron matter is Delta/E_F approximately 0.3, reached at -1/(k_F a_nn) = 0.2
- The deuteron size in matter decreases until a minimum at density n approximately 0.036 fm^-3
- The ratio |r_e^nn / a^nn| is approximately 0.15 for neutron matter

## Context

This review extends the line of work comparing cold atom systems to nuclear physics, building on prior comparisons by Zinner (2013), Strinati (2018), and others who have noted the short-range character of interactions in both fields. The paper connects the BCS-BEC crossover framework from cold atoms to nuclear systems including neutron stars and nuclei, following earlier theoretical work by Eagles (1969) and Leggett (2008) on crossover concepts. The statistical optics analogy extends the work of Tavares (2017) who found that ground-state and turbulent BECs share analogies with optical beam propagation. The paper enables future cross-disciplinary work by identifying specific challenges that, if solved in cold atom systems, would advance understanding in nuclear physics and statistical optics.
