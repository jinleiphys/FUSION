# The continuum discretized coupled-channels method and its applications

> This is a review on recent developments of the continuum discretized coupled-channels method (CDCC) and its applications to nuclear physics, cosmology and astrophysics, and nuclear engineering. The theoretical foundation of CDCC is shown, and a microscopic reaction theory for nucleus-nucleus scattering is constructed as an underlying theory of CDCC. CDCC is then extended to treat Coulomb breakup and four-body breakup. We also propose a new theory that makes CDCC applicable to inclusive reactions

## Key claim

This review paper argues that the continuum discretized coupled-channels method (CDCC) is an accurate and widely applicable framework for treating projectile breakup in nuclear reactions, but its validity depends on the \(\ell\)-truncation approximation being the most essential approximation, with the \(k\)-truncation and discretization of the continuum being secondary and controllable through convergence checks.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical foundation of three-body CDCC, which expands the total wave function of an A+b+c three-body system using eigenfunctions of the b+c subsystem Hamiltonian, truncates the continuum states by maximum orbital angular momentum \(\ell_{\mathrm{max}}\) and maximum linear momentum \(k_{\mathrm{max}}\), and discretizes the \(k\)-continuum using the average (Av), midpoint (Mid), or pseudo-state (PS) methods. The authors then present extensions: eikonal-CDCC (E-CDCC) for Coulomb-dominated breakup, four-body CDCC for projectiles with three-body structure, and the eikonal reaction theory (ERT) for inclusive reactions, all built upon a microscopic reaction theory for nucleus-nucleus scattering based on the multiple scattering theory with effective nucleon-nucleon interactions.

## Key numbers

* The \(\ell\)-truncation and \(k\)-truncation are applied with the conditions \(k \le k_{\mathrm{max}}\) and \(\ell \le \ell_{\mathrm{max}}\) as the primary approximation in CDCC.
* The Brieva-Rook localization of nonlocal microscopic optical potentials works well for proton elastic scattering from \({}^{90}\)Zr for momentum transfer \(q \lesssim 2\ \mathrm{fm}^{-1}\) at 65 MeV and for \(q \lesssim 4\ \mathrm{fm}^{-1}\) at 800 MeV.
* The double-folding model with the Melbourne \(g\)-matrix reproduces experimental data for \({}^{12}\)C+\({}^{12}\)C elastic scattering at 135 MeV/nucleon and reaction cross sections for \({}^{12}\)C scattering from \({}^{12}\)C, \({}^{20}\)Ne, \({}^{23}\)Na, and \({}^{27}\)Al around 240 MeV/nucleon.
* The microscopic optical potential approach is validated for incident energies from 65 MeV to 800 MeV for nucleon-nucleus scattering, and from 65 to 800 MeV for the Brieva-Rook localization test.

## Context

This review extends the earlier CDCC reviews by Kamimura (1986) and Austern (1987), which treated nuclear breakup but not Coulomb breakup and relied mainly on phenomenological optical potentials. The paper constructs a microscopic reaction theory for nucleus-nucleus scattering based on the multiple scattering theory of Watson (1953) and the Kerman-McManus-Thaler formalism (1959), and compares CDCC with the Faddeev method, showing that the two solutions agree very well (Del07). The work enables accurate analyses of unstable nuclei scattering, reactions relevant to cosmology and astrophysics (nucleosynthesis, stellar evolution), and nuclear engineering applications such as the Li\((d,n)\) reaction for the international fusion material irradiation facility (IFMIF).
