# Fission Decay Rates Determined from a Quantal Transport Equation

> The decay of a metastable system is described by extending Kramers' method to the quantal regime. For temperatures above twice the crossover value we recover the result known from applying Euclidean path integrals to solvable models. Our derivation is not restricted to a linearly coupled heat bath of oscillators, and thus applicable to nuclear systems.

## Key claim

The authors derive a quantal generalization of Kramers' fission decay rate formula from a transport equation, valid for temperatures above twice the crossover value \(T_c = 2T_0\), and claim their derivation is not restricted to a linearly coupled heat bath of oscillators, making it applicable to nuclear systems.

## Method

The authors start from a Hamiltonian of the form \(\hat H = \hat H_B + \hat H_{SB} + \hat H_S\) and derive a transport equation for the Wigner function in the collective coordinate \(Q\) and momentum \(P\). They obtain local stationary solutions of this transport equation near the potential minimum and near the barrier, using a quasi-stationary approximation. The decay rate is then computed as the ratio of the outward flux at the barrier to the number of particles in the well, expressed in terms of local partition functions.

## Key numbers

* The decay rate formula is \(R = \frac{|\mathcal{Z}_b|}{\mathcal{Z}_a} \frac{\varpi_b}{2\pi} \left(\sqrt{1+\eta_b^2} - \eta_b\right) e^{-\beta B}\), where \(\beta = (kT)^{-1}\), \(B\) is the barrier height, \(\varpi_b\) is the imaginary barrier frequency, and \(\eta_b\) is the effective damping rate at the barrier.
* The quantum correction factor is \(f_Q = \prod_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\nu_n^2 + \nu_n \Gamma_a(\nu_n) + \varpi_a^2}{\nu_n^2 + \nu_n \Gamma_b(\nu_n) - \varpi_b^2}\), with \(\nu_n = 2n\pi / \hbar\beta\) and \(\Gamma = \gamma / M\).
* The derivation is valid for temperatures above twice the crossover value \(T_c = 2T_0\), where \(T_0\) is the crossover temperature defined by \(\lambda_1^b = \lambda_{-1}^b = 0\).

## Context

This work extends Kramers' (1940) classical method for decay rates of metastable systems to the quantal regime, unifying it with results from dissipative tunneling obtained via Euclidean path integrals by Caldeira and Leggett (1983) and others. It builds on a transport theory developed by Hofmann, Samhammer, and Ockenfuss (1989) and connects to the path-integral-based quantal Kramers solution of Ingold (1988) and Ankerhold, Grabert, and Ingold (1993). The result enables calculation of fission decay rates for nuclear systems without the restriction to a linearly coupled oscillator bath, and can be compared with phenomenological treatments by Frobrich and Tillack (1992).
