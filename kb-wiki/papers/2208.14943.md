# Report on Progress in Physics: Observation of the Breit-Wheeler Process and Vacuum Birefringence in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> This Report reviews the effort over several decades to observe the linear Breit-Wheeler process ($γγ\rightarrow e^+e^-$) and vacuum birefringence in high-energy particle and heavy-ion collider experiment. This Report, motivated by STAR's recent observations, attempts to summarize the key issues related to the interpretation of polarized $γγ\rightarrow l^+l^-$ measurements in high-energy experiments. To that end, we start by reviewing the historical context and essential theoretical developments, before focusing on the decades of progress made in high-energy collider experiments. Special attention is given to the evolution in experimental approaches in response to various challenges and the demanding detector capabilities required to unambiguously identify the linear Breit-Wheeler process and to detect the signatures of vacuum birefringence. We close the report with a discussion, followed by a look at near-future opportunities for utilizing these discoveries and for testing QED in previously unexplored regimes.

# Observation of the Breit-Wheeler Process and Vacuum Birefringence in Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The STAR Collaboration's recent observation of the linear Breit-Wheeler process and a related vacuum polarization effect in heavy-ion collisions constitutes the first unambiguous detection of real photon-photon collisions producing matter, contingent on the validity of using highly Lorentz-contracted nuclear electromagnetic fields as a source of quasi-real photons and on the ability to isolate the signal from competing background processes.

## Method

The authors review the historical and theoretical development of the linear Breit-Wheeler process (γγ → e⁺e⁻) and vacuum birefringence, then examine the experimental evolution from the SLAC E-144 experiment (which observed the nonlinear multiphoton Breit-Wheeler process using a 46.6 GeV electron beam and a terawatt laser) to the STAR experiment at RHIC (which measured polarized γγ → l⁺l⁻ production in ultra-peripheral heavy-ion collisions). The report systematically addresses key interpretive issues, including whether the Weizsäcker-Williams method of equivalent photons provides a valid photon source for the Breit-Wheeler process in heavy-ion collisions, how background processes can be isolated, and how the azimuthal angle modulation observed by STAR relates to vacuum birefringence and dichroism.

## Key numbers

- The QED critical field strength for electron-positron pair production is E_c ≃ 1.3×10¹⁶ V/cm, derived from E_c = m_e²c³/(eℏ)
- The SLAC E-144 experiment achieved a peak laser intensity of ∼1.3×10¹⁸ W/cm², corresponding to a parameter value of Υ = 0.3 (where Υ = E*_rms/E_c)
- The SLAC E-144 experiment observed 106 ± 14 positrons from the multiphoton Breit-Wheeler process, using 527 nm laser photons (ℏω₀ = 2.35 eV) and a 46.6 GeV electron beam producing Compton-backscattered photons with maximum energy 29.2 GeV
- The Breit-Wheeler cross section for γγ → e⁺e⁻ is zero when the center-of-mass energy √s = W is less than twice the electron mass (2m_e), with the peak cross section given by Eq. (1) involving the electromagnetic coupling constant α_em and the invariant mass W
- Magnetars produce magnetic fields on the order of 10¹²–10¹⁵ Gauss, sufficient to make vacuum birefringence effects potentially visible over macroscopic lengths

## Context

This report extends the decades-long effort to observe the linear Breit-Wheeler process, which Breit and Wheeler (1934) themselves described as "hopeless to try to observe...in laboratory experiments with two beams of x-rays or gamma-rays meeting each other." The work builds on the theoretical foundations of Dirac (1928), Euler and Heisenberg (1936), and Weisskopf (1936) on vacuum polarization, and on Toll's (1952) pioneering work on vacuum birefringence. The report contrasts the SLAC E-144 experiment's observation of the nonlinear multiphoton Breit-Wheeler process with STAR's recent observation of the linear process, and compares terrestrial vacuum birefringence searches (PVLAS, OVAL, BMV) with astrophysical evidence from the neutron star RX J1856.5-3754 observed by Mignani (2017). The review enables future tests of QED in previously unexplored regimes using heavy-ion collisions and next-generation laser experiments.
