# Correlations of Feed-down Hadrons in a Thermal Model

> We examine the potential impact of strong decays on the magnitude of fluctuations of net quantum numbers and integrals of balance functions based on a thermal hadron gas model. The calculations are based on a comprehensive list of known hadrons with masses up to 2.5 GeV/$c^2$ and include all decays of these hadrons with known branching fractions. The calculations are performed at vanishing baryo-chemical potential for temperatures between 140 and 200 MeV. We show that the decays feed-down substantially impact the single yield of measurable ``stable particles" as well as those of correlated densities of these species. Decays can then potentially have large and non-trivial impacts on measurements of net quantum number cumulants and balance functions. These observations are particularly important in the context of the search for the QCD critical point at the RHIC Beam Energy Scan as well as efforts to determine chemical susceptibilities near the phase transition at RHIC or LHC energies. Results obtained in this work also shed light on the importance of feed-down in measurements of balance functions in elementary p-p and nucleus-nucleus collisions.

# Correlations of Feed-down Hadrons in a Thermal Model

## Key claim

Strong decays of hadrons with masses up to 2.5 GeV/c² produce feed-down contributions that dominate the yields of measurable "stable particles" and their correlated pair densities in a thermal hadron gas model, but the quantitative impact on net quantum number fluctuations and balance functions depends on the assumption that all hadrons reach chemical freeze-out at the same temperature, which may not hold for expanding systems.

## Method

The authors used the Grand Canonical Model (GCM) to compute thermal densities of 353 hadron species at vanishing baryo-chemical potential for temperatures between 140 and 200 MeV, using particle and decay tables from the Therminator2 package. They iteratively calculated the probability of each unstable hadron decaying into stable species (pions, kaons, protons, neutrons, and lambdas) via all known branching fractions, then summed thermal and feed-down contributions to obtain total single-particle densities and two-particle correlated cumulants.

## Key numbers

- Pion yield increases due to feed-down by a factor of 2.64 at T=140 MeV and by a factor of 10.6 at T=200 MeV
- At T=160 MeV, feed-down increases proton density by a factor of approximately 3.1
- At T=160 MeV, the total baryon density relates to observable proton density as ρ_B^TH = 2.87 × ρ_p^(TH+FD), with ρ_n^(TH+FD)/ρ_p^(TH+FD)=1.13 and ρ_Λ^(TH+FD)/ρ_p^(TH+FD)=0.74
- At T=160 MeV, the thermal yield of protons is 3.66×10⁻³ fm⁻³, while the thermal+feed-down yield is 0.0114 fm⁻³; in a 1000 fm³ volume this corresponds to 3.6 thermal protons per event versus 11.4 total protons per event
- At T=160 MeV, the correlated pair cumulant C₂(p|π⁺)=3.7×10⁻³ fm⁻³, which is a factor of approximately 23 larger than the uncorrelated thermal pair density ρ₁^TH(π⁺)×ρ₁^TH(p)=1.56×10⁻⁴ fm⁻³
- At T=160 MeV, the correlated pair cumulant C₂(K⁻|K⁺)=4.02×10⁻⁴ fm⁻³, which is a factor of approximately 12 larger than the thermal pair density ρ₁^TH(K⁺)×ρ₁^TH(K⁻)=1.88×10⁻⁴ fm⁻³
- Between T=140 and 200 MeV, normalized correlated densities of π⁰+π⁺ decrease by 14.7%, π⁻+π⁺ decrease by 9.7%, while π⁺+π⁺ rise by 43%, K⁺+π⁺ rise by 77%, and p+π⁺ rise by 108%
- In Pythia 8.3 at √s=13 TeV, a π⁺ is balanced by π⁻ (88%), K⁻ (9%), and antiproton (3%); the thermal model at T=160 MeV gives corresponding fractions of 89%, 8.0%, and 3.1%

## Context

This work extends thermal model calculations of feed-down effects on single-particle yields, previously implemented in Therminator2 by Chojnacki et al. (2012), to the domain of two-particle correlations, specifically net quantum number cumulants and balance functions. The results are directly relevant to the RHIC Beam Energy Scan search for the QCD critical point, where net-proton fluctuations are used as a proxy for net-baryon fluctuations, and to measurements of chemical susceptibilities near the phase transition at RHIC and LHC energies. The authors note that while Pratt et al. (2022) proposed using balance functions in invariant momentum Q² to identify resonance decays, this technique is limited to narrow resonances, and the present work provides a baseline for the magnitude of feed-down effects that must be accounted for in fluctuation and correlation measurements. The comparison with Pythia 8.3 results from Pruneau et al. (2024) shows that charge balance fractions are only modestly sensitive to whether thermalization is achieved, suggesting that feed-down effects may be similar in elementary and nuclear collisions.
