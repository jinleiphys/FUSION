# Photon-Photon Physics in Very Peripheral Collisions of Relativistic Heavy Ions

> In central collisions at relativistic heavy ion colliders like the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider RHIC/Brookhaven and the Large Hadron Collider LHC (in its heavy ion mode) at CERN/Geneva, one aims at detecting a new form of hadronic matter - the Quark Gluon Plasma. It is the purpose of this review to discuss a complementary aspect of these collisions, the very peripheral ones. Due to coherence, there are strong electromagnetic fields of short duration in such collisions. They give rise to photon-photon and photon-nucleus collisions with high flux up to an invariant mass region hitherto unexplored experimentally. After a general survey photon-photon luminosities in relativistic heavy ion collisions are discussed. Special care is taken to include the effects of strong interactions and nuclear size. Then photon-photon physics at various gamma-gamma-invariant mass scales is discussed. The region of several GeV, relevant for RHIC is dominated by QCD phenomena (meson and vector meson pair production). Invariant masses of up to about 100 GeV can be reached at LHC, and the potential for new physics is discussed. Photonuclear reactions and other important background effects, especially diffractive processes are also discussed. A special chapter is devoted to lepton-pair production, especially electron-positron pair production; due to the strong fields new phenomena, especially multiple e+-e- pair production, will occur there.

# Photon-Photon Physics in Very Peripheral Collisions of Relativistic Heavy Ions

## Key claim

Very peripheral relativistic heavy ion collisions provide a high-flux source of photon-photon and photon-nucleus interactions up to invariant masses of about 100 GeV at LHC, but the practical utility of these collisions for "clean" photon-photon physics is substantially limited by strong interaction absorption and, for heavy systems like Pb-Pb, by a high probability (about 75%) of accompanying nuclear excitation that modifies the final state.

## Method

The authors derive impact-parameter-dependent equivalent photon spectra for relativistic heavy ions, incorporating the coherence condition that restricts photon virtuality to Q² ≲ 1/R². They fold these spectra with a sharp cutoff at impact parameter b = R₁ + R₂ (or a smooth Glauber-model overlap function) to account for strong interaction absorption, obtaining the photon-photon luminosity dL/dW_γγ as a function of invariant mass. The formalism is applied to lepton-pair production, meson production, and potential new physics signals at RHIC and LHC energies, with separate treatment of multiple e⁺e⁻ pair production where the equivalent photon approximation fails.

## Key numbers

- Maximum photon energy: ω_max ≈ γ/R, giving up to several GeV at RHIC (γ ≈ 100) and up to about 100 GeV at LHC (γ ≈ 3000)
- Coherence condition: x_max = λ_C(A)/R ≈ 0.175/A^(4/3), yielding x ≲ 10⁻³ for Ca ions and x ≲ 10⁻⁴ for Pb ions
- For Pb-Pb at LHC: γ = 2950, √s = 1148 TeV, beam luminosity L_AA = 1 × 10²⁶ cm⁻² s⁻¹
- For Ca-Ca at LHC: γ = 3750, √s = 280 TeV, beam luminosity L_AA = 4 × 10³⁰ cm⁻² s⁻¹
- For Au-Au at RHIC: γ = 106, √s = 20 TeV, beam luminosity L_AA = 2 × 10²⁶ cm⁻² s⁻¹
- Giant dipole resonance excitation probability parameter S = (10.4 fm)² for Pb-Pb using TRK sum rule; S = (17.4 fm)² including all additional contributions
- For Ca-Ca: S = (0.86 fm)² using TRK sum rule
- About 75% of photon-photon events in Pb-Pb collisions are accompanied by nuclear excitation; for Ca-Ca this is less than 2%
- The function f(τ) with τ = W_γγ R/γ provides a universal luminosity parameterization for symmetric collisions

## Context

This review extends the earlier work of Baur and collaborators (1988) on two-photon physics in heavy ion collisions, and incorporates the impact-parameter-space method developed by Baur (1990), Baur and Ferreira (1990), and Cahn and Jackson (1990) to properly account for strong absorption effects that distinguish heavy ion collisions from e⁺e⁻ collider two-photon physics. The paper builds on the foundational calculation of e⁺e⁻ pair production in nucleus-nucleus collisions by Landau and Lifshitz (1934) and subsequent studies by Soff (1980), Alexander, Gotsman, and Maor (1987), and others. The results enable concrete experimental planning at RHIC (Klein and collaborators, 1995, 1997) and LHC (Hencken, Kienle, and collaborators, 1996; Felix, 1997; Baur, Hencken, Trautmann, and collaborators, 1998), and open the possibility to study photon-photon invariant mass regions up to about 100 GeV at LHC, including potential Higgs boson production via γγ fusion as suggested by Grabiak, Muller, and Greiner (1989) and Papageorgiu (1989).
