# Fluctuating temperature and baryon chemical potential in heavy-ion collisions and the position of the critical end point in the effective QCD phase diagram

> We use the linear sigma model with quarks to locate the critical end point in the effective QCD phase diagram accounting for fluctuations in temperature and quark chemical potential. For this purpose, we use the non-equilibrium formalism provided by the superstatistics framework. We compute the effective potential in the high- and low-temperature approximations up to sixth order and include the contribution of ring diagrams to account for plasma screening effects. We fix the model parameters from relations between the thermal sigma and pion masses imposing a first order phase transition at zero temperature and a finite critical value for the baryon chemical potential that we take of order of the nucleon mass. We find that the CEP displacement due to fluctuations in temperature and/or quark chemical potential is almost negligible.

## Key claim

Using the linear sigma model with quarks (LSMq) and a superstatistics framework to account for fluctuations in temperature and baryon chemical potential, the authors find that the displacement of the critical end point (CEP) due to these fluctuations is almost negligible, contrary to what might be expected from the importance of fluctuations in heavy-ion collision data analysis.

## Method

The authors compute the effective potential of the LSMq in high- and low-temperature approximations up to sixth order, including ring diagrams for plasma screening effects and effective couplings. They then incorporate superstatistics corrections by expanding the generalized Boltzmann factor to first order in 1/N, where N is the number of subsystems, and apply this to the partition function to obtain a superstatistics-corrected effective potential. Model parameters (lambda, g, a) are fixed by enforcing a first order phase transition at zero temperature for a critical baryon chemical potential taken of order the nucleon mass (approximately 1 GeV), using relations between thermal sigma and pion masses.

## Key numbers

*   For lambda = 1.5 and mu_c = 300 MeV (with a = 102 MeV, g = 1.77), the CEP is located at mu_q approximately 291 MeV and T approximately 40 MeV in the thermodynamic limit (N = infinity).
*   For lambda = 1.6 and mu_c = 290 MeV (with a = 93.4 MeV, g = 1.85), the CEP is located at mu_q approximately 271 MeV and T approximately 51 MeV in the thermodynamic limit.
*   The crossover transition at mu_B = 0 occurs at T around 155-170 MeV from lattice QCD, and the authors require T_c(mu=0) < 200 MeV, which restricts lambda to the range 1.4 < lambda < 1.5 for mu_c = 300 MeV and lambda = 1.6 for mu_c = 290 MeV.
*   Superstatistics effects are tested with N = 100 and N = 300 subsystems, and the CEP displacement is described as "quite small" and "negligible" for all combinations of fluctuations in temperature (S_T), mu_q/T (S_eta), and both (S_Teta).

## Context

This work extends the study of the QCD phase diagram using effective models, specifically the LSMq, by incorporating fluctuations via the superstatistics framework of Beck (2001). It contrasts with standard mean-field treatments and lattice QCD calculations (which suffer from the sign problem at large mu_B) by providing an analytic method to assess fluctuation effects on the CEP location. The results enable future work to determine whether more sophisticated treatments of fluctuations or different effective models might yield larger CEP displacements, and they provide a benchmark for interpreting heavy-ion collision data from the STAR BES-I program and future experiments.
