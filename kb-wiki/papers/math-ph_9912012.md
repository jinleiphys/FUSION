# Trapping of a model-system for a soliton in a well

> The nature of the interaction of a soliton with an attractive well is elucidated using a model of two interacting point particles. The system shows the existence of trapped states at positive kinetic energy, as well as reflection by an attractive impurity, as found when a topological soliton scatters off an attractive well.

# Trapping of a model-system for a soliton in a well

## Key claim

The peculiar trapping and reflection behavior previously observed for topological solitons scattering off attractive wells is not specific to soliton dynamics; a simple classical two-particle system with a spring and repulsive core reproduces all the same effects, including trapped states at positive kinetic energy and reflection by an attractive impurity.

## Method

The author constructs a classical one-dimensional Lagrangian for two equal point masses connected by a harmonic spring with an additional repulsive inverse-power potential, placed in an attractive Gaussian well. The equations of motion are solved numerically for a range of initial center-of-mass velocities, with the system prepared at equilibrium separation far from the well; the final velocity (trapped, reflected, or transmitted) is recorded as a function of initial velocity.

## Key numbers

- For parameters k=1, α=1, n=2, A=2, β=1, the system shows trapping (v_final=0), reflection (v_final<0), and transmission (v_final>0) as initial speed is varied.
- A velocity grid of dv=0.001 was used for the initial scan; a finer grid of dv=0.0002 around v≈0.12 reveals additional structure.
- The equilibrium interparticle separation is given by r_0^(n+2) = nα/k, with n=2 used in the calculations.

## Context

This work extends the collective-coordinate treatment of Kivshar et al. (1990s), who showed that a soliton's center coupled to impurity and shape modes reproduces trapping and reflection for delta-function wells, and the author's own prior work (Kalbermann 1997) extending this to finite-size wells. The present result demonstrates that the phenomena are generic to any extended object, not requiring the specific soliton dynamics, and opens the possibility of observing analogous trapping in simple mechanical systems such as two masses on a spring sliding over a carved well, or in quasi-classical molecular scattering.
