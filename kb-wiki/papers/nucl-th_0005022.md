# Effects of the Neutron Spin-Orbit Density on Nuclear Charge Density in Relativistic Models

## Key claim

The neutron spin-orbit density contributes to the nuclear charge density as a relativistic effect, and this contribution is enhanced by the effective mass arising from the Lorentz-scalar potential in relativistic models. This enhancement explains the difference between elastic electron scattering cross sections off 40Ca and 48Ca that was not reproduced in previous non-relativistic models, but the effect depends on the specific subshell occupation and can cancel in certain nuclei.

## Method

The authors calculated elastic electron scattering cross sections using phase shift analyses with nuclear charge densities obtained from the Horowitz-Serot relativistic mean-field model. The nucleon densities and spin-orbit densities were computed from single-particle wave functions solving the Dirac equation with Lorentz scalar and vector potentials, using dipole-type nucleon form factors with experimental neutron data. The difference between cross sections for 40Ca and 48Ca was compared with experimental data and with non-relativistic Hartree-Fock calculations using the Skyrme force I.

## Key numbers

- The proton Sachs form factor uses a charge radius of r_p = 0.81 fm, with the dipole form G_Ep = 1/(1 + r_p^2 q^2/12)^2
- The neutron Sachs form factor uses parameters r_+^2 = (0.9)^2 + 0.06 fm^2 and r_-^2 = (0.9)^2 - 0.06 fm^2
- The anomalous magnetic moments used are mu_p = 1.793 and mu_n = -1.913
- The electron beam energy for the scattering calculations is 250 MeV
- The non-relativistic model with only proton charge density (thin solid curve) and with full charge density (solid curve) both fail to reproduce experimental data, particularly at scattering angles between 60 and 90 degrees
- The relativistic model with full density including the enhanced neutron spin-orbit density reproduces the experimental data for the 40Ca-48Ca cross section difference

## Context

This work extends earlier non-relativistic calculations by Bertozzi et al. (1972) who first discussed the neutron spin-orbit charge density from the Pauli current but found it insufficient to explain the calcium isotope data. It also builds on a relativistic analysis by Miller that similarly could not reproduce the experimental data. The authors use the Horowitz-Serot model (1992) which provides a more developed relativistic framework than earlier approaches. The results enable future experimental tests using electron scattering off unstable nuclei, such as those planned at RIKEN (Suda), to explore the effective mass effects in neutron-rich isotopes like 52Ca and Zr isotopes.
