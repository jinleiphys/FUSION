# Isospin-Asymmetry Dependence of the Thermodynamic Nuclear Equation of State in Many-Body Perturbation Theory

> The computation of the thermodynamic properties of nuclear matter is a central task of theoretical nuclear physics. The nuclear equation of state is an essential quantity in nuclear astrophysics and governs the properties of neutron stars and core-collapse supernovæ. The framework of chiral effective field theory provides the basis for the description of nuclear interactions in terms of a systematic low-energy expansion. In this thesis, we apply chiral two- and three-nucleon interactions in perturbative many-body calculations of the thermodynamic equation of state of infinite homogeneous nuclear matter. The conceptual issues that arise concerning the consistent generalization of the standard zero-temperature form of many-body perturbation theory to finite temperatures are investigated in detail. The structure of many-body perturbation theory at higher orders is examined, in particular concerning the role of the so-called anomalous contributions. The first-order nuclear liquid-gas phase transition is analyzed with respect to its dependence on temperature and the neutron-to-proton ratio. Furthermore, the convergence behavior of the expansion of the equation of state in terms of the isospin asymmetry is examined. It is shown that the expansion coefficients beyond the quadratic order diverge in the zero-temperature limit, implying a nonanalytic form of the isospin-asymmetry dependence at low temperatures. This behavior is associated with logarithmic terms in the isospin-asymmetry dependence at zero temperature.

## Key claim

The thesis demonstrates that the isospin-asymmetry expansion coefficients of the nuclear equation of state beyond quadratic order diverge in the zero-temperature limit, implying a nonanalytic form of the isospin-asymmetry dependence at low temperatures, which is associated with logarithmic terms in the isospin-asymmetry dependence at zero temperature.

## Method

The author applies chiral two- and three-nucleon interactions within many-body perturbation theory (MBPT) to compute the thermodynamic equation of state of infinite homogeneous nuclear matter. The conceptual issues regarding the consistent generalization of zero-temperature MBPT to finite temperatures are investigated, including the role of anomalous contributions at higher orders. The first-order nuclear liquid-gas phase transition is analyzed as a function of temperature and neutron-to-proton ratio, and the convergence behavior of the isospin-asymmetry expansion is examined.

## Key numbers

- For the noninteracting free Fermi gas at T=25 MeV and symmetric nuclear matter, the relativistic chemical potential is -25.72 MeV at density 0.04 fm^-3, 16.95 MeV at 0.16 fm^-3, and 44.48 MeV at 0.32 fm^-3.
- The relativistic free energy per particle at T=25 MeV is -52.09 MeV at 0.04 fm^-3, -13.38 MeV at 0.16 fm^-3, and 9.18 MeV at 0.32 fm^-3.
- The first-order canonical relativistic correction to the free energy density at zero temperature is 5/(112 pi^2 M^3) (k_F)^7.
- The second-order canonical relativistic correction to the free energy density at zero temperature is 7/(2304 pi^2 M^5) (k_F)^9.
- For the nonrelativistic free Fermi gas, the density threshold for negative chemical potential is approximately 3.6 x 10^-4 (T/MeV)^(3/2) fm^-3.

## Context

This work extends the application of chiral effective field theory (chiral EFT) to finite-temperature nuclear matter, building on the zero-temperature framework developed by Machleidt (2011) and others. The thesis addresses conceptual issues in finite-temperature MBPT that were not fully resolved in prior work, such as the treatment of relativistic corrections in the canonical versus grand-canonical formalisms, where the author notes inconsistencies in the implementation by Fritsch (2002) and in a 2014 Physical Review C article. The results enable more reliable predictions for the nuclear equation of state at the temperatures and isospin asymmetries relevant to neutron stars and core-collapse supernovae, while also identifying the breakdown of the standard quadratic isospin-asymmetry expansion at low temperatures.
