# Analytical structure of the equation of state at finite density: Resummation versus expansion in a low energy model

> For theories plagued with a sign problem at finite density, a Taylor expansion in the chemical potential is frequently used for lattice gauge theory based computations of the equation of state. Recently, in arXiv:2106.03165, a new resummation scheme was proposed for such an expansion that resums contributions of correlation functions of conserved currents to all orders in the chemical potential. Here, we study the efficacy of this resummation scheme using a solvable low energy model, namely the mean-field quark-meson model. After adapting the scheme for a mean-field analysis, we confront the results of this scheme with the direct solution of the model at finite density as well as compare with results from Taylor expansions. We study to what extent the two methods capture the analytical properties of the equation of state in the complex chemical potential plane. As expected, the Taylor expansion breaks down as soon as the baryon chemical potential reaches the radius of convergence defined by the Yang-Lee edge singularity. Encouragingly, the resummation not only captures the location of the Yang-Lee edge singularity accurately, but is also able to describe the equation of state for larger chemical potentials beyond the location of the edge singularity for a wide range of temperatures.

## Key claim

The resummation scheme proposed in Mondal (2021) can capture the location of the Yang-Lee edge singularity accurately and describe the equation of state for chemical potentials beyond that singularity for a wide range of temperatures, but only when thermal cuts are not the dominant singularities; the scheme fails for temperatures below approximately 90 MeV where thermal cuts become the closest singularities.

## Method

The authors used the mean-field quark-meson model with two degenerate quark flavors and three colors as a solvable low-energy proxy for QCD. They computed the thermodynamic potential directly at finite density, then compared it to both the conventional Taylor expansion in the chemical potential and the resummation scheme of Mondal (2021), where the fermion determinant is expanded to order N inside the path integral before the ensemble average, resulting in a thermodynamic potential that contains infinite powers of the chemical potential. The Yang-Lee edge singularities were located by solving the system of equations for the first and second derivatives of the thermodynamic potential with respect to the sigma field vanishing simultaneously in the complex chemical potential plane.

## Key numbers

- The pseudocritical temperature at zero baryon chemical potential is T_pc = 163.62 MeV, defined via the maximum of the correlation length m_sigma^{-1}
- The critical endpoint is located at (T_CEP, mu_B,CEP) = (27.46, 895.85) MeV
- The resummation at order N=2 already reproduces the Yang-Lee edge singularity location accurately for temperatures above approximately 120 MeV
- At T=100 MeV, order N=8 is required for accurate reproduction of the edge singularity location
- The resummation fails completely for temperatures below approximately 90 MeV, where the thermal cut at |mu_B| = 3 pi T becomes the closest singularity
- The range of validity of the resummation is limited to |mu_B| less than 3 pi T in the quark-meson model

## Context

This work tests the resummation scheme proposed by Mondal (2021) for lattice QCD calculations, which was designed to resum contributions of correlation functions of conserved currents to all orders in the chemical potential. The study extends prior work on Taylor expansions in lattice gauge theory (Bazavov 2017, Datta 2016) and Padé approximations for locating singularities (Gavai 2008, Karsch 2010, Basar 2021, Schmidt 2021). The results demonstrate that the resummation scheme can overcome the fundamental limitation of Taylor expansions, which break down at the radius of convergence defined by the Yang-Lee edge singularity, and can provide accurate thermodynamics for a wider range of chemical potentials. The next step would be to test this scheme beyond mean-field approximation and ultimately in full lattice QCD.
