# Microscopic study of freeze-out in relativistic heavy ion collisions at SPS energies

> The freeze-out conditions in the light (S+S) and heavy (Pb+Pb) colliding systems of heavy nuclei at 160 AGeV/$c$ are analyzed within the microscopic Quark Gluon String Model (QGSM). We found that even for the most heavy systems particle emission takes place from the whole space-time domain available for the system evolution, but not from the thin ''freeze-out hypersurface", adopted in fluid dynamical models. Pions are continuously emitted from the whole volume of the reaction and reflect the main trends of the system evolution. Nucleons in Pb+Pb collisions initially come from the surface region. For both systems there is a separation of the elastic and inelastic freeze-out. The mesons with large transverse momenta, $p_t$, are predominantly produced at the early stages of the reaction. The low $p_t$-component is populated by mesons coming mainly from the decay of resonances. This explains naturally the decreasing source sizes with increasing $p_t$, observed in HBT interferometry. Comparison with S+S and Au+Au systems at 11.6 AGeV/$c$ is also presented.

## Key claim

The Quark Gluon String Model (QGSM) predicts that particle emission in relativistic heavy ion collisions occurs continuously from the entire space-time volume of the reaction, not from a thin freeze-out hypersurface as assumed in fluid dynamical models; this conclusion holds even for the heaviest systems like Pb+Pb at 160 AGeV/c, though the authors note that microscopic models cannot give a sharp freeze-out due to the lack of attractive forces that would keep particles together.

## Method

The authors analyzed freeze-out conditions in S+S and Pb+Pb collisions at 160 AGeV/c using the Quark Gluon String Model (QGSM), a microscopic Monte Carlo model based on Regge and string phenomenology that includes string fragmentation, resonance formation, and hadron rescatterings. They studied distributions of last interaction points (coordinates and times) for nucleons and pions separately for elastic collisions, inelastic collisions, and resonance decays, using 1000 S+S and 50 Pb+Pb very central events (b=0.1 fm) with calculations stopped at t_f=50 fm/c.

## Key numbers

- Pb+Pb central collisions at 160 AGeV/c produce about 416 baryons and 3830 mesons, with approximately 18500 elementary interactions (6500 inelastic, 11500 elastic, plus 2000 resonance decays)
- S+S central collisions at 160 AGeV/c produce about 58 baryons and 340 mesons, with approximately 410 elementary interactions (200 inelastic, 200 elastic, plus 116 resonance decays)
- Mean number of collisions per hadron is about 9 for Pb+Pb and about 2 for S+S
- First nucleon-nucleon collision occurs at t_0 approximately 4.85 fm/c in S+S (R_S=3.56 fm) and approximately 7.40 fm/c in Pb+Pb (R_Pb=6.64 fm)
- Average decoupling times for mesons are approximately 17.5 fm/c (Pb+Pb) and 10 fm/c (S+S); for baryons approximately 26.5 fm/c (Pb+Pb) and 17.5 fm/c (S+S)
- Width of dN/dt distributions: mesons Delta t approximately 7.5 fm/c (Pb+Pb) and 6.5 fm/c (S+S); baryons Delta t approximately 8.5 fm/c (Pb+Pb) and 9.0 fm/c (S+S)
- In Pb+Pb collisions, half of nucleons come from resonance decays, 40% from elastic collisions, and about 10% from inelastic collisions; for pions, one third come from resonance decays, 23% from inelastic collisions, and 52% from elastic collisions
- In S+S collisions, 23% of pions come from inelastic collisions and 23% from elastic collisions

## Context

This work extends earlier freeze-out studies by Bravina et al. (1995) using the QGSM and compares with results from the RQMD model (Sorge, 1996-1997) and UrQMD model (Bass, 1994). The findings contradict the sharp freeze-out hypersurface assumption used in Landau's and Bjorken's hydrodynamic models (Landau, 1953; Bjorken, 1983), showing instead that particle emission is continuous and that elastic and inelastic freeze-out are well separated. The results imply that more realistic source shapes and freeze-out criteria should be used in the analysis of HBT interferometric data, as also argued by Ma and Su (1999).
