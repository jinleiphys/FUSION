# Temperature, chemical potential and the rho meson

> We describe some applications of the Dyson-Schwinger equations at nonzero-(T,mu). Employing a simple model dressed-gluon propagator we determine the boundary of the deconfinement phase transition and the medium dependence of rho-meson properties. We introduce an extension to describe the time-evolution of scalar and vector self energies.

## Key claim

Using a simple model dressed-gluon propagator within the Dyson-Schwinger equation framework, the deconfinement phase boundary and the medium dependence of rho-meson properties can be determined, with the rho-meson mass showing an anti-correlated response to temperature and chemical potential that leads to an edge along which the mass remains unchanged up to the transition point.

## Method

The dressed-quark Dyson-Schwinger equation was solved at nonzero temperature and chemical potential using an infrared-dominant Ansatz for the dressed-gluon propagator, which ensures quark confinement and dynamical chiral symmetry breaking. The phase boundary was determined by the condition that the pressure difference between the Nambu-Goldstone (confined) and Wigner-Weyl (deconfined) phases vanishes, and rho-meson masses were obtained by solving the Bethe-Salpeter equation in the vector channel. A Vlasov equation was introduced to describe the time-evolution of scalar and vector self energies in the deconfined phase.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature at zero chemical potential: T_c = 158 MeV
- Critical chemical potential at zero temperature: mu_c = 275 MeV
- In the chiral limit, the transverse rho-meson mass squared is M_rho-^2 = (1/2) eta^2, independent of T and mu
- For nonzero current-quark mass, the transverse rho-meson mass changes by less than 1% as T and mu increase from zero toward their critical values
- In the chiral limit, the longitudinal rho-meson mass squared is M_rho+^2 = (1/2) eta^2 - 4(mu^2 - pi^2 T^2)

## Context

This work extends Dyson-Schwinger equation models previously applied at zero temperature (Roberts and Williams, 1994; Bender et al., 1996) to nonzero temperature and chemical potential, providing a unified description of deconfinement and chiral symmetry restoration. The phase transition is first order for any nonzero chemical potential and second order at zero chemical potential, with mean field critical exponents characteristic of the rainbow-ladder truncation (Holl et al., 1999). The results enable future studies of nonequilibrium dynamics in the deconfined phase using the Vlasov equation, where the persistence of the vector self energy is expected to yield significantly different results compared to simpler models without confinement (Huefner et al., 1994).
