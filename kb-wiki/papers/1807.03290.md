# Super-Knee Cosmic Rays from Galactic Neutron Star Merger Remnants

> The detection of gravitational waves and electromagnetic counterparts from a binary neutron star (BNS) merger confirmed that it is accompanied by the launch of fast merger ejecta. Analogous to supernova remnants, forward shocks formed by the interaction of the ejecta with interstellar material will produce high-energy cosmic rays. We investigate the possibility that Galactic neutron star merger remnants (NSMRs) significantly contribute to the observed cosmic rays in the energy range between the knee and the ankle. Using typical parameters obtained by modeling of GW170817, we find that NSMRs can accelerate iron nuclei up to $\sim500$ PeV. We calculate the cosmic-ray spectrum and composition observed on Earth, and show that the Galactic NSMR scenario can account for the experimental cosmic-ray data in the 20 -- 1000 PeV range. Our model can naturally explain the hardening feature around 20 PeV for total cosmic-ray spectrum, which has been observed by the Telescope Array Low Energy extension and the IceTop air shower array.

# Super-Knee Cosmic Rays from Galactic Neutron Star Merger Remnants

## Key claim

Galactic neutron star merger remnants (NSMRs) can significantly contribute to the observed cosmic-ray flux in the 20–1000 PeV range, provided that magnetic field amplification at forward shocks is efficient (parameterized by epsilon_B ~ 10^{-3}) and that the merger ejecta parameters are similar to those inferred from GW170817. The model can naturally explain the hardening feature around 20 PeV seen by Telescope Array Low Energy extension and IceTop, but the contribution depends on the assumed spectral index at the source (s_inj = 2.0) and a fairly high cosmic-ray production efficiency (epsilon_CR = 0.2).

## Method

The authors model forward shocks formed by binary neutron star merger ejecta interacting with the interstellar medium, analogous to supernova remnants. They calculate the maximum energy of accelerated particles by balancing acceleration time against the remnant age, using the Sedov-Taylor solution for the dynamical evolution after the deceleration time. The cosmic-ray spectrum at Earth is computed by equating the injection rate from NSMRs (using the Galactic merger rate from GW170817) with the escape rate from the Galactic halo, parameterized by the grammage derived from the Boron-to-Carbon ratio.

## Key numbers

- Iron nuclei can be accelerated up to ~500 PeV (5 × 10^17 eV) at NSMRs
- Protons can be accelerated up to ~2 × 10^16 eV at NSMRs
- The deceleration radius is ~3.9 × 10^18 cm for M_ej = 0.03 solar masses and n_ISM = 0.1 cm^{-3}
- The deceleration time is ~5.2 × 10^8 s for V_ini = 0.25c
- The magnetic field at the forward shock is ~0.41 mG for epsilon_B = 10^{-3} and n_ISM = 0.1 cm^{-3}
- The Galactic binary neutron star merger rate is ~1.5 × 10^{-4} yr^{-1}
- The grammage traversed by cosmic rays is X_esc ~ 8.7 (R/10 GV)^{-delta} g cm^{-2} with delta = 0.46 below 250 GV and delta = 1/3 above
- The NSMR component dominates the cosmic-ray flux in the energy range 2 × 10^16 eV to 10^18 eV

## Context

This work extends the study of hadronic cosmic-ray production from neutron star merger remnants, which was previously discussed by Takami et al. (2014) without quantitative comparison to experimental data. The paper contrasts with the standard paradigm where supernova remnants are responsible for cosmic rays below the knee (~3-4 PeV), noting that historical SNRs show maximum energies lower than the knee (Aharonian 2013; MAGIC Collaboration 2017; H.E.S.S. Collaboration 2018). The model enables future tests through dipole anisotropy measurements and searches for magnetar-powered remnants, and it suggests that neutron star-black hole mergers could produce even more energetic outflows.
