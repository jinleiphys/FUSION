# Determining Transport Coefficients for a Microscopic Simulation of a Hadron Gas

> Quark-Gluon plasmas produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions quickly expand and cool, entering a phase consisting of multiple interacting hadronic resonances just below the QCD deconfinement temperature, $T\sim 155$ MeV. Numerical microscopic simulations have emerged as the principal method for modeling the behavior of the hadronic stage of heavy-ion collisions, but the transport properties that characterize these simulations are not well understood. Methods are presented here for extracting the shear viscosity, and two transport parameters that emerge in Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics. The analysis is based on studying how the stress-energy tensor responds to velocity gradients. Results agree with expectations based on Kubo relations.

## Key claim

The paper presents methods for extracting shear viscosity and two Israel-Stewart transport parameters from hadron cascade simulations, but the extracted values depend on including a second-order coefficient (γ_π ≈ 0.96) to achieve consistency between small and large velocity gradients; without this term, viscosities extracted under conditions typical of heavy-ion collisions would be understated by approximately 30%.

## Method

The authors initialized the B3D hadron cascade code with a longitudinally boost-invariant Bjorken geometry and extracted the stress-energy tensor components π_zz, pressure P, and the fluctuation quantity F_π from particle momenta at each time step. They then fit the time evolution of π_zz to Israel-Stewart hydrodynamic equations, varying the shear viscosity η and the second-order coefficient γ_π as free parameters, while determining the relaxation time τ_π from the Kubo relation η = τ_π F_π / T. The procedure was repeated for four initial temperatures (120, 135, 150, and 165 MeV) using both small velocity gradients (large initial proper time τ_0 = 250-1000 fm/c) and large velocity gradients (τ_0 reduced by a factor of 50, to values characteristic of heavy-ion collisions).

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity over entropy density η/s ≈ 0.3 at temperatures near 160 MeV, extracted from fits with γ_π = 0.96
- The Israel-Stewart coefficient κ_π was determined to be 1.13 for a hadron gas, varying by only 1% over the temperature range 100 < T < 170 MeV
- The second-order coefficient γ_π was found to be 0.96, compared to the kinetic theory value of 5/7 ≈ 0.714 for a massless gas with fixed cross sections
- The extracted relaxation time τ_π consistently matched twice the collision time (τ_π ≈ 2τ_coll) across all temperatures studied
- Initial velocity gradients were imposed with τ_0 values of 1000, 750, 500, and 250 fm/c for temperatures 120, 135, 150, and 165 MeV respectively
- Simulations used approximately 5 × 10^7 particles per run with an oversampling factor S = 4

## Context

This work extends previous attempts to extract shear viscosity from hadron cascade codes, particularly addressing the high values (η/s ≈ 1) reported by Demir (2008) for temperatures below 100 MeV, which the authors find qualitatively inconsistent with falling collision times at higher density. The results contrast with those of Demir (2008) by showing a temperature-dependent η/s that rises to approximately 0.3 near 160 MeV, while agreeing with Wiranata (2012, 2013) at low temperatures. The finding that γ_π = 0.96 is important for interpreting hydrodynamic analyses of RHIC and LHC data, such as those by Pratt (2015) which used γ_π = 0; the authors suggest that including a nonzero γ_π could increase extracted viscosities by several tens of percent, potentially placing η/s near 3-4 times the AdS/CFT limit of 1/4π rather than the 2-3 times previously reported.
