# Present Status of Fission Research Based on TDDFT

> Fission resulting from collision of atomic nuclei is systematically investigated based on time-dependent density functional calculations. Time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) is a framework, which enables us to treat quantum many-body dynamics with nucleon degrees of freedom. In this article a theoretical framework called Composite-Nucleus Constrained TDDFT is introduced, and charge equilibrium hypothesis for collision fission dynamics is examined.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a "Composite-Nucleus Constrained TDDFT" formalism to examine the charge equilibrium hypothesis for collision-fission dynamics, and confirms that the hypothesis is satisfied rather well for a composite nucleus of Thorium-240 when the initial relative velocity of the colliding nuclei is set to zero.

## Method

The authors construct a composite nucleus of mass 240 and proton number 90 by placing two nuclei at a fixed distance of 13.125 fm with zero relative velocity, using six different entrance-channel combinations of proton and mass numbers. They then perform time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) calculations with the SV-bas interaction parameter set to evolve the system and determine whether binary fission occurs and whether the resulting fragments have Z/N ratios close to the composite nucleus value of 0.60.

## Key numbers

- The composite nucleus has mass number A=240 and proton number Z=90, giving a charge equilibrium Z/N ratio of 0.60 (Z/N = 90/150).
- The initial distance between the two nuclei is set to D = 13.125 fm, while the diameter of an A=240 nucleus is roughly 14.9 fm (using R = 1.2 A^(1/3)).
- Six entrance-channel combinations are studied: (Z1,A1) = (10,20), (20,40), (30,60), (30,80), (40,80), and (40,100).
- The Z/N-range of the distribution decreases from 0.55 (1.00 minus 0.45) in the entrance channel to 0.20 (0.74 minus 0.54) in the exit channel.
- The duration from entrance to exit channels is roughly 10^(-20) seconds.

## Context

This work extends earlier TDDFT-based fission studies by Wakhle (2014), Oberacker (2014), Simenel (2014), and Guillaume (2015), and introduces a formalism originally proposed by Iwata and Heinz to overcome the difficulty that standard TDDFT can lose localization effects such as clustering. The results enable microscopic examination of the charge equilibrium hypothesis previously assumed in phenomenological treatments by Wahl (1988), Nishinaka (2007), and Nishinaka (2011), and suggest that the effect of initial relative velocity on charge equilibration is quite small.
