# Rapidity and Energy Dependences of Temperatures and Volume Extracted from Identified Charged Hadron Spectra in Proton-Proton Collisions at a Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS)

> The standard (Bose-Einstein/Fermi-Dirac or Maxwell-Boltzmann) distribution from the relativistic ideal gas model is used to study the transverse momentum ($p_{T}$) spectra of identified charged hadrons ($π^-$, $π^+$, $K^-$, $K^+$, $\bar p$, and $p$) with different rapidities produced in inelastic proton-proton ($pp$) collisions at the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS). The experimental data measured by the NA61/SHINE Collaboration at the center-of-mass (c.m.) energies $\sqrt{s}=6.3$, 7.7, 8.8, 12.3, and 17.3 GeV are fitted well by the distribution. It is shown that the effective temperature ($T_{eff}$ or $T$), kinetic freeze-out temperature ($T_{0}$), and initial temperature ($T_{i}$) decrease with the increase in rapidity and increase with the increase in c.m. energy. The kinetic freeze-out volume ($V$) extracted from the $π^-$, $π^+$, $K^-$, $K^+$, and $\bar p$ spectra decreases with the rapidity and increase with the c.m. energy. The opposite tendency of $V$, extracted from the $p$ spectra, is observed to be increasing with the rapidity and decreasing with the c.m. energy due to the effect of leading protons.

## Key claim

The standard (Bose-Einstein/Fermi-Dirac or Maxwell-Boltzmann) distribution from the relativistic ideal gas model can successfully fit the transverse momentum spectra of identified charged hadrons in proton-proton collisions at SPS energies, but only in the narrow, low-transverse-momentum region (below 1-2 GeV/c) where soft excitation processes dominate.

## Method

The authors fitted the double-differential transverse momentum spectra (d²N/dydpT) of π⁻, π⁺, K⁻, K⁺, antiprotons, and protons measured by the NA61/SHINE Collaboration using the standard Bose-Einstein distribution for mesons and Fermi-Dirac distribution for baryons. The effective temperature T was extracted as a free parameter from the fits, then used to derive the kinetic freeze-out temperature T₀ via T₀ = ⟨pT⟩/(2×3.07) and the initial temperature Tᵢ via the string percolation model relation Tᵢ = √(⟨pT²⟩/2). The kinetic freeze-out volume V was obtained from the normalization constant of the fits.

## Key numbers

- Center-of-mass energies studied: √s = 6.3, 7.7, 8.8, 12.3, and 17.3 GeV
- Rapidity intervals analyzed: width of 0.2 units, integrated from y_min = -0.1 to y_max = 0.1 for mid-rapidity results
- Chemical potential ranges from 6.3 to 17.3 GeV: μ_π⁺ ≈ 0.041 to 0.017 GeV; μ_K⁺ ≈ 0.110 to 0.042 GeV; μ_p ≈ 0.510 to 0.180 GeV
- Ratio of effective temperature to mean transverse momentum: T_π⁻ ≈ 0.351 ⟨pT⟩_π⁻; T_π⁺ ≈ 0.348 ⟨pT⟩_π⁺; T_K⁻ ≈ 0.284 ⟨pT⟩_K⁻; T_K⁺ ≈ 0.293 ⟨pT⟩_K⁺; T_antiproton ≈ 0.234 ⟨pT⟩_antiproton; T_p ≈ 0.240 ⟨pT⟩_p
- Transition energy for saturation of temperatures: 7.7 GeV, identified as the boundary between proton-dominated and meson-dominated final states

## Context

This work extends the application of classical thermal distributions to proton-proton collisions at SPS energies, complementing prior studies that used Tsallis statistics for wider pT ranges (Tsallis 1988; Cleymans and Worku 2012) and heavy-ion collision analyses at RHIC and LHC. The results enable systematic comparison of freeze-out parameters between pp and heavy-ion systems, and the observed opposite volume trend for protons (increasing with rapidity) due to leading protons provides a baseline for distinguishing produced particles from pre-existing ones in small collision systems. The authors note that their extracted volumes are larger than Hanbury-Brown-Twiss (HBT) results because HBT measures initial-state system size while their volume reflects the expanded fireball at kinetic freeze-out.
