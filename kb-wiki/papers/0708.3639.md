# Bimodality - a Sign of Critical Behavior in Nuclear Reactions

> The recently discovered coexistence of multifragmentation and residue production for the same total transverse energy of light charged particles can be well reproduced in numerical simulations of the heavy ion reactions. A detailed analysis shows that fluctuations (introduced by elementary nucleon-nucleon collisions) determine which of the exit states is realized. Thus we observe for the first time nonlinear dynamics in heavy ion reactions. Also the scaling of the coexistence region with beam energy is well reproduced in these results from the QMD simulation program.

## Key claim
The experimentally observed bimodality, the coexistence of multifragmentation and residue production for the same total transverse energy of light charged particles, is reproduced in Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) simulations, and the authors argue that fluctuations from elementary nucleon-nucleon collisions, not thermal equilibrium, determine which exit state is realized, marking the first observation of nonlinear dynamics (bifurcation) in heavy ion reactions.

## Method
The authors performed QMD simulations of Au+Au collisions at bombarding energies of 80, 100, and 150 A.MeV, using a soft equation of state and randomly chosen initial nucleon positions and momenta. They compared the simulated bimodality signal, quantified by the asymmetry parameter a2 = (Z_max - Z_max-1)/(Z_max + Z_max-1) in the quasi-projectile, against experimental data from the INDRA collaboration. To trace the origin of the two event classes, they used the most-bound-configuration method (simulated annealing) to identify fragment patterns early in the reaction, and analyzed the longitudinal and transverse momenta of nucleons at 60 fm/c.

## Key numbers
- The transition between large and small a2 values scales with the scaled total transverse energy Et/(E_cm/A) for Au+Au at 60, 80, 100, and 150 A.MeV bombarding energies, and the numerical value of this transition agrees between experiment and QMD simulations.
- For Au+Au at 100 A.MeV, the differential reaction cross-section of a2 as a function of Et shows two maxima separated by a minimum, reproduced qualitatively and quantitatively by QMD simulations.
- In the transition region, the total binding energy of a multifragment configuration and a residue configuration differ by as little as 100 keV, allowing a single nucleon-nucleon collision to determine the exit channel.
- QMD simulations extended to 150 A.MeV show that bimodality survives at higher incident energies.

## Context
This work extends the experimental discovery of bimodality by the INDRA collaboration (2007) in Xe+Sn and Au+Au collisions between 60 and 100 A.MeV, which had been predicted theoretically by Gross (1997) and by F. Gulminelli and P. Chomaz (1999) as a signature of a first-order phase transition in finite systems. The QMD results show that bimodality arises from nonlinear dynamics (bifurcation) rather than thermal equilibrium, contradicting the interpretation that it requires a liquid-gas phase transition. This enables future studies to distinguish between statistical and dynamical origins of multifragmentation observables, as previously debated by Bonasera (1995), B. Borderie (2002), Aichelin (1991), and Hartnack (1998).
