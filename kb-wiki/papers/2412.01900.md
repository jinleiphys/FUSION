# Estimating energy levels from lattice QCD correlation functions using a transfer matrix formalism

> We present an efficient method for extracting energy levels from lattice QCD correlation functions by computing the eigenvalues of the transfer matrix associated with the lattice QCD Hamiltonian. While mathematically and numerically equivalent to the recently introduced Lanczos procedure, our approach introduces a novel prescription for removing spurious eigenvalues using a kernel density estimator (KDE) and Gaussian-convoluted histogram method. This strategy yields a robust and stable estimate of the energy spectrum, outperforming the Cullum-Willoughby filtering technique in efficiency. In addition, we detail how this method can be applied to extract overlap factors from two-point correlation functions, as well as matrix elements from three-point functions with a current insertion. Furthermore, we extend the methodology to accommodate correlation matrices constructed from a variational basis of operators, with its Block formulation. We demonstrate the efficacy of this framework by computing the two lowest energy levels for a broad range of hadrons, including several nuclei. Although the signal-to-noise ratio is not significantly improved, the extracted energy levels are found to be more reliable than those obtained with conventional techniques. Within a given statistical ensemble, the proposed method effectively captures both statistical uncertainties and systematic errors, including those arising from the choice of fitting window, making it a robust and practical tool for lattice QCD analysis.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method for extracting energy levels from lattice QCD correlation functions that is mathematically equivalent to the recently introduced Lanczos procedure (Wagman, 2024), but introduces a novel data-driven filtering technique using kernel density estimators and Gaussian-convoluted histograms to remove spurious eigenvalues. The authors claim the method yields more reliable energy levels than conventional techniques, though they explicitly note that the signal-to-noise ratio is not significantly improved.

## Method

The authors reformulate the oblique Lanczos approach as a generalized eigenvalue problem (GEVP) on a Krylov subspace, which they call the Transfer Matrix GEVP (TGEVP). From two-point correlation functions C(n), they construct matrices T^m and V^m and solve T^m x = lambda V^m x to obtain eigenvalues of the transfer matrix, from which energies are extracted as E = -log(lambda). To filter spurious eigenvalues arising from statistical noise, they apply Gaussian-convoluted histograms and adaptive kernel density estimators (KDEs) to the bootstrap-resampled eigenvalue distributions, identifying peaks and using their full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) to define outlier rejection thresholds.

## Key numbers

* The histogram-based density estimator converges as n^{-2/3} with sample size n, while the KDE converges as n^{-4/5}, as cited from statistical literature.
* The global bandwidth for KDE pilot estimation is chosen using Silverman's rule of thumb: h = 0.9 x min(sigma, IQR/1.34) x n^{-1/5}, where IQR is the inter-quartile range, sigma is the standard deviation, and n is the sample size.
* The adaptive KDE uses a regularization parameter alpha optimized via K-fold cross-validation, with the optimal alpha determined by minimizing the average cross-validated negative log-likelihood across a grid of alpha values.
* Eigenvalues outside the range 0 < lambda <= 1 are discarded, as the transfer matrix is a positive operator.

## Context

This work extends the Lanczos-based transfer matrix approach introduced by Wagman (2024) and Hackett (2024), which used a Cullum-Willoughby filtering technique that the authors find depends sensitively on three hyperparameters that vary across datasets. The proposed KDE-based filtration provides a data-driven, hyperparameter-light alternative that achieves comparable or superior stability without manual tuning. The method also connects to the Prony GEVP method (Fleming, 2004; Fischer, 2020) and can be incorporated into multi-operator GEVP approaches to access excited states and finite-volume amplitude analysis.
