# Properties of nuclear matter from macroscopic-microscopic mass formulas

> Based on the standard Skyrme energy density functionals together with the extended Thomas-Fermi approach, the properties of symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter represented in two macroscopic-microscopic mass formulas: Lublin-Strasbourg nuclear drop energy (LSD) formula and Weizsäcker-Skyrme (WS*) formula, are extracted through matching the energy per particle of finite nuclei. For LSD and WS*, the obtained incompressibility coefficients of symmetric nuclear matter are $K_\infty=230 \pm 11$ MeV and $235\pm 11$ MeV, respectively. The slope parameter of symmetry energy at saturation density is $L=41.6\pm 7.6$ MeV for LSD and $51.5\pm 9.6$ MeV for WS*, respectively, which is compatible with the liquid-drop analysis of Lattimer and Lim [ApJ. \textbf{771}, 51 (2013)]. The density dependence of the mean-field isoscalar and isovector effective mass, and the neutron-proton effective masses splitting for neutron matter are simultaneously investigated. The results are generally consistent with those from the Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations and nucleon optical potentials, and the standard deviations are large and increase rapidly with density. A better constraint for the effective mass is helpful to reduce uncertainties of the depth of the mean-field potential.

## Key claim

The incompressibility coefficient and symmetry-energy slope parameter of nuclear matter can be extracted from macroscopic-microscopic mass formulas (LSD and WS*) by matching the liquid-drop energy of finite nuclei with Skyrme energy density functionals, but the extracted values are model-dependent and carry uncertainties due to the parameter space of the Skyrme forces rather than directly from experimental data.

## Method

The authors used the extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) approach with standard 10-parameter Skyrme energy density functionals to compute the "macroscopic" energy of spherical nuclei described by Fermi-function density distributions. They then minimized the rms deviation between this macroscopic energy per particle and the liquid-drop energy per particle from the LSD and WS* mass formulas for 70 nuclei with mass numbers from 40 to 10^6 and isospin asymmetries from 0 to 0.3, using 100 different Skyrme parameter sets as initial values and a downhill optimization algorithm.

## Key numbers

- Incompressibility coefficient of symmetric nuclear matter: \(K_\infty = 230 \pm 11\) MeV for LSD; \(235 \pm 11\) MeV for WS*
- Slope parameter of symmetry energy at saturation density: \(L = 41.6 \pm 7.6\) MeV for LSD; \(51.5 \pm 9.6\) MeV for WS*
- Symmetry energy at saturation density: \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho_0) = 29.2 \pm 0.2\) MeV for LSD; \(29.7 \pm 0.3\) MeV for WS*
- Energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter at saturation: \(e_\infty = -15.494 \pm 0.004\) MeV for LSD; \(-15.583 \pm 0.007\) MeV for WS*
- Curvature of symmetry energy at saturation: \(K_{\rm sym} = -152 \pm 41\) MeV for LSD; \(-117 \pm 46\) MeV for WS*
- Slope parameter of symmetry energy at sub-normal density \(\rho_c = 0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\): \(L_c = 40.4 \pm 2.4\) MeV for LSD; \(44.8 \pm 3.4\) MeV for WS*
- Symmetry energy at sub-normal density \(\rho_c = 0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\): \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho_c) = 23.1 \pm 0.5\) MeV for LSD; \(23.3 \pm 0.7\) MeV for WS*
- Rms deviation between Skyrme macroscopic energy and LSD liquid-drop energy: \(\sigma = 8 \pm 2\) keV for 70 nuclei
- Rms deviation for WS*: \(\sigma = 30 \pm 5\) keV
- Isoscalar effective mass at saturation density: \(m^*_s/m \approx 0.86\) for LSD; \(\approx 0.82\) for WS*
- Isovector effective mass at saturation density: \(m^*_v/m \approx 0.67\) for LSD; \(\approx 0.63\) for WS*

## Context

This work extends the approach of extracting nuclear matter properties from mass formulas by using Skyrme energy density functionals within the ETF framework, rather than from direct Hartree-Fock calculations. The results are compatible with the liquid-drop analysis of Lattimer and Lim (2013) and with Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations such as HFB-27, as well as with nucleon optical potential analyses by Xu (2010) and Li (2015). The extracted slope parameters are lower than those from relativistic calculations, and the large standard deviations for effective masses at higher densities indicate that additional constraints from other observables are needed to reduce uncertainties in the mean-field potential depth.
