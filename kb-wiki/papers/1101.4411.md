# Chiral extrapolation beyond the power-counting regime

> Chiral effective field theory can provide valuable insight into the chiral physics of hadrons when used in conjunction with non-perturbative schemes such as lattice QCD. In this discourse, the attention is focused on extrapolating the mass of the rho meson to the physical pion mass in quenched QCD (QQCD). With the absence of a known experimental value, this serves to demonstrate the ability of the extrapolation scheme to make predictions without prior bias. By using extended effective field theory developed previously, an extrapolation is performed using quenched lattice QCD data that extends outside the chiral power-counting regime (PCR). The method involves an analysis of the renormalization flow curves of the low energy coefficients in a finite-range regularized effective field theory. The analysis identifies an optimal regulator, which is embedded in the lattice QCD data themselves. This optimal regulator is the regulator value at which the renormalization of the low energy coefficients is approximately independent of the range of quark masses considered. By using recent precision, quenched lattice results, the extrapolation is tested directly by truncating the analysis to a set of points above 380 MeV, while being blinded of the results probing deeply into the chiral regime. The result is a successful extrapolation to the chiral regime.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a finite-range regularized chiral effective field theory can successfully extrapolate the rho meson mass from quenched lattice QCD data at pion masses above 380 MeV down to the chiral regime, even though the data extend outside the chiral power-counting regime. The extrapolation is tested by blinding the analysis to lattice data below 380 MeV and then comparing the predictions to those hidden results, showing the method can make unbiased predictions without prior phenomenological input.

## Method

The authors constructed a chiral extrapolation formula for the rho meson mass in quenched QCD using finite-range regularization (FRR) with a triple-dipole regulator. They analyzed the renormalization flow curves of the low-energy coefficients to identify an optimal regularization scale embedded in the lattice data themselves, where the renormalization becomes approximately independent of the range of quark masses considered. The extrapolation was tested by fitting only to lattice data points with pion masses above 380 MeV (18 data points), while keeping the 7 lighter-mass points hidden, then comparing the resulting predictions to those hidden lattice results.

## Key numbers

* The lattice simulation used a 20^3 x 32 lattice with spacing 0.153 fm, determined from the Sommer scale parameter, with 197 gauge configurations at beta = 2.264 using the Iwasaki gauge action
* The data were split at m_pi^2 = 0.143 GeV^2 (corresponding to m_pi approximately 380 MeV); 18 data points above this threshold were used for fitting, while 7 points below were kept hidden for validation
* The coupling constant g_2 was chosen as 0.65 +/- 0.09 GeV, derived from g_omega_rho_pi = 14 +/- 2 GeV^{-1} and f_pi = 0.0924 GeV
* The hairpin coupling M_0^2 was taken as 0.6 +/- 0.2 GeV^2, with A_0 = 0 +/- 0.2
* The finite-volume correction for the lowest rho meson mass at m_pi approximately 200 MeV was estimated as delta_L m_rho approximately 13 MeV, about half the statistical error of the lattice data
* For all pion masses above 400 MeV used in the fit, m*Sigma*V > 7, indicating negligible zero mode effects

## Context

This work extends the finite-range regularization approach developed by Hall (2010) and Young (2009) for extracting optimal regularization scales from lattice data, applying it specifically to the quenched rho meson mass. The quenched approximation provides an unbiased testing ground because no physical target value exists, unlike in full QCD where experimental values could introduce prejudice. The method enables reliable chiral extrapolations from lattice data that lie outside the power-counting regime, which is important because modern lattice simulations often produce data at heavier quark masses where the chiral expansion in dimensional regularization would not converge.
