# Physics and Signatures of the Quark - Gluon Plasma

> This is a critical review of the various observables that have been proposed to signal the change from dense hadronic matter to a quark-gluon plasma at high temperature or baryon density. I discuss current models of quark-gluon plasma formation in relativistic heavy ion collisions and analyze the virtues and ambiguities of various signatures. [The author especially invites comments by interested readers concerning possible inaccuracies or omissions before this review appears in print.]

## Key claim

This critical review argues that while many observables have been proposed to signal quark-gluon plasma formation, no single unambiguous signature exists because all signals are processed through or receive background from the hot hadronic gas phase that follows hadronization; the author explicitly invites reader comments on possible inaccuracies or omissions before the review appears in print.

## Method

The author groups proposed quark-gluon plasma signatures into five categories according to the physical properties of superdense hadronic matter to which they are sensitive: thermodynamic variables measuring the equation of state; probes for chiral symmetry restoration; probes of the color response function; probes of the electromagnetic response function; and exotic signatures. For each category, the review analyzes current models of quark-gluon plasma formation in relativistic heavy ion collisions, discussing both the virtues and ambiguities of each proposed signal based on theoretical studies including perturbative QCD, lattice gauge theory, and hydrodynamic models.

## Key numbers

- Energy densities in excess of 1 GeV/fm³ are inferred from rapidity distributions and transverse energy measurements at Brookhaven and CERN
- Thermalization time estimates yield values below 0.5 fm/c at collider energies above 100 GeV per nucleon in the center-of-mass system
- The QCD scale parameter Lambda is approximately 200 MeV
- The critical temperature for the phase transition is T_c approximately 170 MeV (for mu = 0), with T_c approximately 0.8 B^(1/4)
- The Debye screening length is lambda_D = (gT)^(-1), approximately 0.4 fm at T approximately 250 MeV
- The static magnetic screening length for SU(3) gauge theory is lambda_M^(-1) = C g² T with C approximately 0.31
- The effective gluon (plasmon) mass is m_g* = (1/sqrt(3)) gT, of order the temperature itself at T approximately 300 MeV and alpha_s approximately 0.3
- Gluon damping rate Gamma_G approximately 0.54 g² T; quark damping rate Gamma_Q approximately (g²/(3 pi)) T (1 + ln(1/alpha_s))
- Transport rates: Gamma_Q^(tr) approximately 2.3 alpha_s² T ln(1/alpha_s); Gamma_G^(tr) = 3 Gamma_Q^(tr)
- Characteristic equilibration times: tau_G approximately 1 fm/c, tau_Q approximately 3 fm/c
- The Lyapunov exponent for SU(3) gauge theory is lambda_0 approximately 0.54 g² T
- For Au + Au collisions at RHIC energy (E_cm = 100 GeV/u), Geiger found T approximately 325 MeV after proper time tau approximately 1.8 fm/c
- Strange quarks are predicted to equilibrate on a time scale of about 5 fm/c
- The critical temperature for hadronization is T_c approximately 150-200 MeV
- Lower momentum cut-off for perturbative QCD parton scattering is p_T^(min) approximately 1.7-2 GeV/c
- The E-735 collaboration at Fermilab found a continued rise of average transverse momentum for antiprotons and hyperons with multiplicity, exceeding 1 GeV/c for the most violent events (dN/dy > 20)

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews by Kajantie and McLerran and by Singh, as well as the Proceedings of the Strasbourg Workshop, by providing a critical assessment organized by the physical properties probed rather than by signal type. The work synthesizes results from lattice gauge theory (Columbia group, 1992), perturbative QCD studies (Braaten and Pisarski), parton cascade simulations (Geiger, 1993), and hydrodynamic models to evaluate the merits and ambiguities of proposed signatures. It enables future experimental programs at RHIC and with Pb or Au beams to focus on the most promising observables while being aware of their limitations, particularly the need for event-by-event correlation of global parameters with fireball geometry via interferometry.
