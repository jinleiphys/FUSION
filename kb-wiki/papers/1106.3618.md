# Emergence of pygmy dipole resonances: Magic numbers and neutron skins

> The pygmy dipole resonances (PDR) for even-even nuclei in 8=<Z=<40 are studied performing a systematic calculation of the random-phase approximation with the Skyrme functional of SkM*. The calculation is fully self-consistent and does not assume any symmetry in the nuclear shape of the ground state. In every isotopic chain, the PDR emerges by showing a peak of the E1 strength at energies less than 10 MeV. The E1 strength of the PDR strongly depends on the position of the Fermi level and shows a clear correlation with the occupation of the orbits with the orbital angular momenta less than 3\hbar (l =< 2). We also found a strong correlation between the isotopic dependence of the neutron skin thickness and the pygmy dipole strength. The fraction of the energy weighted strength exhausted by the PDR and the neutron skin thickness show a linear correlation with the universal rate of about 0.2/fm.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) emerges in every isotopic chain for even-even nuclei with \(8 \leq Z \leq 40\), but the actual condition is that the PDR strength suddenly increases only after specific neutron numbers (N > 14, N > 28, N > 50) which correspond to the filling of low-orbital-angular-momentum neutron orbits (\(s\), \(p\), \(d\) orbits). The abstract's statement "in every isotopic chain" is qualified by the fact that the PDR is defined as E1 strength below 10 MeV, and its prominence depends strongly on the Fermi level position.

## Method

The authors performed fully self-consistent random-phase approximation (RPA) calculations using the Skyrme functional SkM* on a three-dimensional Cartesian grid with adaptive coordinates within a sphere of radius 15 fm. The finite amplitude method (FAM) was used to evaluate the residual field without explicit construction of unoccupied orbitals, and the linear-response equation was solved at complex energies with a spacing of 0.3 MeV and a smearing width of 1.0 MeV. The calculations covered 322 even-even nuclei from oxygen to zirconium isotopes, including spherical, prolate, oblate, and triaxial ground-state shapes, without assuming any symmetry.

## Key numbers

- The PDR fraction (energy-weighted sum up to 10 MeV divided by total energy-weighted sum) is less than about 1% for nuclei around the stable region, consistent with experimental data.
- The PDR fraction suddenly increases at N = 14 → 16 for O, Ne, and Mg isotopes, at N = 28 → 30 for Si and Ar isotopes, and at N = 50 → 52 for heavier isotopes.
- The linear correlation between PDR fraction and neutron skin thickness has a universal slope of 0.18 to 0.20 fm\(^{-1}\) for isotopes with Z = 16 to 40 in the neutron number regions N = 28–34 and N ≥ 50.
- For \(^{26}\)Ne, the calculated PDR peak is at about 8 MeV carrying 2.6% of the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn (TRK) sum-rule value, compared to experimental values of about 9 MeV and 5%.
- For \(^{68}\)Ni, the calculation predicts a PDR peak around 10 MeV with \(m_1\)(PDR)/\(m_1\) of 4.6% of the TRK value (summed up to 12 MeV), compared to experimental observation around 11 MeV with about 5%.
- Enlarging the box size from 15 fm to 25 fm shifts the PDR fraction from 1.89% to 1.94% for \(^{26}\)Ne and from 4.79% to 4.65% for \(^{60}\)Ca.

## Context

This work extends previous microscopic RPA studies of the PDR, which had been applied only to limited numbers of nuclei (e.g., spherical Sn isotopes and \(^{208}\)Pb) by authors such as Colo, Sarchi, Terasaki, Matsuo, Yoshida, and Ebata (various years), and relativistic mean-field calculations by Vretenar, Paar, Piekarewicz, Liang, and Pena (various years). The paper contradicts the conclusion of Reinhard (year not specified) that there is a very weak correlation between skin thickness and PDR in \(^{208}\)Pb, instead finding a strong linear correlation for a wide range of isotopes. The results enable systematic predictions of PDR properties for neutron-rich nuclei relevant to the rapid neutron capture process in astrophysics, and suggest that measurements of the PDR can provide information on neutron skin thickness and thus the equation of state of neutron matter.
