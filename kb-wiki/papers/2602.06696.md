# Bayesian Constraints on the Neutron Star Equation of State with a Smooth Hadron-Quark Crossover

> We perform a Bayesian inference of the dense-matter equation of state (EOS) within a unified framework that incorporates hadronic matter, quark matter, and a smooth hadron-to-quark crossover. The EOS is constrained using physical consistency conditions, gravitational wave data from GW170817, NICER mass versus radius measurements, and hypothetical future high-precision radius observations. In contrast to most previous studies that assume a sharp first-order phase transition or fix part of the EOS, we simultaneously infer hadronic, quark, and crossover parameters within a single statistical framework. We find that current observations strongly constrain the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy, particularly its slope and curvature. In contrast, the highest density hadronic parameters and quark-matter properties remain only weakly constrained. We further show that the trace anomaly exhibits a remarkably universal behavior across the accepted EOS ensemble and remains largely insensitive to current observational constraints. This indicates that the present data primarily probe the low to intermediate density EOS. At the same time, robust inference of quark matter and genuinely high-density physics will require next-generation precision radius measurements or complementary observables.

## Key claim
Current neutron star observations (GW170817 and NICER) strongly constrain the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy (slope and curvature) but leave quark-matter properties and the highest-density hadronic parameters only weakly constrained, because present data primarily probe the low-to-intermediate density equation of state rather than the deep quark-dominated core.

## Method
The authors performed a Bayesian inference of the dense-matter equation of state using a unified framework that simultaneously infers hadronic, quark, and crossover parameters. The hadronic sector was described by a six-parameter meta-model expanded around saturation density, the quark sector by a two-parameter trace-anomaly parameterization, and the hadron-quark transition by a hyperbolic-tangent switching function with two parameters (center and width). Posterior distributions were sampled via Markov Chain Monte Carlo, constrained by physical consistency conditions, GW170817 data, NICER mass-radius measurements from five pulsars, and a hypothetical future high-precision radius measurement.

## Key numbers
- Crossover centered at energy density approximately 750 MeV/fm^3 (about 5 times nuclear saturation density epsilon_0) with width approximately 100 MeV/fm^3 (about 2/3 epsilon_0), corresponding to a typical crossover region spanning roughly 550 to 950 MeV/fm^3
- Maximum a posteriori values for crossover parameters: center at approximately 750 MeV/fm^3 and width at approximately 100 MeV/fm^3
- The posterior distributions favor a crossover centered at energy density epsilon in the range (4-6) epsilon_0 with width Gamma in the range (0.5-1.0) epsilon_0
- Using NICER data, the predicted radius for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star lies between 11.5 km and 13.0 km, and for a 2.0 solar mass neutron star between 10.5 km and 13.0 km
- The maximum mass M_TOV favored by all likelihood functions is approximately 2.2 solar masses
- The speed of sound peak emerges naturally near the crossover region, typically around 4 epsilon_0, and often coincides with central densities of approximately 2 solar mass neutron stars

## Context
This work extends previous Bayesian analyses by the same group (Zhang, Xie, Li, and collaborators, 2018-2025) that assumed sharp first-order phase transitions using Maxwell or Gibbs constructions, replacing those with a smooth crossover. It contrasts with studies by Brandes (2023), Alarcon (2025), and others that also modeled hadron-quark transitions as first-order. The trace-anomaly-based quark matter parameterization follows the approach of Cai and Li (2024). The results enable future work to identify what next-generation precision radius measurements from third-generation gravitational wave detectors (Chatziioannou 2021, Pacilio 2021, Bandopadhyay 2024) or complementary observables would be needed to constrain the high-density equation of state and quark matter properties.
