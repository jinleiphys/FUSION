# Gauge and Lorentz invariant pionic correlations in quasi-elastic electron scattering

> The role of the pion in the parity-conserving and parity-violating quasi-elastic nuclear response functions is analyzed within a relativistic model which fulfills gauge invariance.

## Key claim

Pionic correlations and meson-exchange currents produce a modest (5–15%) effect on the parity-conserving quasi-elastic nuclear response functions, but the parity-violating asymmetry shows strong sensitivity to these correlations at low scattering angles and momentum transfers, with the caveat that the self-energy contribution grows with momentum transfer rather than decreasing.

## Method

The author constructs a fully relativistic Fermi gas model incorporating one-pion-exchange two-body currents (seagull, pion-in-flight, and correlation currents) that satisfy gauge invariance exactly. The matrix elements of these two-body currents between the Fermi gas ground state and 1p-1h excitations are evaluated, with only the exchange term contributing in spin-isospin saturated systems. The parity-violating asymmetry is computed from the interference of electromagnetic and weak neutral currents, including both one-body and two-body contributions.

## Key numbers

- The relative contribution of two-body currents to the parity-conserving responses ranges from approximately 5% to 15% depending on kinematics
- For 40Ca with Fermi momentum k_F = 237 MeV/c, the self-energy contribution stabilizes at about 30–40% of the free response to the left of the quasi-elastic peak at high momentum transfer
- The two-body processes violate second-kind scaling by roughly three powers of k_F
- For nuclei with Fermi momenta of approximately 55/200/260 MeV/c (2H/4He/heavy nuclei), the 1p-1h MEC contributions amount to 0.1/5/10% of the total transverse response, respectively
- Scaling of the first kind is achieved at momentum transfers somewhat below 2 GeV/c for the MEC and vertex corrections

## Context

This work extends the relativistic Fermi gas model of Donnelly and collaborators (1992, 1999, 2002) by incorporating fully gauge-invariant pionic correlations, building on earlier semi-relativistic analyses by Alberico et al. (1993) and Barbaro et al. (1994, 1996) that showed the dominance of pionic correlations in the longitudinal parity-violating channel. The results enable future extension to heavier meson exchange and short-range correlations, as begun by Amaro et al. (2002), and provide a framework for interpreting parity-violating experiments aimed at extracting nucleon strangeness and axial form factors.
