# Domains of Disoriented Chiral Condensate

> The probability distribution of neutral pion fraction from independent domains of disoriented chiral condensate is characterized. The signal for the condensate is clear for a small number of domains but is greatly reduced for more than three.

## Key claim
The probability distribution of the neutral pion fraction from disoriented chiral condensate becomes indistinguishable from statistical background when more than three independent domains contribute, even though the single-domain signal is dramatic.

## Method
The authors compute the probability distribution \(P_N(f)\) for the neutral pion fraction \(f\) averaged over \(N\) independent domains, using a recursion relation that integrates over the single-domain distribution \(P_1(f)\). They treat two cases: uniform classical pion field in each domain (with \(P_1(f) = 1/(2\sqrt{f})\)) and rapidly varying field as modeled by Anselm and Ryskin (with a numerically computed \(P_1(f)\)). The results for \(N=1\) through \(8\) (uniform) and \(N=1\) through \(5\) (rapidly varying) are obtained analytically where possible and otherwise numerically.

## Key numbers
- For two uniform-field domains, \(P_2(f) = \pi/2\) for \(f < 1/2\) and \(P_2(f) = \pi/2 - 2\arccos(1/\sqrt{2f})\) for \(f > 1/2\).
- The standard deviation of the distribution scales as \(1/\sqrt{N}\), driving the distribution toward a delta function at \(f = 1/3\) for large \(N\).
- The average value of \(f\) is \(1/3\) for any number of domains, for both the uniform and rapidly varying field cases.

## Context
This work extends the single-domain treatment of disoriented chiral condensate by Amado and collaborators (1990s) and by Anselm and Ryskin (1990s) to the more realistic scenario of multiple independent domains expected in heavy-ion collisions. The results show that the characteristic \(1/(2\sqrt{f})\) signal is rapidly washed out beyond three domains, setting a clear experimental challenge: distinguishing condensate production from statistical pion production will require events with very few domains. The recursion relation method enables systematic study of domain averaging and can be generalized to unequal domain weights or mixed domain types.
