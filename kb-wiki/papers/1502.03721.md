# Breakdown of the expansion of finite-size corrections to the hydrogen Lamb shift in moments of charge distribution

> We quantify a limitation in the usual accounting of the finite-size effects, where the leading $[(Zα)^4]$ and subleading $[(Zα)^5]$ contributions to the Lamb shift are given by the mean-square radius and the third Zemach moment of the charge distribution. In the presence of any non-smooth behaviour of the nuclear form factor at scales comparable to the inverse Bohr radius, the expansion of the Lamb shift in the moments breaks down. This is relevant for some of the explanations of the "proton size puzzle". We find, for instance, that the de Rújula toy model of the proton form factor does not resolve the puzzle as claimed, despite the large value of the third Zemach moment. Without relying on the radii expansion, we show how tiny, milli-percent (pcm) changes in the proton electric form factor at a MeV scale would be able to explain the puzzle. It shows that one needs to know all the soft contributions to proton electric form factor to pcm accuracy for a precision extraction of the proton charge radius from atomic Lamb shifts.

## Key claim

The standard expansion of finite-size corrections to the hydrogen Lamb shift in terms of moments of the charge distribution (mean-square radius and third Zemach moment) breaks down when the nuclear form factor exhibits non-smooth behavior at momentum scales comparable to the inverse Bohr radius; a tiny (less than 30 ppm) fluctuation in the proton electric form factor near the muonic-hydrogen inverse Bohr radius can reconcile the electronic and muonic hydrogen proton radius values without relying on the moment expansion.

## Method

The authors derive an exact (non-expanded) expression for the first-order finite-size contribution to the Lamb shift in terms of the electric form factor or charge density, using a dispersion relation for the proton form factor and time-independent perturbation theory. They then introduce a model where the electric form factor separates into a smooth part (taken from the Arrington-Sick fit to electron-proton scattering data) and a non-smooth part described by a single Lorentzian peak centered near the muonic-hydrogen inverse Bohr radius, with parameters adjusted to reproduce the empirical Lamb shift contributions in both electronic and muonic hydrogen.

## Key numbers

- The electronic hydrogen proton charge radius from CODATA: \(R_{Ep}(e\mathrm{H}) = 0.8758(77)\) fm
- The muonic hydrogen proton charge radius: \(R_{Ep}(\mu\mathrm{H}) = 0.84087(39)\) fm
- The Lorentzian peak is placed at \(Q_0 \simeq 1.04657\) MeV, corresponding to the maximum of the weighting function above the muonic-hydrogen inverse Bohr radius
- The parameters solving the puzzle are \(A = 5.65 \times 10^{-6}\) MeV\(^2\) and \(\epsilon = 0.253\) MeV
- The magnitude of the fluctuation relative to the smooth form factor is \(|\widetilde G_E / \ol G_E| < 3 \times 10^{-5}\) for any \(Q^2\)
- The exact first-order Lamb shift from the full model gives \(-3651\) \(\mu\)eV for muonic hydrogen, compared to the expanded result of \(+759\) \(\mu\)eV (which fails)
- The exact first-order Lamb shift from the full model gives \(-0.62007\) neV for electronic hydrogen, consistent with the empirical value \(-0.620(11)\) neV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of finite-size corrections to the Lamb shift beyond the standard moment expansion of Friar (1978) and the treatment in Eides, Grotch, and Shelyuto (2000). It directly addresses the "proton size puzzle" arising from the discrepancy between the proton charge radius extracted from electronic hydrogen (CODATA value) and muonic hydrogen (Antognini et al., 2013). The authors show that the de Rújula (2010) toy model, previously claimed to resolve the puzzle via a large third Zemach moment, does not actually resolve it when the exact non-expanded formula is used. The work demonstrates that sub-percent knowledge of the proton electric form factor at MeV momentum scales is required for a reliable extraction of the proton charge radius from atomic Lamb shifts.
