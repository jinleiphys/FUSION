# Formulating Viscous Hydrodynamics for Large Velocity Gradients

> Viscous corrections to relativistic hydrodynamics, which are usually formulated for small velocity g radients, have recently been extended from Navier-Stokes formulations to a class of treatments based on Israel-Stewart equations. Israel-Stewart treatments, which treat the spatial components of the s tress-energy tensor tau_ij as dynamical objects, introduce new parameters, such as the relaxati on times describing non-equilibrium behavior of the elements tau_ij. By considering linear resp onse theory and entropy constraints, we show how the additional parameters are related to fluctuatio ns of tau_ij. Furthermore, the Israel-Stewart parameters are analyzed for their ability to prov ide stable and physical solutions for sound waves. Finally, it is shown how these parameters, which are naturally described by correlation functions in real time, might be constrained by lattice calcu lations, which are based on path-integral formulations in imaginary time.

## Key claim

The Israel-Stewart (IS) equations, which treat the spatial components of the stress-energy tensor as dynamical objects, can be extended to handle arbitrarily large velocity gradients, but the additional parameters (relaxation times and scaling functions) are constrained by entropy growth and linear response theory to be related to the statistical fluctuations of the stress-energy tensor; the paper shows that these parameters can be expressed in terms of correlation functions, but notes that lattice calculations in imaginary time cannot uniquely determine the real-time transport coefficients without assuming a functional form for the propagator.

## Method

The author derives constraints on the Israel-Stewart parameters (viscosities, relaxation times, and scaling functions) by requiring that entropy production is always positive, which reduces the number of independent parameters from six to four and relates the scaling functions to the fluctuations of the stress-energy tensor. Linear response theory and the Kubo formula are then used to connect these fluctuations to the viscosities and relaxation times. The stability of sound-wave solutions is analyzed by solving the linearized IS equations for plane-wave perturbations, yielding a cubic dispersion relation whose solutions are examined as a function of the ratio of relaxation time to a characteristic viscous time. Finally, the paper discusses how path-integral moments from lattice calculations might constrain the real-time correlation functions needed to determine the transport coefficients.

## Key numbers

- The characteristic time for bulk viscosity is defined as τ_char ≡ ζ / (h c_s^2), where ζ is bulk viscosity, h is enthalpy density, and c_s is speed of sound (fraction of speed of light).
- For bulk viscosity alone, the dispersion relation depends only on the ratio τ_b / τ_char; for τ_b / τ_char > ~0.16, the divergence in ω(k) disappears.
- For shear viscosity alone, the characteristic time is τ_char = 4η / (3 h c_s^2), where η is shear viscosity.
- The entropy production rate in the IS formalism is given by (T/V) dS/dt = b^2/ζ + Σ_i a_i^2/η, where b is the trace deviation and a_i are the shear deviations from equilibrium.
- The variances of the stress-energy fluctuations are related to viscosities and relaxation times by σ_a^2 = Tη/τ_a and σ_b^2 = Tζ/τ_b.

## Context

This work extends the Israel-Stewart (IS) formulation of relativistic viscous hydrodynamics, which was originally developed by Israel and Stewart (1970s) and later applied to heavy-ion collisions by Muronga (2002) and Baier, Romatschke (2006), among others. The paper provides a systematic derivation of constraints on IS parameters from entropy and linear response theory, complementing earlier kinetic-theory derivations by Muronga. It also analyzes the stability of sound waves in IS theory, showing that sufficiently large relaxation times cure the acausal behavior of Navier-Stokes (NS) equations. The discussion of determining IS parameters from lattice calculations addresses a key open problem, noting that while the equal-time fluctuations σ_a^2 and σ_b^2 can be computed on the lattice, the viscosities themselves require real-time information that is not directly accessible from imaginary-time path integrals, as previously noted by Kharzeev (2006) and Paech (2006).
