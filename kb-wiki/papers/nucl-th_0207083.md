# Realistic shell-model calculations for proton particle-neutron hole nuclei around 132Sn

> We have performed shell-model calculations for nuclei with proton particles and neutron holes around 132Sn using a realistic effective interaction derived from the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential. For the proton-neutron channel this is explicitly done in the particle-hole formalism. The calculated results are compared with the available experimental data, particular attention being focused on the proton particle-neutron hole multiplets. A very good agreement is obtained for all the four nuclei considered, 132Sb, 130Sb, 133Te and 131Sb. We predict many low-energy states which have no experimental counterpart. This may stimulate, and be helpful to, future experiments.

# Realistic shell-model calculations for proton particle-neutron hole nuclei around 132Sn

## Key claim

The CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential, when used to derive a low-momentum potential V_low-k and a shell-model effective interaction in the particle-hole formalism, reproduces all available experimental data for 132Sb, 130Sb, 133Te, and 131Sb to within a few tens of keV for most states, though the authors note that experimental data remain "rather scanty" and many predicted low-energy states have no experimental counterpart.

## Method

The authors started from the CD-Bonn free nucleon-nucleon potential, derived a low-momentum potential V_low-k with a cutoff momentum Lambda = 2.1 fm^-1, and then calculated shell-model effective interactions in the particle-hole (proton-neutron), particle-particle (proton-proton), and hole-hole (neutron-neutron) formalisms using a folded-diagram method with the Lee-Suzuki iteration. They considered 132Sn as a closed core with valence protons and neutron holes occupying the five levels 0g7/2, 1d5/2, 1d3/2, 2s1/2, and 0h11/2 of the 50-82 shell, using experimental single-particle energies from 133Sb and single-hole energies from 131Sn.

## Key numbers

- The cutoff momentum for V_low-k was set to Lambda = 2.1 fm^-1
- For 132Sb, the calculated 8^- state lies 226 keV above the 4^+ ground state, consistent with the experimental indication that the 8^- state is about 200 keV above the 4^+ state
- Discrepancies between calculated and experimental energies in 132Sb are "in the order of few tens of keV" except for the 1^+ state of the pi d5/2 nu d^-1_3/2 multiplet (about 300 keV above experiment) and the 9^- state of the pi g7/2 nu h^-1_11/2 multiplet (about 200 keV below experiment)
- For 133Te, discrepancies between calculated and experimental excitation energies are "well below 100 keV for most of the states"
- For 131Sb, discrepancies are larger than 100 keV only for the 3/2^+ and 11/2^+ states
- The Pandya-transformed matrix elements from earlier work differ from the directly calculated particle-hole matrix elements by within 150 keV for all cases except the J=0 matrix elements, which differ by about 700 keV
- Proton single-particle energies used (in MeV): epsilon_g7/2 = 0.0, epsilon_d5/2 = 0.962, epsilon_d3/2 = 2.439, epsilon_h11/2 = 2.793, epsilon_s1/2 = 2.800
- Neutron single-hole energies used (in MeV): epsilon^-1_d3/2 = 0.0, epsilon^-1_h11/2 = 0.100, epsilon^-1_s1/2 = 0.332, epsilon^-1_d5/2 = 1.655, epsilon^-1_g7/2 = 2.434

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous realistic shell-model studies of nuclei around 132Sn (Andreozzi et al. 1997, Covello et al. 1997, 1999, Andreozzi et al. 1999, Coraggio et al. 2002) by applying the particle-hole formalism to proton particle-neutron hole nuclei, reviving an approach first used in the late 1960s and early 1970s for nuclei such as 208Bi, 40Ca, 48Ca, and 208Pb (Kuo 1968, Blomqvist 1969, Kuo 1970) using the Hamada-Johnston potential. The calculations use a new V_low-k approach (Bogner et al. 2002) as an alternative to the traditional Brueckner G-matrix method. The results enable predictions for many unobserved low-energy states that may guide future experiments on neutron-rich nuclei far from stability, and the calculated multiplets show the same pattern as observed in 208Bi (Kuo 1968, Moinester 1969) and agree with the Brennan-Bernstein coupling rule (Brennan and Bernstein 1960).
