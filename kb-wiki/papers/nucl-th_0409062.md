# Nuclear Structure Calculations with Coupled Cluster Methods from Quantum Chemistry

> We present several coupled-cluster calculations of ground and excited states of 4He and 16O employing methods from quantum chemistry. A comparison of coupled cluster results with the results of exact diagonalization of the hamiltonian in the same model space and other truncated shell-model calculations shows that the quantum chemistry inspired coupled cluster approximations provide an excellent description of ground and excited states of nuclei, with much less computational effort than traditional large-scale shell-model approaches. Unless truncations are made, for nuclei like 16O, full-fledged shell-model calculations with four or more major shells are not possible. However, these and even larger systems can be studied with the coupled cluster methods due to the polynomial rather than factorial scaling inherent in standard shell-model studies. This makes the coupled cluster approaches, developed in quantum chemistry, viable methods for describing weakly bound systems of interest for future nuclear facilities.

# Nuclear Structure Calculations with Coupled Cluster Methods from Quantum Chemistry

## Key claim
Coupled cluster methods adapted from quantum chemistry can provide an excellent description of ground and excited states of nuclei like 4He and 16O with much less computational effort than traditional large-scale shell-model approaches, but the accuracy depends on using a G-matrix effective interaction tailored to a specific model space rather than a bare nucleon-nucleon interaction, and the method still misses experimental binding energy by about 1 MeV per particle and root-mean-square radii by a few percent, likely due to missing three-body forces.

## Method
The authors performed coupled-cluster singles and doubles (CCSD) calculations for ground states and equation-of-motion CCSD (EOMCCSD) calculations for excited states of 4He and 16O, using a G-matrix effective two-body Hamiltonian defined with a no-core Pauli operator in a harmonic oscillator basis. The G-matrix was computed in model spaces of up to seven major oscillator shells, and the coupled cluster equations were solved using diagrammatic techniques with recursively generated intermediates. For 16O, noniterative triples corrections (CR-CCSD(T)) were also applied to the ground state energy.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state energy of 4He at CCSD level: approximately -26.5 MeV for a starting energy of -30.0 MeV (compared to -27.40 MeV from Navratil and Ormand (2002) using the Idaho-A interaction)
- Ground-state binding energy of 16O at CCSD level with seven major oscillator shells: 7.52 MeV per particle using the Idaho-A potential (experimental value: 7.98 MeV per particle)
- Ground-state energy of 16O in a four-oscillator-shell model space: CCSD = -139.310 MeV, CR-CCSD(T) = -139.467 MeV, compared to SM-SDTQ (shell-model singles, doubles, triples, quadruples) = -138.387 MeV
- Excitation energies of 4He from EOMCCSD: J=1 state at 11.791 MeV, J=0 state at 21.203 MeV, J=2 state at 22.435 MeV (exact diagonalization values: 11.465 MeV, 21.569 MeV, 22.697 MeV respectively)
- First 3_1^- state in 16O from EOMCCSD: 13.57 MeV for five shells, 12.98 MeV for six shells (experimental value: 6.13 MeV)
- Root-mean-square radius of 16O: 2.389 fm for five shells, 2.437 fm for six shells, 2.445 fm for seven major oscillator shells (experimental value: 2.73 ± 0.025 fm)

## Context
This work extends the application of coupled cluster methods from quantum chemistry to nuclear structure, building on early nuclear coupled cluster studies by Coester (1958), Kummel, Luhrmann, and Zabolitzky (1978), and more recent work by Bishop et al. and Mihaila and Heisenberg (1999). The authors compare their results to exact diagonalization in the same model space and to truncated shell-model calculations (SM-SD, SM-SDT, SM-SDTQ), demonstrating that CCSD recovers the bulk of correlation effects. The approach enables studies of nuclei like 16O that are beyond the reach of full shell-model calculations with four or more major shells, which scale factorially rather than polynomially. The method is intended for future application to weakly bound nuclei near the dripline, where coupling to continuum structures becomes important, building on the Gamow shell model approach of Betan et al. and Michel et al.
