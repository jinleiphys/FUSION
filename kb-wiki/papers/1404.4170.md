# Relativistic R matrix and continuum shell model

> Background: The $R$ matrix formalism of Lane and Thomas has proven to be a convenient reaction theory for solving many-coupled channel systems. The theory provides solutions to bound states, scattering states, and resonances for microscopic models in one formalism. Purpose: The first purpose is to extend this formalism to the relativistic case so that the many-coupled channels problem may be solved for systems in which binary breakup channels satisfy a relative Dirac equation. The second purpose is to employ this formalism in a relativistic continuum shell model. Methods: Expressions for the collision matrix and the scattering amplitude, from which observables may be calculated, are derived. The formalism is applied to the 1p-1h relativistic continuum shell model with an interaction extracted from relativistic mean-field theory. Results: The simplest of the $σ+ω+ρ$ exchange interactions produces a good description of the single-particle energies in $^{16}$O and $^{90}$Zr and a reasonable description of proton scattering from $^{15}$N. Conclusions: The development of a calculable, relativistic $R$ matrix and its implementation in a $1p-1h$ relativistic continuum shell model provide a simple relatively self-consist, physically justifiable model for use in knockout reactions.

## Key claim

The paper extends the non-relativistic R-matrix formalism of Lane and Thomas to the relativistic case, enabling coupled-channels solutions for systems where binary breakup channels satisfy a relative Dirac equation; the simplest sigma+omega+rho exchange interaction with QHD coupling constants provides a "surprisingly good" description of single-particle energies and proton scattering, though the authors note this agreement is unexpected given that the coupling constants were not originally fit for structure calculations.

## Method

The authors derived expressions for the collision matrix and scattering amplitude within a relativistic R-matrix framework, using Dirac oscillator wave functions as an expansion basis. They applied the formalism to a 1p-1h relativistic continuum shell model (Tamm-Dancoff approximation) for 16O, replacing the classical meson fields of quantum hydrodynamics (QHD) with one-meson exchange potentials. Single-particle energies were calculated for 90Zr and 16O, and elastic proton scattering cross sections from 15N at 39.84 MeV were computed and compared to experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Single-particle energies for 90Zr: FH QHD calculation gives 0s1/2(p) at -52.43 MeV, while the one-meson exchange (FH, E^x_jj') gives -49.11 MeV; 0g9/2(n) at -12.26 MeV (QHD) vs -10.08 MeV (one-meson exchange)
- Single-particle energies for 16O: 1p3/2(p) at -16.7 MeV (QHD) vs -17.8 MeV (one-meson exchange), compared to experimental -17.4 MeV; 1p1/2(p) at -8.8 MeV (QHD) vs -4.0 MeV (one-meson exchange), compared to experimental -12.1 MeV
- Proton elastic scattering from 15N calculated at 39.84 MeV, with the FH parameter set producing forward-angle agreement comparable to a non-relativistic calculation using the M3Y interaction
- Coupling constants used: sigma mass 520 MeV with g^2=109.6 (FH); omega mass 783 MeV with g^2=190.4 (FH); rho mass 770 MeV with g^2=65.2 (FH); pion mass 138 MeV with g^2=181 (HF)

## Context

This work extends the R-matrix formalism of Lane and Thomas (1958) to the relativistic domain, building on the authors' prior demonstrations that an R-matrix theory exists for the Dirac equation (Halderson 1988) and that Dirac oscillator wave functions provide a convenient expansion basis (Halderson 2009). The relativistic continuum shell model with QHD-derived interactions provides a simple, relatively self-consistent framework for investigating relativistic effects in knockout reactions, complementing non-relativistic approaches such as the recoil corrected continuum shell model with the M3Y interaction (Bertsch 1977). The inclusion of pions via pseudoscalar and pseudovector coupling shows significant effects on single-particle energies and cross sections, but the authors conclude that a better approximation for pseudovector matrix elements is needed before definitive conclusions can be drawn.
