# The q-Deformed NJL Model "Revisited"

> In this work we investigate the chiral symmetry breaking in the q-deformed version of the NJL Model and its consequent mass generation mechanism . We show that the deformation of the NJL model, in the mean field approximation, may take into account correlations that go beyond the mean field and, in a certain limit, approaches the more realistic lattice calculations.

## Key claim

The q-deformed NJL model, in the mean field approximation, can mimic correlations that go beyond the mean field and, for certain choices of the deformation parameter, reproduces the qualitative behavior of more realistic lattice Monte Carlo and Schwinger-Dyson calculations, specifically a smaller condensate and a larger critical coupling constant.

## Method

The authors map the NJL Hamiltonian in the Hartree approximation onto an su(2) angular momentum algebra using a BCS-type variational ansatz. They then replace the su(2) algebra with the su_q(2) deformed algebra, introduce a q-deformed exponential in the variational state, and minimize the mean value of the Hamiltonian to obtain a modified gap equation. Two deformation prescriptions are considered (r=q and r=1), and two ways of treating the kinetic term (non-deformed kinetic, NDK, and deformed kinetic, DK) are compared.

## Key numbers

- The critical coupling constant for the deformed model is given by $G_c^q = \frac{2G_c}{[2]}$, where $G_c = \pi^2 / \Lambda^2$ and the cutoff $\Lambda = 600$ MeV.
- For the case $r=1$ with $q=2$, the deformed kinetic (DK) results produce strong quantitative modifications to the phase transition point.
- For complex $q$ values (with $r=q$) or for $q < 1$ (with $r=1$), the condensate decreases relative to the $q=1$ case, mimicking a repulsive interaction.
- For real $q$ (with $r=q$) or $q > 1$ (with $r=1$), the condensate increases relative to the $q=1$ case, mimicking an attractive interaction.

## Context

This work extends the earlier q-deformed NJL analysis of Celso (1999) by using an su_q(2) algebraic formulation that allows different deformation schemes and yields a q-dependent critical coupling, a feature absent in the prior study. The results are compared qualitatively with lattice Monte Carlo and Schwinger-Dyson calculations by Khan (1999), which show that better approximations give a larger critical coupling and a smaller condensate than the standard gap equation. The q-deformed model can reproduce this behavior for deformation parameters that simulate a reduced attractive interaction, suggesting it may serve as a simpler effective description of beyond-mean-field effects.
