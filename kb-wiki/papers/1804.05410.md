# Scission Dynamics with K Partitions

> We propose a framework to calculate the dynamics at the scission point of nuclear fission, based as far as possible on a discrete representation of orthogonal many-body configurations. Assuming axially symmetric scission shapes, we use the $K$ orbital quantum number to build a basis of wave functions. Pre-scission configurations are stable under mean-field dynamics while post-scission configurations evolve to separated fragments. In this first exploratory study, we analyze a typical fission trajectory through to scission in terms of these configurations. We find that there is a major rearrangement of the $K$ occupancy factors at scission. Interestingly, very different fragment shapes occur in the post-scission configurations, even starting from the same pre-scission configuration.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a configuration-interaction (CI) framework for fission scission dynamics based on discrete, orthogonal many-body configurations labeled by the K orbital quantum number, but explicitly states this is only a first exploratory study addressing the feasibility of constructing a useful orthogonal basis from self-consistent mean-field orbitals, leaving the treatment of post-scission states and coupling between configurations for future work.

## Method

The authors model fission of 236U by following a single trajectory of generator-coordinate-method (GCM) constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) configurations using the quadrupole operator Q20 as the generator, with the Gogny D1S interaction. They then project the HFB wave functions onto Hartree-Fock (HF) configurations by adiabatically reducing the pairing strength until orbital occupancy factors approach zero or one, characterizing the resulting configurations by their K-partition (the number of pairs of different |K| values). The HF minima are then computed both with and without shape constraints to identify pre-scission and post-scission configurations.

## Key numbers

* The HFB trajectory shows a cliff near Q20 ~ 310 b where the energy drops by about 10 MeV and the neck size precipitously drops to a small value.
* The single-particle Hamiltonian has a largest block (K = 1/2) with dimension 140; the harmonic oscillator basis uses N_r = 12 with q = 1.7, and length parameters b_z = 2.97 fm and b_r = 1.9 fm.
* From the second saddle to the Glider configuration, there are 15 pair jumps; the final transition from Glider to Bobsled has N_J = 6 (3 for neutrons and 3 for protons).
* There are 2160 minimal-length paths connecting Glider and Bobsled according to the formula N_P = N_J! * (N_Jp! * N_Jn!) / (Π_{Kσ} ΔN_{Kσ}!).
* Converged shape parameters for named configurations: Lighthouse Q20 = 262 b, Q30 = 42.3 b^{3/2}, neck parameter 6.9; Buenavista Q20 = 394 b, Q30 = 65.1 b^{3/2}, neck 1.9; Glider Q20 = 416 b, Q30 = 66.6 b^{3/2}, neck 0.1; Bobsled Q20 = 434 b, Q30 = 42.9 b^{3/2}, neck ~0.0.
* Fragment properties: Bobsled yields 132Sn (Z=50, N=82, Q20=0.4 b) and 104Mo (Z=42, N=62, Q20=-5.3 b); Glider yields 136Te (Z=52, N=84, Q20=4.0 b) and 100Zr (Z=40, N=60, Q20=7.6 b).
* The estimated number of unpaired particles in the initial compound nucleus for induced fission of 236U by thermal neutrons is in the range 15-20, using the formula ν = (aU)^{1/2} log 4 with a ≈ A/8.

## Context

This work extends the generator coordinate method (GCM) approach to fission dynamics, which has been highly successful for potential energy surfaces but becomes problematic for calculating induced fission dynamics due to competition between many configurations and non-orthogonality of basis functions (as noted by Bender and Heenen (1990) and others). The paper proposes an alternative configuration-interaction (CI) representation using deformed mean-field orbitals rather than spherical shell model orbitals, contrasting with the GCM approach of shape variables used by Dubray and Regnier (2012) and others. The results enable future work on computing overlaps between shape-constrained configurations, calculating interaction matrix elements between configurations, and ultimately determining whether fission dynamics is dominated by inertial or dissipative processes, building on statistical models by Wilkins (1976) and Panebianco (2012) and quantum treatments by Goutte (2005) and others.
