# Gravitational waves from neutron star mergers and their relation to the nuclear equation of state

> In this article, I introduce ideas and techniques to extract information about the equation of state of matter at very high densities from gravitational waves emitted before, during and after the merger of binary neutron stars. I also review current work and results on the actual use of the first gravitational-wave observation of a neutron-star merger to set constraints on properties of such equation of state. In passing, I also touch on the possibility that what we observe in gravitational waves are not neutron stars, but something more exotic. In order to make this article more accessible, I also review the dynamics and gravitational-wave emission of neutron-star mergers in general, with focus on numerical simulations and on which representations of the equation of state are used for studies on binary systems.

## Key claim

Gravitational-wave observations of binary neutron-star mergers can constrain the nuclear equation of state at densities above nuclear saturation, but the single event GW170817 may not have provided new insights beyond what was already available from nuclear physics theory, experiments, and preceding astrophysical observations, as argued by a few authors (Zhang 2018, Lim 2018, Tews 2019).

## Method

The paper reviews numerical-relativity simulations of binary neutron-star mergers, focusing on how the gravitational-wave signal before merger (inspiral phase), during merger, and after merger (post-merger oscillations and black-hole ringdown) encodes information about the equation of state. It describes how the tidal deformability extracted from the inspiral waveform, the post-merger oscillation frequencies, and the collapse timescale of the hypermassive neutron-star remnant each correlate with the stiffness of the equation of state. The review also covers parameterizations of the equation of state, specifically piecewise-polytropic and spectral parameterizations, that are used to map gravitational-wave observables to nuclear-matter properties.

## Key numbers

- The chirp mass for GW170817 was measured as \(M_{\rm chirp} = 1.186 \pm 0.001 M_\odot\) by the LIGO-Virgo Collaborations (Abbott 2018a).
- The initial coordinate separation between stellar centres in a representative simulation was 45 km (Baiotti 2008).
- During the rapid infall after merger, the cores experience a decompression of approximately 15% for about 1 ms.
- The hypermassive neutron star in the representative simulation collapsed to a black hole about 15 ms after merger.
- The accretion torus formed after collapse has an average density between \(10^{12}\) and \(10^{13}\) g/cm\(^3\).
- The post-merger gravitational-wave signal from before collapse is at frequencies of several kHz, and the ringdown signal is at even higher frequencies, making detection probably limited to close sources.
- Current detectors have maximum sensitivity around 1 kHz.

## Context

This review extends the line of work connecting gravitational-wave observations to the nuclear equation of state, building on numerical-relativity simulations by groups such as Baiotti (2008), Shibata (2000), and others. It summarizes the constraints obtained from the first binary neutron-star merger detection GW170817 (Abbott 2017) and discusses how future detections with advanced detectors like Advanced LIGO, Advanced Virgo, KAGRA, and planned instruments such as the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer will improve constraints. The review also notes open questions about the role of magnetic fields, neutrino radiation transport, and the possibility that observed compact objects may be exotic stars such as quark stars, hybrid stars, or boson stars rather than ordinary neutron stars.
