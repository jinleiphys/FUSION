# Towards a Global Search for New Physics with Isotope Shifts

> Isotope shifts have emerged as a sensitive probe of new bosons that couple to electrons and neutrons, and of nuclear structure. The recent Hz- or even sub-Hz-level isotope shift measurements across different elements call for a global assessment of all available data. In this work, we present the fit framework kifit that for the first time enables a combined analysis of isotope shift data from several elements, taking into account correlations. We provide a thorough comparison of analytical methods and the fit to analyse linear and nonlinear King plots and quantify their uncertainties. Finally, we provide recommendations for future measurements that could enhance the sensitivity to new physics and offer new insights into nuclear structure.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first framework (kifit) that enables a combined, global analysis of isotope shift data from multiple elements to constrain new physics, but it explicitly restricts the analysis to linear King plots and leaves the incorporation of higher-order nuclear effects (needed for nonlinear King plots) to future work.

## Method

The authors review algebraic methods for extracting new physics bounds from King plots (Minimal King Plot, No-Mass King Plot, Projection Method) and then introduce their new fit framework kifit, which performs a combined fit to linear King plots plus new physics across multiple elements while accounting for correlations. The electronic coefficients for new physics sensitivity are calculated using the public code AMBiT, which applies a non-perturbative finite field method that adds the new physics Yukawa potential directly to the Dirac Hamiltonian. The framework is validated by comparing its results to the algebraic methods on selected transitions in Ca+ and Yb+.

## Key numbers

* The first observation of a nonlinear King plot in Yb+ used transitions measured at a precision of about 300 Hz (Counts 2020).
* The King nonlinearity in Yb+ is currently found to be at the level of 20.17(2) kHz (Door 2024).
* Nuclear mass ratios for Ca+ and Ca14+ have relative uncertainties below 4 x 10^{-11} (Wilzewski 2024).
* Optical atomic clocks have reached relative precision of 10^{-18} (Ye 2024).

## Context

This work extends the fit approach first presented in Frugiuele (2016) to handle contemporary high-precision isotope shift data from multiple elements, building on the King plot method for new physics searches proposed by Delaunay (2016) and Berengut (2017). The framework enables a global assessment of all available isotope shift data, which previously could only be analyzed element-by-element, and provides recommendations for future measurements that could enhance sensitivity to new physics and offer new insights into nuclear structure.
