# r-process nucleosynthesis with ab initio nuclear masses around the N=82 shell closure

> Our understanding of the origin of heavy elements beyond iron relies on the rapid neutron capture process (r-process), which accounts for roughly half of their cosmic abundance. However, the extreme neutron-rich conditions required for the r-process involve many nuclei that remain experimentally inaccessible, making theoretical predictions essential. We explore the impact of nuclear masses calculated with the ab initio valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group, focusing on the region around the N = 82 shell closure. We show that such ab initio mass calculations can refine the r-process predictions compared to global, but more phenomenological mass models. With the ab initio masses, the waiting point of the second r-process peak is strengthened, which leads to an overall slower nucleosynthesis flow, lower abundances of nuclei beyond the peak, and a stronger shift of the third r-process peak.

## Key claim

Using ab initio VS-IMSRG nuclear masses for 70 isotopes around the N=82 shell closure, the r-process waiting point at the second peak is strengthened, leading to slower nucleosynthesis flow and lower abundances beyond the second peak, but the magnitude of this effect depends on the astrophysical environment and is most pronounced for neutron star merger conditions.

## Method

The authors performed valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) calculations for 70 isotopes (Pd, Ag, Cd, In, Sn) using NN+3N 1.8/2.0 (EM) chiral interactions, with two different valence spaces (VS1 and VS2) to assess many-body truncation uncertainties. These ab initio masses were incorporated into the WinNet reaction network on top of a baseline FRDM2012 plus AME2020 mass model, with neutron capture rates recalculated using TALYS2.0 and photo-dissociation rates from detailed balance. Nucleosynthesis calculations were performed for representative trajectories from neutron star merger (NSM), NSM disk ejecta, magneto-rotational supernova (MRSN), and neutron star black hole merger (NSBH) simulations, plus mass-integrated calculations for 11,218 NSM trajectories.

## Key numbers

- 70 isotopes calculated with VS-IMSRG: 122-134Pd, 123-135Ag, 124-137Cd, 125-139In, and 126-140Sn
- Second peak abundances at A=132 are higher by a factor of 2-3 when using VS-IMSRG masses for the NSM trajectory
- The final phase of (n,gamma)-(gamma,n) equilibrium occurs around Sn_eq approximately 1.3-1.7 MeV for the NSM trajectory
- The r-process crosses the N=82 region around 0.2 s in the NSM trajectory, with the second waiting region at N=86
- 11,218 mass-weighted trajectories from the NSM simulation were used for integrated abundance calculations

## Context

This work extends the traditional approach of supplementing experimental masses with global phenomenological models like FRDM2012 (Moller 2019) by incorporating ab initio VS-IMSRG masses, which had previously been successful in predicting the two-neutron shell gap in N=82 isotones (Manea 2020) and empirical pairing gaps in Sn isotopes (Mollaebrahimi 2025). The results contradict the predictions of global mass models for the region beyond N=82 and demonstrate that high-fidelity theoretical masses can refine r-process predictions where experimental data remain inaccessible. This enables future studies to target a larger set of nuclei and include consistent ab initio calculations of beta decay half-lives and beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities.
