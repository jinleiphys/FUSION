# Thermalization of non-abelian gauge theories at next-to-leading order

> We provide the first next-to-leading-order (NLO) weak-coupling description of the thermalization process of far-from-equilibrium systems in non-abelian gauge theory. We study isotropic systems starting from either over- or under-occupied initial conditions and follow their time evolution towards thermal equilibrium by numerically solving the QCD effective kinetic theory at NLO accuracy. We find that the NLO corrections remain well under control for a wide range of couplings and that the overall effect of NLO corrections is to reduce the time needed to reach thermal equilibrium in the systems considered.

## Key claim

The first next-to-leading-order (NLO) weak-coupling description of thermalization in non-abelian gauge theory shows that NLO corrections remain well under control for a wide range of couplings, but the abstract's claim that corrections "reduce the time needed to reach thermal equilibrium" glosses over the condition that this reduction is modest (5-20% for small couplings) and that the corrections are only fully controlled for isotropic systems where the dependence on transverse momentum broadening is significantly reduced.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the QCD effective kinetic theory at NLO accuracy for isotropic systems, extending the NLO formulation to far-from-equilibrium conditions. They constructed two separate collision operator schemes (scheme 1 and scheme 2) that both include all O(lambda T_*/m_d) effects but differ at higher orders, using the spread between schemes as an uncertainty estimate. The evolution was followed from either under-occupied initial conditions (a gaussian distribution centered at momentum scale Q embedded in a soft thermal bath carrying 10% of energy density) or over-occupied initial conditions (starting from the self-similar scaling solution), with thermalization time defined by the condition that effective temperatures T_0 and T_1 agree within 10%.

## Key numbers

- For underoccupied initial conditions at lambda = 1 and Q/T = 35, the LO thermalization time (lambda^2 T t_eq) is 749.3, while NLO scheme 1 gives 712.5 and NLO scheme 2 gives 720.7, representing roughly a 5% reduction
- For overoccupied initial conditions at lambda = 1, the LO thermalization time is 76.2, while NLO scheme 1 gives 65.4 and NLO scheme 2 gives 66.6, representing roughly a 14% reduction
- For underoccupied initial conditions at lambda = 10 and Q/T = 50, the LO thermalization time is 1129.1, while NLO scheme 1 gives 1027.6 and NLO scheme 2 gives 1086.4
- For overoccupied initial conditions at lambda = 10, the LO thermalization time is 101.0, while NLO scheme 1 gives 59.3 and NLO scheme 2 gives 70.7, representing up to a 40% reduction
- The NLO correction for underoccupied small-lambda systems follows approximately t_LO/t_NLO = 1 + lambda^(1/2) * (0.22 - 0.05 log(Q/T))
- The NLO correction for overoccupied small-lambda systems follows approximately t_LO/t_NLO = 1 + 0.14 * lambda^(1/2)

## Context

This work extends the leading-order effective kinetic theory framework of Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2002) and the LO thermalization studies of Kurkela and collaborators (2014) to NLO accuracy for the first time. It contrasts with prior NLO calculations for transport coefficients by Ghiglieri and collaborators (2018), which found that NLO corrections completely overtake LO results for lambda near 10; the authors argue that the better convergence here arises because isotropic systems have reduced sensitivity to the transverse momentum broadening coefficient q_hat. The results enable improved phenomenological modeling of bottom-up thermalization in heavy-ion collisions, though the authors caution that extension to anisotropic systems undergoing Bjorken expansion would require including isotropization effects and plasma instabilities.
