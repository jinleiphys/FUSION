# Final state interaction in lepton scattering off deuterons

> The role of the final state interactions (FSI) in inclusive electro disintegration of the deuteron, D(e,e')X, is being investigated within different approaches. A detailed comparison between an improved Glauber method and the standard Schroedinger approach is presented. It is shown that both methods become inadequate at large values of Q^2, where the virtuality of the hit nucleon after photon absorbtion is very high. The concept of finite formation time (FFT) required by the hit hadron to reach its asymptotic form is introduced by a Feynman diagram approach and by explicitly taking into account the dependence of the ejected nucleon on its virtuality. The approach has been applied both at x_{Bj} ~= 1, as well as in the so called cumulative region i.e. at x_{Bj} > 1. Numerical calculations show that the effects of the FFT almost completely cancel the contribution from rescattering processes. In the cumulative region the color transparency or finite formation time effects become fairly visible.

# Final state interaction in lepton scattering off deuterons

## Key claim

At high momentum transfer (large Q²), both the standard Schroedinger approach and the improved Glauber method become inadequate because the virtuality of the hit nucleon after photon absorption is very high; however, introducing the concept of finite formation time (FFT) via a Feynman diagram approach that accounts for the dependence of the ejected nucleon on its virtuality shows that FFT effects almost completely cancel the contribution from rescattering processes, with color transparency or FFT effects becoming fairly visible in the cumulative region (x_Bj > 1).

## Method

The inclusive electro disintegration of the deuteron, D(e,e')X, was computed within the plane wave impulse approximation (PWIA) and then with final state interactions (FSI) included via three approaches: the Schroedinger approach using continuum two-nucleon wave functions, the Glauber eikonal approximation with an elastic profile function, and a Feynman diagram method that introduces finite formation time by subtracting an excited-state propagator term to account for the virtuality of the struck nucleon. The cross sections and scaling functions were compared at various momentum transfers, both at x_Bj ~ 1 and in the cumulative region x_Bj > 1.

## Key numbers

- At low total energies sqrt(s) of the np pair (below pion production threshold), the Schroedinger approach provides a good description of data, while the Glauber approximation is not valid at such relative energies.
- The formation length is given by l(Q²) = Q² / (x_Bj m m*²), which increases with Q² and at Q² → infinity completely cancels the contribution from the usual eikonal rescattering term.
- In the kinematical region of interest (y < -0.8 GeV/c, s below the pion production threshold), relativistic corrections (P-waves) may be disregarded and the covariant relativistic momentum distribution resembles the non-relativistic distribution.
- Present experimental data are far from the asymptotic region where FFT effects would fully cancel rescattering.

## Context

This work extends the comparison of FSI treatments in deuteron electro disintegration previously studied by Ciofi degli Atti, Kaptari, and Treleani (2001) using the Schroedinger and Glauber approaches, and by Braun, Ciofi degli Atti, and Treleani (2000) who introduced the FFT method. The paper shows that at high Q² the one-channel Glauber approximation overestimates FSI because it neglects the finite formation time required for the struck hadron to reach its asymptotic form, a phenomenon related to color transparency predicted by Zamolodchikov, Kopeliovich, and Lapidus (1981) and reviewed by Nikolaev (1994) and Jain, Pire, and Ralston (1996). The approach enables more reliable extraction of ground state correlations from semi-inclusive and exclusive reactions on light nuclei, which is relevant to new experimental programs discussed at the Third International Conference on Perspectives in Hadronic Physics (2001).
