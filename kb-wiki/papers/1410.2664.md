# Half-lives of $α$ decay from natural nuclides and from superheavy elements

> Recently, experimental researches on the $α$ decay with long lifetime are one of hot topics in the contemporary nuclear physics [e.g. N. Kinoshita {\sl et al.} (2012) and J. W. Beeman {\sl et al.} (2012) ]. In this study, we have systematically investigated the extremely long-lived $α$-decaying nuclei within a generalized density-dependent cluster model involving the experimental nuclear charge radii. In detail, the important density distribution of daughter nuclei is deduced from the corresponding experimental charge radii, leading to an improved $α$-core potential in the quantum tunneling calculation of $α$-decay width. Besides the excellent agreement between theory and experiment, predictions on half-lives of possible candidates for natural $α$ emitters are made for future experimental detections. In addition, the recently confirmed $α$-decay chain from $^{294}$117 is well described, including the attractive long-lived $α$-decaying $^{270}$Db, i.e., a positive step towards the "island of stability" in the superheavy mass region.

## Key claim

The authors claim that incorporating experimental nuclear charge radii of daughter nuclei into a generalized density-dependent cluster model improves the description of the alpha-core potential and yields calculated half-lives that agree with experimental data within a mean factor of 1.5, but this agreement is conditional on fixing the diffuseness parameter at different constants for medium and heavy nuclei and on using constant alpha-preformation factors fitted separately for even-even and odd-A nuclei.

## Method

The authors constructed the alpha-core potential via a double-folding integral of the M3Y-Reid-type nucleon-nucleon interaction with the density distributions of the alpha particle and the daughter nucleus, where the daughter density was parameterized in a Fermi form with its half-density radius derived from experimental root-mean-square charge radii. The alpha-decay width was then calculated by solving the Schrodinger equation for the bound state using the Wildermuth condition for the node number and applying the two-potential approach for the tunneling problem, with the nuclear potential renormalization factor adjusted to reproduce the experimental Q value. The final half-life was obtained by dividing the decay width by a constant alpha-preformation factor (0.42 for even-even nuclei, 0.15 for odd-A nuclei) fitted to experimental data.

## Key numbers

* The calculated half-lives agree with experimental data within a mean factor of 1.5 for the natural alpha emitters studied.
* The renormalization factor lambda for the nuclear potential varied in a small range of 0.613-0.707 across the natural alpha emitters.
* The alpha-preformation factor was taken as 0.42 for even-even nuclei and 0.15 for odd-A nuclei, consistent with Buck's model and close to the microscopic calculation for Po-212.
* The diffuseness parameter a was fixed at 0.54 fm for heavy nuclei with N greater than 126 and at 0.52 fm for medium nuclei with N less than or equal to 126.
* The standard half-density radius parameter r0 was taken as 1.07 fm from the nuclear textbook for the superheavy region.

## Context

This work extends the density-dependent cluster model previously developed by the authors' group (Qian et al., 2012; Qian et al., 2013) by incorporating experimental charge radii to determine the density distribution of daughter nuclei, rather than using fixed textbook parameters. The study provides predictions for natural alpha emitters such as Sm-149, Hf-174,176,178, Os-184, and Pt-192 for future experimental detection, and it successfully describes the newly confirmed alpha-decay chain from element 117 (Khuyagbaatar et al., 2014), including the long-lived Db-270, which represents a step toward the "island of stability" in the superheavy mass region. The results are compared with the UMADAC model (Denisov and Khudenko, 2009) and the analytic formulas of Royer (2000).
