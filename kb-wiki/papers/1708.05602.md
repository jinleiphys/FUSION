# Nonequilibrium thermodynamics with binary quantum correlations

> The balance equations for thermodynamic quantities are derived from the nonlocal quantum kinetic equation. The nonlocal collisions lead to molecular contributions to the observables and currents. The corresponding correlated part of the observables is found to be given by the rate to form a molecule multiplied with its lifetime which can be considered as collision duration. Explicit expressions of these molecular contributions are given in terms of the scattering phase shifts. The two-particle form of the entropy is derived. This extends the Landau quasiparticle picture by two-particle molecular contributions. There is a continuous exchange of correlations into kinetic parts condensing into the rate of correlated variables for energy and momentum. For the entropy, an explicit gain remains and Boltzmann's H-theorem is proved including the molecular parts of the entropy.

## Key claim

The paper derives balance equations for thermodynamic quantities from a nonlocal quantum kinetic equation, showing that the finite duration of binary collisions leads to molecular contributions to observables and currents; these correlated parts are given by the rate to form a molecule multiplied by its lifetime (the collision duration), and the two-particle form of the entropy is derived extending the Landau quasiparticle picture, with Boltzmann's H-theorem proved including these molecular parts.

## Method

The author starts from a nonlocal quantum kinetic equation (generalized Kadanoff-Baym formalism) that includes first-order gradient corrections expressed as space and time shifts derived from the scattering phase shift. The kinetic equation is multiplied by variables corresponding to particle number, momentum, energy, and entropy (specifically \(1, \vec{k}, \varepsilon_1, -k_{\mathrm B}\ln[f_1/(1-f_1)]\)) and integrated over momentum to obtain balance equations. The nonlocal collision integrals are symmetrized using transformations that interchange incoming/outgoing particles and collision partners, and the resulting molecular contributions are expressed in terms of the T-matrix and its phase shifts.

## Key numbers

* The scattering phase shift \(\phi\) defines the displacement vectors: \(\Delta_t = \partial\phi/\partial\omega\) (collision duration), \(\Delta_E = -\frac{1}{2}\partial\phi/\partial t\) (energy shift), and \(\vec{\Delta}_K = \frac{1}{2}\partial\phi/\partial\vec{r}\) (momentum shift).
* The energy-conserving delta function in the scattering integral includes a shift \(2\Delta_E\) relative to the sum of quasiparticle energies.
* The centered collision energy is defined as \(E = \frac{1}{2}(\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2+\varepsilon_3+\varepsilon_4)\) and \(\bar{E} = \frac{1}{2}(\varepsilon_1+\bar{\varepsilon}_2+\bar{\varepsilon}_3+\bar{\varepsilon}_4)\).
* The invariant combination \(\vec{\Delta}_{\mathrm{fl}} = \frac{1}{2}(\vec{\Delta}_3+\vec{\Delta}_4-\vec{\Delta}_2)\) is interpreted as the distance particles travel during the collision duration \(\Delta_t\).

## Context

This work extends the Landau quasiparticle picture by incorporating two-particle molecular contributions from nonlocal collisions, building on prior kinetic theories by Enskog (1921) for hard spheres, Waldmann (1957) and Snider (1960, 1964) for quantum gases, and Laloë, Mullin, Nacher, and Tastevin (1989-1991) for non-degenerate systems. The approach enables explicit expressions for correlated observables in terms of scattering phase shifts, providing a framework for implementing nonlocal corrections in simulations of nuclear reactions and dense Fermi systems, where earlier attempts using Enskog-type corrections (e.g., by Bonasera et al., 1994) proved inadequate because the dominant correction is collision duration rather than spatial nonlocality.
