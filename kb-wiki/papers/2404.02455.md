# An implementation of nuclear many-body wave functions by the superposition of localized Gaussians

> We introduce a new framework for the low-energy nuclear structure calculations, which describes the single-particle wave function as a superposition of localized Gaussians. It is a hybrid of the Hartree-Fock and antisymmetrized molecular dynamics models. In the numerical calculations of oxygen, calcium isotopes and 100Sn, the framework shows its potential by significantly improving upon AMD and yielding the results consistent with or even better than Hartree-Fock(-Bogoliubov) calculations based on harmonic oscillator expansions. In addition to the basic equations, general form of the matrix elements is also given.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a hybrid framework that describes single-particle wave functions as superpositions of localized Gaussians, combining features of antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) and Hartree-Fock theory. The authors claim the method significantly improves upon AMD for oxygen and calcium isotopes and yields results consistent with or better than Hartree-Fock(-Bogoliubov) calculations based on harmonic oscillator expansions, but they note that pairing correlations are not included, so the method underestimates binding energies for mid-shell nuclei compared to HFB.

## Method

The single-particle wave function is expressed as a superposition of localized Gaussian basis functions, each characterized by a complex three-dimensional vector representing its mean position and momentum. The energy variation yields two sets of equations: one determines the positions and momenta of the Gaussians (extending wave packet models like AMD), and the other determines the coefficients of the superposition (a generalized Hartree-Fock equation). The authors performed numerical calculations for oxygen isotopes (16-24O) and calcium isotopes (40-60Ca) using the Gogny D1S effective interaction, optimizing the variational parameters via steepest descent for the Gaussian parameters and conjugate gradient for the coefficients, without parity or angular momentum projection.

## Key numbers

* For 16O, the optimal Gaussian width parameter nu was 0.2000 fm^-2 with M=40 localized Gaussians, compared to nu=0.158 fm^-2 for AMD.
* For 24O, the present model required M=50 localized Gaussians with nu=0.1875 fm^-2, while AMD used nu=0.149 fm^-2.
* For 40Ca, the optimal nu was 0.1750 fm^-2 with M=60 Gaussians, versus nu=0.129 fm^-2 for AMD.
* For 60Ca, the present model required M=80 Gaussians with nu=0.1750 fm^-2, while AMD used nu=0.116 fm^-2.
* For 100Sn, the binding energy obtained was 828.7 MeV using M=100 Gaussians with nu=0.1625 fm^-2, which is 0.5 MeV deeper than the HFB result from Hilaire (2007).
* The particle-particle correlation energies from HFB for mid-shell oxygen and calcium isotopes were approximately 5 to 10 MeV, explaining the difference between the present model and HFB for those nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) approach of Kanada-Enyo (2003, 2012) and Kimura (2016) by allowing superposition of multiple localized Gaussians per single-particle state, overcoming AMD's limitation of using a single Gaussian per nucleon. It is similar in spirit to fermionic molecular dynamics (FMD) of Feldmeier (1990, 1995) and AMD-HF of Dote (1997), but uses the same set of Gaussians for both protons and neutrons for greater efficiency. The method enables calculations for heavier nuclei like 100Sn that were previously inaccessible to ordinary AMD, and it provides a framework for future extensions including pairing correlations, angular momentum projection, and time-dependent calculations for nuclear reactions.
