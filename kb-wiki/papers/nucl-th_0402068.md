# Ambiguities in statistical calculations of nuclear fragmentation

> The concept of freeze out volume used in many statistical approaches for disassembly of hot nuclei leads to ambiguities. The fragmentation pattern and the momentum distribution (temperature) of the emanated fragments are determined by the phase space at the freeze-out volume where the interaction among the fragments is supposedly frozen out. However, to get coherence with the experimental momentum distribution of the charged particles, one introduces Coulomb acceleration beyond this freeze-out. To be consistent, we investigate the effect of the attractive nuclear force beyond this volume and find that the possible recombination of the fragments alters the physical observables significantly casting doubt on the consistency of the statistical model.

# Ambiguities in statistical calculations of nuclear fragmentation

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard statistical model assumption of a "freeze-out volume" where all interactions among fragments cease is inconsistent, because the attractive nuclear force beyond that volume can cause fragment recombination that significantly alters charge yields and extracted temperatures, even at freeze-out volumes as large as 16 times the normal nuclear volume.

## Method

The authors extend the grand-canonical multifragmentation model by adding a dynamical step after freeze-out: fragments are placed non-overlapping within the freeze-out volume, given Maxwell-Boltzmann velocities at the microcanonical temperature, and then evolved under both Coulomb and nuclear forces. Two fragments that touch are assumed to coalesce; if the resulting excited fragment has excitation energy above 8 MeV, it undergoes binary decay via the transition state model of Swiatecki. Calculations are performed for the system $^{197}$Au at excitation energies $E^*/A = 3$, 4, and 5 MeV and freeze-out volumes from $6V_0$ to $16V_0$, with $10^5$ microcanonical events per condition.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out volumes considered: $3V_0$ to $8V_0$ (typical range in prior statistical models), with explicit calculations at $6V_0$ and $16V_0$ ($V_0$ = normal nuclear volume of the fragmenting system).
- At $E^*/A = 4$ MeV and $V_f = 6V_0$, recombination enhances yields of relatively heavy fragments significantly; at $V_f = 16V_0$, the effect persists for fragments with $Z > 10$.
- The average number of fragment pairs within nuclear force range (taken as 1.4 fm) remains significant even at $V_f = 16V_0$.
- Isotopic double-ratio temperatures extracted after recombination are reduced dramatically, to approximately 4 MeV, and are not sensitive to excitation energy in the range 3–6 MeV per nucleon.
- Without recombination, extracted temperatures are consistent with the Fermi gas model; with recombination, they show an anomalous fall at $E^*/A = 4$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the preliminary study of Pal et al. (2004) on recombination effects in multifragmentation, which was restricted to a single freeze-out volume and excitation energy. It directly challenges the consistency of the freeze-out concept used in statistical models such as the grand canonical model of Koonin (1980), the canonical model of Bonasera (1994), and the microcanonical model of Gross (1990). The results cast doubt on temperature extractions from isotopic double-ratio thermometers (as used by Albergo et al., 1985) and on inferences about the liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclei. The authors propose that nonequilibrium dynamical features, rather than statistical freeze-out, should be incorporated from the outset.
