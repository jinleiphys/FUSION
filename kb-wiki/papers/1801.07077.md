# Neutrino-nucleon scattering in the neutrino-sphere

> We calculate the differential scattering rate for thermal neutrinos in a hot and dilute gas of interacting neutrons using linear response theory. The dynamical structure factors for density and spin fluctuations of the strongly interacting neutron matter, expected in the neutrino decoupling regions of supernovae and neutron star mergers, are calculated in the virial expansion for the first time. Correlations due to nucleon-nucleon interactions are taken into account using a pseudo-potential that reproduces measured nucleon-nucleon phase shifts, and we find that attractive s-wave interactions enhance the density response and suppress the spin response of neutron matter. The net effect of neutron correlations is to strongly suppress backscattering. Moreover, we find nearly exact scaling laws for the response functions, valid for the range $T = 5 - 10$ MeV and q < 30 MeV, allowing us to obtain analytic results for the dynamic structure factors at second-order in the fugacity of the neutron gas. We find that the modification of scattering rates depends on the energy and momentum exchanged, implying that dynamical structure factors are essential to describe neutrino decoupling in supernovae and neutron star mergers.

## Key claim

The authors calculate neutrino-nucleon scattering rates in hot dilute neutron matter using linear response theory and a virial expansion, finding that attractive s-wave interactions enhance the density response and suppress the spin response, with the net effect of strongly suppressing backscattering; however, the results are limited to the regime where the fugacity \(z \lesssim 0.47\) and the temperature range \(T = 5-10\) MeV with momentum transfer \(q < 30\) MeV, and the method relies on a pseudo-potential approximation that may not capture exact non-static properties.

## Method

The dynamical structure factors for density and spin fluctuations of neutron matter were computed using linear response theory within a virial expansion up to second order in the fugacity \(z = e^{\mu/T}\). Neutron-neutron interactions were modeled using a pseudo-potential that reproduces measured nucleon-nucleon phase shifts in the \(^1S_0\) channel, with contributions from one-loop and two-loop diagrams (self-energy and vertex corrections) evaluated in the Matsubara formalism and related to structure factors via the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. The calculation neglected higher partial waves (\(L \geq 1\)), proton contributions, charged weak currents, and multi-particle excitations, and was validated against thermodynamic and f-sum rules.

## Key numbers

- The virial expansion is valid for fugacity \(z \lesssim 0.47\), corresponding to densities \(\rho \lesssim (T/5\ \text{MeV})^{3/2} \times 10^{12}\ \text{g/cm}^3\).
- The temperature range studied is \(T = 5-10\) MeV, with momentum transfer \(q < 30\) MeV.
- The second virial coefficient for neutrons in the \(^1S_0\) channel is \(b_2 = 0.305\) in the temperature range \(T = 5-10\) MeV.
- The third virial coefficient for the neutron gas is estimated as \(b_3 = -0.291(1)\) from the BEC-BCS crossover region.
- The free second virial coefficient for fermions is \(b_{2,\text{free}} = -2^{-5/2}\).
- The neutron density at lowest order in fugacity is \(n \simeq 2 (MT/2\pi)^{3/2} z\), with the condition \(z \lesssim 1/10\) implying \(n \lesssim 0.0005\ (T/5\ \text{MeV})^{3/2}\ \text{fm}^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends the virial expansion approach previously used to calculate the equation of state of dilute neutron matter (Horowitz 2005, Horowitz 2006) to the dynamical structure factors needed for neutrino scattering, going beyond static approximations that neglect energy and momentum transfer dependence. The results enable more accurate modeling of neutrino decoupling in supernovae and neutron star mergers, where previous calculations often relied on the elastic approximation or replaced structure factors by their long-wavelength limits, and the authors note that future work should include higher partial waves, proton contributions, and charged weak currents.
