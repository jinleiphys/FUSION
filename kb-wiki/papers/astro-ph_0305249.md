# Strange Quark Matter in Neutron Stars? - New Results from Chandra and XMM

> It has been predicted that quark and hybrid stars, containing strange quark matter in their core, have a significantly smaller radius than ordinary neutron stars. Preliminary X-ray observations of isolated neutron stars indicated a surprisingly small radius consistent with quark matter in the interior of the star. However, a new analysis of the data led to a radius corresponding to an ordinary neutron star. In the present talk we will discuss theoretical calculations of the mass-radius relation for quark and hybrid stars, taking into account medium effects in quark matter, and report on recent X-ray observations by Chandra and XMM and their latest interpretation.

# Strange Quark Matter in Neutron Stars? - New Results from Chandra and XMM

## Key claim

The paper argues that recent Chandra and XMM observations of the isolated neutron star RXJ1856, combined with optical data, do not require the star to contain strange quark matter, contrary to earlier preliminary claims. The observed data can be explained by conventional neutron star models if one accounts for a two-temperature surface, a strong magnetic field causing spectral line smearing, or a grey-body X-ray emission from a condensed matter surface.

## Method

The authors computed mass-radius relations for strange quark stars and hybrid stars using the Effective Mass Bag Model, which incorporates medium effects via effective quark masses from the Hard-Dense-Loop limit of perturbative QCD, with the strong coupling constant g treated as a free parameter between 0 and 4 and bag constants between B^{1/4}=165 and 200 MeV. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation numerically using these equations of state combined with four different relativistic mean-field hadronic models including hyperons. Separately, they analyzed Chandra and XMM X-ray spectra of RXJ1856 together with Hubble optical data, fitting black-body, two-temperature, and grey-body models to the combined flux measurements.

## Key numbers

- For a 1.4 solar mass star, pure hadronic stars have radii larger than 13 km, while hybrid stars with a quark matter core have radii between 9 and 11 km
- Strange quark stars are located at radii less than 9 km in the Effective Mass Bag Model
- The X-ray spectrum of RXJ1856 gives a temperature of kT_infinity^X = 63 ± 0.5 eV, yielding an observed radius of R_infinity^X = 4.3 ± 0.2 km (d/120 pc) under a pure black-body assumption
- The distance to RXJ1856 from optical parallax measurements is d = 117 ± 12 pc
- The optical flux exceeds the extrapolated X-ray black-body flux by a factor of about 7
- In the two-temperature model, the soft component gives T_infinity^opt less than 33.6 eV and R_infinity^opt greater than 16.3 km (d/120 pc)
- In the grey-body model with a condensed surface, the best fit gives surface temperature kT_infinity = 54 eV and observed radius R_infinity ≥ 13.3 km, corresponding to an actual radius R greater than 10.3 km for a 1.4 solar mass star
- The electron plasma frequency in a condensed matter surface is estimated as hbar omega_pl ≈ 0.66 B_12^(3/5) keV, where B = B_12 × 10^12 G
- A hydrogen condensate surface would require a magnetic field greater than 5 × 10^13 G
- XMM found an upper limit of 1.5% for periodic variations in the X-ray data of RXJ1856

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical predictions that quark and hybrid stars have significantly smaller radii than ordinary neutron stars, as proposed in the MIT bag model by various authors (e.g., the original strange quark matter stability hypothesis from 1984). The paper directly addresses the preliminary claim from earlier Chandra data analysis that RXJ1856 had a radius less than 6 km, which suggested it was a strange quark star. The new analysis shows that combining X-ray data from Chandra and XMM with optical data from the Hubble Space Telescope yields radii consistent with conventional neutron stars, either through a two-temperature model, a strong magnetic field causing line smearing (as also discussed for the isolated neutron stars RXJ1308 and RXJ0720), or a grey-body spectrum from a condensed iron or hydrogen surface. The theoretical uncertainty in mass-radius relations from different quark matter models, including the NJL model and perturbative corrections to the bag model, is noted as too large to distinguish quark from hadronic matter unless the radius is clearly below 8 km.
