# Electroproduction of $Λ(1520)$ off the nucleon target with the nucleon resonances

> We investigate the unpolarized electroproduction of $Λ(1520)\equivΛ^*$ off the nucleon target, using the effective Lagrangian method at the tree-level Born approximation with the nucleon-resonance contributions from $S_{11}(2090)$, $D_{13}(2080)$, and $D_{15}(2200)$. First, we compute the various physical quantities for the proton target case, such as the total and differential cross sections, $t$-momentum transfer distribution, and $K^-$ decay-angle ($ϕ$) distribution. It turns out that $D_{13}$ plays an important role to reproduce the electroproduction data properly. The numerical results for the $ϕ$ distribution shows obvious different structures from that for the photoproduction, due to the enhancement of the kaon exchange by the longitudinal polarization of the virtual photon as expected. Numerically, we observe that the kaon-exchange contribution in the $t$ channel becomes about a half of that from the contact-term one that dominates the photoproduction of $Λ^*$. We also provide theoretical estimations for the $Λ^*$ electroproduction off the neutron target, showing that its production rate is saturated almost by the resonance contributions. Finally, the contact-term dominance, which is the key ingredient for the $Λ^*$ electromagnetic productions, is briefly discussed.

# Electroproduction of Λ(1520) off the nucleon target with the nucleon resonances

## Key claim

The contact-term dominance, previously established for Λ(1520) photoproduction, remains a key feature in electroproduction, but the longitudinal polarization of the virtual photon enhances the kaon-exchange contribution in the t-channel to about half the strength of the contact-term contribution, producing a qualitatively different decay-angle distribution compared to photoproduction. The D13(2080) resonance plays a crucial role in reproducing the electroproduction data, particularly in the forward-scattering region at W ≲ 2.4 GeV.

## Method

The authors employed an effective Lagrangian approach at the tree-level Born approximation using the Rarita-Schwinger vector-spinor formalism for the spin-3/2 Λ(1520) field, incorporating nucleon-resonance contributions from S11(2090), D13(2080), and D15(2200) in the s-channel. Phenomenological form factors with a gauge-conserving term were used, with dipole and monopole electromagnetic form factors for the vertices to account for Q² dependence, and all calculations were performed in the K-Λ* center-of-mass frame with transverse polarization parameter ε = 0.5.

## Key numbers

- The kaon-exchange contribution in the t-channel becomes about a half of that from the contact-term contribution that dominates photoproduction of Λ(1520)
- The coupling constant g_{KNΛ*} ≈ 11, computed from Γ_{Λ*} ≈ 15.6 MeV and Γ_{Λ*→K̅N}/Γ_{Λ*} ≈ 0.45
- The strong coupling constants for resonances: |g₁₁| = 1.53, |g₁₃| = 1.25, |g₁₅| = 0.40
- The helicity amplitude coupling strengths for proton resonances: h₁₁⁽ᵖ⁾ = -0.055, h₁₃⁽ᵖ⁾ = +0.608, h₂₃⁽ᵖ⁾ = -0.620, h₁₅⁽ᵖ⁾ = +0.123, h₂₅⁽ᵖ⁾ = +0.011
- The helicity amplitude coupling strengths for neutron resonances: h₁₁⁽ⁿ⁾ = +0.018, h₁₃⁽ⁿ⁾ = -0.770, h₂₃⁽ⁿ⁾ = +0.531, h₁₅⁽ⁿ⁾ = -0.842, h₂₅⁽ⁿ⁾ = -0.872
- The kinematic region studied: Q² = (0.9–2.4) GeV² and W = (1.95–2.65) GeV
- The γK*±K∓ coupling: g_{γK*±K∓} = 0.254/GeV; for γK*0K0: g_{γK*0K0} = 0.358/GeV
- The K*NΛ* coupling: |g_{K*NΛ*}| ≈ 1.5 from coupled chiral unitary approach

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies on Λ(1520) photoproduction (Nam 2005, Nam 2009, Nam 2010) to the electroproduction case, motivated by the CLAS experiment (Barrow 2001) which showed a K⁻ decay-angle distribution ∝ (1/3 + cos²φ) in the Gottfried-Jackson frame, contrasting with the photoproduction distribution ∝ sin²φ observed by LAMP2 (Barber 1980) and LEPS (Muramatsu 2009). The resonance contributions from D13(2080) were previously suggested for photoproduction by Xie (2010) and He (2012). The results enable future experimental studies of Λ(1520) electroproduction off neutron targets, where the production rate is saturated almost entirely by resonance contributions due to the absence of the contact term, making neutron targets ideal for isolating nucleon-resonance effects.
