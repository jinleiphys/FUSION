# Trends in Nuclear Astrophysics

> Nuclear Astrophysics is a vibrant field at the intersection of nuclear physics and astrophysics that encompasses research in nuclear physics, astrophysics, astronomy, and computational science. This paper is not a review. It is intended to provide an incomplete personal perspective on current trends in nuclear astrophysics and the specific role of nuclear physics in this field.

## Key claim

This paper provides an incomplete personal perspective on current trends in nuclear astrophysics, emphasizing that nuclear physics enables progress through three broad themes: validation of stellar models, constraining stellar properties and system parameters, and predicting nucleosynthesis contributions; the author explicitly states the paper is not a review.

## Method

The author surveys major open questions in nuclear astrophysics including the primordial lithium problem, solar metallicity discrepancies, the unknown site of the r-process, and the mechanisms of stellar explosions. The paper then examines how nuclear physics data from experiments and theory enable model validation across multiple nucleosynthesis sites (s-process, r-process, i-process, p-process, and novae), using comparisons with observed abundance patterns, neutrino signals, and meteoritic data.

## Key numbers

- Solar metallicity revised from the canonical 2% down to 1.4% (Asplund 2009)
- 5 isotopes produced in the Big Bang, with the predicted 7Li production discrepancy remaining unresolved
- 35 stable isotopes are produced by the p-process, typically with factors of 10-100 lower abundance than more neutron rich isotopes of the same element
- Most p-process models reproduce most p-process abundances within typically a factor of 3
- Current neutrino detectors would detect 1000s of neutrinos from a Galactic supernova event

## Context

This paper extends the long history of nuclear astrophysics from Eddington (1920), von Weizsäcker (1938), Bethe and Critchfield (1938), B2FH (1956), and Cameron (1957) to current frontiers. It builds on a white paper developed by the US nuclear astrophysics community (WhitePaper 2016) and discusses how new multi-messenger observations (including the first direct detection of gravitational waves with LIGO in 2016), multi-facility nuclear experiments, and advanced 3D computer models create opportunities to address long-standing questions. The work enables future discrimination between proposed r-process sites such as neutron star mergers or rapidly rotating magnetic supernovae, and validation of stellar models through comparison with observables.
