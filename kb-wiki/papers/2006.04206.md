# Far From Equilibrium Hydrodynamics and the Beam Energy Scan

> The existence of hydrodynamic attractors in rapidly expanding relativistic systems has shed light on the success of relativistic hydrodynamics in describing heavy-ion collisions at zero chemical potential. As the search for the QCD critical point continues, it is important to investigate how out of equilibrium effects influence the trajectories on the QCD phase diagram. In this proceedings, we study a Bjorken expanding hydrodynamic system based on DMNR equations of motion with initial out of equilibrium effects and finite chemical potential in a system with a critical point. We find that the initial conditions are not unique for a specific freeze-out point, but rather the system can evolve to the same final state freeze-out point with a wide range of initial baryon chemical potential, $μ_B$. For the same initial energy density and baryon density, depending on how far out of equilibrium the system begins, the initial $μ_B$ can vary by $Δμ_B\sim 350$ MeV. Our results indicate that knowledge of the out-of-equilibrium effects in the initial state provide vital information that influences the search for the QCD critical point.

## Key claim
For a Bjorken-expanding system with finite baryon chemical potential and a critical point, the initial conditions are not uniquely determined by a given freeze-out point; a wide range of initial baryon chemical potential (varying by up to Δμ_B ∼ 350 MeV) can lead to the same final freeze-out state, depending on how far out of equilibrium the system begins.

## Method
The authors solve the DNMR (Denicol–Niemi–Molnár–Rischke) relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations in a (0+1)-dimensional Bjorken expanding geometry, including both shear stress π^{η}_{η} and bulk pressure Π initialized out of equilibrium. They use a non-conformal equation of state with a parametrized critical point placed at (T, μ_B) = (143, 350) MeV, temperature-dependent shear viscosity (η T/w) matched to a hadron resonance gas and QCD parameterization, and a bulk viscosity (ζ T/w) proportional to (1/3 − c_s^2). Initial values of the dissipative stresses are varied between χ_0 = π^{η}_{η}/(ε+p) = ±0.5 and Ω_0 = Π/(ε+p) = ±0.5, and the initial baryon density is adjusted so that all trajectories pass through a common freeze-out region.

## Key numbers
- Initial μ_B can vary by Δμ_B ∼ 350 MeV for the same initial energy density and baryon density, depending on the initial out-of-equilibrium shear and bulk stresses.
- Initial energy density for trajectories away from the critical point is 7.0 GeV/fm³; for trajectories passing through the critical region it is 1.5 GeV/fm³.
- The critical point is placed at (T, μ_B) = (143, 350) MeV, with Ising parameters α₁ = 3, α₂ = 93, ω = 1, ρ = 2.
- At μ_B = 0, the minimum of η T/w is 0.08, occurring at T = 196 MeV.
- The bulk viscosity parameterization yields a maximum ζ T/w ∼ 0.3.
- The equation of state is considered reliable up to μ_B ∼ 400 MeV, extended to μ_B ∼ 600 MeV in the high-temperature region.

## Context
This work extends studies of hydrodynamic attractors (Heller 2015, Romatschke 2017, Strickland 2018) from zero chemical potential to finite baryon chemical potential, and is the first to study out-of-equilibrium effects on trajectories through the QCD phase diagram in the context of the Beam Energy Scan. It builds on the equation of state with a critical point from Parotto (2018) and on DNMR equations of motion from Denicol (2012). The results imply that connecting initial state to final state observables at BES energies requires knowledge of the full initial T^{μν} (including shear and bulk stresses), not just energy density and baryon density, and motivate future work with event-by-event fluctuations and diffusive dynamics (Martinez 2019).
