# Antimatter Regions in the Early Universe and Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

> We have studied big bang nucleosynthesis in the presence of regions of antimatter. Depending on the distance scale of the antimatter region, and thus the epoch of their annihilation, the amount of antimatter in the early universe is constrained by the observed abundances. Small regions, which annihilate after weak freezeout but before nucleosynthesis, lead to a reduction in the 4He yield, because of neutron annihilation. Large regions, which annihilate after nucleosynthesis, lead to an increased 3He yield. Deuterium production is also affected but not as much. The three most important production mechanisms of 3He are 1) photodisintegration of 4He by the annihilation radiation, 2) pbar-4He annihilation, and 3) nbar-4He annihilation by "secondary" antineutrons produced in anti-4He annihilation. Although pbar-4He annihilation produces more 3He than the secondary nbar-4He annihilation, the products of the latter survive later annihilation much better, since they are distributed further away from the annihilation zone.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the presence of antimatter regions in the early universe can significantly alter light element abundances from Big Bang Nucleosynthesis, with the specific effect depending on the size of the antimatter region and thus the epoch of its annihilation; small regions (annihilating before nucleosynthesis) reduce helium-4 yield via neutron annihilation, while larger regions (annihilating after nucleosynthesis) increase helium-3 yield, and the authors find a larger helium-3 yield than the qualitatively similar prior work by Rehm and Jedamzik.

## Method

The authors performed detailed inhomogeneous nucleosynthesis calculations using a spherically symmetric geometry with a spherical antimatter region surrounded by a thick matter shell, assuming equal initial baryon and antibaryon densities corresponding to an average baryon-to-photon ratio eta_10 = 6. They modeled diffusion, annihilation, and nucleosynthesis simultaneously, including antinucleosynthesis up to mass number 4, photodisintegration of light nuclei (D, H-3, He-3, He-4), and a detailed treatment of the electromagnetic cascade spectrum from annihilation radiation, particularly at lower temperatures where the standard cascade approximation breaks down.

## Key numbers

* For antimatter regions with radius r_A ~ 1 cm at T = 100 GeV (corresponding to 2 km at T = 1 MeV or 3x10^6 m at T = 1 keV), the antimatter-matter ratio is constrained to R < 10^-2 based on helium-4 underproduction.
* The comoving distance scale conversion: 1 meter at T = 1 keV corresponds to 4.24x10^6 m or 1.37x10^-10 pc today; distances given by Rehm and Jedamzik at T = 100 GeV are larger by a factor of 3.0x10^8 when converted to the authors' convention.
* The average yields of low-energy antiproton-He-4 annihilation are 0.210 +/- 0.009 He-3, 0.437 +/- 0.032 H-3, and 0.07-0.19 D per annihilation event.
* The mean free path for a 70 MeV neutron scattering on protons is approximately 4.7x10^9 m (T/keV)^(-2) for eta = 6x10^-10; at T < 0.36 keV the mean free time exceeds the neutron lifetime.
* Photodisintegration of He-4 begins at T = 0.6 keV when E_max exceeds the He-4 binding energy; photodisintegration of D begins at T = 5.3 keV, He-3 at T = 2.2 keV, H-3 at T = 1.9 keV, and Li-7 at T = 4.7 keV.
* The AMS precursor flight observed 2.86x10^6 helium nuclei but no antihelium, giving an upper limit antihelium/helium flux ratio < 1.1x10^-6 in cosmic rays.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of antimatter regions in the early universe by Steigman and by Rehm and Jedamzik (1998), who considered only small regions annihilating before nucleosynthesis at T > 80 keV; the present authors include larger regions that annihilate during or after nucleosynthesis. The results are in qualitative agreement with Rehm and Jedamzik (1998) for small scales but yield a larger He-3 production for larger scales, and the authors note that their first results reported in Kurki-Suonio and Sihvola (2000) omitted antinucleosynthesis, photodisintegration of isotopes other than He-4, and the dependence of the cascade spectrum on the initial photon spectrum. The work enables tighter constraints on inhomogeneous baryogenesis scenarios, particularly those connected to inflation where the distance scale of matter-antimatter regions is not a priori constrained.
