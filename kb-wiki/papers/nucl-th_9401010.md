# Missing and Quenched Gamow Teller Strength

> Gamow-Teller strength functions in full $(pf)^{8}$ spaces are calculated with sufficient accuracy to ensure that all the states in the resonance region have been populated. Many of the resulting peaks are weak enough to become unobservable. The quenching factor necessary to bring into agreement the low lying observed states with shell model predictions is shown to be due to nuclear correlations. To within experimental uncertainties it is the same that is found in one particle transfer and (e,e') reactions. Perfect consistency between the observed $^{48}Ca(p,n)^{48}Sc$ peaks and the calculation is achieved by assuming an observation threshold of 0.75\% of the total strength, a value that seems typical in several experiments

## Key claim
The quenching factor of approximately 0.6 for Gamow-Teller strength is entirely due to nuclear correlations, and the missing strength arises because many weak peaks fall below an observation threshold of 0.75% of the total strength, rather than from non-nucleonic degrees of freedom.

## Method
The authors performed full (pf)⁸ shell-model calculations for ⁴⁸Ca(p,n)⁴⁸Sc using the KB3 interaction, obtaining exact eigenstates of the target and using them as pivots in a Lanczos tridiagonal construction to generate strength functions. For the β decay of ⁴⁸Mn to ⁴⁸Cr, they used a restricted configuration space with t ≤ 3 and performed up to 300 Lanczos iterations to ensure convergence of states below 10.5 MeV. They then applied a detection threshold of 0.75% of the total S₋ strength to compare with experimental data.

## Key numbers
- The observation threshold of 0.75% of the total S₋ strength achieves perfect consistency between calculated and observed ⁴⁸Ca(p,n)⁴⁸Sc peaks.
- In the ⁴⁸Ca(p,n)⁴⁸Sc data, the strong isolated peak at 2.52 MeV collects a strength of 6.8 against 38.7 for all states in the interval 4.5-14.5 MeV, giving a ratio of 5.7; the calculation with the 0.75% threshold reproduces this ratio.
- For the β decay of ⁴⁸Mn to ⁴⁸Cr, 81.5% of the strength is distributed among peaks whose share is less than 1% of the total.
- The factor 0.7 for the model-space sum rule (S₋ − S₊)ₘ comes from (d,p) data of Vold et al. and is consistent with occupancies near the Fermi level from (e,e') scattering.
- In the ⁴⁸Ca(p,n)⁴⁸Sc experiment, approximately 25% of the model strength goes unobserved.

## Context
This work extends the understanding of Gamow-Teller quenching and missing strength beyond the standard factor of 0.6 reduction, which had been inferred from beta decay measurements (Brown and Wildenthal; Caurier et al.) and the Ikeda sum rule (Gaarde et al.). The authors provide a clean separation of model-space and correlation contributions to the sum rule, relating the quenching factor to occupancies at the Fermi level from transfer reactions (Vold et al.) and electron scattering (Cavedon et al.; Pandharipande et al.; Benhar et al.). The results enable future experiments such as ⁴⁸Ca(n,p)⁴⁸K to confirm the predicted 50% loss factor, and they caution that the observed fraction of strength will vary with nuclear level density.
