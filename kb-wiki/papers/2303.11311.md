# On excess entropy and latent heat in crystallizing white dwarfs

> Based on the linear mixing approach, we calculate the latent heat for crystallizing fully-ionized $^{12}$C/$^{16}$O and $^{16}$O/$^{20}$Ne mixtures in white dwarf (WD) cores for two different parametrizations of the corrections to the linear-mixing energies and with account of ion quantum effects. We report noticeable composition-dependent deviations of the excess entropy in both directions from the standard value of 0.77 per ion. Within the same framework, we evaluate the excess entropy and released or absorbed heat accompanying the exsolution process in solidified WD layers. The inclusion of this effect is shown to be important for reliable interpretation of WD cooling data. We also analyze the latent heat of crystallizing eutectic $^{12}$C/$^{22}$Ne mixture, where we find a qualitative dependence of both the phase diagram and the latent heat behaviour on ion quantum effects. This may be important for the model with $^{22}$Ne distillation in cooling C/O/$^{22}$Ne WD proposed as a solution for the ultramassive WD multi-Gyr cooling anomaly. Astrophysical implications of our findings for crystallizing WD are discussed.

## Key claim

The latent heat and excess entropy in crystallizing white dwarf mixtures deviate noticeably from the standard one-component-plasma value of 0.77 per ion, with the direction and magnitude depending on mixture composition, energy parametrization, and ion quantum effects; these deviations can either delay or accelerate white dwarf cooling depending on the specific composition and process.

## Method

The author uses the linear mixing formalism to compute phase diagrams and excess entropies for fully-ionized binary ionic mixtures (12C/16O, 16O/20Ne, and 12C/22Ne) in white dwarf cores, applying two different parametrizations of corrections to the linear-mixing energies (from Ogata et al. 1993 and DeWitt et al. 2003) and including ion quantum effects at densities up to 10^11 g cm^-3. The excess entropy at crystallization is obtained by taking the difference between liquid and solid entropies at their phase boundaries, while the excess entropy of exsolution is computed from the entropy difference between parent and newly nucleated solid solutions within the miscibility gap.

## Key numbers

- Standard excess entropy per ion for pure one-component plasma is 0.77 per ion
- For classic 12C/16O mixture with Ogata et al. (1993) fit: minimum excess entropy Δs^cryst ≈ 0.65 at x_O^liq ≈ 0.36 and melting temperature T_m ≈ 0.99 T_1m^cl; maximum Δs^cryst ≈ 0.91 at x_O^liq ≈ 0.78 and T_m ≈ 1.37 T_1m^cl
- For classic 12C/16O mixture with DeWitt et al. (2003) fit: minimum Δs^cryst ≈ 0.59 at x_O^liq ≈ 0.41 and T_m ≈ 0.86 T_1m^cl; maximum Δs^cryst ≈ 0.98 at x_O^liq ≈ 0.77 and T_m ≈ 1.29 T_1m^cl
- For 12C/22Ne eutectic mixture at classic density with Ogata et al. (1993) fit: excess entropy discontinuity at eutectic point from ≈ 0.45 to ≈ 0.83; maxima reach 1.07–1.09 at x_Ne^liq ≈ 0.62–0.65; additional strong maximum of ≈ 0.93 at low x_Ne^liq ≈ 0.13
- For pure quantum carbon, excess entropy reduced to ≈ 0.67
- Exsolution onset occurs at internal temperature ≈ 3.18 times lower than crystallization onset temperature for the example with x_O^liq = 0.7
- Ratio of exsolution to crystallization cooling age effects: τ^exsol/τ^cryst ∼ -0.05, -0.1, and -0.2 for 0.6, 0.8, and 1 M_sun white dwarfs respectively (for x_O^liq = 0.7 case); or ∼ 0.2, 0.5, and 0.8 for the same masses (for x_O^liq = 0.37 case)

## Context

This work extends the linear mixing formalism from Paper I (Baiko 2022) to calculate calorific effects in crystallizing white dwarfs, directly addressing the standard assumption from Salaris et al. (2000) that latent heat equals 0.77 T per ion. The results are relevant to the ultramassive white dwarf cooling anomaly discovered by Cheng et al. (2019) and the 22Ne distillation model proposed by Blouin et al. (2021) as a potential solution. The findings enable more accurate white dwarf cooling models by providing composition-dependent latent heat values and demonstrating that exsolution processes in solidified layers produce non-negligible thermal effects (estimated at 50–800 Myr depending on stellar mass and composition), which must be included for reliable interpretation of Gaia white dwarf cooling data.
