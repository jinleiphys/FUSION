# Analysis of the image of pion-emitting sources in source center of mass frame

> In this paper, we try a method to extract the image of pion-emitting source function in the center-of-mass frame of source (CMFS). We choose the identical pion pairs according to the difference of their energy and use these pion pairs to build the correlation function. The purpose is to reduce the effect of $\triangle E \triangle t$, thus the corresponding imaging result can tend to the real source function. We examine the effect of this method by comparing its results with real source functions extracted from models directly.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that by selecting identical pion pairs with a small energy difference (ΔE < 10 MeV) to build the correlation function, the imaging technique can reconstruct the source function in the center-of-mass frame of the source (CMFS) that closely approximates the real source function, despite the non-zero ΔEΔt term that normally distorts the image.

## Method

The authors apply the one-dimensional imaging technique (Brown and Danielewicz, 1997) to extract the source function S(r) from two-pion correlation functions, but perform the analysis in the CMFS rather than the usual center-of-mass frame of the pion pair (CMFP). To reduce the distortion caused by the ΔEΔt term in the CMFS, they impose a cut on the energy difference of the pion pairs (ΔE < 10 MeV) and use only those pairs to construct the correlation function. They test the method using a homogeneous spherical model source and the AMPT model for Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV.

## Key numbers

- The homogeneous spherical model source has radius r = 10 fm with Boltzmann momentum distribution at T_f = 158 MeV and emission time standard deviation τ = 10 fm/c.
- The energy cut ΔE < 10 MeV is found to be small enough that the reconstructed source in the CMFS agrees well with the true model source.
- For the AMPT model test, 200 events are used for b = 0 fm, 500 events for b = 5 fm, and 10000 events for b = 10 fm.
- The model source is computed using pion pairs with relative momentum less than 60 MeV/c, following the approach of Brown and Danielewicz (2005) and Panitkin (1999).

## Context

This work extends the imaging technique introduced by Brown and Danielewicz (1997, 1998, 2001) for extracting source functions from HBT correlation functions. While standard imaging is performed in the CMFP where the source function is distorted by Lorentz transforms and depends on the momentum distribution, the authors show that imaging in the CMFS yields a source function that directly reflects the physical source scale and is largely independent of the momentum spectrum. The method enables future experimental analysis of source geometry in the CMFS using Coulomb-corrected correlation data, though it remains a one-dimensional approach that ignores pion interactions.
