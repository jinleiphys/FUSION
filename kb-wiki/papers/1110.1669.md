# Oscillations in the decay law: A possible quantum mechanical explanation of the anomaly in the experiment at the GSI facility

> We study the deviations from the usual exponential decay law for quantum mechanical systems. We show that simple and physically motivated deviations from the Breit-Wigner energy distribution of the unstable state are sufficient to generate peculiar deviations from the exponential decay law. Denoting with $p(t)$ the survival probability, its derivative $h(t)$ shows typically an oscillating behavior on top of the usual exponential function. We argue that this can be a viable explanation of the observed experimental results at GSI Darmstadt, where the function $h(t)$ has been experimentally measured for electron capture decays of Hydrogen-like ions. Moreover, if our interpretation is correct, we predict that by measuring $h(t)$ at times close to the initial one, the number of decays per second rapidly drops to zero.

## Key claim

Simple, physically motivated deviations from the Breit-Wigner energy distribution of an unstable state, specifically, introducing energy cutoffs that suppress the distribution far from the peak, are sufficient to generate oscillations superimposed on the exponential decay law, providing a possible quantum mechanical explanation for the GSI anomaly without invoking neutrino oscillations or other exotic physics.

## Method

The authors model the energy-dependent decay width \(\Gamma(y)\) of an unstable state using a step-function cutoff: \(\Gamma(y) = \Gamma_0 \theta(y + \Lambda_1) \theta(\Lambda_2 - y)\), where \(\Lambda_1\) and \(\Lambda_2\) are positive numbers larger than the natural width \(\Gamma_0\). They compute the survival probability \(p(t)\) and its derivative \(h(t) = -dp(t)/dt\) (the decay probability density) via Fourier transform of the modified mass distribution, and compare the resulting oscillations to the experimental fit function \(dN_{dec}/dt \propto e^{-\lambda t}(1 + a \cos(\omega t + \phi))\) used in the GSI experiment.

## Key numbers

* For the ion \(^{142}\)Pm, the total decay rate from the GSI experiment is \(\Gamma_0 = 0.0224\) s\(^{-1}\) (approximately \(10^{-17}\) eV).
* With a symmetric cutoff \(\Lambda = 32\Gamma_0\), the model qualitatively reproduces the oscillation frequency observed in the GSI data for \(^{142}\)Pm.
* The modulation frequencies for \(^{140}\)Pr and \(^{142}\)Pm are \(0.890\) s\(^{-1}\) and \(0.885\) s\(^{-1}\) respectively.
* The cooling times for decay products are \(900\) ms for \(^{140}\)Pr and \(1400\) ms for \(^{142}\)Pm.
* For the \(\beta^+\) decay channel of \(^{142}\)Pm, the estimated cutoff is \(\Lambda_{\beta^+} \sim 10^{-7}\) eV, corresponding to roughly \(10^9 \Gamma_0\).

## Context

This work extends the known result that the exponential decay law is only an approximation in quantum mechanics, with deviations occurring at short and long times (Fonda et al. 1978, Peres 1980, Nicolaides and Beck 1996). The authors specifically address the GSI anomaly observed by Litvinov et al. (2008), where oscillations were measured in the electron-capture decays of Hydrogen-like ions \(^{140}\)Pr and \(^{142}\)Pm. The proposed mechanism contrasts with earlier explanations involving neutrino oscillations (Cohen et al. 2008, Gal 2009, Merle 2010) and quantum beats (Wu et al. 2010, Giunti 2010), which the authors deem unsatisfactory. The model predicts that at times below about 5 seconds, the number of decays per second should rapidly drop to zero, a testable signature that could confirm or reject the interpretation.
