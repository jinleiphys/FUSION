# Challenging cosmic ray propagation with antiprotons. Evidence for a "fresh" nuclei component?

> Recent measurements of the cosmic ray (CR) antiproton flux have been shown to challenge existing CR propagation models. It was shown that the reacceleration models designed to match secondary to primary nuclei ratios (e.g., boron/carbon) produce too few antiprotons. Matching both the secondary to primary nuclei ratio and the antiproton flux requires artificial breaks in the diffusion coefficient and the primary injection spectrum suggesting the need for other approaches. In the present paper we discuss one possibility to overcome these difficulties. Using the measured antiproton flux AND B/C ratio to fix the diffusion coefficient, we show that the spectra of primary nuclei as measured in the heliosphere may contain a fresh local "unprocessed" component at low energies perhaps associated with the Local Bubble, thus decreasing the measured secondary to primary nuclei ratio. The independent evidence for SN activity in the solar vicinity in the last few Myr supports this idea. The model reproduces antiprotons, B/C ratio, and elemental abundances up to Ni (Z<=28). Calculated isotopic distributions of Be and B are in perfect agreement with CR data. The abundances of three "radioactive clock" isotopes in CR, 10Be, 26Al, 36Cl, are all consistent and indicate a halo size z_h~4 kpc based on the most accurate data taken by the ACE spacecraft.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the observed discrepancy between cosmic ray antiproton fluxes and secondary-to-primary nuclei ratios (e.g., boron/carbon) in reacceleration propagation models can be resolved by introducing a fresh local "unprocessed" component of primary nuclei at low energies, possibly associated with the Local Bubble, rather than requiring artificial breaks in the diffusion coefficient or injection spectrum.

## Method

The authors used the GALPROP propagation model in a two-dimensional cylindrically symmetric configuration, fitting the diffusion coefficient normalization and energy dependence using both the antiproton flux at its peak (~2 GeV) and the high-energy part of the B/C ratio. They introduced a local component with a spectrum of the form df/dρ ∝ ρ^(-η) exp(-ρ/ρ_b) to represent contributions from the Local Bubble, and tuned its parameters (cutoff rigidity ρ_b and elemental abundances) to match low-energy secondary-to-primary ratios measured by ACE and Ulysses.

## Key numbers

- Diffusion coefficient normalization D_0 = (3.3 ± 0.8) × 10^28 cm^2 s^(-1) at ρ_0 = 3 GV, fixed using antiproton flux at ~2 GeV
- Diffusion coefficient power-law index δ = 0.47, chosen as best match to B/C ratio data (excluding pure Kolmogorov δ = 1/3)
- Halo size z_h ~ 4 kpc, derived from radioactive clock isotopes 10Be, 26Al, 36Cl using ACE data
- Alfvén speed v_A = 23 km s^(-1) in reacceleration models
- Injection index γ = 2.28 for reacceleration models
- Local Bubble cutoff energy E_b = 500 MeV/nucleon (with acceptable range 400–600 MeV/nucleon)
- Antiproton flux discrepancy in standard reacceleration models: ~40% at ~2 GeV, while measurement uncertainties are ~20%
- B/C ratio at low energies reduced by including Local Bubble component with E_b = 500 MeV/nucleon
- Calculated 13C/12C ratio ~0.14 at 120 MeV/nucleon (modulation potential 500 MV), a factor of two larger than observed when propagation parameters are tuned to B/C ratio
- Plain diffusion model (PD): D_0 = 3.10 × 10^28 cm^2 s^(-1), δ = 0.60, injection index γ = 2.16

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Moskalenko et al. (2002) which showed that reacceleration models matching secondary-to-primary nuclei ratios produce too few antiprotons, and that matching both requires artificial breaks in the diffusion coefficient and injection spectrum. The paper proposes an alternative explanation, a local "fresh" component from the Local Bubble, building on earlier discussions by Morfill (1982) and Davis (2001). The model enables simultaneous fitting of antiproton fluxes, B/C ratios, and elemental abundances up to nickel (Z ≤ 28), and provides consistent radioactive clock isotope abundances that support a halo size of ~4 kpc.
