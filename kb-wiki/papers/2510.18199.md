# Nonlocality Effect in the Tunneling of Alpha Radioactivity with the Aid of Machine Learning

> Recently, building upon the research findings of E. L. Medeiros, we have extended the alpha-particle non-locality effect to the two-potential approach (TPA). This extension demonstrates that the integration of the alpha-particle nonlocality effect into TPA yields relatively favorable results. In the present work, we employ machine learning methods to further optimize the aforementioned approach, specifically utilizing three classical machine learning models: decision tree regression, random forest regression, and XGBRegressor. Among these models, both the decision tree regression and XGBRegressor models exhibit the highest degree of agreement with the reference data, whereas the random forest regression model shows inferior performance. In terms of standard deviation, the results derived from the decision tree regression and XGBRegressor models represent improvements of 54.5% and 53.7%, respectively, compared to the TPA that does not account for the coordinate-dependent effective mass of alpha particles. Furthermore, we extend the decision tree regression and XGBRegressor models to predict the alpha-decay half-lives of 20 even-even nuclei with atomic numbers Z=118 and Z=120. Subsequently, the superheavy nucleus half-life predictions generated by our proposed models are compared with those from two established benchmarks: the improved eight-parameter Deng-Zhang-Royer (DZR) model and the new empirical expression (denoted as "New+D") proposed by V. Yu. Denisov, which explicitly incorporates nuclear deformation effects. Overall, the predictions from these models and formulas are generally consistent. Notably, the predictions of the decision tree regression model show a high level of consistency with those of the New+D expression, while the XGBRegressor model exhibits deviations from the other two comparative models.

## Key claim

Integrating the alpha-particle nonlocality effect into the two-potential approach (TPA) and then optimizing the coordinate-dependent effective mass parameter using machine learning models yields improved agreement with experimental alpha-decay half-lives, but the improvement is conditional on the specific machine learning model used: decision tree regression and XGBRegressor outperform random forest regression, and the predictions for superheavy nuclei (Z=118, 120) are generally consistent with established empirical formulas, though the XGBRegressor model shows deviations from the other two comparative models.

## Method

The authors extended the two-potential approach (TPA) by incorporating a spatially variable effective mass for the alpha particle, following the nonlocality formalism of Medeiros (2022). They then used three supervised machine learning models, decision tree regression, random forest regression, and XGBRegressor, to optimize the adjustable mass parameter \(\rho_S\) for each nucleus, using \(\frac{Z}{\sqrt{Q}}\) and mass number \(A\) as input features and pre-optimized \(\rho_S\) values from 196 even-even nuclei (Z=52 to 118, N≥52) as training targets. The trained models were applied to calculate alpha-decay half-lives and to predict half-lives for 20 even-even superheavy nuclei with Z=118 and Z=120, using Q-values from the WS4+RBF mass model.

## Key numbers

- Standard deviation \(\sigma\) for the TPA without nonlocality effect: 0.573; with machine learning optimization: 0.306 (random forest), 0.264 (decision tree), 0.259 (XGBRegressor).
- Improvement in standard deviation relative to TPA without coordinate-dependent effective mass: 54.5% for decision tree regression, 53.7% for XGBRegressor.
- For specific nuclei, adjusting \(\rho_S\) with the decision tree model reduced the deviation between calculated and experimental half-lives by 93.82% for \(^{108}_{54}\)Xe (\(\rho_S = 0.347\)), 99.64% for \(^{114}_{56}\)Ba (\(\rho_S = 2.585\)), 50.67% for \(^{184}_{80}\)Hg (\(\rho_S = -0.347\)), and 100% for \(^{290}_{114}\)Fl (\(\rho_S = -2.182\)).
- Predicted logarithmic half-lives for 20 superheavy nuclei (Z=118, 120) range from approximately -6.903 to -2.405 (in seconds), with specific values such as -4.307 (DT) and -4.304 (XG) for \(^{288}\)118, and -6.378 (DT) and -6.691 (XG) for \(^{306}\)120.

## Context

This work extends the nonlocality effect formalism introduced by Medeiros (2022) for alpha decay, applying it within the two-potential approach and then using machine learning to optimize the mass parameter rather than manual tuning. The results improve upon the standard TPA without nonlocality and are compared with predictions from the improved eight-parameter Deng-Zhang-Royer (DZR) model by Deng (2020) and the new empirical expression ("New+D") by Denisov (2024) that incorporates nuclear deformation. The approach enables more accurate half-life predictions for superheavy nuclei, which can guide experimental synthesis of new elements and isotopes, and suggests that neutron numbers 178 and 184 may be submagic and magic numbers, respectively.
