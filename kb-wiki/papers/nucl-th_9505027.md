# Delta degrees of freedom in antisymmetrized molecular dynamics and (p,p') reactions in the delta region

> Delta degrees of freedom are introduced into antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD). This is done by increasing the number of basic states in the AMD wave function, introducing a Skyrme-type delta-nucleon potential, and including $NN\leftrightarrow NΔ$ reactions in the collision description. As a test of the delta dynamics, the extended AMD is applied to (p,p$'$) recations at $E_{\rm lab}=800$ MeV for a $^{12}$C target. It is found that the ratio and the absolute values for delta peak and quasielastic peak (QEP) in the $^{12}$C(p,p$'$) reaction are reproduced for angles $Θ_{\rm lab} \agt 40^\circ$, pointing to a correct treatment of the delta dynamics in the extended AMD. For forward angles the QEP is overestimated. The results of the AMD calculations are compared to one-step Monte Carlo (OSMC) calculations and a detailed analysis of multi-step and delta potential effects is given. As important side results we present a way to apply a Gallilei invariant theory for (N,N$'$) reactions up to $E_{\rm lab} \approx 800$ MeV which ensures approximate Lorentz invariance and we discuss how to fix the width parameter $ν$ of the single particle momentum distribution for outgoing nucleons in the AMD calculation.

## Key claim

The extended antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) model, which includes delta degrees of freedom via a Skyrme-type delta-nucleon potential and $NN\leftrightarrow N\Delta$ reactions, reproduces the ratio and absolute values of the delta peak and quasielastic peak in $^{12}$C(p,p$'$) reactions at $E_{\rm lab}=800$ MeV for laboratory angles $\Theta_{\rm lab} \agt 40^\circ$, but overestimates the quasielastic peak at forward angles.

## Method

The authors extended the AMD framework by increasing the number of basic states in the Slater determinant wave function to include four spin-averaged delta states ($\Delta^{++},\Delta^{+},\Delta^{0},\Delta^{-}$), introducing a zero-range Skyrme-type delta-nucleon potential with parameters from the delta-hole model, and incorporating $NN\rightarrow N\Delta$ and $N\Delta\rightarrow NN$ collision terms using VerWest-Arndt parametrizations and a momentum-dependent delta width. The extended AMD was applied to simulate (p,p$'$) reactions on a $^{12}$C target at 800 MeV, with the delta decay calculated in the final time step assuming isotropic decay in the delta rest frame, and the cross sections computed using a combination of Lorentz and Galilei transformations to ensure approximate Lorentz invariance.

## Key numbers

- Incident proton energy: $E_{\rm lab}=800$ MeV for $^{12}$C(p,p$'$) reactions.
- Delta rest mass: $M_\Delta=1232$ MeV; delta width at resonance: $\Gamma_r = 110$ MeV.
- Width parameter for Gaussian wave packets: $\nu=0.16$ fm$^{-2}$.
- Gogny force incompressibility: $K = 228$ MeV; calculated binding energy of $^{12}$C: 92.6 MeV (observed 92.2 MeV); calculated r.m.s. radius of $^{12}$C: 2.55 fm.
- Delta-nucleon potential parameters: $t_{0\, \Delta}= -700$ MeV fm$^3$, $t_{3\, \Delta}= 1750$ MeV fm$^5$, $\tau = 5/3$, giving delta mean field $U_\Delta(\rho_0)= -30$ MeV at normal nuclear density.
- Switching time from dynamical to evaporation stage: 150 fm/c.
- Best fit for neutron momentum spectrum in p(p,n)p$\pi^+$ at $E_{\rm lab}=830$ MeV obtained with phase-space suppression factor exponent $n=2$ in Eq. (ref{factor}).

## Context

This work extends the AMD model, previously successful for heavy ion collisions below 200 MeV/nucleon (Ono et al., 1992; Horiuchi, 1991), to higher energies where delta excitation becomes important. The approach is similar to the inclusion of delta degrees of freedom in BUU and QMD models (Cugnon, 1981; Sorge et al., 1989; Wolf et al., 1990) and builds on earlier studies of pion-nucleus reactions using BUU (Engel et al., 1994). The results enable future application of the extended AMD to heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies and provide a framework for studying multi-step processes and delta potential effects in nucleon-nucleus reactions.
