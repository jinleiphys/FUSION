# Taming nuclear complexity with a committee of multilayer neural networks

> We demonstrate that a committee of deep neural networks is capable of predicting the ground-state and excited energies of more than 1800 atomic nuclei with an accuracy akin to the one achieved by state-of-the-art nuclear energy density functionals (EDFs) and a major speed-up. An active learning strategy is proposed to train this algorithm with a minimal set of 210 nuclei. This approach enables future fast studies of the influence of EDFs parametrizations on structure properties over the whole nuclear chart and suggests that for the first time an artificial intelligence successfully encoded the laws of nuclear deformation.

## Key claim
A committee of deep neural networks can predict ground-state and excited energies of more than 1800 atomic nuclei with accuracy comparable to state-of-the-art nuclear energy density functionals (EDFs), but the claim is conditional on training the algorithm on only about 210 nuclei (10% of the database) using an active learning strategy, and the reported root mean square error of 716 keV on the correlated ground state energy is relative to the multireference EDF calculation, not directly to experimental data.

## Method
The authors used the five-dimensional collective Hamiltonian (5DCH) approach, which requires constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations to obtain eight functions: the HFB energy surface, zero-point energy correction, vibrational inertia tensor components, and rotational inertia along three axes. They trained a committee of multilayer neural networks (each with five hidden layers of sizes 600-300-150-100-75 neurons) to predict these eight functions on a 64x64 deformation grid from only the neutron and proton numbers as input, using an active learning procedure that iteratively selects the most informative nuclei to add to the training set.

## Key numbers
- Root mean square (RMS) on the correlated ground state energy for 1666 test nuclei: 716 keV, achieved with a training set of 10% of the database (approximately 210 nuclei)
- RMS on the HFB energy for test nuclei: 557 keV at 10% training set size, decreasing to 388 keV at 20% training set size
- RMS on the HFB energy for test nuclei at 5% training set size: 1190 keV
- RMS on the zero-point energy correction at 10% training set: 312 keV
- RMS on rotational inertia components at 10% training set: 1.40, 2.25, and 0.76 (in units of hbar^2 x MeV^-1) for the three axes
- RMS on vibrational inertia components at 10% training set: 11.7, 10.2, and 23.9 (in units of MeV^-1) for the three tensor components
- For the nucleus 178Os, the committee predicted the rotational band (2+, 4+, 6+ states) within 8% deviation from the HFB reference, and the first excited 0+ level within 13%
- The training set consisted of 2100 even-even nuclei from the AMEDEE database with charge Z in [10,120] and neutron number N in [10,260], using the Gogny D1S effective interaction

## Context
This work extends prior machine learning approaches to nuclear masses, such as Athanassopoulos (2006) who achieved 950 keV RMS on masses with neural networks, and later studies by Utama (2017), Zhang (2017), and Niu (2018) that used neural networks or Gaussian processes to predict residuals between theory and experiment. Unlike those approaches, which required training on 80% of experimental data and could predict only one observable, this method learns multiple intermediate quantities (potential energy surfaces and inertia) from a minimal set of 210 nuclei, enabling prediction of both ground-state and excited energies. This enables fast studies of how different EDF parametrizations affect structure properties across the entire nuclear chart and opens the possibility of fitting new EDFs at the multireference level with multiple observables, which was previously prohibitive due to computational cost.
