# The r-Process: Current Understanding and Future Tests

> Current understanding of the r-process is summarized in terms of the astrophysical sites, the yield patterns, and the role of neutrinos. The importance of observational and experimental tests is emphasized. A number of future tests regarding the above three aspects of the r-process are discussed.

# The r-Process: Current Understanding and Future Tests

## Key claim
Core-collapse supernovae are favored over neutron star mergers as the major r-process site based on observations of metal-poor stars, but this conclusion depends on the assumption that the Galactic rate of neutron star mergers is much lower than that of core-collapse supernovae (approximately 1 per 100 years versus 1 per 100,000 years).

## Method
The paper reviews observational constraints from metal-poor Galactic halo stars (CS 22892-052, CS 31082-001, HD 115444, HD 122563, HE 2148-1247) and meteoritic data on extinct radioactivities to infer the number and types of r-process sources. Abundance patterns are compared with the solar r-process pattern and with chemical evolution models that test both core-collapse supernova and neutron star merger scenarios.

## Key numbers
- Galactic rate of core-collapse supernovae: approximately 1 per 100 years; Galactic rate of neutron star mergers: approximately 1 per 100,000 years
- Stars observed with Fe abundances as low as approximately 10^{-3} times solar show substantial Eu abundances; stars with Fe abundances as low as approximately 10^{-4} times solar show substantial Ba abundances
- Absolute abundances of heavy r-process elements (Ba and above) differ by a factor of approximately 100 between CS 31082-001, HD 115444, and HD 122563, while abundances of elements from O to Ge are approximately the same
- Abundances of Fe and lower atomic numbers in HE 2148-1247 are approximately 10^{-2} times solar, while neutron-capture elements Ba and above are at the solar level
- The r-process pattern in CS 22892-052 and CS 31082-001 covers elements from Sr to Cd (A=88-116) and elements Ba and above (A>130)

## Context
This review extends the argument from Qian (2000) favoring core-collapse supernovae over neutron star mergers as the major r-process site, which was supported by a detailed numerical study by Argast et al. (2004). The paper builds on observations of metal-poor stars by Sneden et al. (2000), Hill et al. (2002), and Westin et al. (2000), and on meteoritic data from Wasserburg et al. (1996). The proposed fission scenario with neutrino-enhanced fission from Qian (2002) and Kolbe et al. (2004) offers a mechanism to explain the observed abundance patterns, including the Th/Eu ratio difference between CS 22892-052 and CS 31082-001. Future tests include direct gamma-ray detection of r-process nuclei such as 126Sn in supernova remnants by INTEGRAL, identification of supernova progenitors via Hubble Space Telescope archival images, and searches for neutron star companions of metal-poor stars with high neutron-capture abundances.
