# Non-equilibrated post freeze out distributions

> We discuss freeze out on the hypersurface with time-like normal vector, trying to answer how realistic is to assume thermal post freeze out distributions for measured hadrons. Using simple kinetic models for gradual freeze out we are able to generate thermal post FO distribution, but only in highly simplified situation. In a more advanced model, taking into account rescattering and re-thermalization, the post FO distribution gets more complicated. The resulting particle distributions are in qualitative agreement with the experimentally measured pion spectra. Our study also shows that the obtained post FO distribution functions, although analytically very different from the Juttner distribution, do look pretty much like thermal distributions in some range of parameters.

## Key claim

The paper argues that assuming thermal (Jüttner) post-freeze-out distributions for hadrons measured from a time-like freeze-out hypersurface is not generally realistic; although such thermal distributions can be reproduced in a highly simplified kinetic model, a more advanced model including rescattering and re-thermalization yields non-thermal post-freeze-out distributions that nevertheless resemble thermal distributions over a certain parameter range.

## Method

The authors construct a kinetic freeze-out model in which the matter is described by two components: an interacting component \(f_{int}\) and a frozen-out component \(f_{free}\), evolving in proper time \(\tau\) from an initial Jüttner distribution at \(\tau_0\). In the simplest version, the interacting component decays exponentially with a constant freeze-out time \(\tau_f\); in the improved version, a relaxation term with relaxation time \(\tau_r\) drives \(f_{int}\) toward a re-equilibrated Jüttner distribution whose parameters (temperature, baryon density, flow velocity) evolve according to conservation laws. The model is applied to a massless pion gas and to a nucleon gas, and the resulting post-freeze-out distributions are compared to the Jüttner form.

## Key numbers

* For the massless pion gas with initial temperature \(T(\tau_0)=170\ \text{MeV}\) and zero flow velocity, the final frozen-out distribution at \(\tau\to\infty\) is given by \(f_{free}(\infty) = -\frac{4}{(2\pi)^3}Ei\left(-\frac{p^\mu u_\mu}{T(\tau_0)}\right)\).
* The ratio of entropy densities for the massless pion gas is \(R = s(\tau=\infty)/s(\tau_0) = 0.9449\), independent of initial temperature.
* For the nucleon case, the initial baryon density is \(n(\tau_0)=0.0025\ \text{fm}^{-3}\) and the initial chemical potential is \(\mu(\tau_0)=31\ \text{MeV}\), both at \(T(\tau_0)=170\ \text{MeV}\).
* The temperature of the interacting component evolves as \(T(\tau)=T(\tau_0)e^{-(\tau-\tau_0)/(4\tau_f)}\) for the massless pion gas.

## Context

This work extends the gradual freeze-out approach previously developed for space-like freeze-out surfaces (Bugaev 1996; Csernai et al. 2001-2003) to time-like freeze-out hypersurfaces, which are commonly used in Bjorken-type hydrodynamic models. The authors show that the cut Jüttner distribution proposed by Bugaev (1996) is recovered only in an oversimplified kinetic model, while including rescattering and re-thermalization yields non-thermal post-freeze-out distributions that qualitatively reproduce the experimentally observed decrease of the slope parameter with increasing transverse momentum in pion spectra at SPS and RHIC. The work enables more realistic modeling of freeze-out in relativistic heavy-ion collisions and warns that observed spectra may be misinterpreted as thermal when they are not.
