# Is nucleon spin thermalized in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions?

> Despite the success of the spin-thermalized assumption in explaining hyperon spin polarizations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, challenges begin to arise especially at lower collision energies. The present study compares the nucleon spin polarization during the collision process and at the freeze-out stage from a non-relativistic spin-dependent transport model with spin-thermalized approaches in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions, where the relativistic effect and the temperature gradient have shown to be unimportant. It is found that both the global and local spin polarizations are largely overestimated from spin-thermalized approaches, compared to those generated by the spin-orbit mean-field potential in transport simulations.

# Is nucleon spin thermalized in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions?

## Key claim

The nucleon spin is not thermalized in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions; spin-thermalized approaches overestimate both global and local spin polarizations by a factor of roughly 2-5 compared to the spin-orbit mean-field potential dynamics simulated in a non-relativistic spin-dependent transport model, at beam energies around 50-150 AMeV where relativistic effects and temperature gradients are unimportant.

## Method

The author uses the non-relativistic spin- and isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (SIBUU) transport model, which explicitly includes nucleon spin degrees of freedom via 2x2 matrix phase-space distribution functions and single-particle energies. The spin-orbit coupling from a Skyrme-type interaction generates spin dynamics through equations of motion for nucleon coordinates, momenta, and spin vectors, with spin- and isospin-dependent collision terms from phase-shift data. The spin polarization from SIBUU is compared to predictions from three spin-thermalized approaches: the non-relativistic kinematic vorticity formula P = omega/(2T), the thermal vorticity formula, and the spin-vector approach, all using vorticity and temperature fields extracted from the same SIBUU bulk dynamics.

## Key numbers

- Au+Au collisions at beam energy 100 AMeV and impact parameter b=8 fm: kinematic vorticity reaches about 0.05 fm^{-1} at the most compressed stage (t=30 fm/c)
- Spin-thermalized approaches predict P_y as large as ~10% for free nucleons, compared to a few percent from SIBUU (depending on spin-orbit coupling coefficient W_0)
- SIBUU gives P_y about 8% in the participant region at late stages, while spin-thermalized approaches give up to 20% in the same region
- Longitudinal spin polarization P_z from spin-thermalized approaches can be as large as ±10% compared to ±4% from SIBUU around t=50 fm/c
- SIBUU predicts maximum P_y around 50-100 AMeV, with smaller values at higher collision energies; spin-thermalized approaches predict increasing P_y with increasing collision energy
- Freeze-out occurs at local density below rho_0/8 (rho_0=0.16 fm^{-3})
- Default spin-orbit coupling coefficient W_0=150 MeVfm^5 used in SIBUU simulations

## Context

This work extends the spin-dependent transport approach initiated by Xu (2012) and developed further by Xia (2016, 2019) and Liu (2023), which previously established the SIBUU model framework including lattice Hamiltonian evolution and angular momentum conservation. The study directly challenges the spin-thermalized assumption that successfully described hyperon spin polarizations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions (Karpenko 2016, Li 2017) but has shown discrepancies at lower energies, particularly the non-monotonic energy dependence observed by HADES (2022). The results suggest that at intermediate energies, the spin-orbit mean-field potential rather than vorticity-driven thermalization is the dominant mechanism for nucleon spin polarization, and that covariant transport models will be needed to bridge to higher collision energies where relativistic effects become important.
