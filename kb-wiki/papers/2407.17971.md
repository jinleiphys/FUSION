# Dimensionality reduction through tensor factorization : application to \textit{ab initio} nuclear physics calculations

> The construction of predictive models of atomic nuclei from first principles is a challenging (yet necessary) task towards the systematic generation of theoretical predictions (and associated uncertainties) to support nuclear data evaluation. The consistent description of the rich phenomenology of nuclear systems indeed requires the introduction of reductionist approaches that construct nuclei directly from interacting nucleons by solving the associated quantum many-body problem. In this context, so-called \textit{ab initio} methods offer a promising route by deriving controlled (and systematically improvable) approximations both to the inter-nucleon interaction and to the solutions of the many-body problem. From a technical point of view, approximately solving the many-body Schrödinger equation in heavy open-shell systems typically requires the construction and contraction of large mode-4 (mode-6) tensors that need to be stored repeatedly. Recently, a new dimensionality reduction method based on randomized singular value decomposition has been introduced to reduce the numerical cost of many-body perturbation theory. This work applies this lightweight formalism to the study of the Germanium isotopic chain, where standard approaches would be too expansive to run. Inclusion of triaxiality is found to improve the overall agreement with experimental data on differential quantities.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a recently introduced dimensionality reduction method based on randomized singular value decomposition (RSVD) enables second-order many-body perturbation theory (BMBPT(2)) calculations along the Germanium isotopic chain that would otherwise be computationally prohibitive, but the method introduces a controlled truncation error of 0.1% and the dominant source of remaining error stems from the truncation of the perturbative expansion itself rather than the tensor factorization.

## Method

The authors apply the RSVD-based tensor factorization formalism to compute second-order BMBPT(2) correlation energies for even-even Germanium isotopes from A=60 to A=108. The method factorizes the large mode-4 tensor H^{40} (dimension N^4 with N=1820) without ever constructing the full tensor, using a blocked-Lanczos implementation with a stochastic error estimator. The energy denominator is separately factorized via Laplace transform with approximately 10 vectors, and the second-order energy is computed from the factorized representations of both the Hamiltonian and denominator tensors.

## Key numbers

- Single-particle basis: spherical harmonic oscillator with frequency ℏω=12 MeV truncated to 13 major shells, giving N=1820 basis states
- Three-body operator truncation: e_{3max}=24
- Controlled truncation error from SVD: 0.1% on ground state energies
- Isotopic range studied: even-even Germanium isotopes from A=60 to A=108
- Triaxial minima found with γ values in the range [15°,45°] for most isotopes
- Neutron number symmetry breaks spontaneously only near ¹⁰²Ge, with isotopes A=98,100,102,104 showing significant neutron number variance at their triaxial minima
- Discrepancy at A=72 attributed to predicted spherical shape leading to overestimation of binding energy

## Context

This work extends the RSVD-based tensor factorization method introduced by Frosini et al. (2024) from proof-of-principle calculations to systematic application along an entire isotopic chain. The Germanium chain is chosen because it hosts a variety of collective phenomena (triaxiality, superfluidity, shape coexistence) that prevent using symmetry-based dimensionality reduction techniques. The calculations use the EM1.8/2.0 Hamiltonian from Hebeler et al. (2011) with matrix elements generated by the Nuhamil code of Miyagi (2023), and three-body forces are approximated via the rank-reduction method of Frosini et al. (2021). The next step identified by the authors is extending the formalism to third-order perturbation theory to improve predictions of drip-line properties.
