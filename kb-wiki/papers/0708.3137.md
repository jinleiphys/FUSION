# Recent Developments on Kaon Condensation and Its Astrophysical Implications

> We discuss three different ways to arrive at kaon condensation at n_c = 3 n_0 where n_0 is nuclear matter density: (1) Fluctuating around the n=0 vacuum in chiral perturbation theory, (2) fluctuating around n_VM near the chiral restoration density n_chi where the vector manifestation of hidden local symmetry is reached and (3) fluctuating around the Fermi liquid fixed point at n_0. They all share one common theoretical basis, "hidden local symmetry." We argue that when the critical density n_c < n_chi is reached in a neutron star, the electrons turn into K^- mesons, which go into an S-wave Bose condensate. This reduces the pressure substantially and the neutron star goes into a black hole. Next we develop the argument that the collapse of a neutron star into a black hole takes place for a star of M = 1.5 M_sun. This means that Supernova 1987A had a black hole as result. We also show that two neutron stars in a binary have to be within 4% of each other in mass, for neutron stars sufficiently massive that they escape helium shell burning. For those that are so light that they do have helium shell burning, after a small correction for this they must be within 4% of each other in mass. Observations support the proximity in mass inside of a neutron star binary. The result of strangeness condensation is that there are 5 times more low-mass black-hole, neutron-star binaries than double neutron-star binaries although the former are difficult to observe.

# Recent Developments on Kaon Condensation and Its Astrophysical Implications

## Key claim

Kaon condensation occurs at a critical density of approximately three times nuclear matter density (n_c ≈ 3 n_0), and this condensation causes neutron stars above about 1.5 solar masses to collapse into black holes; the authors argue this mechanism implies that Supernova 1987A produced a black hole and that low-mass black-hole/neutron-star binaries are about five times more common than double neutron-star binaries, though the former are difficult to observe.

## Method

The authors approach kaon condensation from three theoretical vantage points: (1) chiral perturbation theory fluctuating around the matter-free vacuum, (2) hidden local symmetry theory fluctuating downward from the vector manifestation fixed point near chiral restoration density, and (3) Fermi liquid theory fluctuating around the nuclear matter density fixed point. They then apply renormalization-group flow analysis to determine the critical density, incorporating the concept of "hadronic freedom", a region near chiral restoration where interactions become weak, and use the resulting equation of state to model neutron star stability and binary population statistics.

## Key numbers

- Critical density for kaon condensation: n_c ≈ 3 n_0, where n_0 ≈ 0.16 fm^{-3} is nuclear matter density
- Neutron star collapse into black hole occurs for stars of mass M ≈ 1.5 M_sun (solar masses)
- The Tomozawa-Weinberg vector potential for K^- mesons: V^{K^-}_{TW} = -60 MeV × (n/n_0)
- The pion decay constant: f_π ≈ 93 MeV
- The sigma term from lattice calculations: Σ_{KN} ≈ 389 ± 14 MeV
- The in-medium parametric pion decay constant at nuclear matter density: F_π^*(n_0) ≈ 0.8 F_π
- The flash density (where hadronic freedom begins): n_flash ≈ 3 n_0
- Two neutron stars in a binary must be within 4% of each other in mass to avoid one collapsing into a black hole
- The result of strangeness condensation is approximately 5 times more low-mass black-hole/neutron-star binaries than double neutron-star binaries

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies on kaon condensation and its astrophysical consequences, building on the hidden local symmetry framework of Harada and Yamawaki and the scaling properties of chiral symmetry (Brown-Rho scaling). The calculation of kaon condensation from chiral perturbation theory follows the approach of Kaplan and Nelson, while the most complete prior calculation along those lines was performed by Thorsson et al. The paper argues against the objection by Carlson et al. that short-range nuclear correlations could push the critical density above 7 n_0, using the concept of hadronic freedom to show that repulsion ceases at high density. The results enable predictions for the population statistics of neutron-star and black-hole binaries that can be tested against observations.
