# On self-gravitating strange dark matter halos around galaxies

> A new family of nonrelativistic, Newtonian, non-quantum equilibrium configurations describing galactic halos is introduced, by considering strange quark matter conglomerates with masses larger than about 8 GeV as new possible components of the dark matter. Originally introduced to explain the state of matter in neutron stars, such conglomerates may also form in the high-density and temperature conditions of the primordial Universe and then decouple from ordinary baryonic matter, providing the fundamental components of dark matter for the formation of pristine gravitational potential wells and the subsequent evolution of cosmic structures. The obtained results for halo mass and radius are consistent with the rotational velocity curve observed in the Galaxy. Additionally, the average density of such dark matter halos is similar to that derived for halos of dwarf spheroidal galaxies, which can therefore be interpreted as downscaled versions of larger dark matter distributions around Milky Way-sized galaxies and hint for a common origin of the two families of cosmic structures.

## Key claim

Strange quark matter conglomerates with masses larger than about 8 GeV can form nonrelativistic, Newtonian, non-quantum equilibrium configurations that describe galactic dark matter halos, but the model requires that the halo follows a classical (Boltzmann/King) distribution rather than a semi-degenerate quantum statistics, and the results depend only on the particle mass, not on the specific nature of the dark matter candidate.

## Method

The authors solve the Poisson equation for a self-gravitating system using a semi-degenerate Fermi distribution function with a cutoff in energy, then find that the semi-degenerate regime yields incorrect halo parameters (too large densities, too small masses and radii). They instead adopt a classical Boltzmann (King) distribution with cutoff, tuning the central density and concentration parameter to match Milky Way-sized halo properties. They then compare the average density of their model halos to dark matter halo parameters derived from a spherical Monte-Carlo Jeans analysis of nine dwarf spheroidal galaxies using the CLUMPY software.

## Key numbers

- Lower mass limit for stable SQM conglomerates: m* > 7.46 GeV (corresponding to baryon multiplicity A > 8, from the model by Akaishi and collaborators)
- For a Milky Way-sized halo with particle mass m* = 10 GeV and central density ρ₀ = 10⁻²⁴ g cm⁻³: halo mass M = 9.98 × 10¹¹ M⊙, halo radius R = 89.41 kpc, mean halo density ρ̄ = 2.16 × 10⁻²⁶ g cm⁻³, velocity dispersion σ = 405 km s⁻¹
- Scaling relations: M ∝ ρ₀¹/² m*⁻⁴ and R ∝ ρ₀⁻¹/⁶ m*⁻⁴/³ for a 10 GeV particle
- General relativistic factor GM/Rc² = 4.8 × 10⁻⁷, confirming Newtonian regime
- Critical density for 10 GeV particles: ρ_cr = 7.8 × 10¹⁹ g cm⁻³, much larger than the mean halo density

## Context

This work extends the concept of strange quark matter, originally introduced to explain the state of matter in neutron stars (Bodmer 1971, Witten 1984), to the domain of galactic dark matter halos. The authors build on the model of Akaishi and collaborators (2017) for stable Λ(1405) conglomerates, while noting the critical analysis by Vidana and collaborators (2018) who argued that repulsive interactions prevent stability. The results are compared to dark matter halo parameters derived from Jeans analysis of dwarf spheroidal galaxies (Fermi-LAT collaboration 2015, Bonnivard and collaborators 2015), showing that the average density of strange dark matter halos is similar to that of dwarf spheroidal galaxy halos, suggesting a common origin. The work enables future investigation of gamma-ray signatures from self-interacting dark matter in high-density regions, potentially observable by next-generation Cherenkov telescopes.
