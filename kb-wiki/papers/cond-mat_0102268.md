# The Statistical Theory of Quantum Dots

> A quantum dot is a sub-micron-scale conducting device containing up to several thousand electrons. Transport through a quantum dot at low temperatures is a quantum-coherent process. This review focuses on dots in which the electron's dynamics are chaotic or diffusive, giving rise to statistical properties that reflect the interplay between one-body chaos, quantum interference, and electron-electron interactions. The conductance through such dots displays mesoscopic fluctuations as a function of gate voltage, magnetic field, and shape deformation. The techniques used to describe these fluctuations include semiclassical methods, random-matrix theory, and the supersymmetric nonlinear $σ$ model. In open dots, the approximation of noninteracting quasiparticles is justified, and electron-electron interactions contribute indirectly through their effect on the dephasing time at finite temperature. In almost-closed dots, where conductance occurs by tunneling, the charge on the dot is quantized, and electron-electron interactions play an important role. Transport is dominated by Coulomb blockade, leading to peaks in the conductance that at low temperatures provide information on the dot's ground-state properties. Several statistical signatures of electron-electron interactions have been identified, most notably in the dot's addition spectrum. The dot's spin, determined partly by exchange interactions, can also influence the fluctuation properties of the conductance. Other mesoscopic phenomena in quantum dots that are affected by the charging energy include the fluctuations of the cotunneling conductance and mesoscopic Coulomb blockade.

## Key claim

This review argues that the statistical properties of conductance and addition spectra in quantum dots with chaotic or diffusive electron dynamics can be described by random-matrix theory (RMT) and related methods, but only under specific conditions: for open dots the noninteracting quasiparticle approximation is justified with interactions contributing indirectly through dephasing, while for almost-closed dots electron-electron interactions play an important role through Coulomb blockade and exchange interactions, leading to statistical signatures in the addition spectrum that go beyond RMT predictions.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches including semiclassical methods, random-matrix theory, and the supersymmetric nonlinear sigma model applied to quantum dots with chaotic or diffusive classical dynamics. It connects these to experimental measurements of conductance fluctuations as functions of gate voltage, magnetic field, and shape deformation, and to Coulomb-blockade peak statistics in closed dots. The review draws analogies between quantum dot physics and compound-nucleus resonance statistics, using R-matrix theory (originally developed by Wigner and Eisenbud (1947) for nuclear reactions) to relate the Hamiltonian of the closed system to scattering resonances of the weakly open system.

## Key numbers

- Dot linear size: 0.1-1 micrometer; effective electron mass in GaAs: m* = 0.067 m_e
- Typical sheet density: n_s ~ 4 x 10^11 cm^-2, corresponding to Fermi wavelength lambda_F ~ 40 nm and Fermi energy E_F ~ 14 meV
- Mean free path in GaAs/AlGaAs heterostructures: l ~ 0.1-10 micrometers (from mobility mu_e ~ 10^4-10^6 cm^2/V-s)
- Mean single-particle level spacing for dot area ~0.3 micrometer^2: Delta ~ 11 micro-eV, resolvable at temperatures ~100 mK (kT = 8.6 micro-eV)
- Charging energy for GaAs disk of radius 0.2 micrometer: E_C = e^2/C ~ 1000 micro-eV
- Condition for observing charge quantization: G << e^2/h and kT << e^2/C
- Conductance fluctuations in open dots: rms fluctuations ~ e^2/h, independent of average conductance
- In closed dots, the distribution of normalized conductance peak heights follows RMT prediction (Jalabert, Stone, and Alhassid, 1992) with no free parameters, agreeing with experiment over almost three orders of magnitude

## Context

This review extends the Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit conjecture (1984) that classically chaotic systems follow RMT predictions to the specific case of quantum dots, linking the field to earlier work on universal conductance fluctuations in disordered systems by Altshuler (1985) and Lee and Stone (1985). It connects quantum dot physics to compound-nucleus resonance statistics, showing that open dots exhibit Ericson fluctuations (Ericson, 1960, 1963) analogous to those in nuclear reactions, while closed dots show Porter-Thomas distributions of resonance widths similar to neutron resonances in heavy nuclei. The review enables a statistical approach to quantum dot transport that treats measured quantities as random variables sampled over different shapes and magnetic fields, rather than attempting to calculate precise sequences for specific devices.
