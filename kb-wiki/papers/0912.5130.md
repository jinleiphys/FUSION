# Study of fragmentation using clusterization algorithm with realistic binding energies

> We here study fragmentation using \emph{simulated annealing clusterization algorithm} (SACA) with binding energy at a microscopic level. In an earlier version, a constant binding energy (4 MeV/nucleon) was used. We improve this binding energy criterion by calculating the binding energy of different clusters using modified Bethe-Weizsäcker mass (BWM) formula. We also compare our calculations with experimental data of ALADiN group. Nearly no effect is visible of this modification.

# Study of fragmentation using clusterization algorithm with realistic binding energies

## Key claim
Replacing the constant average binding energy check of -4 MeV/nucleon with a fragment-mass-dependent binding energy calculated from the modified Bethe-Weizsäcker mass (BWM) formula produces nearly no effect on the predicted fragment multiplicities and largest-fragment sizes in simulated heavy-ion collisions, thereby validating the simpler constant-binding-energy approach used in the original SACA algorithm.

## Method
The authors used the quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) model to simulate collisions of 197Au + 197Au at 600 AMeV, then applied the simulated annealing clusterization algorithm (SACA) to identify fragments. In the original SACA (version 1.1), each fragment with N_f >= 3 was required to have a binding energy below -4 MeV/nucleon; in the extended version (SACA 2.1), this criterion was replaced by the fragment-specific binding energy calculated from the modified Bethe-Weizsäcker mass (BWM) formula of Samanta et al., which includes volume, surface, Coulomb, asymmetry, and pairing terms with parameters a_v=15.777 MeV, a_s=18.34 MeV, a_c=0.71 MeV, a_sym=23.21 MeV, and a_p=12 MeV. Fragment multiplicities and the average mass of the heaviest fragment were compared between the two versions and against experimental data from the ALADiN collaboration.

## Key numbers
- The constant binding energy used in original SACA (1.1) was -4 MeV/nucleon for fragments with N_f >= 3, and 0 otherwise.
- The BWM formula parameters: a_v = 15.777 MeV, a_s = 18.34 MeV, a_c = 0.71 MeV, a_sym = 23.21 MeV, a_p = 12 MeV.
- Simulations were performed for 197Au + 197Au at incident energy 600 AMeV (also tested at 400 and 1000 AMeV) at impact parameters up to 12 fm.
- Fragment identification time was chosen as 60 fm/c, when the average mass of the heaviest fragment reaches its minimum.
- For the reaction at 600 AMeV and impact parameter 12 fm, the average mass of the heaviest fragment ⟨A^max⟩ and multiplicities of free nucleons, LCPs (2 ≤ A ≤ 4), MMFs (5 ≤ A ≤ 20), HMFs (21 ≤ A ≤ 65), and IMFs (5 ≤ A ≤ 65) showed nearly identical values between SACA (1.1) and SACA (2.1).
- In central collisions, SACA (2.1) predicted a smaller ⟨A^max⟩ than SACA (1.1); the trend reversed in peripheral collisions.

## Context
This work extends the simulated annealing clusterization algorithm (SACA) previously developed by the same group (Vermani et al., earlier papers), which itself improved upon the minimum spanning tree (MST) algorithm that significantly underestimated fragment multiplicities for larger impact parameters in QMD simulations (Goyal et al., Blaich et al., Tsang et al.). The constant -4 MeV/nucleon binding energy used in original SACA had already reproduced ALADiN experimental data for 197Au + 197Au at 600 AMeV (Schüttauf et al.), and the present study confirms that using realistic mass-dependent binding energies from the modified Bethe-Weizsäcker formula (Samanta et al., 2007) does not alter the results, validating the simpler approach. This enables future studies to use the computationally cheaper constant-binding-energy SACA version for fragmentation studies in heavy-ion collisions.
