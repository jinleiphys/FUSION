# Highly efficient nuclear population transfer through physics-informed neural networks

> Nuclear coherent population transfer (NCPT) offers numerous potential applications, particularly in next-generation nuclear clocks and nuclear batteries. However, the realization of high fidelity, fast operation, and low energy consumption in NCPT remains so far challenging. Here, we employ physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) to the population transfer in an open three-level nuclear system with spontaneous emission. The method embeds the system's control equations and boundary conditions into the loss function, thereby enabling the automatic learning of optimal laser pulse sequences that drive highly efficient population transfer. We take a short-lived excited state of $^{172}\mathrm{Yb}$ and a long-lived state of $^{229}\mathrm{Th}$ as representative examples, and systematically compare the performance of the PINNs approach with three conventional control strategies. We show that PINNs can achieve higher transfer efficiency with smaller pulse areas and shorter durations across different lifetime regimes. Our results provide a new perspective to overcome the lifetime limitation and enhance the efficiency of nuclear state transfer.

# Highly efficient nuclear population transfer through physics-informed neural networks

## Key claim
Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) can achieve nearly perfect nuclear population transfer (infidelity at the 10⁻⁴ level) with smaller pulse areas and shorter durations than conventional methods, but only when the system's master equation and boundary conditions are embedded into the loss function and the network is trained for 30,000 epochs with manually tuned loss weights.

## Method
The authors model an open three-level Λ-type nuclear system interacting with two x-ray laser pulses, incorporating spontaneous emission from the excited state. They reformulate the density matrix master equation into a 9-dimensional real-valued nonautonomous linear differential equation and embed it into a fully connected neural network with five hidden layers of 128 neurons each, using tanh activation functions. The network takes discrete time points as input and outputs the state vector and two control Rabi frequencies, which are constrained by exponential envelopes to satisfy initial conditions. The loss function combines three weighted terms: model dynamics deviation, deviation from target state, and a physical constraint penalizing ground and intermediate state populations.

## Key numbers
- For ¹⁷²Yb (excited state lifetime 11 fs): PINNs achieve population transfer efficiency P₂ = 0.9999 within 2 fs using a pulse area of 2.11, compared to coincident pulses (P₂ = 0.9422, 40 fs, area 12.56), STIRAP (P₂ = 0.0979, 10 fs, area 11.57), and MIE (P₂ = 0.9999, 10 fs, area 12.74)
- For ²²⁹Th (excited state lifetime 0.172 ns): PINNs achieve P₂ = 0.9999 within 200 fs using a pulse area of 1.79, compared to coincident pulses (P₂ = 0.9569, 1000 fs, area 12.56), STIRAP (P₂ = 0.6044, 500 fs, area 10.75), and MIE (P₂ = 0.9999, 500 fs, area 11.43)
- The PINNs training uses learning rate 2 × 10⁻⁴ over 30,000 epochs with loss weights η₁ = 0.0899, η₂ = 0.040, and η₃ = 0.800
- Maximum Rabi frequency constrained to Ω_{s,p} ≤ 5 for all methods

## Context
This work extends prior nuclear coherent population transfer (NCPT) studies that used stimulated Raman adiabatic passage (STIRAP), coincident pulses, and mixed-state inverse engineering (MIE) as in Liao (2011), PhysRevC.94.054601, and PhysRevC.110.034606. The PINNs approach, originally developed for solving differential equations by Raissi (2019) and Karniadakis (2021), is here applied for the first time to nuclear population transfer. The results enable improved control schemes for next-generation nuclear clocks based on the ²²⁹Th isomeric state (Seiferle 2019) and for nuclear batteries, and suggest that machine learning optimization can overcome lifetime limitations that hinder conventional adiabatic methods in short-lived nuclear systems.
