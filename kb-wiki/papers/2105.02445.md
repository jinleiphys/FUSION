# Machine learning the nuclear mass

> Background: The masses of about 2500 nuclei have been measured experimentally, however more than 7000 isotopes are predicted to exist in the nuclear landscape from H (Z=1) to Og (Z=118) based on various theoretical calculations. Exploring the mass of the remains is a hot topic in nuclear physics. Machine learning has been served as a powerful tool in learning complex representations of big data in many fields. Purpose: We use Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM) which is a highly efficient machine learning algorithm to predict the masses of unknown nuclei and to explore the nuclear landscape in neutron-rich side from learning the measured nuclear masses. Results: By using the experimental data of 80 percent of known nuclei as the training dataset, the root mean square deviation (RMSD) between the predicted and the experimental binding energy of the remaining 20% is about 0.234 MeV, 0.213 MeV, 0.170 MeV, and 0.222 MeV for the LightGBM-refined LDM, DZ, WS4, and FRDM models, respectively. These values are of about 90%, 65%, 40%, and 60% smaller than the corresponding origin mass models. The RMSD for 66 newly measured nuclei that appeared in AME2020 is also significantly improved on the same foot. One-neutron and two-neutron separation energies predicted by these refined models are in consistence with several theoretical predictions based on various physical models. Conclusions: LightGBM can be used to refine theoretical nuclear mass models so as to predict the binding energy of unknown nuclei. Moreover, the correlation between the input characteristic quantities and the output can be interpreted by SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP, a popular explainable artificial intelligence tool), this may provide new insights on developing theoretical nuclear mass models.

# Machine learning the nuclear mass

## Key claim
LightGBM can refine four theoretical nuclear mass models (LDM, DZ, WS4, FRDM) to predict binding energies of unknown nuclei, achieving root mean square deviations on the order of 0.2 MeV for a held-out test set, but the performance is notably worse for light-mass nuclei (A < 60) and for nuclei far from the training distribution, where the microstructure effects are stronger and training data are sparser.

## Method
The authors trained a Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM) to learn the residual δ(Z,A) between experimental binding energies from AME2016 (2408 nuclei from 16O to 270Ds) and the predictions of four theoretical mass models: the liquid-drop model (LDM), Duflo-Zucker (DZ), Weizsäcker-Skyrme (WS4), and finite-range droplet model (FRDM). Ten input features were used per nucleus, including mass number, proton number, neutron number, N/Z ratio, the LDM binding energy, pairing index, shell numbers for the last proton and neutron, and distances to the nearest magic numbers. The model was trained on 80% of the data with 10-fold cross-validation, and the refined binding energy was obtained as B_LightGBM = B_th + δ.

## Key numbers
- RMSD for LightGBM-refined LDM on the testing set (20% of 2408 nuclei): 0.234 ± 0.022 MeV, representing about a 90% reduction from the original LDM RMSD of 2.467 ± 0.092 MeV.
- RMSD for LightGBM-refined DZ on the testing set: 0.213 ± 0.018 MeV, about 65% smaller than the original DZ RMSD of 0.614 ± 0.028 MeV.
- RMSD for LightGBM-refined WS4 on the testing set: 0.170 ± 0.011 MeV, about 40% smaller than the original WS4 RMSD of 0.303 ± 0.011 MeV.
- RMSD for LightGBM-refined FRDM on the testing set: 0.222 ± 0.016 MeV, about 60% smaller than the original FRDM RMSD of 0.599 ± 0.034 MeV.
- For 66 newly measured nuclei from AME2020, the RMSD of LightGBM-refined models were 0.452 MeV (LDM), 0.320 MeV (DZ), 0.222 MeV (WS4), and 0.292 MeV (FRDM), compared to original RMSDs of 2.468, 0.821, 0.350, and 0.778 MeV respectively.
- Training set RMSD for LightGBM-refined models: 0.058 ± 0.011 MeV (LDM), 0.066 ± 0.010 MeV (DZ), 0.055 ± 0.011 MeV (WS4), 0.077 ± 0.013 MeV (FRDM).

## Context
This work extends the application of machine learning to nuclear mass predictions, following earlier efforts using Bayesian neural networks (BNN) by Utama (2015) and Niu (2018), neural networks by Zhang (2017), and decision tree algorithms by Carnini (2020). The LightGBM approach achieves lower RMSD on the testing set than these prior methods for all four mass models tested. The use of SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) for feature importance analysis provides interpretability that may guide future theoretical model development. The refined models enable predictions of one-neutron and two-neutron separation energies that agree with recent experimental data for neutron-rich isotopes of Ca, Ti, Pm, and Sm, and can be used to explore the nuclear landscape toward the neutron drip line.
