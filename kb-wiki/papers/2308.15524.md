# Interfacing Electron and Neutrino Quasielastic Scattering Cross Sections with the Spectral Function in GENIE

> Progress in neutrino-nucleus cross section models is being driven by the need for highly accurate predictions for the neutrino oscillation community. These sophisticated models are being developed within a microscopic description of the nucleus with the goal of encompassing all reaction modes relevant for the accelerator neutrino program. The disconnect between these microscopic models and the event generators that will be used in the next generation of experiments represents a critical obstacle that must be overcome in order to precisely measure the neutrino oscillation parameters. To this end we have developed a Fortran wrapper for lepton-nucleus quasielastic (QE) scattering within the GENIE event generator as a proof of principle, with the broader goal of creating an efficient pipeline for incorporating advanced theoretical models in event generators. As a demonstration of this interface, we have implemented the Spectral Function model into GENIE, offering a more complete description of the nuclear ground state, as well as the ability to provide quantifiable theoretical uncertainties. We validate this implementation and compare its predictions against data and against QE models already available in GENIE.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate a proof-of-principle Fortran wrapper that interfaces the Spectral Function model for quasielastic lepton-nucleus scattering with the GENIE event generator, but they acknowledge that the current implementation omits two-body currents and final-state interactions, so the predicted cross sections systematically undershoot inclusive data at the quasielastic peak and beyond.

## Method

The authors factorize the lepton-nucleus quasielastic cross section within the Impulse Approximation, expressing it as an incoherent sum over scattering from individual nucleons whose energy and momentum distributions are given by a Spectral Function. They implement a Fortran wrapper that computes the nucleon-level hadronic response tensor from an external theory code, which GENIE then uses to sample final-state lepton kinematics in the center-of-momentum frame via Monte Carlo integration. The Spectral Function for carbon-12 and oxygen-16 is taken from the Correlated Basis Function approach, with the mean-field piece fit to (e,e'p) data and the correlation piece computed using the Local Density Approximation.

## Key numbers

* The Impulse Approximation is expected to hold for momentum transfers |q| > 400 MeV.
* The correlation piece of the Spectral Function comprises approximately 20% of the single nucleon strength and dominates for momenta above the Fermi momentum k_f.
* Inclusive electron-carbon scattering predictions are validated against data at beam energies of 0.961 and 1.108 GeV at a scattering angle of 37.5 degrees.
* The axial form factor uses a dipole model with M_A = 1.0 GeV.
* The MINERvA low-energy data have an average neutrino energy of 3 GeV; the medium-energy data have an average neutrino energy of 6 GeV.
* The T2K beam energy peaks at around 600 MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to incorporate microscopic nuclear models into event generators, specifically addressing the limitations of the hadron tensor table framework in GENIE which had been used for models like SuSAv2 and Valencia 2p2h (Gonzalez-Rosa 2022, Gran 2013, Nieves 2011, Dolan 2021). The Spectral Function implementation improves upon the Relativistic Fermi Gas and Local Fermi Gas models already in GENIE by providing a correct relationship between nucleon momentum and removal energy, and it enables fully exclusive predictions unlike the inclusive SuSAv2 implementation (Dolan 2019). The interface is designed to be adaptable to other nuclear models and to allow parameter constraints from charged lepton scattering to be directly applied to neutrino scattering, enabling future work on a consistent theoretical framework encompassing all reaction mechanisms.
