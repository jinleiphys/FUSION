# Evidence of nonlocality due to a gradient term in the optical model

> We demonstrate that the presence of a velocity-dependent term in the phenomenological optical potential simulates a source of nonlocality. This is achieved by showing that, in the interior of the nucleus, the nonlocal wave functions are different from the corresponding local ones obtained in the absence of the velocity-dependent term in accordance with the Perey effect. It is also shown that the enhancement or suppression of the nonlocal wave function is energy as well as angular momentum dependent. The latter is in line with the results of previous works that introduced parity dependent terms in the conventional optical potential.

# Evidence of nonlocality due to a gradient term in the optical model

## Key claim
The presence of a velocity-dependent (gradient) term in the phenomenological optical potential simulates a source of nonlocality, as demonstrated by the fact that the nonlocal wave function differs from the corresponding local wave function in the nuclear interior in accordance with the Perey effect. However, the enhancement or suppression of the nonlocal wave function is both energy and angular momentum dependent, and the gradient term only accounts for part of the nonlocalities present in the optical model.

## Method
The authors introduced a Kisslinger-type velocity-dependent potential into the Schrodinger equation for nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering, expressed as an effective mass term with radial dependence. They solved for the nonlocal wave function using this velocity-dependent potential, then adjusted parameters of an equivalent local potential (without the gradient term) to fit the same N-12C elastic scattering angular distribution data and produce the same phase shifts. The Perey damping factor F(r)^2 was determined by comparing the maxima of the real parts of the nonlocal and local wave functions in the nuclear interior for incident energies from 12 to 20 MeV and orbital angular momenta l=0 through l=5.

## Key numbers
- The Perey factor F(r)^2 for s-wave (l=0) scattering ranges from 1.746 at 12 MeV to 0.928 at 20 MeV, showing enhancement at most energies but suppression at 20 MeV.
- For p-wave (l=1) scattering, F(r)^2 values are 0.838 (12 MeV), 0.779 (14 MeV), 0.882 (16 MeV), 0.636 (18 MeV), and 0.700 (20 MeV), all showing suppression.
- For l=2, F(r)^2 values are 0.876 (12 MeV), 0.835 (14 MeV), 1.171 (16 MeV), 1.227 (18 MeV), and 0.988 (20 MeV), showing a mixed pattern.
- For l=3, F(r)^2 values are 1.451 (12 MeV), 1.477 (14 MeV), 0.918 (16 MeV), 2.001 (18 MeV), and 1.522 (20 MeV), showing enhancement at most energies.
- For l=4 and l=5, F(r)^2 is approximately 1.00 for all energies, indicating no significant nonlocal effect.
- The trivially equivalent local potential yields maximum F(r)^2 values of 1.82 (12 MeV), 1.73 (14 MeV), 1.57 (16 MeV), 2.25 (18 MeV), and 2.00 (20 MeV), all showing enhancement.
- The local potential central strength V0 varies from 41.0 to 50.0 MeV across the energy range 12-20 MeV, showing the largest energy variation among parameters.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study (Jaghoub and Rawitscher, 2011) that introduced a velocity-dependent gradient term to fit N-12C elastic scattering data, particularly the large-angle backscattering minima associated with nonlocalities. The results are consistent with earlier work by Mackintosh and Cooper (1980s) that introduced parity-dependent terms in the optical potential for low-energy proton scattering from 16O, and with the Perey effect originally described by Perey and Buck (1960s) and Austern. The angular momentum dependence of the Perey factor suggests the inclusion of a parity-dependent term (-1)^l in the conventional optical potential as previously considered by Rawitscher (1969) and others. The work enables future tests of the velocity-dependent potential on other systems such as p-16O and n-40Ca to determine whether the surface-term form of the effective mass remains valid for heavier nuclei or should be replaced by a volume term proportional to nuclear density.
