# An extended equation of state for core-collapse simulations

> In stellar core-collapse events matter is heated and compressed to densities above nuclear matter saturation density. For progenitors stars with masses above about 25 solar masses, which eventually form a black hole, the temperatures and densities reached during the collapse are so high that a traditional description in terms of electrons, nuclei, and nucleons is no longer adequate. We present here an improved equation of state which contains in addition pions and hyperons. They become abundant in the high temperature and density regime. We study the different constraints on such an equation of state, coming from both hyperonic data and observations of neutron star properties. In order to test the zero-temperature versions, we perform numerical simulations of the collapse of a neutron star with such additional particles to a black hole. We discuss the influence of the additional particles on the thermodynamic properties within the hot versions of the equation of state and we show that in regimes relevant to core-collapse and black hole formation, the effects of pions and hyperons on pressure, internal energy and sound speed are not negligible.

## Key claim
The paper presents an extended equation of state (EOS) for core-collapse simulations that includes pions and hyperons in addition to the standard nucleonic degrees of freedom, but the authors hedge that the effects are significant only in the high-temperature (above about 20 MeV) and high-density (above nuclear saturation density) regime relevant for progenitors above about 25 solar masses that form black holes.

## Method
The authors extend the Lattimer and Swesty (1991) EOS by adding pions and muons as non-interacting ideal gases and hyperons via a finite-temperature extension of the nonrelativistic potential model of Balberg and Gal (1997). The hyperon-nucleon interaction parameters are adjusted to reproduce experimental hypernuclear data, and the low-density regime (below 10⁻⁶ fm⁻³) is treated with a nuclear statistical equilibrium approach following Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich (2009). Zero-temperature versions of the extended EOS are tested by performing numerical simulations of neutron star collapse to a black hole.

## Context
This work extends the commonly used Lattimer and Swesty (1991) EOS, which only includes nucleons, α-particles, and a single heavy nucleus, to incorporate hyperons and pions that become abundant at high temperatures and densities. It builds on earlier attempts to include hyperons in the Shen et al. (1998) EOS by Sumiyoshi et al. (2008), Ishizuka et al. (2008), and H. Shen et al. (2011), and on the work of Sagert et al. (2008) who found that a QCD phase transition could induce a second shock wave leading to successful explosion. The extended EOS enables more realistic simulations of core-collapse and black hole formation for massive progenitors, and allows comparison of results from different nuclear interaction models.
