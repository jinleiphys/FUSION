# Role of tensor forces in nuclei

> Recently, calculations of the ground states of the lightest nuclei have been performed using highly accurate data on realistic internucleon forces. In this paper, these results were used to describe the properties of nuclei with nucleon numbers $A>4$. Taking into account tensor forces leads to the conclusion that the four subsystems in the nucleus with zero nucleon orbital momenta are combined predominantly into the $^1S_0$ cluster. Subsystems with nonzero orbital momenta also combine into clusters with lower potential energy. This approach allows us to consistently explain the lifetime of the $^8$Be nucleus, the Hoyle state, the sequential mechanism of the reaction with the emission of $α$ particles, the shift of the reaction threshold, and more. The assumption of the existence of a one-dimensional effective interaction of nucleons in the nucleus leads to the conclusion that the nucleus contains a "power center" and, accordingly, nucleons have orbital angular momenta relative to this "power center." Our approach does not predict the presence of such a "power center" in the nucleus.

# Role of tensor forces in nuclei

## Key claim
The paper argues that taking tensor forces into account leads to a picture where the four nucleon subsystems with zero orbital angular momenta combine predominantly into a $^1S_0$ cluster, and that this cluster-based approach, without invoking a "power center", can explain the long lifetime of $^8$Be, the Hoyle state, and threshold shifts in cluster emission reactions. The author explicitly states that their approach does not predict the presence of a "power center" in the nucleus, contrasting with models that assume a one-dimensional effective interaction.

## Method
The author uses previously published precise calculations of ground-state properties of nuclei with $A \leq 4$ (using realistic NN potentials CD-Bonn and Argonne AV18, plus three-nucleon forces TM and Urbana IX, solved via Faddeev and Yakubovsky methods) and extends the analysis to nuclei with $A > 4$ by considering how tensor forces cause nucleons to form clusters with different orbital angular momenta. The paper then applies this cluster picture to explain the lifetime of $^8$Be, the Hoyle state in $^{12}$C, and threshold energies for $\alpha$-particle emission, using mass-defect estimates derived from experimental photon energies.

## Key numbers
- For $^4$He with the CD-Bonn potential: $S$-state probability = 88.54%, $S'$-state = 0.50%, $P$-state = 0.23%, $D$-state = 10.73%.
- For $^3$He with the CD-Bonn potential: $S$-state probability = 91.45%, $S'$-state = 1.53%, $P$-state = 0.05%, $D$-state = 6.98%.
- Binding energy of a three-nucleon system is approximately 2.6 MeV per nucleon; binding energy of a four-nucleon system is approximately 7 MeV per nucleon.
- Mass difference M($^8$Be) - 2M($\alpha$) = 0.092 MeV.
- Decay width of $^8$Be: $\Gamma = 5.57$ eV, corresponding to lifetime $\tau(^8\text{Be}) > 10^6 \tau_{\text{nucl}}$, where $\tau_{\text{nucl}} \sim 10^{-22}$ s.
- Hoyle state photon energy: $E_\gamma = 7.68 \pm 0.03$ MeV from experimental data; the author estimates the actual threshold at $E_\gamma = 7.65$ MeV.
- Estimated mass defect difference for D cluster relative to $\alpha$ particle: $\Delta M(D) = 0.19$ MeV.
- Estimated binding energy of $^8$Be: $E_{\text{bind}}(^8\text{Be}) = -0.098$ MeV.
- Estimated threshold for $^{16}$O disintegration into 4$\alpha$ particles: $E_{\text{thres}} = 15.01$ MeV (adding $3 \times 0.19$ MeV to the nominal 14.44 MeV).

## Context
This work extends precise few-body calculations (using CD-Bonn and AV18 potentials from Machleidt (2001) and Wiringa et al. (1995), with three-nucleon forces from Coon and Gloeckle (1981) and Pudliner et al. (1997)) to describe heavier nuclei via a cluster model that respects the Pauli exclusion principle. The approach contradicts standard shell-model and cluster-model treatments that assume a "power center" or effective one-dimensional potentials, and it specifically addresses the long-standing paradox of the $^8$Be lifetime and the Hoyle state in $^{12}$C. The paper enables a consistent explanation of sequential $\alpha$-particle emission and threshold shifts in reactions, and suggests that similar effects should appear in other nuclei and reactions.
