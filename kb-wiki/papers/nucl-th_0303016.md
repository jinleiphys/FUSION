# Light-front gauge propagator reexamined

> Gauge fields are special in the sense that they are invariant under gauge transformations and \QTR{em}{``ipso facto''} they lead to problems when we try quantizing them straightforwardly. To circumvent this problem we need to specify a gauge condition to fix the gauge so that the fields that are connected by gauge invariance are not overcounted in the process of quantization. The usual way we do this in the light-front is through the introduction of a Lagrange multiplier, $(n\cdot A)^{2}$, where $n_μ$ is the external light-like vector, i.e., $n^{2}=0$, and $A_μ$ is the vector potential. This leads to the usual light-front propagator with all the ensuing characteristics such as the prominent $(k\cdot n)^{-1}$ pole which has been the subject of much research. However, it has been for long recognized that this procedure is incomplete in that there remains a residual gauge freedom still to be fixed by some ``ad hoc'' prescription, and this is normally worked out to remedy some unwieldy aspect that emerges along the way. In this work we propose a new Lagrange multiplier for the light-front gauge that leads to the correctly defined propagator with no residual gauge freedom left. This is accomplished via $(n\cdot A)(\partial \cdot A)$ term in the Lagrangian density. This leads to a well-defined and exact though Lorentz non invariant propagator.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard light-front gauge condition \(n\cdot A = 0\) is necessary but not sufficient to fully fix the gauge; the correct and complete gauge fixing requires the additional condition \(\partial \cdot A = 0\), implemented via a new Lagrange multiplier term \((n\cdot A)(\partial \cdot A)\) in the Lagrangian density, which yields a propagator with no residual gauge freedom.

## Method

The authors construct the Abelian gauge field Lagrangian density with a gauge-fixing term proportional to \((n\cdot A)(\partial \cdot A)\) and derive the corresponding propagator by inverting the differential operator in momentum space using a general tensor decomposition. They then take the light-front limit \(n^2 = 0\) and the limit \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\) to obtain the final propagator. The result is compared with the standard two-term propagator obtained from the usual \((n\cdot A)^2\) gauge-fixing term and with the Lorentz gauge propagator.

## Key numbers

* The standard light-front propagator (from \(n\cdot A = 0\) alone) is \(G^{\mu\nu}(k) = -\frac{1}{k^2}\left[ g^{\mu\nu} - \frac{k^\mu n^\nu + n^\mu k^\nu}{k\cdot n} \right]\) (Eq. 24).
* The new propagator (from the combined condition \(n\cdot A = \partial \cdot A = 0\)) is \(G^{\mu\nu}(k) = -\frac{1}{k^2}\left\{ g^{\mu\nu} - \frac{k^\mu n^\nu + n^\mu k^\nu}{k\cdot n} + \frac{n^\mu n^\nu}{(k\cdot n)^2} k^2 \right\}\) (Eq. 27), which includes the previously omitted contact term proportional to \(k^2 n^\mu n^\nu / (k\cdot n)^2\).
* In the derivation, the limit \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\) is taken, with \(n^2 = 0\) for the light-front.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of light-front gauge fixing first introduced by Dirac (1949) and later developed by Kogut and others, where the standard propagator (Eq. 24) was derived from the condition \(n\cdot A = 0\). The authors address the long-recognized problem of residual gauge freedom in that approach, which had previously been handled by ad hoc prescriptions. The new propagator (Eq. 27) includes the contact term that had been consistently dropped in earlier calculations (e.g., by Hari Dass) on the grounds that it had no physical significance, but which more recent work by Sales and others showed to carry relevant information. The complete gauge fixing enables a consistent two-component formalism in the light-front and resolves issues related to zero modes and residual gauge freedom.
