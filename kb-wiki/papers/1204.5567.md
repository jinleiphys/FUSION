# Non-congruence of liquid-gas phase transition of asymmetric nuclear matter

> We first explore the liquid-gas mixed phase in a bulk calculation, where two phases coexist without the geometrical structures. In the case of symmetric nuclear matter, the system behaves congruently, and the Maxwell construction becomes relevant. For asymmetric nuclear matter, on the other hand, the phase equilibrium is no more attained by the Maxwell construction since the liquid and gas phases are non-congruent; the particle fractions become completely different with each other. One of the origins of such non-congruence is attributed to the large symmetry energy. Subsequently we explore the charge-neutral nuclear matter with electrons by fully applying the Gibbs conditions to figure out the geometrical (pasta) structures in the liquid-gas mixed phase. We emphasize the effects of the surface tension and the Coulomb interaction on the pasta structures. We also discuss the thermal effects on the pasta structures.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for asymmetric nuclear matter, the liquid-gas phase transition is non-congruent (the coexisting phases have different proton fractions), and this non-congruence arises primarily from the large symmetry energy; the Maxwell construction is therefore inapplicable for such systems.

## Method

The authors performed bulk calculations using a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with sigma, omega, and rho meson exchange, solving the Gibbs conditions (equality of temperature, pressure, and chemical potentials) for coexisting liquid and gas phases. They then extended the analysis to charge-neutral nuclear matter with electrons, incorporating the Coulomb interaction and surface tension to determine the geometrical pasta structures (droplet, rod, slab, tube, bubble) under the local-density approximation.

## Key numbers

- At T=10 MeV, the binodal curves in the Y_p-P_B plane show that for symmetric nuclear matter (Y_p=0.5) the phase transition is congruent, while for Y_p=0.3 the transition is non-congruent with different proton fractions in the two phases.
- Below 4 MeV, the left part of the binodal curve touches Y_p=0, meaning the gas phase becomes pure neutron matter or vacant.
- The binodal region where pasta structures appear is inside the binodal region, and near the higher-density boundary the pasta structures are suppressed by Coulomb and surface energies.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of liquid-gas phase transitions in nuclear matter by Maruyama (2005) and Maruyama (review), and builds on the pasta structure predictions of Ravenhall (1983) and Hashimoto (1984). The results show that for asymmetric nuclear matter, the Maxwell construction used in simpler treatments (e.g., for boiling water) is invalid, and the Gibbs conditions must be fully applied. The findings enable more realistic equations of state for supernova collapse and compact star crusts, where non-congruent phase transitions and pasta structures are expected.
