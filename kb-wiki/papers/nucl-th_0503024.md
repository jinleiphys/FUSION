# Transfer to the continuum and Breakup reactions

> Reaction theory is an essential ingredient when performing studies of nuclei far from stability. One approach for the calculation of breakup reactions of exotic nuclei into two fragments is to consider inelastic excitations into the single particle continuum of the projectile. Alternatively one can also consider the transfer to the continuum of a system composed of the light fragment and the target. In this work we make a comparative study of the two approaches, underline the different inputs, and identify the advantages and disadvantages of each approach. Our test cases consist of the breakup of $^{11}$Be on a proton target at intermediate energies, and the breakup of $^8$B on $^{58}$Ni at energies around the Coulomb barrier. We find that, in practice the results obtained in both schemes are in semiquantitative agreement. We suggest a simple condition that can select between the two approaches.

# Transfer to the continuum and Breakup reactions

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that breakup reactions of weakly-bound nuclei can be described either as inelastic excitations into the projectile continuum (CDCC) or as transfer to the target continuum (TR*), and that these two approaches produce semiquantitative agreement in practice, but only when the same physical ingredients are used; the authors suggest a simple condition to select between the two approaches based on the system and energy regime.

## Method

The authors performed comparative calculations using the Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (CDCC) method, which treats breakup as inelastic excitation of the projectile into its single-particle continuum, and a prior-form transfer-to-continuum (TR*) method, which expands the final three-body wavefunction in terms of fragment-target continuum states. Both methods were applied to the same three-body model Hamiltonian, using identical fragment-target interactions and projectile binding potentials wherever possible, for two test cases: the breakup of ¹¹Be on a proton target at 38.5 MeV/u, and the breakup of ⁸B on ⁵⁸Ni at 25.6 MeV. All calculations were performed with the computer code FRESCO.

## Key numbers

- Total integrated cross section for ¹¹Be breakup on protons at 38.5 MeV/u: σ_BU = 125 mb (direct breakup method) and σ_TR = 140 mb (transfer to continuum method)
- In the BU calculation for ¹¹Be+p, most breakup strength lies below excitation energy ε_x(¹¹Be) ≈ 5 MeV; in the TR* calculation, strength concentrates around ε_x(d) ≈ 20 MeV
- For ¹¹Be+p, the total angular momentum distribution peaks at J = 5 in both methods, corresponding to impact parameter b ≈ 4 fm
- For ¹¹Be+p, the ¹⁰Be fragment energy distribution in the overall center-of-mass peaks around 3 MeV and extends from zero to about 6 MeV in both methods
- The forward-angle cross section for ¹¹Be+p in the TR* calculation is an order of magnitude larger than the BU calculation, while the backward-angle cross section is an order of magnitude lower
- For ⁸B+⁵⁸Ni at 25.6 MeV, the BU calculation required L_max = 1000 for distorted waves and R_max = 500 fm; the TR* calculation required L_max = 120 and R_bin = 120 fm
- The converged BU calculation for ¹¹Be+p used 48 MB RAM and approximately 10 minutes on a 2.4 GHz PC; the TR* calculation used 1.4 GB RAM and approximately 30 minutes on the same machine
- For ⁸B+⁵⁸Ni, the converged TR* calculation required 400 MB RAM and approximately 8 hours

## Context

This work extends the comparison of reaction models for breakup of exotic nuclei, following earlier studies comparing eikonal, adiabatic, and local momentum approximations by Bonaccorso (2001), Shyam (2001), and Zadmajid (2004). The CDCC method was developed by Yahiro (1982, 1986) and Austern (1987), while the semiclassical transfer-to-continuum approach was developed by Brink and Bonaccorso (1988, 1991, 1992, 2001). The paper enables a systematic understanding of when each approach is computationally feasible and physically appropriate, particularly for reactions with radioactive beams at low and medium energies where full quantum mechanical calculations are possible.
