# Focusing of high-energy particles in the electrostatic field of a homogeneously charged sphere and the effective momentum approximation

> The impact of the strongly attractive electromagnetic field of heavy nuclei on electrons in quasi-elastic (e,e') scattering is often accounted for by the effective momentum approximation. This method is a plane wave Born approximation which takes the twofold effect of the attractive nucleus on initial and final state electrons into account, namely the modification of the electron momentum in the vicinity of the nucleus, and the focusing of electrons towards the nuclear region leading to an enhancement of the corresponding wave function amplitudes. The focusing effect due to the attractive Coulomb field of a homogeneously charged sphere on a classical ensemble of charged particles incident on the field is calculated in the highly relativistic limit and compared to results obtained from exact solutions of the Dirac equation. The result is relevant for the theoretical foundation of the effective momentum approximation and describes the high energy behavior of the amplitude of continuum Dirac waves in the potential of a homogeneously charged sphere. Our findings indicate that the effective momentum approximation is a useful approximation for the calculation of Coulomb corrections in (e,e') scattering off heavy nuclei for sufficiently high electron energies and momentum transfer.

## Key claim

The effective momentum approximation (EMA) is a useful approximation for Coulomb corrections in quasi-elastic (e,e') scattering off heavy nuclei, but only for sufficiently high electron energies and momentum transfer; the correct application requires using average values of the focusing factor and effective momenta over the nuclear volume, not values valid only at the nuclear center.

## Method

The authors derived the classical focusing factor for an ensemble of highly relativistic particles incident on a homogeneously charged sphere by calculating the transverse acceleration and trajectory modification in three spatial regions (outside, inside, and downstream of the sphere) using a straight-line approximation. They compared this classical focusing factor to the probability density obtained from exact solutions of the Dirac equation for electrons with definite helicity incident on the same potential, evaluating the ratio of the two descriptions as a function of energy, impact parameter, and longitudinal position.

## Key numbers

- For a homogeneously charged sphere, the effective momentum is given by \(k^{eff}_{i,f} = (k_{i,f} - \frac{4}{5} V(0)) \hat{k}_{i,f}\), where \(V(0) = -\frac{3 \alpha Z}{2 R}\) is the potential energy at the nuclear center.
- For electron scattering off \(^{208}\)Pb with initial momentum \(|k_i| = 485\) MeV/c and \(\omega = 100\) MeV, \(V(0) = -25\) MeV and the effective potential is \(4V(0)/5 = -20\) MeV, giving \(k_i^{eff} = (485+20)\) MeV and \(k_f^{eff} = (385+20)\) MeV.
- The focusing factors enhance the cross section by a factor of \((k_i^{eff}/k_i)^2 (k_f^{eff}/k_f)^2 = 1.2\) for this example.
- The average focusing factor inside the sphere is \(\bar{f}^{II} = \frac{4}{5} f^{II}(0)\), where \(f^{II}(0) = 1 + \frac{3 \alpha Z}{E R}\) is the focusing at the nuclear center.
- For the EMA to hold, electron energies above 200 MeV and \(Q^2 > (200\ \text{MeV})^2\) (corresponding to a length scale of 1 fm) should be required for \(^{208}\)Pb.

## Context

This work provides a classical derivation of the effective momentum approximation, extending earlier treatments by Knoll (1974), Lenz and Rosenfelder (1971), and others who derived focusing effects from high-energy partial wave expansions. The results are relevant for the theoretical foundation of the EMA, which is widely used in experimental data analysis for quasi-elastic electron scattering, and are timely given the approved TJNAF proposal for measurements on \(^4\)He, \(^{12}\)C, \(^{56}\)Fe, and \(^{208}\)Pb in the momentum transfer range 0.55 to 1.0 GeV/c. The paper clarifies that correct EMA calculations must use volume-averaged effective values rather than central values, correcting a common misinterpretation in the literature discussed by Traini (2001).
