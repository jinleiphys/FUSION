# Quantal Extension of Mean-Field Dynamics

> A method is presented for numerical implementation of the extended TDHF theory in which two-body correlations beyond the mean-field approximation are incorporated in the form of a quantal collision term. The method is tested in a model problem in which the exact solution can be obtained numerically. Whereas the usual TDHF fails to reproduce the long time evolution, a very good agreement is found between the extended TDHF and the exact solution.

## Key claim

The paper presents a numerical method for implementing extended Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (ETDHF) theory that incorporates two-body correlations via a quantal collision term, and demonstrates that this method reproduces the exact one-body dynamics for long times in a model system where standard TDHF fails; the key qualifier is that the method is tested only on a simple model of two distinguishable particles in a one-dimensional anharmonic potential, not on a realistic nuclear system.

## Method

The authors coarse-grain the time evolution into two steps: first, the Hartree-Fock states are evolved via the usual TDHF equation over a macroscopic time interval Δt; second, a perturbative correction is applied to the one-body density operator to account for the accumulated effect of two-body collisions. At each time step, an instantaneous single-particle basis is constructed using an imaginary-time method to include occupied states and a limited set of unoccupied states with energies below a truncation energy ε_Max, and the collision term is evaluated using a master equation for the diagonal occupation numbers with gain and loss rates computed from the antisymmetrized two-body interaction matrix elements.

## Context

This work extends the TDHF approach of Bonche et al. (1970s) and the ETDHF framework developed by Abe and others, which had previously only been applied under strong approximations due to numerical complexity. The method improves upon earlier semi-classical approaches such as the BUU (Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck) model by treating the collision term in a fully quantal picture, and addresses the criticism by Tohayama that non-diagonal elements of the density matrix are important. The authors state that in forthcoming work they will apply the numerical procedure to realistic nuclei, with the goal of improving predictions for damping of giant resonances and thermalization of nuclei.
