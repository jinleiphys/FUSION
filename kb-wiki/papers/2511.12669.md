# Thermal Quarkyonic Matter and Its Implications for Neutron Star Structure

> The structure and basic properties of dense nuclear matter still remain one of the open problems of Physics. In particular, the composition of the matter that composes neutron stars is under theoretical and experimental investigation. Among the theories that have been proposed, apart from the classical one where the composition is dominated by hadrons, the existence or coexistence of deconfined quark matter is a dominant guess. An approach towards this solution is the phenomenological view according to which the existence of quarkyonic matter plays a dominant role in the construction of the equation of state (EOS). According to it the structure of the EOS is based on the existence of the quarkyonic particle which is a hybrid state of a particle that combines properties of hadronic and quark matter with a corresponding representation in momentum space. In this paper we propose a phenomenological model for hot quarkyonic matter, borrowed from corresponding applications in hadronic models, where the interaction in the quarkyonic matter depends not only on the position but also on the momentum of the quarkyonic particles. This consideration, as we demonstrate, can have a remarkable consequence on the shape of the EOS and thus on the properties of neutron stars, especially in those for which the effect of temperature is significant, offering a sufficiently flexible model. Comparison with recent observational data can place constraints on the parameterization of the particular model and help improve its reliability.

## Key claim
The paper proposes a phenomenological model for hot quarkyonic matter where the interaction between baryons depends on both position and momentum, and claims that temperature has a dramatic effect on the equation of state (EOS) of quarkyonic matter, especially at high densities, in contrast to its modest effect on pure neutron matter, but this result is conditional on the specific choice of transition density and model parametrization.

## Method
The authors extend a cold quarkyonic matter model to finite temperature by incorporating momentum-dependent interactions for neutrons (treated via a parametrized single-particle potential with finite-range terms) while treating up and down quarks as non-interacting Fermi gases. They impose charge neutrality and chemical equilibrium between neutrons and quarks, compute the EOS for various temperatures (up to 10 MeV) and transition densities (0.2 and 0.3 fm⁻³), then solve the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain neutron star mass, radius, and tidal deformability.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear saturation density: \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm⁻³
- Transition densities considered: \(n_{\rm tr} = 0.2\) fm⁻³ and \(0.3\) fm⁻³
- Temperatures studied: \(T = 5\) MeV and \(10\) MeV (plus cold case)
- Quark masses set as \(m_i = m_N / N_c\) with \(N_c = 3\)
- Quarkyonic matter introduced at baryon density around \(n_B = 0.2\)–\(0.3\) fm⁻³
- Width of momentum shell \(\Delta\) computed using \(\Lambda_{Qyc} = 200\) MeV and \(\kappa = 0.3\)
- Tidal deformability \(\Lambda\) compared to constraints from GW170817 for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star

## Context
This work extends the cold quarkyonic matter framework of McLerran and collaborators (2019–2024) and the authors' own prior cold model (Folias 2025) to finite temperature, which had only been addressed previously by Sen (2021) using a different methodology. The results enable future studies of proto-neutron stars, neutron star mergers, cooling evolution, and heavy-ion collisions, all of which require thermal equations of state. The paper notes that the model's reliability at high temperatures remains uncertain and calls for a more refined approach.
