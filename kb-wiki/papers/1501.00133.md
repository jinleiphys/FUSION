# Highlights from COMPASS in hadron spectroscopy

> Since Quantum Choromdynamics allows for gluon self-coupling, quarks and gluons cannot be observed as free particles, but only their bound states, the hadrons. This so-called confinement phenomenon is responsible for $98\%$ of the mass in the visible universe. The measurement of the hadron excitation spectra therefore gives valuable input for theory and phenomenology to quantitatively understand this phenomenon. One simple model to describe hadrons is the Constituent Quark Model (CQM), which knows two types of hadrons: mesons, consisting of a quark and an antiquark, and baryons, which are made out of three quarks. More advanced models, which are inspired by QCD as well as calculations within Lattice QCD predict the existence of other types of hadrons, which may be e.g. described solely by gluonic excitations (glueballs) or mixed quark and gluon excitations (hybrids). In order to search for such states, the COMPASS experiment at the Super Proton Synchrotron at CERN has collected large data sets, which allow to study the light-quark meson and baryon spectra in unmatched precision. The overview shown here focuses on the light meson sector, presenting a detailed Partial-Wave Analysis of the processes: $π^- p \to π^-π^+π^- p$ and $π^-p\to π^-π^0π^0p$. A new state, the $a_1(1420)$ with $J^{PC}=1^{++}$ is observed. Its Breit-Wigner parameters are found to be in the ranges: $m = 1412-1422\,\mathrm{MeV}/c^2$ and $Γ= 130-150\,\mathrm{MeV}/c^2$. In the same analysis, a signal in a wave with $J^{PC}=1^{-+}$ is observed. A resonant origin of this signal would not be explicable within the CQM. In addition to this possibility of an exotic state, a possible non resonant origin of this signal is discussed.

## Key claim

The COMPASS experiment observes a new state, the a1(1420) with JPC = 1++, whose Breit-Wigner parameters are found in the ranges m = 1412-1422 MeV/c2 and Gamma = 130-150 MeV/c2, and also observes a signal in a wave with exotic quantum numbers JPC = 1-+ that would not be explicable within the Constituent Quark Model if resonant, though a non-resonant origin (the Deck effect) is discussed as an alternative.

## Method

A detailed Partial-Wave Analysis (PWA) was performed on diffractively produced three-pion final states from two processes: pi- p -> pi- pi+ pi- p and pi- p -> pi- pi0 pi0 p, using the isobar model with a wave set of 87 waves up to spin 6. The analyses of both channels were performed independently using different software packages, relying on different parts of the spectrometer, allowing for an effective cross-check of results. The production amplitudes T(m_X) were extracted from the data by fitting the intensity in bins of the invariant three-pion mass m_3pi, with no assumptions regarding three-pion resonances.

## Key numbers

* 3.5 million events recorded in the neutral channel (pi- pi0 pi0) and 50 million events in the charged channel (pi- pi+ pi-), with the charged data set constituting the world's largest 3pi data set for this process.
* The a1(1420) resonance has Breit-Wigner mass in the range m = 1412-1422 MeV/c2 and width in the range Gamma = 130-150 MeV/c2.
* The 1++0+ rho(770) pi S wave accounts for 33% of the intensity in the charged channel, the 2++1+ rho(770) pi D wave takes 8%, and the 2-+0+ f2(1270) pi S wave takes 7%.
* The 4++1+ rho(770) pi G wave takes only about 0.76% intensity but clearly shows the a4(2040) resonance in both channels.

## Context

This work extends the search for exotic hadrons beyond the Constituent Quark Model (CQM), which only describes mesons (quark-antiquark) and baryons (three quarks), while QCD-inspired models and Lattice QCD calculations predict glueballs and hybrids. The observation of the a1(1420) is notable because no model or lattice QCD predicted a resonance in this mass region, and its decay into f0(980) pi with unusually small relative intensity, combined with the f0(980) coupling to KK states and the mass being slightly above the KK* threshold, suggests a possible dynamic nature involving KK* loop diagrams as discussed by Basdevant and Berger (1977). The spin exotic signal in the 1-+ wave enables further studies to disentangle resonant and non-resonant contributions using t' binning, with the Deck effect explaining the signal well at low four-momentum transfer while an excess remains at high t'.
