# Dependence of elastic hadron collisions on impact parameter

> Elastic proton-proton collisions represent probably the greatest ensemble of available measured data, the analysis of which may provide large amount of new physical results concerning fundamental particles. It is, however, necessary to analyze first some conclusions concerning pp collisions and their interpretations differing fundamentally from our common macroscopic experience. It has been argued, e.g., that elastic hadron collisions have been more central than inelastic ones, even if any explanation of the existence of so different process, i.e., elastic and inelastic (with hundreds of secondary particles) collisions, under the same conditions has not been given until now. The given conclusion has been based on a greater number of simplifying mathematical assumptions (done already in earlier calculations), without their influence on physical interpretation being analyzed and entitled; the corresponding influence has started to be studied in the approach based on eikonal model. The possibility of peripheral interpretation of elastic collisions will be demonstrated and corresponding results summarized. The arguments will be given why no preference may be given to the mentioned centrality against the standard peripheral behaviour. The corresponding discussion of contemporary description of elastic hadronic collision in dependence on impact parameter will be summarized and the justification of some important assumptions will be considered.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the widely accepted conclusion that elastic hadron collisions are more central than inelastic ones is not uniquely determined by data, but rather results from simplifying mathematical assumptions in earlier models; the authors demonstrate that a peripheral interpretation of elastic collisions is equally viable when the t-dependence of the hadronic phase is properly accounted for, and that no preference can be given to centrality over peripheral behavior.

## Method

The authors apply the eikonal model formalism, using a more general Coulomb-hadronic interference formula derived by Kundrat and Lokajicek (1994) that avoids the restrictive assumptions of the West and Yennie (1968) approach (such as t-independent rho and diffractive slope). They analyze elastic proton-proton differential cross section data at 53 GeV center-of-mass energy, extracting the hadronic amplitude and its impact-parameter profile functions via Fourier-Bessel transforms, with careful separation of physical and unphysical t-regions as required at finite energies following Adachi and Kotani (1965-1968) and Islam (1968-1976).

## Key numbers

* At 53 GeV center-of-mass energy, the elastic differential cross section data are analyzed using the eikonal model approach with the general formula of Kundrat and Lokajicek (1994) that is valid for any s and t value, in contrast to the West-Yennie formula which is limited to small |t|.
* The minimal kinematically allowed momentum transfer squared is given by t_min = -s + 4m^2, where m is the nucleon mass.
* The fine structure constant alpha = 1/137.036 appears in the Coulomb amplitude and relative phase calculations.
* The Euler constant gamma = 0.577215 appears in the simplified West-Yennie phase formula for small t.

## Context

This work extends the critique of the West and Yennie (1968) approach to elastic hadron scattering, which had been the standard method for determining total cross sections since the ISR era but neglected impact-parameter dependence entirely. It directly challenges the earlier conclusion of Miettinen (1974) that elastic collisions are more central than inelastic ones, which was based on models with restrictive assumptions about the hadronic phase. The paper builds on the more general eikonal model formulation of Kundrat and Lokajicek (1994) and the finite-energy unitarity considerations of Adachi and Kotani (1965-1968) and Islam (1968-1976), enabling future analyses that properly account for impact-parameter dependence in elastic scattering data interpretation.
