# Velocity fluctuations of fission fragments

> We propose event by event velocity fluctuations of nuclear fission fragments as an additional interesting observable that gives access to the nuclear temperature in an independent way from spectral measurements and relates the diffusion and friction coefficients for the relative fragment coordinate in Kramer-like models (in which some aspects of fission can be understood as the diffusion of a collective variable through a potential barrier). We point out that neutron emission by the heavy fragments can be treated in effective theory if corrections to the velocity distribution are needed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that event-by-event velocity fluctuations of fission fragments, as opposed to the more commonly studied kinetic energy fluctuations, provide a direct observable for extracting the nuclear temperature of the fissioning nucleus and for relating the diffusion and friction coefficients in Kramers-like models, because the velocity is largely unaffected by subsequent neutron emission from the fragments.

## Method

The authors derive a fluctuation-dissipation relation from a three-dimensional Langevin equation for the relative coordinate between the two fission fragments, obtaining an expression for the velocity variance in terms of the diffusion tensor, friction tensor, and reduced mass. They then apply Einstein's relation to connect this variance to the nuclear temperature, and estimate the required experimental precision for measuring the variance. They also develop a heavy-fragment effective field theory argument, analogous to Heavy Quark Effective Theory, to show that neutron emission from the fragments changes the fragment momentum but leaves the fragment velocity unchanged at leading order in 1/M.

## Key numbers

* For asymmetric fission of 252Cf with temperature ~1.4 MeV and reduced mass ~55 GeV, the predicted velocity variance is sigma_v^2 ~ 2.5 x 10^-5.
* To extract this variance experimentally, the required precision in fragment kinetic energy measurement is Delta T_KE / T_KE ~ (0.7 - 1.4) x 10^-2, i.e., about 0.5% precision.
* From a GANIL experiment using proton-induced fission of 208Pb, the fitted reduced dissipation coefficient is gamma* = 4.5 x 10^21 s^-1 ~ 2.96 MeV.
* Using the estimated sigma_v^2 and gamma*, the derived momentum-space diffusion coefficient is D ~ 225 MeV^3.
* Average kinetic energies for thermal-neutron induced fission are: 170.5 +/- 0.5 MeV for 235U, 177.9 +/- 0.5 MeV for 239Pu, and 184.1 +/- 1.3 MeV for spontaneous fission of 252Cf.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing study of fission fluctuations, which previously focused on kinetic energy fluctuations (Grossmann 1988) and velocity fluctuations as a function of mass number (Straede), by proposing event-by-event velocity fluctuations as a new observable. The authors connect their approach to Kramers' original Langevin treatment of fission (Kramers 1940) and to the Fluctuation-Dissipation Theorem. The work enables an independent method for measuring nuclear temperature that does not rely on spectral measurements of gamma rays or neutrons, and it provides a way to extract the diffusion coefficient from velocity fluctuation data combined with separate measurements of the dissipation coefficient from hydrodynamic fits (such as those from GANIL experiments by Sanchez).
