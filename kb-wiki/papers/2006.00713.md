# Network structure of thermonuclear reactions in nuclear landscape

> Nucleosynthesis is a complex process in astro-nuclear evolution. In this work, we construct a directed multi-layer nuclear reaction network using the substrate-product method from a thermonuclear reaction database, JINA REACLIB. The network contains four layers, namely $n$-, $p$-, $h$- and $r$, corresponding to the reaction types involved in neutrons, protons, $^4$He and the remainder, respectively. The degree values (i.e. numbers of reactions) for three layers of n-, p- and h- have a significant correlation with one another, and their topological structures exhibit a similar regularity. However, the $r$-layer has a more complex topological structure than others and has less correlation with the other three layers. A software package named `mfinder' is employed to analyze the motif structure of the nuclear reaction network. We thus identify the most frequent reaction patterns of interconnections occurring among different nuclides. This work provides a novel approach to study the nuclear reaction network prevailing in the astrophysical context.

# Network structure of thermonuclear reactions in nuclear landscape

## Key claim

The authors construct a directed multi-layer nuclear reaction network from the JINA REACLIB database and find that the $n$-, $p$-, and $h$-layers (neutron, proton, and $^4$He reactions) have strong degree-degree correlations with coefficients close to 1, while the $r$-layer (remainder reactions) shows weaker correlations with coefficients only about 0.6 and exhibits more complex topological structure. The claim is qualified by the observation that these regularities hold across the nuclear landscape but fade away or change when the proton number exceeds the magic number 82.

## Method

The authors applied the substrate-product method to map 8048 nuclides and 82851 nuclear reactions from the JINA REACLIB database into a directed un-weighted multi-layer network with four layers ($n$, $p$, $h$, $r$) classified by the type of particle involved in each reaction. They calculated degree values for each nucleus in each layer and computed degree-degree correlation coefficients between layer pairs using the Pearson correlation formula. The software package `mfinder` was employed to identify motif structures by comparing motif frequencies in the real network against those in randomized networks with the same size, number of edges, and degree values.

## Key numbers

- 8048 nuclides and 82851 nuclear reactions from the JINA REACLIB database form the network.
- Degree correlation coefficients $R_{np}$, $R_{nh}$, and $R_{ph}$ are very close to 1, indicating strong linear correlation among $n$-, $p$-, and $h$-layers.
- Degree correlation coefficients $R_{nr}$, $R_{pr}$, and $R_{hr}$ are only about 0.6, indicating weaker correlation between the $r$-layer and the other three layers.
- Five types of three-node motifs have the highest frequencies, with motif id238 having frequency 3.87 $\times$ 10$^4$.
- Seven types of four-node motifs have the highest frequencies, with motif id13278 having frequency 11.27 $\times$ 10$^4$.
- Over 80% of stable nuclides satisfy the condition $(K_{i,\mathrm{in}}^{[r]}-K_{i,\mathrm{out}}^{[r]}=2) \cap (K_{i,\mathrm{in}}-K_{i,\mathrm{out}}=0)$ from the authors' prior work.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous complex network construction for nuclear reactions (Liu et al. 2016) by analyzing degree correlations between layers and identifying motif structures, moving beyond the earlier finding of bimodal degree distributions. The approach contrasts with traditional nucleosynthesis calculations that solve time-dependent differential equations for abundance evolution, as done in standard stellar evolution codes. The identification of repetitive motif patterns in the nuclear reaction network provides a novel topological framework for understanding thermonuclear reaction pathways, and the authors suggest future work could apply this method to study the $r$-process and other nuclear reaction processes involving all nuclides.
