# Hadron resonance gas is not a good model for hadronic matter in a strong magnetic field

> We study the effect of magnetic field on particle yields and charge fluctuations in hadron resonance gas. We argue that the big changes in the proton yield and baryon number susceptibility are due to ill-defined description of higher-spin states, and that because of detailed balance, neutral resonances must be affected by the field too.

## Key claim

The strong dependence of proton yields and baryon number susceptibility on magnetic field in the hadron resonance gas model is an artefact of the model's ill-defined description of higher-spin states; the model contradicts its own premises for magnetic fields above approximately 0.0356 GeV², where the concept of a resonance becomes questionable.

## Method

The authors calculated particle yields and charge fluctuations in the hadron resonance gas model with a constant external magnetic field, using Landau quantisation for charged particles with dispersion relations that include spin-dependent contributions. They compared the HRG model results for net-baryon number susceptibility to lattice QCD calculations from Ding (2025). They also evaluated the effect of detailed balance on neutral resonances by introducing an effective chemical potential for pions and by constructing a spectral function for the ρ⁰ meson using a π–ρ interaction Lagrangian with the Schwinger propagator.

## Key numbers

- The HRG model systematically underestimates the lattice QCD result for χ_BB at eB ≤ 0.22 GeV² at both T = 145 MeV and T = 155 MeV
- The contribution of s ≥ 3/2 resonances to χ_BB ranges from 75% at vanishing eB to 95% at eB = 0.3 GeV²
- The requirement for Δ⁺⁺ decay (E_Δ⁺⁺ ≥ E_p + E_π⁺) is no longer valid once eB ≳ 0.0356 GeV²
- The ρ⁰ meson density decreases with increasing magnetic field, with the effect being stronger at lower temperatures (T = 155 MeV shown)
- The coupling constant g = 20.72 GeV⁻² was fixed from the vacuum decay width Γ_ρ→ππ = 150 MeV

## Context

This work extends the HRG model generalised to finite magnetic fields by Endrodi (2013) and compares to lattice QCD results from Ding (2025). The authors argue that the standard HRG treatment fails for strong magnetic fields because it neglects hadron compositeness and detailed balance, which affects even neutral resonances. This enables future development of a consistent model of hadronic interactions using π–π scattering data for a unitary, model-independent treatment, as noted by the authors with reference to work in progress by Karmakar and Lo.
