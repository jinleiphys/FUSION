# Nuclear response in a finite-temperature relativistic framework

> A thermal extension of the relativistic nuclear field theory is formulated for the nuclear response. The Bethe-Salpeter equation (BSE) with the time-dependent kernel for the particle-hole response is treated within the Matsubara Green's function formalism. We show that, with the help of a temperature-dependent projection operator on the subspace of the imaginary time (time blocking), it is possible to reduce the BSE for the nuclear response function to a single frequency variable equation also at finite temperature. The approach is implemented self-consistently in the framework of quantum hadrodynamics based on the meson-nucleon Lagrangian. The method is applied to the monopole, dipole and quadrupole response of $^{48}$Ca and to the dipole response of the tin isotopes $^{100,120,132}$Sn, in particular, to a study of the evolution of nuclear collective oscillations with temperature. The article is dedicated to the memory of Pier Francesco Bortignon and devoted to the developments related to his pioneering ideas.

## Key claim

The paper formulates a thermal extension of relativistic nuclear field theory (RNFT) that reduces the Bethe-Salpeter equation for the nuclear response function to a single frequency variable equation at finite temperature, using a temperature-dependent projection operator on the subspace of imaginary time (time blocking). The approach is implemented self-consistently within quantum hadrodynamics based on a meson-nucleon Lagrangian, but the authors note that at high temperatures the saturation of results with respect to the truncation on reduced transition probabilities is rather slow, and the 5% cutoff used is at the limit of current computational capabilities.

## Method

The authors start from the finite-temperature relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory by minimizing the grand potential with Lagrange multipliers for temperature and chemical potential, obtaining Fermi-Dirac occupation factors for the single-particle states. They then formulate the particle-hole response function via the Bethe-Salpeter equation in imaginary-time Matsubara formalism, separating the interaction kernel into a short-range instantaneous part (effective meson exchange) and a long-range energy-dependent part induced by medium polarization (particle-vibration coupling). The key technical step is introducing a temperature-dependent projection operator that, after Fourier transformation and summation over fermionic Matsubara frequencies, reduces the integral equation to a Dyson-type equation with a single frequency variable, which is then solved in a truncated configuration space of particle-hole and particle-hole-plus-phonon states.

## Key numbers

* The particle-hole basis was limited by single-particle energy differences ε_ph ≤ 100 MeV and ε_αh ≥ -1800 MeV relative to the positive-energy continuum; a direct verification with ε_ph ≤ 300 MeV showed that physical states converge reasonably well with the 100 MeV cutoff.
* The smearing parameter Δ = 500 keV was adopted for strength function calculations of ⁴⁸Ca, and Δ = 200 keV for ¹⁰⁰,¹²⁰,¹³²Sn.
* The phonon space included vibrations with J^π = 2⁺, 3⁻, 4⁺, 5⁻, 6⁺ below energy cutoffs of 15 MeV for heavy nuclei and 20 MeV for medium-mass nuclei.
* At T ~ 5-6 MeV, the number of phonon modes in the truncated model space becomes by an order of magnitude larger than at T = 0.
* Contributions with |n(ε₂,T) - n(ε₁,T)| ≤ 0.01 were excluded from the Bethe-Salpeter equation; at high temperatures this truncation was released as much as possible to remove spurious translational mode contributions.
* The excitation energy E*(T) shows a nearly parabolic dependence for T ≥ 1 MeV, with the quadratic coefficient in good agreement with the empirical Fermi gas level density parameter a = A/k where 8 ≤ k ≤ 12.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic nuclear field theory (RNFT) developed by Litvinova and collaborators (Litvinova, Ring, Tselyaev, 2007-2018) from zero temperature to finite temperature, building on the pioneering ideas of Pier Francesco Bortignon on nuclear response at finite temperature and the thermal NFT (Bortignon, 1986; Broglia, Bortignon, Bracco, 1992). The approach goes beyond the simplest one-loop approximations such as thermal random phase approximation (TRPA) (Ring, Robledo, Egido et al., 1984) and thermal quasiparticle RPA (TQRPA) (Yueksel, Colo, Khan et al., 2017), as well as earlier extensions like finite-temperature second RPA (Adachi, Schuck, 1989) and the quasiparticle-phonon model in thermofield dynamics (Storozhenko, 2004). The method enables studies of the evolution of nuclear collective oscillations with temperature, relevant for astrophysical applications such as neutron star mergers and core-collapse supernovae, and can be applied to monopole, dipole, and quadrupole response of medium-mass and heavy nuclei.
