# A Hydrodynamical Study on the Conversion of Hadronic Matter to Quark Matter: II. Diffusion-Induced Conversion

> We study transitions of hadronic matter (HM) to 3-flavor quark matter (3QM), regarding the conversion processes as combustion and describing them hydrodynamically. Under the assumption that HM is metastable with their free energies being larger than those of 3QM but smaller than those of 2-flavor quark matter (2QM), we consider in this paper the conversion induced by diffusions of seed 3QM. This is a sequel to our previous paper, in which the shock-induced conversion was studied in the same frame work. We not only pay attention to the jump condition on both sides of the conversion front but the structures inside the front are also considered by taking into account what happens during the conversion processes on the time scale of weak interactions. We employ for HM the Shen's EOS, which is based on the relativistic mean field theory, and the bag model-based EOS for QM just as in the previous paper. We demonstrated in that paper that in this combination of EOS's the combustion will occur for a wide range of the bag constant and strong coupling constant in the so-called endothermic regime, in which the Hugoniot curve for combustion runs below the initial state. We find that weak deflagration nearly always occurs and that weak detonation is possible only when the diffusion constant is (unrealistically) large and the critical strange fraction is small. The velocities of the conversion front are ~ $10^3-10^7$ cm/s depending on the initial temperature and density as well as the parameters in the QM EOS and become particularly small when the final state is in the mixed phase. Finally we study linear stability of the laminar weak-deflagration front and find that it is unstable in the exothermic regime (Darrius-Landau instability) but stable in the endothermic regime, which is quite contrary to the ordinary combustions.

## Key claim

Under the assumption that hadronic matter is metastable relative to 3-flavor quark matter but more stable than 2-flavor quark matter, diffusion-induced conversion nearly always results in weak deflagration; weak detonation is possible only when the diffusion constant is unrealistically large and the critical strange fraction is small, contrary to the shock-induced conversion studied previously.

## Method

The authors model the conversion of hadronic matter to 3-flavor quark matter as a combustion process described hydrodynamically in plane symmetry, solving conservation equations for mass, momentum, and energy together with a diffusion-reaction equation for the strangeness fraction. They employ Shen's equation of state based on relativistic mean field theory for hadronic matter and the MIT bag model with first-order strong coupling corrections for quark matter, analyzing both a simplified toy model and more realistic parameter sets to determine which combustion modes (weak deflagration or weak detonation) are realized.

## Key numbers

- Conversion front velocities are approximately 10^3 to 10^7 cm/s, depending on initial temperature, density, and quark matter equation-of-state parameters
- The width of the β-equilibration region is evaluated as λ_w ∼ 10^−4 to 10^−1 cm, based on diffusion velocities of 10^4 to 10^7 cm/s and weak interaction time scale of 10^−8 s
- For the realistic diffusion constant D ∼ 1 cm^2/s (D* ∼ 10^−13), no weak detonation is obtained for critical strangeness fractions f_sc ≳ 10^−4 in the toy model
- The initial hadronic matter state is taken at density ρ_i = 3.0 × 10^14 g/cm^3, temperature T = 10 MeV, and proton fraction Y_p = 0.3

## Context

This paper is the second in a series by Furusawa, Sanada, and Yamada, following their previous work on shock-induced conversion (Furusawa 2015). It extends the hydrodynamical combustion approach of Olinto (1987) and others by considering not only the Hugoniot jump conditions but also the internal structure of the conversion front on weak-interaction time scales. The results enable future global simulations of neutron star conversion to quark matter by providing the necessary local combustion conditions, complementing the global studies of Herzog and Pagliara (2013).
