# Novel chiral Hamiltonian and observables in light and medium-mass nuclei

> A novel parameterisation of a Hamiltonian based on chiral effective field theory is introduced. Specifically, three-nucleon operators at next-to-next-to-leading order are combined with an existing (and successful) two-body interaction containing terms up to next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order. The resulting potential is labelled $N\!N\!$+$3N\text{(lnl)}$. The objective of the present work is to investigate the performance of this new Hamiltonian across light and medium-mass nuclei. Binding energies, nuclear radii and excitation spectra are computed using no-core shell model and self-consistent Green's function approaches. Calculations with $N\!N\!$+$3N\text{(lnl)}$ are compared to two other representative Hamiltonians currently in use, namely NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$ and the older $N\!N\!$+$3N(400)$. Overall, the performance of the novel interaction is very encouraging. In light nuclei, total energies are generally in good agreement with experimental data. Known spectra are also well reproduced with a few notable exceptions. The good description of ground-state energies carries on to heavier nuclei, all the way from oxygen to nickel isotopes. Except for those involving excitation processes across the $N=20$ gap, which is overestimated by the new interaction, spectra are of very good quality, in general superior to those obtained with NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$. Although largely improving on $N\!N\!$+$3N(400)$ results, charge radii calculated with $N\!N\!$+$3N\text{(lnl)}$ still underestimate experimental values, as opposed to the ones computed with NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$ that successfully reproduce available data on nickel. On the whole, the new two- plus three-nucleon Hamiltonian introduced in the present work represents a promising alternative to existing nuclear interactions.

## Key claim

The novel chiral Hamiltonian NN+3N(lnl), which combines an N3LO two-nucleon interaction with an N2LO three-nucleon force using both local and nonlocal regulators, reproduces experimental ground-state energies well from light to medium-mass nuclei but still underestimates charge radii, in contrast to the NNLOsat interaction that successfully reproduces nickel radii.

## Method

The authors constructed the NN+3N(lnl) Hamiltonian by keeping the N3LO two-nucleon potential of Entem and Machleidt (2003) unchanged and modifying the three-nucleon sector: they changed the low-energy constants c_D and c_E to 0.70 and -0.06 respectively, introduced a nonlocal regulator at 500 MeV/c on top of the existing local regulator (increased from 400 to 650 MeV/c), and evaluated matrix elements using a completeness in the three-nucleon antisymmetrized harmonic-oscillator basis. Binding energies, charge radii, and excitation spectra were computed using no-core shell model (NCSM) for light nuclei and self-consistent Green's function theory (Dyson ADC(3) and Gorkov ADC(2)) for medium-mass nuclei across oxygen, calcium, and nickel isotopic chains.

## Key numbers

- The NN+3N(lnl) Hamiltonian reproduces the 4He ground-state energy at -28.25 MeV, compared to the experimental value of -28.29 MeV
- For 36Ca, increasing the model space from e_max=11 to e_max=13 gives a 300 keV gain for NN+3N(lnl) versus a 1.5 MeV gain for NNLOsat
- For 68Ni, the same model space increase yields a 2 MeV gain for NN+3N(lnl) versus a 4.9 MeV gain for NNLOsat
- The three-nucleon low-energy constants for NN+3N(lnl) are c_1=-0.81 GeV^-1, c_3=-3.20 GeV^-1, c_4=5.40 GeV^-1, c_D=0.70, c_E=-0.06

## Context

This work extends the line of chiral Hamiltonian development following the NN+3N(400) interaction of Roth (2012), which showed overbinding in medium-mass nuclei and underestimated radii, and the NNLOsat interaction of Ekstrom (2015), which improved radii but was fitted to data up to A=25. The new NN+3N(lnl) Hamiltonian addresses the recently identified error in the axial current fit of c_D (Gazit 2019) and the problematic use of purely local regulators in three-nucleon forces (Hagen 2014, Dyhdalo 2016, Lynn 2017). The results enable systematic comparisons of chiral interactions across light and medium-mass nuclei, though the persistent radius underestimation points to the need for further developments in nuclear interactions.
