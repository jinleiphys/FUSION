# Phase diagram for the asymmetric nuclear matter in the multifragmentation model

> We assume that, in equilibrium, nuclear matter at reduced density and moderate finite temperature, breaks up into many fragments. A strong support to this assumption is provided by date accumulated from intermediate energy heavy ion collisions. The break-up of hot and expanded nuclear matter according to rules of equilibrium statistical mechanics is the multifragmentation model. The model gives a first order phase transition. This is studied in detail here. Phase-equilibrium lines for different degrees of asymmetry are computed.

## Key claim

The multifragmentation model predicts a first-order liquid-gas phase transition for asymmetric nuclear matter, but only within the low-density regime (ρ/ρ₀ ≤ 0.5) and for asymmetry parameters N₀/Z₀ between 1.0 and 1.8; the critical point is not reached within this density range, contrary to what might be inferred from the abstract's claim of a first-order phase transition.

## Method

The authors used a grand canonical multifragmentation model where hot, expanded nuclear matter breaks into composites (nuclei and nucleons) with properties determined by a liquid-drop formula including volume energy, temperature-dependent surface energy, symmetry energy, and excited-state contributions. They solved the coupled equations for neutron and proton chemical potentials (μₙ, μₚ) by summing over all possible composites up to a maximum mass number Aₘₐₓ = 600, with excluded volume corrections, and computed pressure-density isotherms for various asymmetries and temperatures between 4 and 10 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The study is restricted to densities ρ/ρ₀ ≤ 0.5, where ρ₀ is normal nuclear density.
- Temperature range investigated: 4 to 10 MeV.
- Asymmetry parameter N₀/Z₀ spans 1.0 to 1.8 (upper limit corresponds to a highly asymmetric system).
- At T = 6.5 MeV and N₀/Z₀ = 1.4, the flattening of the p-ρ curve (signaling phase coexistence) begins slightly beyond ρ/ρ₀ = 0.1.
- For N₀/Z₀ = 1.4 at T = 6.5 MeV and ρ/ρ₀ = 0.35, the neutron-to-proton ratio in the gas phase is 1.485 and in the liquid phase is 1.375.
- The gas phase is defined as composites with A ≤ 70; the liquid phase consists of composites with A between Aₘₐₓ and Aₘₐₓ − 135.
- For symmetric matter (N₀/Z₀ = 1.0), phase coexistence disappears from the region ρ/ρ₀ ≤ 0.5 above some temperature (not given numerically).
- Bugaev et al. found a critical point at T_c = 18.0 MeV and ρ/ρ₀ = 1.0 for the one-component model, but this lies outside the present study's density range.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier multifragmentation model (Chaudhuri and Das Gupta, 2008) from one-component to two-component (neutron-proton) nuclear matter, and is closest in spirit to De et al. (2008). It contrasts with mean-field approaches such as those of Muller and Serot (1995) and Ducoin et al. (2006), which use Maxwell construction to handle instabilities; the multifragmentation model avoids Maxwell construction but is limited to low densities. The results enable future studies of isospin fractionation and phase coexistence in finite nuclear systems with Coulomb forces, and provide a benchmark for comparing with lattice gas model calculations (Chomaz et al., 2004).
