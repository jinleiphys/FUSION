# Neutrinoless double-beta decay: combining quantum Monte Carlo and the nuclear shell model with the generalized contact formalism

> We devise a framework based on the generalized contact formalism that combines the nuclear shell model and quantum Monte Carlo methods and compute the neutrinoless double-beta decay of experimentally relevant nuclei, including $^{76}$Ge, $^{130}$Te, and $^{136}$Xe. In light nuclei, we validate our nuclear matrix element calculations by comparing against accurate variational Monte Carlo results. Due to additional correlations captured by quantum Monte Carlo and introduced within the generalized contact formalism, in heavier systems, we obtain long-range nuclear matrix elements that are about 30% smaller than previous shell-model results. We also evaluate the recently recognized short-range nuclear matrix element estimating its coupling by the charge-independence-breaking term of the Argonne $v_{18}$ potential used in the Monte Carlo calculations. Our results indicate an enhancement of the total nuclear matrix element by around 30%.

## Key claim

The authors claim that combining the generalized contact formalism (GCF) with the nuclear shell model (SM) and quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) methods yields neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) that are about 30% smaller for the long-range part than previous shell-model results, while the total NME is enhanced by around 30% when including the short-range contribution, but these results depend on estimating the short-range coupling from the charge-independence-breaking term of the Argonne v18 potential.

## Method

The authors used variational Monte Carlo (VMC) with the AV18 plus Urbana X interaction to compute transition densities for light nuclei (A ≤ 12), and the nuclear shell model with Woods-Saxon single-particle orbitals for both light and heavier nuclei including ⁷⁶Ge, ¹³⁰Te, and ¹³⁶Xe. They applied the generalized contact formalism to merge the short-range behavior from VMC with the long-range behavior from the shell model, using contact ratios that are assumed model-independent to transfer the short-range dynamics from light to heavy nuclei. The short-range NME coupling was estimated from the charge-independence-breaking term of the AV18 potential.

## Key numbers

- Long-range nuclear matrix elements for heavier systems are about 30% smaller than previous shell-model results
- Total nuclear matrix element (including short-range contribution) is enhanced by around 30%
- Current experimental half-life limits: ⁷⁶Ge T^{0ν}_{1/2} > 1.8 × 10²⁶ yr (GERDA 2020) and ¹³⁶Xe T^{0ν}_{1/2} > 1.07 × 10²⁶ yr (KamLAND-Zen 2016)
- Next-generation experiments plan to reach T^{0ν}_{1/2} ∼ 10²⁸ yr
- The tensor component of the NME is estimated from shell-model results with a 50% uncertainty
- For the NV-Ia* chiral potential model, (C₁ + C₂)/2 = -1.03 fm² with regularization radius R_S = 0.8 fm

## Context

This work extends the approach of Wang (2019) who compared VMC and SM calculations for light nuclei, and addresses the known deficiency of shell-model and other non-ab-initio methods (QRPA, energy-density functional theory, interacting boson model) in treating short-range correlations consistently. The method enables NME predictions for experimentally relevant heavy nuclei where direct ab-initio calculations with high-resolution potentials are currently impossible, and can accommodate a variety of nuclear interactions including those from chiral effective field theory.
