# Microscopic calculation of expanding nuclear matter

> Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) calculations are used to study the expansion phase in central collisions between heavy nuclei. The final state of such a reaction can be understood as the result of a entropy conserving expansion starting from a compact source. The properties of this hypothetic source, however, are in conflict with the assumptions used in fireball models. Moreover, this hypothetical source is not formed in the dynamical evolution of the system.

# Microscopic calculation of expanding nuclear matter

## Key claim
The final state of a heavy ion collision can be understood as the result of an entropy-conserving expansion from a single compact source, but the position-velocity correlations of that hypothetical source are never actually traversed by the system during its true dynamical evolution, contradicting the assumptions of fireball models.

## Method
Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) calculations were performed for central Au + Au collisions at 150 MeV/nucleon beam energy with an impact parameter of 3 fm. The system was propagated forward for 400 fm/c, then all particle momenta were mirrored and the system was back-propagated using molecular dynamics (MD) alone (without the collision term) to reverse the isentropic expansion. The one-body distribution function was obtained by superposing many events to extract local velocity distributions, collective motion, and local temperature.

## Key numbers
- Mean displacement after full forward and backward MD propagation: less than 1 fm in configuration space and less than 50 MeV/c in momentum space.
- Beam energy: 150 MeV/nucleon for Au + Au collisions.
- Impact parameter: 3 fm.
- Propagation time: 400 fm/c forward, then 400 fm/c backward.
- Central density of the backpropagated state: twice saturation density.

## Context
This work extends earlier hydrodynamical calculations (Schmidt 1993) and quantum statistical analyses (Kuhn 1993) that suggested hot dense systems expand along isentropes, while challenging fireball models (Gosset 1978) that assume isentropic expansion from an equilibrated compact source. The results show that although final-state data can be reproduced by such models, the underlying dynamical path differs significantly because entropy is produced via two-body scatterings not only in the high-density phase but also during expansion. The analysis enables future work to incorporate flow correlations into equilibrium models and to distinguish between entropy-conserving and stochastic components in transport simulations.
