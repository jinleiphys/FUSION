# Theory summary. Hard Probes 2012

> I provide a summary of the theoretical talks in Hard Probes 2012 together with some personal thoughts about the present and the future of the field.

## Key claim

This is a conference summary paper providing the author's personal perspective on the theoretical talks at Hard Probes 2012, rather than presenting new original research results. The author states that the precision of LHC data is now often smaller than theoretical uncertainties, and that an understanding of experimental data based on solid calculations can be expected before the next LHC running period starts.

## Method

The author reviews theoretical talks presented at the Hard Probes 2012 conference in Sardinia, Italy, organized by topic: initial state effects, quarkonia, electroweak probes, jet quenching (including heavy quarks at high transverse momentum), and the AdS/CFT connection. The summary provides the author's own point of view on where progress in the coming years is both needed and most promising, referencing only the talks presented at the conference.

## Key numbers

- The second nuclear run at the LHC in 2011 produced fifteen times more luminosity than the first run in 2010.
- An updated MEM analysis of charmonia states indicates no signal for bound states above 1.46 T_c.
- The nuclear modification factor R_pPb at the LHC shows large differences from uncertainties in the anomalous dimension of unintegrated gluon distributions, while still providing a reasonable description at RHIC.
- The next proton-lead run at the LHC is scheduled for January-February 2013, just before Long Shutdown 1.

## Context

This paper summarizes the theoretical program of the Hard Probes 2012 conference, covering work that extends the McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model and Color Glass Condensate (CGC) framework for initial state effects, DGLAP-based nuclear PDF analyses by Kari Eskola, and lattice results on quarkonia bound states by Kaczmarek. The review enables future comparisons between LHC proton-lead data and theoretical predictions, particularly for constraining saturation effects and nuclear parton distributions. The author identifies open questions including the role of impact parameter in CGC evolution, the interpretation of quarkonia suppression versus recombination, and the need for rigorous Monte Carlo codes for jet quenching studies.
