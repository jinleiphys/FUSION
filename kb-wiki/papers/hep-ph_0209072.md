# QCD calculations of thermal photon and dilepton production

> In this talk, I review new developments of QCD calculations of photon and dilepton production rates in a Quark-Gluon plasma. All the rates are now known up to $O(α_s)$ both for photons and dileptons, thanks to the resummation of multiple scatterings. For dileptons, a direct numerical calculation on the lattice attempted recently will also be discussed.

# QCD calculations of thermal photon and dilepton production

## Key claim
All photon and dilepton production rates in a Quark-Gluon Plasma are now known up to $O(\alpha_s)$, but this result required resummation of an infinite series of multiple scattering diagrams (the LPM effect) because formally higher-order diagrams are promoted to $O(\alpha_s)$ by collinear singularities. The rates depend only on the quark thermal mass $m_q$ and the Debye screening mass $m_{\text{debye}}$, and the complete leading-order result requires solving an integral equation numerically rather than having a simple analytic form for all kinematic regimes.

## Method
The photon production rate is expressed via the imaginary part of the retarded polarization tensor, which is calculated using thermal field theory cutting rules. For the $O(\alpha_s)$ rate, a Dyson equation for the polarization tensor is combined with a Bethe-Salpeter equation that resums all ladder diagrams representing multiple soft scatterings of the quark that emits the photon; the collision kernel in the Bethe-Salpeter equation depends on the Debye mass. For dileptons, the same formalism is extended to include the longitudinal mode of the virtual photon and the non-zero invariant mass $Q^2$.

## Key numbers
- Photon rate at $O(\alpha_s)$ for hard photons (from $2\to 2$ processes): $\text{Im}\,\Pi_{\text{ret}}^\mu{}_\mu(\omega,\boldsymbol{q}) = 4\pi \frac{5\alpha\alpha_s}{9} T^2 \left[\ln\left(\frac{\omega T}{m_q^2}\right) - \frac{1}{2} - \gamma_E + \frac{7}{3}\ln(2) + \frac{\zeta'(2)}{\zeta(2)}\right]$, where $m_q^2 = \pi\alpha_s C_f T^2$ with $C_f = (N_c^2-1)/(2N_c)$.
- For 3 colors and 2 light flavors, the collinearly-enhanced diagrams contribute exactly: $\text{Im}\,\Pi_{\text{ret}}^\mu{}_\mu(\omega,\boldsymbol{q}) = \frac{32}{3\pi} \frac{5\alpha\alpha_s}{9} \left[\pi^2 \frac{T^3}{\omega} + \omega T\right]$.
- For 3 colors and 3 light flavors, the same quantity has the same energy dependence but a more complicated prefactor involving $\ln(2)$, $\ln(3)$, and dilogarithms $\text{Li}_2(3/4)$ and $\text{Li}_2(-1/2)$.
- The ratio $m_q/m_{\text{debye}} = \sqrt{2/(6+N_f)}$ for 3 colors and $N_f$ flavors in the HTL approximation.
- Lattice results for static dileptons ($\boldsymbol{q}=0$) show the rate scales approximately as a universal function of $\omega/T$, with the Born term dominating above $\omega \approx 4T$ and the rate dropping to very small values below $\omega \approx 3T$.

## Context
This work reviews the resolution of long-standing problems in thermal QCD calculations of electromagnetic probes. The logarithmic singularity at $Q^2\to 0$ in the photon polarization tensor was resolved by Hard Thermal Loop resummation (Braaten and Pisarski, 1990; Frenkel and Taylor, 1990). The collinear enhancement of bremsstrahlung diagrams was first identified by Aurenche et al. (1998) and later shown to require resummation of multiple scatterings due to the LPM effect (Aurenche et al., 2002). The complete resummation was achieved by Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2001-2002), who demonstrated that infrared cancellations remove sensitivity to the magnetic scale, allowing the problem to be reduced to ladder diagrams only. The lattice calculation of static dilepton rates using the Maximum Entropy Method (Karsch et al., 2001) shows a suppression at small $\omega$ that disagrees with perturbative expectations and with the prediction from electric conductivity, indicating that both approaches may be incorrect in the hydrodynamical regime ($\omega \lesssim g_s^4 T$). Future work should extend lattice calculations to real photons and improve treatment of the pre-equilibrium phase, where kinetic equilibration times may be as long as a few fermis (Baier, 2002).
