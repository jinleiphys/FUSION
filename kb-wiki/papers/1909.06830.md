# Monte Carlo variations as a tool to assess nuclear physics uncertainties in nucleosynthesis studies

> The propagation of uncertainties in reaction cross sections and rates of neutron-, proton-, and $α$-induced reactions into the final isotopic abundances obtained in nucleosynthesis models is an important issue in studies of nucleosynthesis and Galactic Chemical Evolution. We developed a Monte Carlo method to allow large-scale postprocessing studies of the impact of nuclear uncertainties on nucleosynthesis. Temperature-dependent rate uncertainties combining realistic experimental and theoretical uncertainties are used. The importance of contributions of cross sections of reactions on excited states of the nuclear targets, which have weights different from from the thermal Boltzmann population factors, is explained. From detailed statistical analyses of the Monte Carlo data uncertainties in the final abundances are derived as probability density distributions. Furthermore, based on rate and abundance correlations an automated procedure identifies the most important reactions in complex flow patterns from superposition of many zones or tracers. The method already has been applied to a number of nucleosynthesis processes.

# Monte Carlo variations as a tool to assess nuclear physics uncertainties in nucleosynthesis studies

## Key claim

The Monte Carlo method can propagate temperature-dependent uncertainties in reaction cross sections and rates into final isotopic abundances, but the resulting production uncertainties for most nuclei are below factors of 2–3 despite much larger assumed theoretical uncertainties, because individual rate uncertainties can cancel out and reaction flow can adapt.

## Method

The Monte Carlo framework PizBuin uses an efficient parallelised reaction network solver to postprocess astrophysical trajectories (temporal evolution of density and temperature) with several thousand nuclides and tens of thousands of reactions. Temperature-dependent uncertainty factors are assigned to each reaction rate by combining experimental 2-sigma uncertainties for ground-state contributions with theoretical uncertainties for excited-state contributions, using a formula that accounts for the temperature-dependent ground-state contribution to the stellar rate. For each of 10000 Monte Carlo iterations, a separate random variation factor drawn from a uniform distribution is applied to each rate, and the resulting abundance distributions are analysed using weighted Pearson product-moment correlation coefficients to identify key reactions that dominate the final uncertainty.

## Key numbers

- Theoretical uncertainty limits assigned: factor of 2 (upper) and 0.5 (lower) for neutron-induced reactions; factor of 2.0 (upper) and 0.33 (lower) for proton-induced reactions; factor of 2.0 (upper) and 0.1 (lower) for alpha-induced reactions.
- 10000 Monte Carlo iterations are used, each with a separate random variation factor for each rate.
- A key rate is defined by having a weighted Pearson correlation coefficient with absolute value greater than or equal to 0.65.
- Production uncertainties for most nuclei are below factors of 2–3, despite larger assumed theoretical uncertainties at high temperature.
- Several 1000 variations are sufficient to obtain a statistically meaningful sample of a rate within its uncertainty.

## Context

This work extends the author's prior development of temperature-dependent uncertainty factors (Rauscher, 2012; Rauscher, 2018) and provides a systematic method to replace manual single-rate sensitivity studies. The method has already been applied to the gamma process (p process) in core-collapse supernovae, the nu-p process in core-collapse supernovae, p-nuclei production in type Ia supernovae, the weak s process in massive stars, and the main s process in AGB stars. It enables astronomers to disentangle nuclear physics uncertainties from astrophysical modelling uncertainties, which are often much larger and systematic.
