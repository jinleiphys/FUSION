# Maximum mass of neutron stars with a quark core

> Massive neutron stars (NS) are expected to possess a quark core. While the hadronic side of the NS equation of state (EOS) can be considered well established, the quark side is quite uncertain. While calculating the EOS of hadronic matter we have used the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone formalism with realistic two-body and three-body forces, as well as a relativistic mean field model. For quark matter we employ the MIT bag model constraining the bag constant by exploiting the recent experimental results obtained at CERN on the formation of a quark-gluon plasma. We calculate the structure of NS interiors with the EOS comprising both phases, and we find that the NS maximum masses fall in a relatively narrow interval, $1.45 M_\odot \leq M_{\rm max} \leq 1.65 M_\odot$, near the lower limit of the observational range.

## Key claim

The maximum mass of neutron stars with a quark core falls in a narrow interval of \(1.45 M_\odot \leq M_{\rm max} \leq 1.65 M_\odot\), near the lower limit of the observational range, but this result depends on the hypothesis that the transition to deconfined quark matter occurs at approximately the same energy density regardless of the thermodynamical conditions (high baryon density and low temperature in stars versus low baryon density and high temperature in heavy-ion collisions).

## Method

The authors calculated the hadronic equation of state (EOS) using the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone (BBG) formalism with the Paris two-nucleon potential and Urbana three-body force, as well as a relativistic mean field (RMF) model. For the quark phase, they employed the MIT bag model with a density-dependent bag constant \(B\), constrained so that the hadron-to-quark transition energy density matches the CERN-SPS value of about 1.1 GeV fm\(^{-3}\) (approximately seven times normal nuclear matter energy density). They then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for neutron star structure using the combined EOS with a mixed hadron-quark phase constructed via Gibbs conditions.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass range: \(1.45 M_\odot \leq M_{\rm max} \leq 1.65 M_\odot\) (with \(M_\odot = 1.99 \times 10^{33}\) g)
- Transition energy density from CERN experiments: approximately 1.1 GeV fm\(^{-3}\) (about seven times normal nuclear matter energy density \(\epsilon_0 \approx 156\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\))
- Normal nuclear matter density: \(\rho_0 \approx 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- BHF maximum mass without quark core: about \(1.26 M_\odot\)
- RMF maximum mass without quark core: close to \(1.7 M_\odot\)
- Asymptotic bag constant \(B_{as} = 50\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\) for BHF case; \(B_{as} \approx 38\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\) for RMF case
- Transition density for BHF case: \(\rho_c = 0.98\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Strange quark mass: 150 MeV
- Observational lower limit cited: \(1.44 M_\odot\) (Hulse, 1975)

## Context

This work extends prior calculations of neutron star structure by combining well-established hadronic EOS (BBG and RMF) with a quark phase described by the MIT bag model, constrained by CERN-SPS heavy-ion collision data on quark-gluon plasma formation (Heinz, 2000; Blaizot, 2000). The results contradict the possibility of very massive neutron stars (e.g., \(M \approx 2.2 M_\odot\) claimed by Kaaret, 2001) if the constant-energy-density transition hypothesis holds. The narrow maximum mass range appears robust across different bag constant parametrizations and hadronic EOS models, and the authors note that other calculations using RMF nuclear EOS with effective mass bag models or Nambu-Jona-Lasinio models also give maximum masses of only about \(1.7 M_\odot\). The work enables future observational tests: detection of a neutron star heavier than \(1.6 M_\odot\) would challenge either the high-density nuclear EOS modeling or the assumption that the deconfinement transition energy density is independent of thermodynamic conditions.
