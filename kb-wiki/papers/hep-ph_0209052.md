# Comment on "Radiative corrections and parity nonconservation in heavy atoms"

## Key claim
The experimental results of Wood et al. (1997) for parity nonconservation (PNC) in cesium do not contradict the standard model, provided that the self-energy and vertex QED radiative corrections (approximately -0.73% to -0.9% for Cs) are included accurately. Without these corrections, the experimental data deviate by more than 2σ from standard model predictions.

## Method
The authors summarize and compare three independent calculations of the QED self-energy and vertex radiative corrections to the PNC amplitude in atoms: Kuchiev and Flambaum (2002) used an identity relating radiative corrections to PNC matrix elements between s₁/₂ and p₁/₂ electron wave functions to radiative corrections to finite nuclear size (FNS) energy shifts, including all orders in the Zα expansion; Milstein et al. (2002) and Kuchiev (2002) calculated the self-energy corrections using the αZ-expansion. The results are presented as a function of atomic nuclear charge Z for Cs (Z=55) and heavier atoms Tl, Pb, Bi (Z near 80).

## Key numbers
- For Cs (Z=55), the self-energy correction to the PNC amplitude is -0.73(20)% (Kuchiev and Flambaum 2002), -0.9(2)% (Kuchiev 2002), and -0.85% (Milstein et al. 2002)
- For heavier atoms Tl, Pb, Bi (Z near 80), the correction is -1.6% (Kuchiev and Flambaum 2002; Kuchiev 2002) and -1.4% (Milstein et al. 2002)
- The experimental accuracy for the 6s-7s PNC amplitude in ¹³³Cs is 0.3% (Wood et al. 1997)
- The perturbative expansion parameter αZ is 0.4 for Cs and 0.6 for Tl, Pb, Bi
- The lowest term of the αZ expansion is given as -1.97 α² Z

## Context
This comment resolves a sharp contradiction between Kuchiev and Flambaum (2002), who found a large negative self-energy correction, and Milstein and Sushkov (2002), who previously insisted the correction was small and positive. The agreement among three independent calculations (Kuchiev and Flambaum 2002; Kuchiev 2002; Milstein et al. 2002) now brings the atomic PNC data of Wood et al. (1997) into full agreement with the standard model. This enables continued use of high-precision atomic PNC measurements as a test of electroweak physics, with the QED radiative corrections now understood to be approximately 1% and negative in sign.
