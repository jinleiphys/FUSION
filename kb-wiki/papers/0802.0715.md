# Spin Effects in Long Range Electromagnetic Scattering

> We analyze the electromagnetic scattering of massive particles with and without spin and, using the techniques of effective field theory, we isolate the leading long distance effects beyond one photon exchange, both classical and quantum mechanical. Spin-independent and spin-dependent effects are isolated and shown to have a universal structure.

## Key claim

Using effective field theory techniques, the authors isolate the leading long-distance (small momentum transfer) corrections to electromagnetic scattering beyond one-photon exchange, finding that the quantum mechanical (logarithmic) contributions to the second-order potential are universal and invariant under different definitions of the first-order potential, while the classical (1/r²) term is ambiguous and depends on the choice of iteration scheme.

## Method

The authors evaluate the two-photon exchange Feynman diagrams (box, crossed-box, and vertex-correction diagrams) for distinguishable massive charged particles, keeping only the nonanalytic pieces in momentum transfer \(q^2\) that yield long-range potentials: terms proportional to \(1/\sqrt{-q^2}\) (classical, \(\hbar\)-independent) and \(\log(-q^2)\) (quantum, \(\hbar\)-dependent). They define the second-order potential by subtracting the iterated (second Born) amplitude from the full two-photon amplitude before Fourier transforming to coordinate space, and extend this procedure from spin-0–spin-0 scattering to spin-0–spin-1/2 and spin-1/2–spin-1/2 systems.

## Key numbers

* For spin-0–spin-0 scattering, the second-order potential using a simple Coulomb iteration is \(V^{(2)}(r) = -\frac{\alpha^2 (m_a+m_b)}{2 m_a m_b r^2} - \frac{7\alpha^2 \hbar}{6\pi m_a m_b r^3}\), where the \(1/r^2\) term is classical and the \(1/r^3\) term is quantum.
* The quantum mechanical \(1/r^3\) coefficient is invariant across three different iteration schemes (simple Coulomb, Feinberg-Sucher, and Sucher-Spruch), while the classical \(1/r^2\) coefficient changes sign or vanishes depending on the scheme.
* For spin-0–spin-1/2 scattering, the lowest-order spin-orbit potential is \(-\frac{\alpha}{r^3} \frac{m_a+2m_b}{2 m_a m_b^2} \, \vec{L}\cdot\vec{S}_b\).
* The two-photon amplitude for spin-0–spin-1/2 scattering contains terms proportional to \(S = \pi^2/\sqrt{-q^2}\) and \(L = \log(-q^2)\), with coefficients involving the masses \(m_a\) and \(m_b\) and the spin-dependent structures \(\bar{u}(p_4)u(p_3)\) and \(\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta} p_1^\alpha p_3^\beta q^\gamma S_b^\delta\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier dispersive calculations of Feinberg and Sucher (1990) and the nonrelativistic perturbation theory of Iwasaki (1970) for spinless scattering, resolving discrepancies in the classical potential by showing it depends on the definition of the first-order potential used in iteration. The authors also compare with the NRQED calculations of Manohar and Stewart (2000), noting that their quantum \(1/r^3\) terms are absent in vNRQED matching but present in pNRQED (Pineda and Soto, 1998), and that their spin-orbit and spin-spin corrections are higher order in velocity than the Manohar-Stewart results. The universal structure found enables a multipole-expansion-like interpretation of spin-dependent long-range electromagnetic interactions.
