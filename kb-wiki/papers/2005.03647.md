# Inclusive and effective bulk viscosities in the hadron gas

> We estimate the temperature dependence of the bulk viscosity in a relativistic hadron gas. Employing the Green-Kubo formalism in the SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons) transport approach, we study different hadronic systems in increasing order of complexity. We analyze the (in)validity of the single exponential relaxation ansatz for the bulk-channel correlation function and the strong influence of the resonances and their lifetimes. We discuss the difference between the inclusive bulk viscosity of an equilibrated, long-lived system, and the effective bulk viscosity of a short-lived mixture like the hadronic phase of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, where the processes whose inverse relaxation rate are larger than the fireball duration are excluded from the analysis. This clarifies the differences between previous approaches which computed the bulk viscosity including/excluding the very slow processes in the hadron gas. We compare our final results with previous hadron gas calculations and confirm a decreasing trend of the inclusive bulk viscosity over entropy density as temperature increases, whereas the effective bulk viscosity to entropy ratio, while being lower than the inclusive one, shows no strong dependence to temperature.

## Key claim

The paper distinguishes between the "inclusive" bulk viscosity of an equilibrated, long-lived hadron gas and the "effective" bulk viscosity relevant for short-lived systems like the hadronic phase of heavy-ion collisions, where processes with inverse relaxation rates longer than the fireball duration are excluded. The inclusive bulk viscosity over entropy density decreases with increasing temperature, while the effective bulk viscosity to entropy ratio shows no strong temperature dependence and is lower than the inclusive value.

## Method

The authors employ the Green-Kubo formalism within the SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons) transport approach to extract the bulk viscosity from equilibrium simulations of hadronic matter in a box with periodic boundary conditions. They study systems of increasing complexity: a simple pion gas with constant cross section (calibrated against Chapman-Enskog solutions), a pion gas with a single rho meson resonance, and finally the full hadron gas including all well-established hadrons from the Particle Data Group with resonance excitation and decay, elastic and inelastic 2-to-2 processes. The bulk viscosity is obtained from the time integral of the pressure autocorrelation function, and the authors analyze the validity of the single exponential relaxation ansatz for this correlation function.

## Key numbers

- For the simple pion gas (m=138 MeV, g=3, constant cross section sigma=20 mb), the bulk viscosity extracted via Green-Kubo agrees with third-order Chapman-Enskog calculations between T=100 MeV and T=175 MeV, with agreement breaking down at lower temperatures due to poor statistics.
- The correlation function at t=0, V*C_zeta(0), for the simple pion gas shows good agreement between SMASH extraction and the analytical formula from Eq. (C0) across temperatures from 75 MeV to 175 MeV.
- For the full hadron gas, the correlation functions at all temperatures (ranging from approximately 100 MeV to 175 MeV) display a two-stage decay: a rapid exponential decorrelation in the first 2-3 fm followed by a slower decay over longer times, demonstrating that a single exponential ansatz is inadequate.
- In the pion-rho system at T=150 MeV, varying the rho meson lifetime from zero to its full physical value shows that even a very small resonance lifetime profoundly modifies the correlation function, producing a large increase in fluctuations and a reduction of the relaxation time.

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of bulk viscosity in hadron gas using various methods, including diagrammatic Green-Kubo approaches (FernandezFraile 2008), Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck equation (Dobado 2011), and studies of slow 2-to-4 pion inelastic processes (Lu 2011). The paper clarifies the conceptual difference between including or excluding very slow processes, which had led to order-of-magnitude discrepancies among prior results. The findings provide external input for Bayesian extractions of transport coefficients in heavy-ion collisions (Bernhard 2016, Bernhard 2019) and for hydrodynamic simulations that require temperature-dependent bulk viscosity, particularly near the crossover region where lattice QCD calculations (Karsch 2007) indicate an enhancement.
