# Fluid velocity from transverse momentum spectra

> We parametrize the transverse momentum distribution of outgoing hadrons in ultrarelativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions as a superposition of boosted thermal distributions. In this approach, which generalizes the conventional blast wave, the momentum distribution is determined by the distribution of the fluid velocity. We analyze the difference between this generalized blast-wave parametrization and a full hydrodynamic calculation. We then apply the generalized blast-wave fit to experimental data on Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76\ \mathrm{TeV}$. The fit is reasonable up to $p_t\sim 6\ \mathrm{GeV}$, much beyond the range where hydrodynamics is usually applied, but not perfect. Based on the differences between the fit and the data, we argue that an ideal hydrodynamic calculation cannot fit simultaneously all identified particle spectra, irrespective of the specific implementation. In particular, data display a significant excess of pions at low $p_t$, whose physical interpretation is discussed. Data also show that the distribution of the fluid velocity becomes broader as the collision becomes less central. This broadening is explained by event-by-event hydrodynamic calculations, where it results from the centrality dependence of initial-state fluctuations.

## Key claim

The generalized blast-wave fit to Pb+Pb collision data at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV is reasonable up to $p_t\sim 6$ GeV, but not perfect; based on the differences between fit and data, the authors argue that no ideal hydrodynamic calculation can simultaneously fit all identified particle spectra, regardless of the specific implementation.

## Method

The authors generalize the conventional blast-wave parametrization by expressing the transverse momentum distribution as a superposition of boosted thermal distributions weighted by the distribution of fluid velocity $\Omega(u)$, which they discretize with step $\Delta u=0.4$. They apply this "semi-Cooper-Frye" approximation to experimental Pb+Pb collision data at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV, performing combined fits of pion, kaon, and proton spectra up to $p_t=7$ GeV/c using a freeze-out temperature $T_f=135$ MeV, and compare the extracted fluid velocity distributions with those from event-by-event ideal hydrodynamic simulations using TRENTo initial conditions and the MUSIC code.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out temperature chosen as $T_f=135$ MeV, giving reasonable agreement with experimental relative abundances of pions, kaons, and protons in 0-5% central Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV
- The semi-Cooper-Frye approximation underestimates particle yields by 14%, 16%, 16% for pions, kaons, protons respectively, and underestimates mean transverse momentum by 17%, 17%, 14% for pions, kaons, protons in central collisions
- The total energy (multiplicity times mean transverse momentum) is underestimated by almost 30% under the semi-Cooper-Frye approximation
- The fit to experimental data is reasonable up to $p_t\sim 6$ GeV, much beyond the typical $p_t\sim 2-3$ GeV range used in hydrodynamic calculations or blast-wave fits
- The most probable values of transverse fluid velocity $u$ are around unity, corresponding to 70% of the speed of light; the highest velocity bin $2.6<u<3$ corresponds to 93-95% of the speed of light
- The hydrodynamic simulation uses starting time $\tau_0=0.4$ fm/c and freeze-out temperature $T_f=135$ MeV, evolving approximately 30-40 different initial conditions per centrality bin

## Context

This work extends the conventional blast-wave parametrization (Siemens 1978, Schnedermann 1993) by generalizing it to an arbitrary linear superposition of boosted thermal distributions, rather than assuming a specific functional form for the fluid velocity as in Retiere (2003). The authors compare their generalized blast-wave fits to full ideal hydrodynamic calculations and to experimental data from ALICE (Adam 2015). The finding that data show a significant excess of pions at low $p_t$ relative to ideal hydrodynamics suggests either viscous corrections with a non-standard momentum dependence or a separate superfluid-like component for low-momentum pions as proposed by Grossi (2021). The extracted fluid velocity distributions enable comparison with event-by-event hydrodynamic calculations using TRENTo initial conditions (Moreland 2014) and the MUSIC code (Schenke 2010, 2011), showing that the broadening of the velocity distribution with decreasing centrality results from initial-state fluctuations.
