# The shear viscosity of parton matter under anisotropic scatterings

> The shear viscosity $η$ of a quark-gluon plasma in equilibrium can be calculated analytically using multiple methods or numerically using the Green-Kubo relation. It has been realized, which we confirm here, that the Chapman-Enskog method agrees well with the Green-Kubo result for both isotropic and anisotropic two-body scatterings. We then apply the Chapman-Enskog method to study the shear viscosity of the parton matter from a multi-phase transport model. In particular, we study the parton matter in the center cell of central and midcentral Au+Au collisions at $200A$ GeV and Pb+Pb collisions at $2760A$ GeV, which is assumed to be a plasma in thermal equilibrium but partial chemical equilibrium. As a result of using a constant Debye mass or cross section $σ$ for parton scatterings, the $η/s$ ratio increases with time (as the effective temperature decreases), contrary to the trend preferred by Bayesian analysis of the experimental data or pQCD results that use temperature-dependent Debye masses. At $σ=3$ mb that enables the transport model to approximately reproduce the elliptic flow data of the bulk matter, the average $η/s$ of the parton matter in partial equilibrium is found to be very small, between one to two times $1/(4π)$.

# The shear viscosity of parton matter under anisotropic scatterings

## Key claim

The Chapman-Enskog method agrees well with the Green-Kubo numerical results for both isotropic and anisotropic two-body scatterings, confirming an earlier finding; however, when applied to the parton matter from the AMPT model using a constant Debye mass or cross section, the η/s ratio increases with time as the effective temperature decreases, which is contrary to the trend preferred by Bayesian analysis of experimental data or pQCD results that use temperature-dependent Debye masses.

## Method

The authors compared four analytical methods (Israel-Stewart, Navier-Stokes, modified relaxation time approximation, and Chapman-Enskog) with numerical Green-Kubo calculations for massless partons in equilibrium under isotropic or forward-angle two-body scatterings. They then applied the Chapman-Enskog method to calculate the time evolution of shear viscosity and η/s in the center cell of the parton matter from the string melting AMPT model, approximating the parton matter as a QGP in thermal equilibrium at effective temperature T⟨p_T⟩ but partial chemical equilibrium with energy density corresponding to a higher effective temperature Tε.

## Key numbers

- For isotropic scatterings at a fixed cross section, the Israel-Stewart, modified relaxation time approximation, and Chapman-Enskog methods all give the same viscosity, while the Navier-Stokes result is about 5% higher.
- For the AMPT model with σ=3 mb that approximately reproduces elliptic flow data, the average η/s of the parton matter in partial equilibrium is found to be between one to two times 1/(4π) for the four collision systems studied.
- If the parton matter were in full equilibrium at temperature Tε, the average η/s would be around three times 1/(4π) for the four systems.
- The Green-Kubo numerical results used a gluon gas with σ=2.6 mb and αs=√2/3, corresponding to μ≈0.69 GeV, at temperatures including T=0.2 GeV, T=0.5 GeV, and T=0.7 GeV with various cross sections.
- For the AMPT model, the center cell was studied for central Au+Au collisions at 200A GeV (b<3 fm) and midcentral Au+Au at b=7.3 fm, as well as central Pb+Pb at 2760A GeV (b<3.5 fm) and midcentral Pb+Pb at b=7.8 fm, up to the time when Tε reaches 155 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier comparisons of analytical viscosity methods with Green-Kubo results, particularly confirming the finding of Plumari (2012) that the Chapman-Enskog method agrees well with numerical results for anisotropic scatterings while the relaxation time approximation does not. The study applies these methods to the AMPT model, building on previous work by Lin (2014) that calculated effective temperatures in the center cell of Au+Au and Pb+Pb collisions. The results enable future improvements to the AMPT model by replacing the constant Debye mass with a temperature-dependent one, which would reverse the temperature dependence of η/s to match pQCD results from Arnold (2000, 2003) and the trend preferred by Bayesian analyses such as JETSCAPE (2020).
