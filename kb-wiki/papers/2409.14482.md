# Finite-Element Simulations of Rotating Neutron Stars with Anisotropic Crusts and Continuous Gravitational Waves

> ``Mountains'', or non-axisymmetrical deformations in the elastic crust of rotating neutron stars are efficient radiators of continuous gravitational waves. Recently, small anisotropies were observed in the solid innermost inner core of the Earth. We implement three-dimensional finite-element simulations to study mountains sourced by modest anisotropies in the solid crust of rotating neutron stars. We find that anisotropic mountains may be detectable by current ground-based gravitational-wave detectors and might explain several observed phenomena, confirming the results of a previous work on less realistic neutron star models. In particular, we find that a slightly anisotropic neutron star crust that changes its rotation rate modestly can support an ellipticity of a few $\times$ 10$^{-9}$, which is equivalent to the upper bounds on the ellipticity of some nearby and rapidlly spinning pulsars.

## Key claim
An anisotropic neutron star crust with a small degree of anisotropy (⟨ψ⟩ ≪ 1) can support ellipticities of a few × 10⁻⁹ when the star changes its rotation rate modestly, confirming earlier results from simplified two-dimensional models with constant density (Morales 2023) and matching the upper bounds on ellipticity for some nearby, rapidly spinning pulsars.

## Method
The authors construct a non-rotating, canonical 1.4 M⊙ neutron star with a 10 km radius using a polytropic equation of state (n=1) and treat the centrifugal force from a change in rotation rate as a perturbation. They solve the perturbed Euler and Poisson equations for a completely fluid star to obtain the perturbed gravitational potential, then use three-dimensional finite-element simulations (via FEniCSx) of one octant of the crust volume to solve the elastic equilibrium equations for an anisotropic shear modulus with a small anisotropic perturbation ⟨ψ⟩. The crust is modeled with an angle-averaged shear modulus μ = κρ (κ = 10¹⁶ cm² s⁻²) and a bulk modulus K = cₛ²ρ, with mass densities at the bottom (2 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³) and top (10¹² g cm⁻³) of the crust.

## Key numbers
- Neutron star mass: 1.4 M⊙; radius: 10 km
- Crust thickness: outermost ∼ 1 km layer
- Mass density at core-crust interface (bottom): ρ_bottom = 2 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³
- Mass density at crust-ocean interface (top): ρ_top = 10¹² g cm⁻³
- Shear modulus constant: κ = 10¹⁶ cm² s⁻²
- Achievable ellipticity from anisotropic crust: a few × 10⁻⁹
- Maximum ellipticity a traditional isotropic crust can support: e_max ∼ 10⁻⁶ (from Gittins 2024, Morales 2022, Ushomirsky 2000)
- Most constraining upper limits on ellipticity for nearby rapidly spinning pulsars: e_min ∼ 10⁻⁹ (from Abbott 2022)
- Breaking strain of neutron star crust: as large as 0.1 (from Horowitz 2009)

## Context
This work extends the earlier two-dimensional study by Morales (2023) that used a constant-density crust model, now implementing three-dimensional finite-element simulations with a realistic density profile and polytropic equation of state. The results confirm that small crustal anisotropies can produce ellipticities in the observationally interesting range between the maximum isotropic crust ellipticity (∼10⁻⁶, from Gittins 2024, Morales 2022, Ushomirsky 2000) and the most constraining upper limits (∼10⁻⁹, from Abbott 2022), making such mountains potentially detectable by current ground-based gravitational-wave detectors. The approach follows the perturbative framework of Gittins, Andersson, and Jones (2021) and the crust-breaking energy release ideas of Franco (2000) and Fattoyev (2018).
