# Accessing ground state and excited states energies in many-body system after symmetry restoration using quantum computers

> We explore the possibility to perform symmetry restoration with the variation after projection technique on a quantum computer followed by additional post-processing. The final goal is to develop configuration interaction techniques based on many-body trial states pre-optimized on a quantum computer. We show how the projection method used for symmetry restoration can prepare optimized states that could then be employed as initial states for quantum or hybrid quantum-classical algorithms. We use the quantum phase estimation and quantum Krylov approaches for the post-processing. The latter method combined with the quantum variation after projection (Q-VAP) leads to very fast convergence towards the ground-state energy. The possibility to access excited states energies is also discussed. Illustrations of the different techniques are made using the pairing hamiltonian.

# Accessing ground state and excited states energies in many-body system after symmetry restoration using quantum computers

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that symmetry restoration via quantum projection (Q-VAP) can prepare optimized many-body states on quantum computers, and that combining these states with quantum Krylov post-processing yields very fast convergence toward the ground-state energy, though the Quantum Phase Estimation method's precision is insensitive to the quality of the initial state and the Q-VAP approach actually reduces the number of accessible excited states compared to simpler initial states like Hartree-Fock.

## Method

The authors implement a hybrid quantum-classical variation after projection (Q-VAP) method on a quantum computer emulator, using the pairing Hamiltonian with 8 particles on 8 doubly-degenerate single-particle levels as a test system. They first prepare a BCS-type trial state via independent qubit rotations, then apply Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE) as a symmetry restoration projector to enforce particle number conservation, and finally perform variational optimization of the projected state using the COBYLA optimizer with a cost function that includes a Lagrange multiplier for particle number constraint. For post-processing, they compare QPE and quantum Krylov approaches applied to Hartree-Fock, Q-PAV (projection after variation), and Q-VAP initial states.

## Key numbers

- System: 8 particles (4 pairs) on N=8 doubly-degenerate equidistant single-particle levels with spacing Δe, pairing strength g/Δe ranging from 0.2 to 1.2 in steps of 0.1
- The Q-VAP method achieves energy precision (ΔE/E) closer to exact than Q-PAV or BCS across the entire coupling range, with the black solid line indicating exact result (ΔE/E = 0)
- For QPE, the energy precision scales as δE = π/(2^{n_q-1} τ_QPE), where τ_QPE = 2π/(E_max - E_min); each additional ancillary qubit halves the bin size in energy
- The total propagation time for QPE scales as τ_QPE_tot(n_q) = (2^{n_q} - 1)τ_QPE, and the total number of operations scales as N_tot = (2^{n_q} - 1)N_op
- Trotter-Suzuki time step used: Δτ Δe ≈ 10^{-2}
- Tolerance parameter ε_tol = 10^{-3} for the particle number constraint in the variational procedure
- The overlap with the ground state follows: p_GS^HF < p_GS^Q-PAV < p_GS^Q-VAP

## Context

This work extends the quantum symmetry restoration technique proposed by Lacroix (2020) and Siwach (2021) by combining it with variational optimization on a quantum computer, creating the quantum analogues of classical PAV and VAP methods used in nuclear structure physics (Ring and Schuck 1980, Blaizot and Ripka 1986, Bender 2003). The pairing Hamiltonian test case connects to prior quantum computing studies by Ovrutin (2003, 2007), Lacroix (2020), and Ruiz Guzman (2021). The paper enables future development of configuration interaction techniques based on many-body trial states pre-optimized on quantum computers, though the authors note that QPE's resource scaling makes it impractical for the NISQ era and that quantum Krylov methods may offer a more viable alternative for post-processing.
