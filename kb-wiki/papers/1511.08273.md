# Influence of binding energies of electrons on nuclear mass predictions

> Nuclear mass contains a wealth of nuclear structure information, and has been widely employed to extract the nuclear effective interactions. The known nuclear mass is usually extracted from the experimental atomic mass by subtracting the masses of electrons and adding the binding energy of electrons in the atom. However, the binding energies of electrons are sometimes neglected in extracting the known nuclear masses. The influence of binding energies of electrons on nuclear mass predictions are carefully investigated in this work. If the binding energies of electrons are directly subtracted from the theoretical mass predictions, the rms deviations of nuclear mass predictions with respect to the known data are increased by about $200$ keV for nuclei with $Z, N\geqslant 8$. Furthermore, by using the Coulomb energies between protons to absorb the binding energies of electrons, their influence on the rms deviations is significantly reduced to only about $10$ keV for nuclei with $Z, N\geqslant 8$. However, the binding energies of electrons are still important for the heavy nuclei, about $150$ keV for nuclei around $Z=100$ and up to about $500$ keV for nuclei around $Z=120$. Therefore, it is necessary to consider the binding energies of electrons to reliably predict the masses of heavy nuclei at an accuracy of hundreds of keV.

# Influence of binding energies of electrons on nuclear mass predictions

## Key claim

If binding energies of electrons are directly subtracted from theoretical nuclear mass predictions, the rms deviation from known data increases by about 200 keV for nuclei with Z, N ≥ 8; however, if the Coulomb energy between protons is used to absorb the electron binding energies, the rms deviation increase is reduced to only about 10 keV for the same set of nuclei, though the effect remains significant for heavy nuclei (about 150 keV at Z=100 and up to 500 keV at Z=120).

## Method

The authors took theoretical nuclear masses from three existing mass models (RMF, DZ28, and WS4) and converted them to atomic masses both with and without including the electron binding energy B_e(Z) calculated from a fitted formula. They then computed the rms and mean deviations between these theoretical atomic masses and the experimental atomic masses from AME12 for two groups of nuclei: those with Z, N ≥ 8 and those with Z ≥ 60. In a second approach, they fitted the Coulomb energy formula from the WS4 model to the electron binding energies for all known nuclei with Z, N ≥ 8, obtaining a fitted B_e^fit, and subtracted only the residual difference B_e − B_e^fit from the theoretical masses.

## Key numbers

- Direct subtraction of electron binding energies increases rms deviations by about 200 keV for nuclei with Z, N ≥ 8 (specifically: RMF* rms = 2.441 MeV vs RMF rms = 2.217 MeV; DZ28* rms = 0.583 MeV vs DZ28 rms = 0.394 MeV; WS4* rms = 0.501 MeV vs WS4 rms = 0.298 MeV).
- For nuclei with Z ≥ 60, direct subtraction increases rms deviations by about 350 keV and mean deviations by about 500 keV (e.g., WS4* rms = 0.618 MeV vs WS4 rms = 0.242 MeV).
- Using Coulomb energies to absorb electron binding energies reduces the rms deviation change to within about 10 keV for nuclei with Z, N ≥ 8 (WS4# rms = 0.304 MeV vs WS4 rms = 0.298 MeV).
- For heavy nuclei, the residual difference B_e − B_e^fit is about 150 keV for nuclei around Z=100 and up to about 500 keV for nuclei around Z=120.
- The binding energy of electrons reaches about 760 keV for uranium.

## Context

This work addresses a systematic correction that has been neglected in several nuclear mass models, including the relativistic mean-field (RMF) model of Geng (2005), the Duflo-Zuker (DZ) model of Duflo (1995), and the Weizsacker-Skyrme (WS) series of Wang (2010-2014). The authors show that as mass model accuracy has improved to rms deviations around 500 keV or less, the electron binding energy correction becomes non-negligible. The study enables more reliable mass predictions for heavy and superheavy nuclei (Z ≥ 100), which are critical for understanding the r-process path in astrophysics and for future experiments at radioactive ion beam facilities.
