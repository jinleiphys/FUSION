# Laser Spectroscopy for the Study of Exotic Nuclei

> Investigation into the properties and structure of unstable nuclei far from stability remains a key avenue of research in modern nuclear physics. These efforts are motivated by the continual observation of unexpected structure phenomena in nuclei with unusual proton-to-neutron ratios. In recent decades, laser spectroscopy techniques have made significant contributions in our understanding of exotic nuclei in different mass regions encompassing almost the entire nuclear chart. This is achieved through determining multiple fundamental properties of nuclear ground and isomeric states, such as nuclear spins, magnetic dipole and electric quadrupole moments and charge radii, via the measurement of hyperfine structures and isotope shifts in the atomic or ionic spectra of the nuclei of interest. These properties, when measured with sufficient precision for a long range of isotopes, offer prominent tests of recently developed state-of-the-art theory and help to stimulate new developments to improve the many-body methods and nucleon-nucleon interactions at the core of these models. With the aim of exploring more exotic short-lived nuclei located ever closer to the proton and neutron driplines, laser spectroscopy techniques, with their continuous technological developments towards higher resolution and higher sensitivity, are extensively employed at current- and next-generation radioactive ion beam facilities worldwide. Ongoing efforts in parallel promise to make even more exotic species available for study at next-generation facilities. Very recently, an innovative application of laser spectroscopy on molecules containing short-lived nuclei has been demonstrated offering additional opportunities for several fields of research, e.g. fundamental symmetry studies and astrophysics.

## Key claim

Laser spectroscopy techniques can determine multiple fundamental nuclear properties (spins, electromagnetic moments, charge radii) in a nuclear model-independent way, and their application to over one thousand isotopes and isomers has provided stringent tests for state-of-the-art nuclear theory, though the abstract's claim of covering "almost the entire nuclear chart" is qualified by the fact that these measurements remain limited to nuclei producible at radioactive ion beam facilities and that many regions of the chart remain unexplored.

## Method

The paper reviews the application of laser spectroscopy to exotic nuclei, where hyperfine structures and isotope shifts in atomic or ionic spectra are measured to extract nuclear spins, magnetic dipole moments, electric quadrupole moments, and changes in mean-square charge radii. Multiple experimental approaches are described including collinear laser spectroscopy, collinear resonance ionization spectroscopy, in-source laser ionization spectroscopy, in-gas-cell and in-gas-jet laser ionization spectroscopy, and radiation-detected resonance ionization spectroscopy, each deployed at different radioactive ion beam facilities worldwide. The review covers measurements performed on nuclei from helium (Z=2) through nobelium (Z=102) using these techniques, with a comprehensive table listing all published laser spectroscopy measurements before October 2022.

## Key numbers

* Over one thousand ground- and long-lived isomeric states of unstable nuclei have been investigated by laser spectroscopy techniques, out of roughly three thousand species producible at radioactive ion beam facilities.
* Laser spectroscopy measurements exist for nuclei from helium (Z=2) through nobelium (Z=102), covering elements from He to No in the periodic table.
* The review documents measurements of nuclear spins (I), magnetic dipole moments (mu), electric quadrupole moments (Q_s), and changes in mean-square charge radii (delta<r^2>) for isotopes across the nuclear chart.
* For mercury isotopes specifically, measurements cover A=177-207 for odd-A isotopes and A=178-208 for even-A isotopes, including isomer shifts that provided early evidence for shape coexistence.
* Laser spectroscopy of radium monofluoride (RaF) at ISOLDE-CERN has demonstrated that certain molecules can enhance sensitivity to parity- and time reversal-violating nuclear properties by more than three orders of magnitude compared to atomic systems.

## Context

This review extends the series of comprehensive reviews on laser spectroscopy in nuclear physics, following previous editions by Otten (1989), Billowes and Campbell (1995), Cheal and Flanagan (2010), and Campbell, Moore and Pearson (2016). It covers developments since the 2016 review, including new measurements on isotopes such as calcium (Ca-radii2016, Ca-radii2019), nickel (Ni-radii2022), copper (Cu-radii2020), and nobelium (No-radii2018), as well as the emerging field of radioactive molecules for fundamental symmetry studies. The work enables future systematic studies of nuclei closer to the proton and neutron driplines at next-generation facilities including FRIB at MSU, FAIR at Darmstadt, and SPIRAL2 at GANIL, and opens opportunities for using short-lived radioactive molecules in tests of fundamental symmetries and astrophysics.
