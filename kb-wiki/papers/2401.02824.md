# Nuclear mass predictions using machine learning models

> The exploration of nuclear mass or binding energy, a fundamental property of atomic nuclei, remains at the forefront of nuclear physics research due to limitations in experimental studies and uncertainties in model calculations, particularly when moving away from the stability line. In this work, we employ two machine learning (ML) models, Support Vector Regression (SVR) and Gaussian Process Regression (GPR), to assess their performance in predicting nuclear mass excesses using available experimental data and a physics-based feature space. We also examine the extrapolation capabilities of these models using newly measured nuclei from AME2020 and by extending our calculations beyond the training and test set regions. Our results indicate that both SVR and GPR models perform quite well within the training and test regions when informed with a physics-based feature space. Furthermore, these ML models demonstrate the ability to make reasonable predictions away from the available experimental data, offering results comparable to the model calculations. Through further refinement, these models can be used as reliable and efficient ML tools for studying nuclear properties in the future.

## Key claim
The paper claims that Support Vector Regression (SVR) and Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) models, when supplied with a physics-based feature space of up to 12 inputs, can predict nuclear mass excesses with root-mean-square errors as low as 0.14 MeV for training and 0.26 MeV for test sets, and can extrapolate to newly measured nuclei with errors around 0.67–0.74 MeV. However, the abstract glosses over the condition that extrapolation performance degrades significantly for nuclei far from the training region, especially for SVR models, and that the models do not inherently satisfy Garvey-Kelson mass relations without explicit physical constraints.

## Method
The authors trained SVR and GPR models on mass excess data from AME2020 for nuclei with Z, N ≥ 8 (2386 nuclei total), using 75% (1789 nuclei) for training and 25% (597 nuclei) for testing. They constructed a physics-based feature space of up to 12 inputs including Z, N, A, A^(2/3), (N-Z)/A, valence nucleon numbers, promiscuity factor, odd-even indicators, and shell orbital indices. Extrapolation was tested on 71 newly measured nuclei from AME2020 and on neutron-rich regions beyond experimental data, comparing predictions to the mic-mac model WS4, the non-relativistic BSk24 interaction, and relativistic DD-PCX calculations.

## Key numbers
- GPR-12 achieved root-mean-square (rms) errors of 0.14 MeV for the training set and 0.26 MeV for the test set, using 12 physics-based features.
- SVR-12 achieved rms errors of 0.23 MeV for the training set and 0.39 MeV for the test set, using the same 12 features.
- For the extrapolation set of 71 newly measured nuclei from AME2020, GPR-12 gave an rms error of 0.67 MeV and SVR-12 gave 0.74 MeV.
- Using only 5 features (Z, N, A, A^(2/3), (N-Z)/A), GPR-5 gave rms errors of 0.91 MeV (training) and 1.08 MeV (test), while SVR-5 gave 2.40 MeV (training) and 2.55 MeV (test).
- For the extrapolation set with 5 features, GPR-5 gave an rms error of 1.48 MeV and SVR-5 gave 2.31 MeV.
- Increasing features from 5 to 12 improved extrapolation performance by 54.73% for GPR and 67.96% for SVR.
- With a 25% training and 75% test split, GPR-12 gave rms errors of 0.16 MeV (train) and 0.79 MeV (test); SVR-12 gave 0.13 MeV (train) and 0.91 MeV (test).

## Context
This work extends prior machine learning applications to nuclear mass prediction, such as Support Vector Machines by Clark (2006) which yielded rms errors of 0.35–0.71 MeV, and Mixture Density Networks by other authors (2021, 2023) which achieved rms errors around 0.5–0.6 MeV. The results are comparable to microscopic-macroscopic models like WS4 (rms 0.298 MeV) and FRDM-2012 (rms 0.662 MeV), and to self-consistent mean-field models with rms errors between 0.5 and 0.6 MeV (Grams 2023). The study enables future work on incorporating physical constraints such as Garvey-Kelson relations to improve extrapolation, and on using these ML models as fast alternatives to full nuclear structure calculations for r-process nucleosynthesis studies.
