# A geometrical model to describe the alpha dose rates from particulates of UO$_2$ in water

> A model investigating the role of geometry on the alpha dose rate of spent nuclear fuel has been developed. This novel approach utilises a new piecewise function to describe the probability of alpha escape as a function of particulate radius, decay range within the material, and position from the surface. The alpha dose rates were produced for particulates of radii 1 $μ$m to 10 mm, showing considerable changes in the 1 $μ$m to 50 $μ$m range. Results indicate that for decreasing particulate sizes, approaching radii equal to or less than the range of the $α$-particle within the fuel, there is a significant increase in the rate of energy emitted per unit mass of fuel material. The influence of geometry is more significant for smaller radii, showing clear differences in dose rate curves below 50 $μ$m. These considerations are essential for any future accurate prediction of the dissolution rates and hydrogen gas release, driven by the radiolytic yields of particulate spent nuclear fuel.

# A geometrical model to describe the alpha dose rates from particulates of UO$_2$ in water

## Key claim
The model demonstrates that for UO$_2$ particulates with radii approaching or below the alpha-particle range within the fuel (12.8 μm), there is a significant increase in energy emitted per unit mass, with the most pronounced geometrical effects occurring for particulates below 50 μm radius. The authors claim this piecewise analytical approach is computationally more efficient than full Monte Carlo methods like MCNPX, requiring only 81,800 random particle paths versus 500,000 to converge for a 50 μm radius particulate.

## Method
The authors developed a piecewise analytical function for the probability of alpha escape from spherical UO$_2$ particulates, based on spherical cap surface area ratios between the fuel sphere and the decay sphere. They combined this with SRIM-derived Bragg curves for alpha particles in UO$_2$ and water, using a 2D sphere-point-picking method to generate random decay positions and escape trajectories, then calculating energy deposition in concentric spherical water layers around the particulate.

## Key numbers
- Alpha-particle range within UO$_2$ (δ): 12.8 μm
- Particulate radii studied: 1 μm to 10 mm
- Activity used for comparisons: 4.73 × 10^8 Bq g⁻¹
- Decay energy used: 5.3 MeV
- For a 50 μm radius particulate, the model required 81,800 random paths versus 500,000 for MCNPX to converge
- Total dose rate over 40 μm water layer for 50 μm particulate was within 0.7% of the Tribet (2017) MCNPX result
- Interface refinement step sizes tested: 1 μm, 0.5 μm, and 0.1 μm showed no significant difference in dose rate curves
- Asymptotic average probability of escape for an infinitely large (planar) surface: 0.25

## Context
This work extends the planar alpha dosimetry model of Siberry (2021) to spherical geometries, addressing a gap in literature for particulates in the 1-50 μm range. It directly compares with the MCNPX Monte Carlo simulations of Tribet (2017) for 100 μm, 500 μm, and 1 mm diameter particulates, and with the spherical model of Poulesquen (2006). The model enables prediction of radiolytic yields and hydrogen generation from particulate spent nuclear fuel in wet storage scenarios, particularly relevant to Sellafield Magnox sludge and Hanford K Basin sludge.
