# Interplay of relativistic and nonrelativistic transport in atomically precise segmented graphene nanoribbons

> Graphene's isolation launched explorations of fundamental relativistic physics originating from the planar honeycomb lattice arrangement of the carbon atoms, and of potential technological applications in nanoscale electronics. Bottom-up fabricated atomically-precise segmented graphene nanoribbons, SGNRs, open avenues for studies of electrical transport, coherence, and interference effects in metallic, semiconducting, and mixed GNRs, with different edge terminations. Conceptual and practical understanding of electric transport through SGNRs is gained through nonequilibrium Green's function (NEGF) conductance calculations and a Dirac continuum model that absorbs the valence-to-conductance energy gaps as position-dependent masses, including topological-in-origin mass-barriers at the contacts between segments. The continuum model reproduces the NEGF results, including optical Dirac Fabry-Perot (FP) equidistant oscillations for massless relativistic carriers in metallic armchair SGNRs, and an unequally-spaced FP pattern for mixed armchair-zigzag SGNRs where carriers transit from a relativistic (armchair) to a nonrelativistic (zigzag) regime. This provides a unifying framework for analysis of coherent transport phenomena and interpretation of forthcoming experiments in SGNRs.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a one-dimensional Dirac continuum model with position-dependent masses, including topological mass-barriers at segment interfaces, can reproduce tight-binding nonequilibrium Green's function (TB-NEGF) conductance calculations for segmented graphene nanoribbons, unifying relativistic and nonrelativistic transport regimes under a single framework, but only when the mass barriers are used instead of electrostatic potential barriers (which are ineffective due to Klein tunneling).

## Method

The authors performed tight-binding nonequilibrium Green's function (TB-NEGF) conductance calculations for three-segment atomically-precise graphene nanoribbons (SGNRs) with armchair and zigzag edge terminations, varying segment widths and lead configurations. They then developed a one-dimensional Dirac-Fabry-Perot (DFP) continuum model that incorporates valence-to-conduction energy gaps as position-dependent masses in the Dirac Hamiltonian, including mass barriers at the interfaces between segments, and compared the DFP conductance patterns to the TB-NEGF results. For zigzag segments, the model required additional potential shifts to produce the vanishing gap and nonrelativistic behavior.

- Graphene lattice constant: \(a_0 = 0.246\) nm; hopping parameter: \(t = 2.7\) eV

## Context

This work extends prior theoretical predictions of band gaps in narrow armchair GNRs (Fujita 1996, Son 2006, Wang 2007, Wakabayashi 2010) and builds on recent experimental advances in bottom-up fabrication of atomically-precise segmented GNRs (Ruffieux 2012, Huang 2012, Narita 2014). The DFP model provides a unifying framework that goes beyond the optical Fabry-Perot theory for massless carriers (previously applied to carbon nanotubes by Liang 2001 and graphene 2D p-n junctions by Rickhaus 2013) by incorporating massive relativistic and nonrelativistic regimes, enabling interpretation of forthcoming coherent transport experiments in SGNRs and guiding the development of graphene nanoelectronics.
