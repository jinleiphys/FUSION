# Interaction and heat exchange in two-component relativistic fluid

> A model of two-component relativistic fluid is considered, and the thermal nature of coupling between the fluid constituents is outlined. This thermal coupling is responsible for non-ideality of the fluid composite where the components are not fully independent. The interaction between particles is reflected only in the equation of state of each component, but it deals nothing with the coupling between the fluid components and does not influence the hydrodynamic motion. A general form of two-fluid decomposition is formulated for arbitrary interacting system.

# Interaction and heat exchange in two-component relativistic fluid

## Key claim

The coupling between components of a two-component relativistic fluid arises from heat exchange between them, not from inter-particle interactions; the coefficient Q in the stress-energy tensor, which quantifies non-ideality, is shown via thermo field dynamics to correspond to irreducible Hamiltonian terms that represent thermal excitation exchange, and this thermal coupling is distinct from the interaction between particles which only affects the equation of state of each component individually.

## Method

The authors start from the general stress-energy tensor of a two-component relativistic fluid and identify the coefficient Q as the marker of non-ideality. They quantize the field variables μ_ν and Θ_ν as bosonic operators, construct the Hamiltonian, and diagonalize it using a transformation that mixes the two component operators. By applying the formalism of thermo field dynamics with tilde-conjugation and thermal doublets, they demonstrate that the Hamiltonian contains irreducible terms proportional to ω_k that cannot be eliminated and correspond to heat exchange between components. Separately, they use statistical mechanics with mean-field theory to show that inter-particle interactions modify the equation of state through effective mass M_* and effective chemical potential μ_* but do not generate the coupling term Q.

## Key numbers

- The coupling coefficient Q is defined as Q = (1/z)(∂P/∂z) where z² = -μ^ν Θ_ν, and non-zero Q implies non-ideality of the two-fluid system.
- The relative velocity between components is given by w = √(1 - μ²Θ²/z⁴).
- The Hamiltonian of the non-ideal system contains terms ω_k = λ_k sin(η_k - θ_k) where λ_k = 2Q_k/(XY), and these terms are irreducible to ideal-fluid form.
- The zero-point vibration energy of the non-ideal system is W₀' = (1/2) Σ_k {ε_k^a + ε_k^b + ω_k}.

## Context

This work extends the two-fluid relativistic hydrodynamics formalism of Carter (1989) and the stress-energy tensor formulation of Comer and Langlois (1995), which were applied to relativistic superfluidity (Israel, 1981; Khalatnikov and Lebedev, 1982) and proton-neutron nuclear hydrodynamics (Carter and Jonsson, 2003). The paper resolves a dispute noted in the literature (Khalatnikov and Lebedev, 1982; Israel, 1982) about whether the dependence on relative velocity w in the pressure is physically meaningful, by proving that the coupling arises from thermal contact rather than particle interactions. This enables a clear distinction between two-fluid models with and without coupling, and provides a framework for applying the two-fluid decomposition to strongly-interacting systems such as superfluid nuclear matter, where the mean-field approach of Serot and Walecka (1986) must be used rather than treating the warm component as an ideal gas of thermal excitations.
