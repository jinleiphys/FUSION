# Zipf's law in Nuclear Multifragmentation and Percolation Theory

> We investigate the average sizes of the $n$ largest fragments in nuclear multifragmentation events near the critical point of the nuclear matter phase diagram. We perform analytic calculations employing Poisson statistics as well as Monte Carlo simulations of the percolation type. We find that previous claims of manifestations of Zipf's Law in the rank-ordered fragment size distributions are not born out in our result, neither in finite nor infinite systems. Instead, we find that Zipf-Mandelbrot distributions are needed to describe the results, and we show how one can derive them in the infinite size limit. However, we agree with previous authors that the investigation of rank-ordered fragment size distributions is an alternative way to look for the critical point in the nuclear matter diagram.

## Key claim

The paper argues that previous claims of Zipf's law (a simple power law with exponent λ=1) in rank-ordered fragment size distributions from nuclear multifragmentation are not supported; instead, the more general Zipf-Mandelbrot distribution is required, and the observation of λ=1 in experimental data is "more coincidental than profound" and not a reliable signature of the critical point, especially for the small system sizes typical of nuclear fragmentation.

## Method

The authors performed analytic calculations assuming independent Poisson emission probabilities for cluster production at the critical point, where the cluster size distribution follows a pure power law n(A) ∝ A^(-τ). They also conducted Monte Carlo simulations of three-dimensional bond percolation on simple cubic lattices of two sizes: a small lattice with 216 sites (matching nuclear fragmentation system sizes) and a large lattice with 10^6 sites (to reduce finite-size effects). The average sizes of the n-th largest fragments ⟨A_n⟩ were computed as a function of rank n and fitted to both Zipf's law and the Zipf-Mandelbrot distribution.

## Key numbers

- Critical point of the nuclear matter liquid-gas transition: T_c = 8.3 ± 0.2 MeV, ρ_c/ρ_0 = 0.35 ± 0.1
- For an infinite system with τ=2, the average size of the n-th largest fragment follows ⟨A^∞_n⟩ = a (n-1)^(-1), which is a Zipf-Mandelbrot distribution with k=-1 and λ=1, not Zipf's law
- For the Poissonian ansatz with finite systems (V=10^2 to 10^6), the fitted Zipf exponent λ decreases from λ(τ=2)=1.25 to λ(τ=3)≈0.75
- For the percolation model on the small lattice (216 sites), the critical bond probability is p_c≈0.253, with effective τ≈2.14 at the critical point
- For the percolation model on the large lattice (10^6 sites), the critical bond probability is p_c=0.249, and the ratio ⟨A_{n+1}⟩/⟨A_n⟩ shows a significant minimum at p_c for n≥2
- The relation between the Zipf-Mandelbrot exponent λ and the critical exponent τ is approximately λ ≈ 1/(τ-1) for 2 ≤ τ ≤ 3

## Context

This work extends the analysis of critical phenomena in nuclear multifragmentation beyond the traditional power-law mass distributions studied by Kleine Berkenbusch (2001) and others. It directly contradicts the claims of Watanabe (1996) and Ma (1999, 2004) that Zipf's law applies to rank-ordered fragment sizes and indicates self-organized criticality. The paper shows that for the small systems typical of nuclear fragmentation (around 200 nucleons), the Zipf-Mandelbrot signal is not as reliable as the previously explored scaling analysis from Elliott (2001), Kleine Berkenbusch (2001), and Mader (2003). The results enable future work to use the ratio ⟨A_{n+1}⟩/⟨A_n⟩ as a potential critical point indicator for very large systems, while cautioning against overinterpreting Zipf-law fits for small nuclear systems.
