# Probability for synthesis of superheavy nuclei with Z=121

> In this study the empirical method \cite{RN464} is used for calculating the evaporation residue (ER) cross section in the synthesis of superheavy nuclei (SHN). The superheavy nuclei examined in this work fall within the range $Z=112-118$. The theoretical calculations show good agreement with the experimental data. Furthermore, this model, along with an investigation of the optimal incident energy (OIE), is used to calculate the ER cross section for a hypothetical heavy system with $Z=121$. Five promising combinations are suggested for synthesis of SHN with $Z=121$: (1) ${^{50}}\mathrm{Ti}+{^{252}}\mathrm{Es}$, with the maximum ER cross section $σ_{3n}=24.5~\mathrm{fb}$, at the optimal incident energy, $\mathrm{OIE}=230~\mathrm{MeV}$; (2) ${^{50}}\mathrm{Ti}+{^{254}}\mathrm{Es}$, with the maximum ER cross section $σ_{3n}=11.8~\mathrm{fb}$, at the optimal incident energy, $\mathrm{OIE}=229~\mathrm{MeV}$; (3) ${^{51}}\mathrm{V}+{^{251}}\mathrm{Cf}$, with the maximum ER cross section $σ_{3n}=1.2~\mathrm{fb}$, at the optimal incident energy, $\mathrm{OIE}=238~\mathrm{MeV}$; (4) ${^{51}}\mathrm{V}+{^{249}}\mathrm{Cf}$, with the maximum ER cross section $σ_{3n}=1.0~\mathrm{fb}$, at the optimal incident energy, $\mathrm{OIE}=238~\mathrm{MeV}$; and (5) ${^{54}}\mathrm{Cr}+{^{247}}\mathrm{Bk}$, with the maximum ER cross section $σ_{2n}=0.9~\mathrm{fb}$, at the optimal incident energy, $\mathrm{OIE}=243~\mathrm{MeV}$.

## Key claim
The empirical method, previously validated against experimental data for superheavy nuclei with Z=112-118, predicts that the most promising combination for synthesizing Z=121 is ⁵⁰Ti + ²⁵²Es with a maximum 3-neutron evaporation residue cross section of 24.5 fb at an optimal incident energy of 230 MeV, though the authors note that producing the required einsteinium targets is experimentally challenging due to their availability only in microgram scales.

## Method
The authors employed the empirical method for calculating evaporation residue (ER) cross sections, which involves three stages: capture cross section calculation using the Wong formula with proximity potential including nuclear deformations, compound nucleus formation probability using a logistic function of the Coulomb factor, and survival probability calculated from the competition between neutron emission and fission using the Jackson formula for neutron evaporation probabilities. The model was first validated against experimental data for 10 reaction systems producing superheavy nuclei with Z=112-118 using ⁴⁸Ca projectiles on actinide targets, then applied to predict ER cross sections for 19 projectile-target combinations aimed at producing Z=121.

## Key numbers
- ⁵⁰Ti + ²⁵²Es: maximum ER cross section σ₃ₙ = 24.5 fb at OIE = 230 MeV (center-of-mass)
- ⁵⁰Ti + ²⁵⁴Es: maximum ER cross section σ₃ₙ = 11.8 fb at OIE = 229 MeV
- ⁵¹V + ²⁵¹Cf: maximum ER cross section σ₃ₙ = 1.2 fb at OIE = 238 MeV
- ⁵¹V + ²⁴⁹Cf: maximum ER cross section σ₃ₙ = 1.0 fb at OIE = 238 MeV
- ⁵⁴Cr + ²⁴⁷Bk: maximum ER cross section σ₂ₙ = 0.9 fb at OIE = 243 MeV
- Surface energy constant γ₀ = 0.9180 MeV fm⁻² and surface asymmetric constant kₛ = 0.7546 used in the proximity potential
- For validation, calculated σ₃ₙ = 2.54 pb for ⁴⁸Ca + ²³⁸U at E_CN* = 36.6 MeV compared to experimental σ₃ₙ = 0.5 pb (with uncertainties)

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study on synthesizing Z=119 and Z=120 (Zargini and Seyyedi, 2024) to the next element Z=121, using the same empirical method originally developed by Siwek-Wilczynska and Wilczynski (2004). The predicted cross sections are generally lower than those from other theoretical approaches: for ⁵⁰Ti + ²⁵²Es, the present work gives 24.48 fb (3n) compared to 4 pb (2n) reported by Gharaei and Zangi (2022) and 8.62 fb reported by others, while for ⁵⁴Cr + ²⁴⁹Bk the present work gives 0.39 fb (2n) versus 0.03803 fb (3n) from Nasirov et al. (2022). The results suggest that producing Z=121 will require targets heavier than californium, particularly einsteinium isotopes, which are currently available only in microgram quantities, approximately three orders of magnitude less than typically required for superheavy nucleus synthesis.
