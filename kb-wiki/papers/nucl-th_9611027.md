# Medium effects in high energy heavy-ion collisions

> The change of hadron properties in dense matter based on various theoretical approaches are reviewed. Incorporating these medium effects in the relativistic transport model, which treats consistently the change of hadron masses and energies in dense matter via the scalar and vector fields, heavy-ion collisions at energies available from SIS/GSI, AGS/BNL, and SPS/CERN are studied. This model is seen to provide satisfactory explanations for the observed enhancement of kaon, antikaon, and antiproton yields as well as soft pions in the transverse direction from the SIS experiments. In the AGS heavy-ion experiments, it can account for the enhanced $K^+/π^+$ ratio, the difference in the slope parameters of the $K^+$ and $K^-$ transverse kinetic energy spectra, and the lower apparent temperature of antiprotons than that of protons. This model also provides possible explanations for the observed enhancement of low-mass dileptons, phi mesons, and antilambdas in heavy-ion collisions at SPS energies. Furthermore, the change of hadron properties in hot dense matter leads to new signatures of the quark-gluon plasma to hadronic matter transition in future ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC/BNL.

# Medium effects in high energy heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
The paper reviews theoretical evidence that partial restoration of chiral symmetry in hot dense matter, manifested through reduced hadron masses and modified energies via scalar and vector fields, can consistently explain a broad range of experimental observations from SIS/GSI, AGS/BNL, and SPS/CERN heavy-ion collisions, though the interpretation relies on model-dependent transport simulations rather than direct measurement of in-medium hadron properties.

## Method
The authors incorporate medium-modified hadron masses and energies into a relativistic transport model derived from the nonlinear sigma-omega (Walecka) model, where nucleon effective masses are reduced by the attractive scalar field and energies are shifted by the repulsive vector field. For kaons and antikaons, the in-medium dispersion relations are obtained from an SU(3)L x SU(3)R chiral Lagrangian combined with the Walecka model, using the Kaplan-Nelson term for scalar attraction and a vector potential from the omega meson coupling. The transport model then simulates heavy-ion collisions from SIS (around 1 GeV/nucleon) through AGS (around 10 GeV/nucleon) to SPS (higher energies), comparing final-state particle yields, spectra, and ratios to experimental data.

## Key numbers
- At normal nuclear matter density rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}, the quark condensate decreases by approximately 1/3 from its vacuum value of about -(245 MeV)^3, based on the Feynman-Hellmann theorem with Sigma_{pi N} ≈ 45 MeV.
- The kaon mass increases by about 10 MeV at normal nuclear matter density when using Sigma_{KN} ≈ 350 MeV; the antikaon mass decreases with density due to its attractive vector potential.
- The soft equation of state gives nucleon effective mass m_N*/m_N = 0.83 and compression modulus K = 200 MeV at saturation; the stiff EOS gives m_N*/m_N = 0.68 and K = 380 MeV.
- The kaon vector potential is 1/3 of the nucleon vector potential, i.e., (1/3)(g_omega/m_omega)^2 rho_N, consistent with the constituent quark model where a kaon contains one light quark versus three in a nucleon.
- The kaon-nucleon sigma term Sigma_{KN} ranges from 370 to 405 MeV for nucleon strangeness content y ≈ 0.1-0.2, and up to about 450 MeV for y ≈ 0.33 from lattice calculations.

## Context
This review extends earlier work on hadron properties in dense matter using the Walecka model (QHD-I), Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approaches (Machleidt 1989, 1990), and QCD sum-rule studies (Cohen 1991, Furnstahl 1992, Jin 1993, 1994). It builds on the Kaplan-Nelson (1986) chiral Lagrangian treatment of kaon condensation and the delta-hole model for pions (Pandharipande 1981). The paper enables future searches for quark-gluon plasma signatures at RHIC by showing that medium-modified hadron properties produce observable effects, such as enhanced low-mass dileptons, phi mesons, and antilambdas, that must be disentangled from signals of the deconfinement transition.
