# Particle production and AGK relations in the Color Glass Condensate picture

> In this talk, we discuss some general properties of particle production in a field theory coupled to strong time dependent sources, and techniques to compute the spectrum of the produced particles in such theories. We also discuss the application of these results to the description of hadron or heavy ion collisions in the Color Glass Condensate framework.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Abramovsky-Gribov-Kancheli (AGK) cancellations, originally derived in reggeon field theory, are a general consequence of the factorization of diagrams into connected subdiagrams and therefore apply to particle production in the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) framework, provided one integrates out the number of produced particles.

## Method

The authors derive the generating function for particle multiplicity in a scalar field theory coupled to strong time-dependent sources, showing that its logarithm can be expressed as a sum over connected cut vacuum-vacuum diagrams. They then extend this formalism to QCD, showing that at leading order the average gluon multiplicity can be computed from the retarded solution of the classical Yang-Mills equations with null initial conditions, and that quark production at leading order requires solving the Dirac equation in the classical gluon background field.

## Key numbers

- The hard color sources ρ₁,₂ are both of order g⁻² in the saturated regime.
- The number of produced gluons at leading order scales like g⁻², while the number of produced quarks scales like g⁰ (i.e., is a Next-to-Leading Order effect).
- The average number of cut subdiagrams is ⟨n⟩_cut = Σ_r b_r / g², where b_r/g² is the sum of all connected cut vacuum-vacuum diagrams with exactly r cut lines.
- The probability of having p cut subdiagrams is given by a Poissonian distribution: R_p = exp(-Σ_r b_r/g²) × (1/p!) × (Σ_r b_r/g²)^p.

## Context

This work extends the AGK cancellations from reggeon field theory (AGK, 1970s) to the CGC framework for high-energy hadronic collisions. It builds on the classical solutions for gluon production in nucleus-nucleus collisions (GLO, 2001) and quark production (QLO, 2004). The results enable full Next-to-Leading Order calculations of particle yields using retarded solutions of classical equations of motion and small fluctuation equations, and they establish the connection between NLO divergences in the gluon yield and the JIMWLK evolution of the source distributions.
