# Distribution Functions of the Nucleon and Pion in the Valence Region

> We provide an experimental and theoretical perspective on the behavior of unpolarized distribution functions for the nucleon and pion on the valence-quark domain; namely, Bjorken-x \gtrsim 0.4. This domain is key to much of hadron physics; e.g., a hadron is defined by its flavor content and that is a valence-quark property. Furthermore, its accurate parametrization is crucial to the provision of reliable input for large collider experiments. We focus on experimental extractions of distribution functions via electron and muon inelastic scattering, and from Drell-Yan interactions; and on theoretical treatments that emphasize an explanation of the distribution functions, providing an overview of major contemporary approaches and issues. Valence-quark physics is a compelling subject, which probes at the heart of our understanding of the Standard Model. There are numerous outstanding and unresolved challenges, which experiment and theory must confront. In connection with experiment, we explain that an upgraded Jefferson Lab facility is well-suited to provide new data on the nucleon, while a future electron ion collider could provide essential new data for the mesons. There is also great potential in using Drell-Yan interactions, at FNAL, J-PARC and GSI, to push into the large-x domain for both mesons and nucleons. We argue furthermore that explanation, in contrast to modeling and parametrization, requires a widespread acceptance of the need to adapt theory: to the lessons learnt already from the methods of nonperturbative quantum field theory; and a fuller exploitation of those methods.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a truly explanatory, rather than merely parametric, understanding of valence-quark distribution functions in the nucleon and pion (Bjorken‑x ≳ 0.4) requires a widespread acceptance of nonperturbative quantum field theory methods, because perturbative QCD alone cannot compute the pointwise behavior of these distributions, and no current computation agrees with the QCD parton model predictions.

## Method

The authors review the experimental extraction of unpolarized parton distribution functions (PDFs) from charged‑lepton deep inelastic scattering (electron and muon) and from Drell‑Yan interactions, focusing on the valence region. They present the kinematic formalism (Bjorken scaling, the parton model, DGLAP evolution equations) and discuss how global fits (e.g., CTEQ6L) parametrize PDFs at leading, next‑to‑leading, and next‑to‑next‑to‑leading order. On the theoretical side, they survey nonperturbative approaches (QCD‑motivated models, lattice QCD, Dyson‑Schwinger equations) that aim to explain, rather than just parametrize, the valence‑quark distributions.

## Key numbers

- The valence region is defined as Bjorken‑x ≳ 0.4, because thereafter the gluon distribution is much smaller than the valence u‑quark distribution in the proton.
- The proton structure function is extremely well known for x ≲ 0.7.
- Data evaluations typically impose Q² ≥ 4 GeV² and W ≥ 3.5 GeV to avoid large target‑mass corrections.
- The CTEQ6L leading‑order evaluation is shown at Q² = 10 GeV² for the quark flavor contributions.
- The proton structure function is plotted for three Q² values: 2 GeV², 10 GeV², and 100 GeV², showing weak Q² dependence in the valence region.
- The normalization conditions for valence quarks are ∫₀¹ dx u_v(x) = 2 and ∫₀¹ dx d_v(x) = 1.
- The strong coupling constant is given by α_s(t)/(4π) = 1/(β₀ t) – β₁ ln(t)/(β₀³ t²) + O(1/t³), with t = ln(Q²/Λ_QCD²), β₀ = 11 – 2n_f/3, β₁ = 102 – 38n_f/3.
- The x = 1 value of each distribution function is invariant under DGLAP evolution; the ratio lim_{x→1} d_v(x)/u_v(x) is a scale‑invariant QCD discriminator.

## Context

This review extends the long line of work on parton distribution functions that began with the SLAC deep inelastic scattering experiments (Bloom 1969, Breidenbach 1969) and the parton model (Bjorken 1967, Feynman 1969). It contrasts with global‑fit approaches (e.g., CTEQ, Martin 2007) by emphasizing the need for nonperturbative QCD explanations, particularly for the pion, where the FNAL Drell‑Yan experiment (Conway 1989) found a puzzling discrepancy with QCD parton model predictions. The paper enables future experimental programs at the upgraded Jefferson Lab, a future electron‑ion collider, and Drell‑Yan experiments at FNAL, J‑PARC, and GSI to probe the large‑x domain for both mesons and nucleons.
