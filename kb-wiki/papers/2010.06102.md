# Quantum entanglement in nuclear Cooper pair tunneling with $γ$-rays

> While Josephson-like junctions, transiently established in heavy ion collisions ($τ_{coll}\approx10^{-21}$ s) between superfluid nuclei --through which Cooper pair tunneling ($Q$-value $Q_{2n}$) proceeds mainly in terms of successive transfer of entangled nucleons-- is deprived from the macroscopic aspects of a supercurrent, it displays many of the special effects associated with spontaneous symmetry breaking in gauge space (BCS condensation), which can be studied in terms of individual quantum states and of tunneling of single Cooper pairs. From the results of studies of one- and two- neutron transfer reactions carried out at energies below the Coulomb barrier we estimate the value of the mean square radius (correlation length) of the nuclear Cooper pair. A quantity related to the largest distance of closest approach for which the absolute two-nucleon tunneling cross section is of the order of the single-particle one. Furthermore, emission of $γ$-rays of (Josephson) frequency $ν_J=Q_{2n}/h$ distributed over an energy range $\hbar/τ_{coll}$ is predicted.

# Quantum entanglement in nuclear Cooper pair tunneling with γ-rays

## Key claim
The paper identifies a nuclear analogue of the alternating-current (ac) Josephson effect in heavy-ion collisions between superfluid nuclei, predicting that γ-rays of frequency ν = Q₂ₙ/h will be emitted from the transient Josephson-like junction formed during quasielastic two-neutron transfer, but only when the distance of closest approach is approximately equal to the nuclear Cooper pair correlation length ξ ≈ 13.5 fm, which is the largest distance for which the two-nucleon tunneling cross section remains comparable to the single-nucleon one within a factor of about two.

## Method
The authors analyzed experimental data from one- and two-neutron transfer reactions ¹¹⁶Sn + ⁶⁰Ni at twelve bombarding energies between 140.60 MeV and 167.95 MeV, measured at θ_c.m. = 140° using magnetic and gamma spectrometers. They performed first- and second-order DWBA calculations including both successive (dominant) and simultaneous transfer channels with microscopically calculated optical potentials and two-nucleon spectroscopic amplitudes, then extended the formalism to include coupling to the electromagnetic field in the dipole approximation to compute the γ-emission strength function. A macroscopic calculation using the Ginzburg-Landau formalism and a two-level atom analogy for the Josephson junction was also performed for comparison.

## Key numbers
- Correlation length of the nuclear Cooper pair: ξ ≈ 13.49 fm, extracted from the distance of closest approach D₀ where σ₂ₙ ≈ (π/4)² × σ₁ₙ
- Q-value for two-neutron transfer: Q₂ₙ = 1.307 MeV for the reaction ¹¹⁶Sn + ⁶⁰Ni → ¹¹⁴Sn + ⁶²Ni
- Collision time: τ_coll ≈ 0.5 × 10⁻²¹ s at E_c.m. ≈ 154.26 MeV
- Macroscopic calculation predicts γ-emission cross section: dσ_γ/dΩ|_θ=140° ≈ 0.96 μb/sr at E_γ = 1.307 MeV, with width ℏ/τ_coll ≈ 1.3 MeV
- Microscopic calculation predicts reduced γ-strength function: dσ_γ/dΩ|_θ=140° ≈ 0.57 μb/sr, centroid at 1.1 MeV, FWHM 3.6 MeV
- Microscopic calculation predicts observable γ-strength function: dσ_γ/dΩ|_θ=140° ≈ 5.18 μb/sr, peaked at 4 MeV, FWHM 5 MeV
- Dipole moment from microscopic calculation: ⟨d⟩ = -e × 9.36 fm (⟨r⟩ ≈ 10.52 fm)
- Number of photons emitted per cycle: N ≈ 3.71 × 10⁻⁴
- Experimental two-neutron transfer cross section at E_c.m. = 154.26 MeV: σ₂ⁿ_exp = 2.58 mb/sr (theory: 2.35 mb/sr)
- Experimental one-neutron transfer cross section at E_c.m. = 154.26 MeV: σ₁ⁿ_exp = 7.25 mb/sr (theory: 6.03 mb/sr)

## Context
This work extends the study of Josephson-like effects in nuclear systems previously explored by Goldanskii (1968), Dietrich (1970, 1971), Hara (1971), Kleber (1971), Weiss (1979), and von Oertzen (2001), as well as the theoretical framework of Broglia (2004). It builds directly on experimental measurements of one- and two-neutron transfer reactions by Montanari (2014, 2016) and Corradi (2020) using magnetic and gamma spectrometers. The paper enables experimental searches for nuclear Josephson radiation in heavy-ion collisions between superfluid nuclei, predicting γ-ray emission at energies around 1-4 MeV with cross sections of order μb/sr that could be detected with modern gamma-ray detector arrays. The work also provides a quantitative connection between the nuclear Cooper pair correlation length and the distance of closest approach in sub-barrier transfer reactions.
