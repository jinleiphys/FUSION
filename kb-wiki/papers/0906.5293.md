# From 0 to 5000 in $2\times 10^{-24}$ seconds: Entropy production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We review what is known about the contributions to the final entropy from the different stages of a relativistic nuclear collision, including recent results on the decoherence entropy and the entropy produced during the hydrodynamic phase by viscous effects. We then present a general framework, based on the Husimi distribution function, for the calculation of entropy growth in quantum field theories, which is applicable to the earliest ("glasma") phase of the collision during which most of the entropy is generated. The entropy calculated from the Husimi distribution exhibits linear growth when the quantum field contains unstable modes and is asymptotically equal to the Kolmogorov-Sinaï (KS) entropy. We outline how the approach can be used to investigate the problem of entropy production in a relativistic heavy-ion reaction from first principles.

# From 0 to 5000 in $2\times 10^{-24}$ seconds: Entropy production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
The Husimi distribution provides a general framework for calculating entropy growth in quantum field theories applicable to the earliest "glasma" phase of a relativistic heavy-ion collision, where the entropy calculated from the Husimi distribution exhibits linear growth when the quantum field contains unstable modes and is asymptotically equal to the Kolmogorov-Sinai (KS) entropy. The authors estimate that at least half of the final entropy must be generated during the thermalization process, but the exact contributions from each stage remain model-dependent.

## Method
The authors review five distinct stages of entropy production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions: decoherence of initial nuclear wave functions, thermalization of the partonic plasma (glasma), dissipation due to shear viscosity in hydrodynamic expansion, hadronization with bulk viscosity, and viscous hadronic freeze-out. They then present a formalism based on the Husimi distribution, which is a convolution of the Wigner function with a minimum-uncertainty Gaussian wave packet, to define a coarse-grained entropy (Husimi-Wehrl entropy) that can be calculated for quantum field systems containing unstable modes. The entropy growth rate is shown to be independent of the squeezing parameter and given by the sum of exponential growth rates of all unstable modes, matching the Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy from classical dynamical systems.

## Key numbers
- Final entropy per unit rapidity for the 6% most central Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=200$ GeV: $(dS/dy)_{\rm f}=5600 \pm 500$ at midrapidity, determined from hadron spectra and HBT correlations.
- Alternative determination from hadron abundances yields $(dS/dy)_{\rm ch}=5100 \pm 200$, with the 10% difference attributed to entropy production during hadronic freeze-out.
- Decoherence entropy estimate at midrapidity for central Au+Au: $(dS/dy)_{\rm deco} \approx 1,500$, roughly one quarter of the final entropy.
- Entropy at thermalization (approximately 1 fm/c): $(dS/dy)_{\rm th} \approx 4,500$, implying about 15% entropy increase during the hydrodynamic phase.
- Shear viscosity bounds from RHIC elliptic flow data: $\eta/s \leq (2-3)/4\pi$.
- Characteristic decoherence time: $\tau_{\rm deco} = c Q_s^{-1}$ with $c<1$, corresponding to less than 0.2 fm/c.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of entropy production in heavy-ion collisions by Muller (2005) and Fries (2008) on decoherence entropy, and by Romatschke (2007), Gavin (2007), Luzum (2008), Song (2008), and Lacey (2009) on shear viscosity bounds. The Husimi-Wehrl entropy formalism, building on Husimi (1940) and Wehrl (1978), provides a first-principles approach to calculating entropy growth during the glasma phase, which has been previously studied using classical Yang-Mills fields by Muller (1992) and Biro (1993) and shown to be strongly chaotic with extensive KS entropy by Bolte (1999). The approach enables future calculations of entropy production using lattice-regularized Yang-Mills equations with Gaussian fluctuations, as initiated by Gong (1993), and could incorporate initial quantum fluctuations around classical glue fields as studied by Fukushima (2006).
