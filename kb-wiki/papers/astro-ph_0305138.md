# Realistic Neutrino Opacities for Supernova Simulations With Correlations and Weak Magnetism

> Advances in neutrino transport allow realistic neutrino interactions to be incorporated into supernova simulations. We add tensor couplings to relativistic RPA calculations of neutrino opacities. Our results reproduce free-space neutrino-nucleon cross sections at low density, including weak magnetism and recoil corrections. In addition, our opacities are thermodynamically consistent with relativistic mean field equations of state. We find antineutrino mean free paths that are considerably larger then those for neutrinos. This difference depends little on density. In a supernova, this difference could lead to an average energy of $\barν_μ$ that is larger than that for $ν_μ$ by an amount that is comparable to the energy difference between $ν_μ$ and $\barν_e$

# Realistic Neutrino Opacities for Supernova Simulations With Correlations and Weak Magnetism

## Key claim

The authors find that antineutrino mean free paths are considerably larger than those for neutrinos in dense neutron-rich matter, and this difference depends little on density; however, this result represents a minimal calculation consistent with the TM1 equation of state, as it does not include residual interactions from pion exchange or short-range correlations that could further increase the mean free path at high densities.

## Method

The authors add tensor (weak magnetism) couplings to relativistic Random Phase Approximation (RPA) calculations of neutrino opacities for mu and tau neutrinos and antineutrinos scattering from neutron-rich matter. They use the TM1 relativistic mean field interaction to describe the nuclear medium, calculating the Hartree and RPA response functions at finite temperature and density, and then compute transport mean free paths averaged over a thermal neutrino spectrum. The formalism includes vector, axial-vector, and tensor contributions to the weak interaction vertex, with the RPA corrections accounting for nucleon correlations mediated by meson exchange.

## Key numbers

- Antineutrino mean free paths are considerably larger than neutrino mean free paths; the fractional difference R = (<lambda_bar> - <lambda>)/<lambda> grows rapidly with temperature and is large at high temperatures (e.g., at T=50 MeV the difference is substantial, as shown in Figure 10).
- At a density of rho = 0.01 fm^-3 and T=5 MeV, the uniform phase is unstable, producing a large peak in the RPA cross section near energy transfer q_0 = 0; this peak decreases by rho = 0.1 fm^-3 where the uniform phase is stable.
- The mean free path increases with density between n_B approximately 0.2 and 0.5 fm^-3 because of the rapid decrease of the nucleon effective mass M* with density.
- At T=5 MeV, the energy-averaged mean free path is somewhat smaller than the mean free path evaluated at average energy E = pi*T.
- The average energy difference between nu_mu and bar-nu_mu is comparable to the approximately 1.5 MeV difference in average energy of nu_x (all four bar-nu_mu, bar-nu_tau, nu_mu, and nu_tau combined) compared to bar-nu_e found in modern simulations.

## Context

This work extends previous relativistic RPA calculations of neutrino opacities by Horowitz and Wehrberger (1991, 1992) and TM1-based RPA calculations that did not include weak magnetism (TM1RPA). It also incorporates weak magnetism corrections previously studied in free-space neutrino-nucleon cross sections by other authors (weakmag, weakmag2). The results are thermodynamically consistent with the TM1 relativistic mean field equation of state (TM1eos), satisfying the authors' proposed minimum requirements for realistic opacities: agreement with free-space cross sections at low density and consistency with the equation of state. The work enables future supernova simulations to incorporate these more realistic opacities, and highlights the need for explicit calculations of neutrino opacities in the nonuniform (crust) phase of neutron-rich matter, where collective density oscillations may greatly enhance opacities.
