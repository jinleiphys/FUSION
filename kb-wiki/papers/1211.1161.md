# An analysis of the influence of background subtraction and quenching on jet observables in heavy-ion collisions

> Subtraction of the large background in reconstruction is a key ingredient in jet studies in high-energy heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC. Here we address the question to which extent the most commonly used subtraction techniques are able to eliminate the effects of the background on the most commonly discussed observables at present: single inclusive jet distributions, dijet asymmetry and azimuthal distributions. We consider two different background subtraction methods, an area-based one implemented through the FastJet pack- age and a pedestal subtraction method, that resemble the ones used by the experimental collaborations at the LHC. We also analyze different ways of defining the optimal parame- ters in the second method. We use a toy model that easily allows variations of the background characteristics: average background level and fluctuations and azimuthal structure, but cross- checks are also done with a Monte Carlo simulator. Furthermore, we consider the influence of quenching using Q-PYTHIA on the dijet observables with the different background subtrac- tion methods and, additionally, we examine the missing momentum of particles. The average background level and fluctuations affect both single inclusive spectra and dijet asymmetries, although differently for different subtraction setups. A large azimuthal modulation of the background has a visible effect on the azimuthal dijet distributions. Quenching, as imple- mented in Q-PYTHIA, substantially affects the dijet asymmetry but little the azimuthal dijet distributions. Besides, the missing momentum characteristics observed in the experiment are qualitatively reproduced by Q-PYTHIA.

## Key claim

The paper investigates to what extent commonly used background subtraction techniques can eliminate the effects of the fluctuating heavy-ion background on jet observables, finding that the average background level and fluctuations affect both single inclusive spectra and dijet asymmetries differently for different subtraction setups, and that quenching substantially affects dijet asymmetry but has little effect on azimuthal dijet distributions.

## Method

The authors generated pp collision events at √s = 2.76 TeV using Q-PYTHIA (based on PYTHIA v6.4.18 with the DW tune) and embedded them in a toy model background with a thermal-like spectrum matched to a power law, allowing control of average background level, fluctuations, and azimuthal structure. Two background subtraction methods were applied: an area-based method implemented through the FastJet package and a pedestal subtraction method resembling the CMS approach, with two different procedures for fixing the parameters (κ and E_T,jets) in the pedestal method. Jets were reconstructed using the anti-k_t algorithm with R = 0.3 (or R = 0.4 for missing momentum studies), and the effects of quenching were studied using Q-PYTHIA with different quenching strengths.

## Key numbers

- Background multiplicity set to dN/dη = 2100, with event-by-event fluctuations following a Gaussian distribution with dispersion of 4% of the mean value
- For the toy model background, temperature T values of 0.7, 0.9, and 1.2 GeV were used to control average energy density per unit area (ρ) and region-to-region fluctuations (σ_jet) on a jet of R = 0.4
- Leading jet requirement: E_T1 ≥ 120 GeV; subleading jet requirement: E_T2 ≥ 30 GeV
- Dijet asymmetry cut: Δφ > 2π/3 to select only dijet pairs in opposite direction
- For missing transverse momentum: charged particles with E_Tmin = 0.5 GeV, acceptance |η| < 2.4 for particles and |η| < 1.6 for the dijet pair, jet resolution parameter R = 0.4
- For inclusive jet spectra, the energy at which the subtracted spectrum deviates from PYTHIA is approximately 45 GeV for T = 0.7, 60 GeV for T = 0.9, and 90 GeV for T = 1.2 GeV
- For the pedestal method with κ = 2.2, the input and reconstructed spectra differ less than 10% for E_T > 140 GeV

## Context

This work extends the discussion by Cacciari (2011) on how background fluctuations in a single event can affect dijet energy imbalance and the extraction of medium properties. The study uses a simplified toy model to systematically investigate how different background characteristics (average level, fluctuations, azimuthal modulation) and different subtraction techniques (area-based FastJet method and pedestal method resembling CMS) affect jet observables, enabling a better understanding of which medium characteristics must be carefully modeled in phenomenological analyses of heavy-ion jet data from ATLAS and CMS.
