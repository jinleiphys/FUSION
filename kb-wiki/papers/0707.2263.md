# The Role of Surface Tension for the Equation of State of Quark-Gluon Bags

> The temperature and chemical potential dependent surface tension of bags is introduced into the gas of quark-gluon bags model. The suggested model is solved analytically. It resolves a long standing problem of a unified description of the first and second order phase transition with the cross-over. Such an approach is necessary to model the complicated properties of quark-gluon plasma and hadronic matter from the first principles of statistical mechanics. In addition to the deconfinement phase transition, we found that at the curve of a zero surface tension coefficient there must exist the surface induced phase tranition of the 2-nd or higher order, which separates the pure quark gluon plasma (QGP) from the cross-over states. Thus, the present model predicts that the critical endpoint of quantum chromodynamics is the tricritical endpoint.

## Key claim

The paper claims that introducing a temperature- and chemical-potential-dependent surface tension into the gas of quark-gluon bags model resolves the long-standing problem of unifying first-order phase transitions, second-order phase transitions, and crossovers in a single analytical framework. The model predicts that the critical endpoint of quantum chromodynamics is actually a tricritical endpoint, with an additional surface-induced phase transition of second or higher order occurring along the curve where the surface tension coefficient vanishes.

## Method

The author modifies the gas of bags model by adding a surface tension term to the continuous part of the mass-volume spectrum of quark-gluon bags, parameterized as σ(T) = (σ₀/T) · [(T_cep − T)/T_cep]^(2k+1) with k = 0, 1, 2, ... The isobaric partition function is solved analytically, and the phase structure is determined by analyzing the rightmost singularity of this partition function, which can be either a simple pole (hadronic phase) or an essential singularity (quark-gluon plasma phase). The analysis is extended from zero baryonic density to non-zero baryonic chemical potential by including baryonic charge in the discrete and continuous parts of the spectrum.

## Key numbers

- The Fisher exponent for the statistical multifragmentation model is τ_SMM = 1.825 ± 0.025, which contrasts with the Fisher droplet model value τ_FDM ≈ 2.16
- For the surface tension parameterization, the exponent 2k+1 appears in the expression σ(T) = (σ₀/T) · [(T_cep − T)/T_cep]^(2k+1), where k = 0, 1, 2, ...
- The bag model pressure used for quark-gluon plasma is p_Q = (π²/90)T⁴[95/2 + (10/π²)(μ_B/T)² + (5/9π⁴)(μ_B/T)⁴] − B
- For the surface-induced phase transition, the second derivative of Δ scales as [(T−T_cep)/T_cep]^((2k+1)/κ − 2) for τ ≤ 1 + κ/(2k+1), and as [(T−T_cep)/T_cep]^((3−2τ)/(τ−1)) for τ ≥ 1 + κ/(2k+1)
- The power κ describing the bag's effective surface is a constant less than 1, which can differ from the typical value 2/3

## Context

This work extends the gas of bags model originally developed by Gorenstein (1981) and addresses its inability to introduce a critical endpoint in the phase diagram. The model is inspired by the Fisher droplet model (Fisher, 1967) and the statistical multifragmentation model (Bondorf, 1995), both of which successfully describe phase transitions using surface tension. The Hills and Dales Model (Bugaev, 2004, 2005) provides the theoretical basis for the temperature-dependent surface entropy used here. The results enable phenomenological modeling of the deconfinement phase diagram that can be compared with lattice QCD data at zero baryonic chemical potential (Karsch, 2003) and experimental measurements at RHIC, SPS, and FAIR facilities.
