# Heavy flavors under extreme conditions in high energy nuclear collisions

> Heavy flavor hadrons have long been considered as a probe of the quark gluon plasma created in high energy nuclear collisions. In this paper we review the heavy flavor properties under extreme conditions and the realization in heavy ion experiments. After a short introduction on heavy flavor properties in vacuum, we emphasize the cold and hot nuclear matter effects on heavy flavors, including shadowing effect, Cronin effect and nuclear absorption for the former and Debye screening and regeneration for the latter. Then we discuss, in the frame of transport and coalescence models, these medium induced changes in open and closed heavy flavors in nuclear collisions and the comparison with nucleon-nucleon collisions. Considering the extremely strong electromagnetic and rotational fields generated in non-central nuclear collisions, which are widely studied in recent years, we finally investigate their effects on heavy flavor production and evolution in high energy nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that heavy flavor hadrons serve as sensitive probes of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) created in high-energy nuclear collisions, but the authors emphasize that the interpretation depends on a factorization of cold and hot nuclear matter effects, with heavy quarks being almost entirely produced in initial hard scatterings and then modified by the medium through energy loss, coalescence, and regeneration processes.

## Method

The authors review theoretical frameworks for heavy flavor production and evolution in nuclear collisions, including perturbative QCD calculations (LO, NLO, FONLL), non-relativistic and relativistic potential models for quarkonium and heavy baryon bound states, transport equations with collisional and radiative energy loss terms, and coalescence models for hadronization. They also discuss the effects of strong electromagnetic and rotational fields generated in non-central collisions on heavy flavor production and evolution, using minimal coupling in transport and potential models.

## Key numbers

- QCD crossover temperature from lattice simulation at zero baryon density: T_c = 155 MeV
- Heavy quark masses: m_Q ~ (1-5) GeV, much larger than QGP temperature T ~ (0.3-0.5) GeV at RHIC and LHC
- Heavy quark production time scales: ~0.07 fm for charm quarks, ~0.02 fm for bottom quarks, much shorter than QGP formation time tau_0 ~ 0.5 fm/c
- Colliding energies: sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV at RHIC, sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV at LHC
- Model parameters for Cornell potential: alpha = 0.4105, sigma = 0.2 GeV^2, beta = 0.318 GeV (bottomonium) and 2.06 GeV (charmonium), gamma = 1.982 GeV
- Quark masses used in potential model: m_b = 4.7 GeV, m_c = 1.29 GeV
- Calculated charmonium masses: J/psi(1S) = 3.102 GeV (experimental 3.097 GeV), psi(2S) = 3.720 GeV (experimental 3.686 GeV)
- Calculated bottomonium masses: Upsilon(1S) = 9.459 GeV (experimental 9.460 GeV), Upsilon(2S) = 9.977 GeV (experimental 10.023 GeV)
- Calculated average radii: J/psi(1S) = 0.427 fm, Upsilon(1S) = 0.214 fm
- Predicted heavy baryon masses: Omega_ccc = 4.797 GeV, Omega_bbb = 14.363 GeV
- Predicted heavy baryon root-mean-squared radii: Omega_ccc = 0.289 fm, Omega_bbb = 0.153 fm

## Context

This review extends the long-standing program of using heavy flavor hadrons as QGP probes, building on the sequential dissociation picture for charmonium states from Satz (2005) and lattice-simulated heavy quark potentials from Petreczky (2010). The paper synthesizes cold nuclear matter effects (shadowing from Mueller (1985), Cronin effect from Cronin (1974), nuclear absorption from Gerschel (1988)) with hot medium effects (Debye screening and regeneration) and incorporates recent interest in electromagnetic and rotational field effects from non-central collisions, including the chiral magnetic effect from Fukushima (2008) and chiral vortical effect from Kharzeev (2007, 2010). The work enables future studies at higher energies such as the Future Circular Collider (FCC) with sqrt(s_NN) = 39 TeV, where in-medium charm production through gluon fusion may become significant.
