# Uncovering the sign of nuclear deformations: Determination of prolate or oblate shape via low-energy $α$ inelastic scattering

> Background: Understanding nuclear shape is a crucial problem in nuclear physics. In particular, determining the sign of quadrupole deformation, i.e., whether prolate or oblate, remains a challenging problem. Purpose: Our aim is to propose a method for determining the sign of quadrupole deformation using $α$ inelastic scattering data and to demonstrate its effectiveness. Method: Our approach is the standard coupled-channel method based on the macroscopic model. We utilize the nuclear reorientation effect, a phenomenon associated with the self coupling of excited states, as a probe sensitive to the sign of deformation. Results: We first provide an overview of how the reorientation effect influences inelastic scattering cross sections, and numerically confirm its validity in realistic cases. We then demonstrate that the sign of deformation can be uniquely determined from inelastic scattering cross section data. Conclusion: Our technique offers a systematic approach for determining the sign of deformation in both stable and unstable nuclei. The broad applicability of $α$ inelastic scattering will make it a valuable tool to study shape of nuclei, especially unstable nuclei.

## Key claim

The sign of quadrupole deformation (prolate vs. oblate) can be uniquely determined from low-energy α inelastic scattering cross sections by exploiting the nuclear reorientation effect, provided that the second-order contribution to the T-matrix interferes destructively for prolate and constructively for oblate shapes.

## Method

The authors performed standard coupled-channel calculations based on a macroscopic model with Woods-Saxon potentials, restricted primarily to the 0⁺ and 2⁺ states of an axially deformed target. They analyzed α + ¹⁵⁴Sm scattering at 50 MeV, fitting potential parameters to elastic scattering data and then optimizing the deformation parameter β₂ separately for positive and negative signs against inelastic scattering data from Hendrie et al. (1968). The nuclear reorientation effect was isolated by comparing calculations with and without the 2⁺ diagonal and off-diagonal coupling potentials proportional to β₂.

## Key numbers

- For α + ¹⁵⁴Sm at 50 MeV, the best-fit prolate deformation parameter is β₊^(opt) = +0.25, while the best-fit oblate parameter is β₋^(opt) = −0.16.
- The experimental deformation parameter from B(E2)↑ = 4.32 e²b² (Raman 2001) is β_exp = 0.283 after conversion from the nuclear deformation parameter β̄_exp = 0.339.
- The Woods-Saxon potential parameters used are V₀ = 65.9 MeV, W₀ = 27.3 MeV, R₀ = 1.44 × 154^(1/3) fm, and a = 0.70 fm.
- For forward-angle-only fitting (θ_max ≈ 32°), the optimized parameters shift to β₊^(opt) = +0.27 and β₋^(opt) = −0.21; for sparse data, β₊^(opt) = +0.25 and β₋^(opt) = −0.17.

## Context

This work extends the use of the nuclear reorientation effect, previously studied by Hillis (1976) and Baker (1977), from stable targets to a systematic method applicable to unstable nuclei. It contrasts with Coulomb excitation at sub-barrier energies (Alder 1966, 1975) and hyperfine spectroscopy of electric quadrupole moments, which are difficult to apply to neutron-rich unstable nuclei. The method enables systematic determination of the sign of deformation across the nuclear chart, complementing magnitude-only measurements from B(E2) values (Raman 2001) and total reaction cross sections (Takechi 2012, 2014; Watanabe 2014).
