# Self-consistent calculation of nuclear photoabsorption cross section: Finite amplitude method with Skyrme functionals in the three-dimensional real space

> The finite amplitude method (FAM), which we have recently proposed (T. Nakatsukasa, T. Inakura, and K. Yabana, Phys. Rev. C 76, 024318 (2007)), simplifies significantly the fully self-consistent RPA calculation. Employing the FAM, we are conducting systematic, fully self-consistent response calculations for a wide mass region. This paper is intended to present a computational scheme to be used in the systematic investigation and to show the performance of the FAM for a realistic Skyrme energy functional. We implemented the method in the mixed representation in which the forward and backward RPA amplitudes are represented by indices of single-particle orbitals for occupied states and the spatial grid points for unoccupied states. We solve the linear response equation for a given frequency. The equation is a linear algebraic problem with a sparse non-hermitian matrix, which is solved with an iterative method. We show results of the dipole response for selected spherical and deformed nuclei. The peak energies of the giant dipole resonance agree well with measurements for heavy nuclei, while they are systematically underestimated for light nuclei. We also discuss the width of the giant dipole resonance in the fully self-consistent RPA calculation.

## Key claim
The finite amplitude method (FAM) enables fully self-consistent random-phase approximation (RPA) calculations for nuclear photoabsorption cross sections without explicitly constructing the residual two-body interaction, but the peak energies of the giant dipole resonance are systematically underestimated for light nuclei while agreeing well with measurements for heavy nuclei.

## Method
The authors implemented the FAM in a mixed representation where forward and backward RPA amplitudes are expressed using indices of occupied single-particle orbitals and three-dimensional spatial grid points for unoccupied states. They solved the linear response equation for a given complex frequency using an iterative generalized conjugate residual (GCR) method, evaluating the residual field via finite differences of the static mean-field Hamiltonian rather than constructing the residual two-body kernel explicitly. The Skyrme energy functional was used identically for both ground-state and linear response calculations, including spin-orbit, Coulomb, and time-odd-density terms, with calculations performed inside a spherical box using an adaptive coordinate grid.

## Key numbers
- For $^{16}$O with SkM$^*$ functional and $\gamma=1$ MeV, the GDR peak energy from the photoabsorption cross section converged to 19.165 MeV with width 3.330 MeV at $R_\mathrm{box}=30$ fm.
- For $^{40}$Ca with SkM$^*$ functional and $\gamma=1$ MeV, the GDR peak energy from the photoabsorption cross section converged to 17.959 MeV with width 3.014 MeV at $R_\mathrm{box}=30$ fm.
- The typical number of GCR iterations needed to reach convergence (relative residue $<10^{-6}$) was about $10^3$ around the giant dipole resonance excitation energy region.
- The FAM parameter $\eta_\mathrm{Lin}=10^{-4}$ was used, with the method insensitive to values in the range $10^{-2}$ to $10^{-6}$.
- The adaptive coordinate transformation reduced grid points from 27,609 to 11,777 for $R_\mathrm{box}=15$ fm and from 221,119 to 39,321 for $R_\mathrm{box}=30$ fm.

## Context
This work extends the finite amplitude method previously proposed by Nakatsukasa, Inakura, and Yabana (2007) from a simplified interaction to realistic Skyrme energy functionals, enabling systematic fully self-consistent RPA calculations across a wide mass region ($A \leq 100$). The approach contrasts with earlier fully self-consistent RPA calculations that were mostly restricted to spherical nuclei (Terasaki 2005, 2006; Fracasso and Colo 2005; Sil 2006; Paar et al. 2003) or required explicit construction of the residual interaction. The method enables future systematic investigations of nuclear response for both spherical and deformed nuclei, with extension to include pairing effects identified as a necessary next step for low-lying modes such as quadrupole excitations.
