# Possible neutron halo in triaxial nucleus 42Al

> A microscopic self-consistent triaxial relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (TRHBc), which simultaneously takes into account the triaxiality and pairing correlations as well as continuum effects, is established and applied to explore the novel halo phenomenon in aluminum isotopes. The experimental proton drip line and the available data of neutron separation energies and charge radii are reproduced well without any free parameters. The neutron-richest odd-odd aluminum isotope observed so far, 42Al, is predicted to be triaxially deformed with beta=0.35 and gamma=42. Its one-neutron separation energy is predicted to be 0.68 MeV, in agreement with the AME2020, and the neutron rms radius is 3.94 fm, remarkably larger than the empirical value. The density distribution of the valance neutron, which extends much farther in space than the core, suggests a possible neutron halo in 42Al. The dominant components responsible for the spatial extension of the halo are revealed by the single-neutron orbitals around the Fermi energy. A novel phenomenon, the exchange of the intermediate and short axes between the triaxial core with beta=0.38 and gamma=50, and the triaxial halo with beta=0.79 and gamma=-23, is found. Future experiments to explore the halo phenomenon and the novel shape decoupling in 42Al are highly demanded.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the neutron-richest observed odd-odd aluminum isotope, 42Al, is a possible one-neutron halo nucleus with triaxial deformation, based on predictions from a newly developed microscopic self-consistent triaxial relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (TRHBc). The claim is hedged by the qualifier that the halo is "possible" and that future experiments are needed to confirm the phenomenon and the novel shape decoupling between the core and the halo.

## Method

The authors developed the TRHBc theory, which self-consistently incorporates triaxial deformation, pairing correlations, and continuum effects by solving relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov equations in a Dirac Woods-Saxon basis. They applied this theory to aluminum isotopes using the PC-PK1 density functional (and verified with NL3*, NLSH, and PK1), with the pairing strength V0 = -342.5 MeV fm^3 and saturation density ρ_sat = 0.152 fm^-3. The blocking effects of odd nucleons were treated via the equal filling approximation, and the quasiparticle wave functions were expanded with an energy cutoff of 300 MeV and angular momentum cutoff of J_max = 19/2 ħ.

## Key numbers

- 42Al is predicted to be triaxially deformed with β = 0.35 and γ = 42° (PC-PK1).
- One-neutron separation energy S_n = 0.68 MeV, in agreement with the AME2020 value of 0.67(64) MeV.
- Neutron rms radius = 3.94 fm, remarkably larger than the empirical value.
- The triaxial core has β = 0.38 and γ = 50°, while the triaxial halo has β = 0.79 and γ = -23°, indicating an exchange of the intermediate and short axes.
- The rms radius of the halo component is 5.26 fm, and that of the core is 3.85 fm.
- The valance neutron level (level 4) has 35.3% 2p_1/2 and 11.1% 2p_3/2 components, and its rms radius is 5.4 fm.
- The halo scale S_halo for 42Al shows a sudden increase comparable to that for halo nuclei in zirconium isotopes.

## Context

This work extends the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc) developed by Zhou et al. (2010) and Li et al. (2012), which previously predicted deformed halos in neutron-rich Mg isotopes and shape decoupling between core and halo. The new TRHBc theory adds the capability to treat triaxial deformation self-consistently, going beyond the axial symmetry assumed in earlier DRHBc calculations. The prediction of a triaxial halo in 42Al with shape decoupling (including axis exchange) is more complex than the prolate-core/oblate-halo decoupling previously predicted in 42,44Mg by Zhou et al. (2010) and Li et al. (2012). The work also introduces a new "halo scale" S_halo to characterize halo phenomena in medium and heavy nuclei, validated against earlier RCHB results for zirconium isotopes by Meng et al. (1998). Future experiments measuring the mass, radius, and density distribution of 42Al are needed to confirm the predictions.
