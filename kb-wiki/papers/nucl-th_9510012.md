# Quantum Chaos in Vibrational Nuclei: the Green Function Approach

> We show that the collective vibrational model of atomic nuclei displays quantum chaos. To avoid the problems related to the tunneling effects, a Green function deterministic numerical method has been used to evaluate the energy levels.

## Key claim

The collective vibrational model of atomic nuclei exhibits a transition from quantum chaos to quasi-regular behavior as a function of excitation energy, but the mixed regular-chaotic behavior at very low energies cannot be easily demonstrated by spectral statistics because only a few energy levels are present there.

## Method

The authors used the Green function numerical diagonalization (GFND) method to solve the two-dimensional Bohr-Mottelson collective vibrational Hamiltonian for the rare-earth nuclei 160Gd and 166Er. The Euclidean short-time propagator was expanded to O(ε⁴) and discretized on a finite grid, then diagonalized to obtain approximately one hundred energy levels per nucleus. The resulting level sequences were unfolded to uniform density, and the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution P(s) and spectral rigidity Δ₃(L) were computed and compared to Poisson statistics (integrable systems) and Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) statistics (chaotic systems).

## Key numbers

- 160Gd has a saddle energy of 5.56 MeV; above this saddle, at about 8 MeV, the system displays chaos, while at about 18 MeV it shows quasi-regular behavior.
- 166Er has a saddle energy of 1.89 MeV; above this saddle, at about 4 MeV, there is predominantly chaotic behavior, while at about 15 MeV regular classical trajectories predominate.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of order-to-chaos transitions in the nuclear roto-vibrational model (Manfredi et al., 1995) by focusing specifically on the collective vibrational motion and using the GFND method to avoid tunneling problems that plague direct Hamiltonian diagonalization. The results contradict the earlier analytical criterion of negative curvature of the potential energy used by other authors (Eisenberg and Greiner, 1975), which was shown to be inaccurate. The findings enable future studies of the coexistence of regular, chaotic, and collective states in atomic nuclei, particularly near the yrast line and neutron emission threshold, as noted in experimental nuclear spectroscopy.
