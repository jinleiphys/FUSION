# Electromagnetic excitation rates for nuclear isomers in a hot dense plasma

> In high neutron flux environments where isomers can be strongly populated by nucleonic reactions, isotope abundances from reaction network chains can be affected by the population of nuclear isomers. At high temperatures and densities there is the additional possibility of populating these isomers electromagnetically. Here we examine the rates for electromagnetic excitation of the isotopes of several isomers of interest both in astrophysics and applied physics (e.g. 235U, 193Ir, and 87Y,88Y). We consider six possible electromagnetic processes, namely, photo-absorption, inverse internal conversion, inelastic electron scattering, Coulomb excitation, (gamma,gamma) and (e,e'gamma) reactions. We find that for plasma temperatures kT~ 1-10 keV the electromagnetic reactions rates are negligible. Thus, we conclude that reaction network calculations do not need to include for the possibility of electromagnetically exciting nuclear isomers. This is true in both stellar and terrestrial thermonuclear explosions, as well as in plasma conditions expected at the National Ignition Facility.

## Key claim

For plasma temperatures kT ~ 1–10 keV, the electromagnetic excitation rates for nuclear isomers are negligible, so reaction network calculations do not need to include electromagnetic isomer excitation in stellar explosions, terrestrial thermonuclear explosions, or National Ignition Facility plasma conditions. This conclusion depends on the assumption that nuclei are completely stripped of their electrons in the plasma, though the authors note that even with more sophisticated calculations accounting for partial stripping, the rates remain negligible.

## Method

The authors calculated reaction rates for six electromagnetic processes (photo-absorption, inverse internal conversion, inelastic electron scattering, Coulomb excitation, (γ,γ') and (e,e'γ) reactions) using the Breit-Wigner approximation for narrow resonances, assuming a thermal Planckian photon distribution and a Fermi-Dirac electron distribution. For inverse internal conversion and inelastic electron scattering, they used relativistic hydrogenic wavefunctions with point-nucleus approximation and s-wave dominance, while for Coulomb excitation they employed the adiabaticity parameter ξ to estimate exponential suppression. The calculations were applied to isomers of 235U, 193Ir, 87Y, 88Y, and other isomers listed in Table 1 of the paper.

## Key numbers

- For the 77 eV isomer of 235U (half-life ~26 minutes), temperatures of ~150 keV would be required for the photo-absorption rate per atom to reach one-per-second
- For like-ion Coulomb scattering at kT = 1–10 keV, the adiabaticity parameter ξ ranges from 10^3 to 10^7, giving cross sections scaling as σ ~ e^(-2πξ) which is extremely small
- For inverse internal conversion estimates, the authors used an average electron density of n_e ~ 100 N_A cm^(-3), corresponding to chemical potential μ = -0.00275 keV at kT = 1 keV and μ = -0.0622 keV at kT = 10 keV
- For 89Y, the suppression factor for populating the 7/2+ state at 2529.87 keV versus directly populating the isomer at 909 keV via M4 transition is e^(-1621/kT), which is vanishingly small at kT = 1–10 keV
- The dimensionless parameter Q = ω_γ R/c for 235U photo-absorption is ~2.4 × 10^(-6)

## Context

This work extends prior studies of isomer population in high neutron flux environments, particularly following interest in the 77 eV isomer of 235U where Popeko (1973) and Mostovoi (1984) showed that fission and neutron capture cross sections differ significantly from the ground state. The paper contradicts any assumption that electromagnetic processes could compete with nucleonic reactions for isomer population at the temperatures considered, and it enables reaction network calculations to safely omit electromagnetic excitation channels for isomers in the kT ~ 1–10 keV range. The authors note that Fowler (1980) discussed the general problem of excited states in nucleosynthesis calculations, and they compare their hydrogenic internal conversion coefficients with more realistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock calculations by Band (1993).
