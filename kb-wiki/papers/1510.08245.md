# Competing $γ$-rigid and $γ$-stable vibrations in neutron rich Gd and Dy isotopes

> An exactly separable version of the Bohr Hamiltonian which combines the $γ$-stable and $γ$-rigid axial vibration-rotation is used to describe the collective properties of few neutron rich transitional nuclei. The coupling between the two types of collective motion is managed through a rigidity parameter which also influences the geometry of the shape-phase space. While the $γ$-angular part of the problem associated to axially symmetric shapes is treated within the small angles approximation and the stiff $γ$ oscillation hypothesis, the $β$ vibration is described by means of a Davidson potential. The resulting model have three free parameters not counting the scale and was successfully applied for the description of the collective spectra for few heavier isotopes of Gd and Dy. In both cases a critical nucleus was identified through a discontinuous behavior in respect to the rigidity parameter and relevant experimental observables.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a hybrid Bohr Hamiltonian combining γ-rigid and γ-stable axial vibration-rotation, with a Davidson potential in β and a harmonic oscillator in γ, can successfully describe the collective spectra of neutron-rich Gd and Dy isotopes. The key hedge is that the model's applicability is limited to nuclei where the γ-rigidity parameter χ shows a discontinuous behavior, identifying a "critical nucleus" for each isotopic chain, and that the γ-rigid limit χ=1 must be treated separately due to infinite degeneracy issues.

## Method

The authors constructed a Hamiltonian as a weighted sum of γ-rigid and γ-stable kinetic energy operators, with the weighting controlled by a rigidity parameter χ (0 ≤ χ < 1). The β vibration was described by a Davidson potential u(β)=β²+β₀⁴/β², while the γ degree of freedom was treated within the small-angles approximation using a harmonic oscillator potential u(γ)=(3a)²γ²/2. The Schrödinger equation was separated into β and γ parts, yielding analytical solutions in terms of Laguerre polynomials, and the total energy spectrum was normalized to the ground state. The model was fitted to experimental ground, β, and γ bands for three Gd isotopes (¹⁵⁸Gd, ¹⁶⁰Gd, ¹⁶²Gd) and three Dy isotopes (¹⁶⁰Dy, ¹⁶²Dy, ¹⁶⁴Dy) by minimizing the deviation σ between theoretical and experimental energies.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁵⁸Gd: χ = 3·10⁻⁴, a = 11.349, β₀ = 2.044, σ = 0.601; the ground band energies up to 20⁺ range from 1.00 (2⁺) to 48.74 (20⁺) in units of E(2⁺).
- For ¹⁶⁰Gd: χ = 0.826, a = 51.538, β₀ = 2.840, σ = 0.768; the ground band energies up to 20⁺ range from 1.00 (2⁺) to 54.44 (20⁺).
- For ¹⁶²Gd: χ = 0.092, a = 9.052, β₀ = 2.963, σ = 0.574; the ground band energies up to 20⁺ range from 1.00 (2⁺) to 55.53 (20⁺).
- For ¹⁶⁰Dy: the ground band energies up to 28⁺ range from 1.00 (2⁺) to 82.20 (28⁺); the β band head 0β⁺ is at 15.85 (theory) vs. 14.75 (experiment).
- For ¹⁶²Dy: the ground band energies up to 24⁺ range from 1.00 (2⁺) to 75.19 (24⁺); the β band head 0β⁺ is at 21.21 (theory) vs. 20.66 (experiment).
- For ¹⁶⁴Dy: the ground band energies up to 28⁺ range from 1.00 (2⁺) to 97.90 (28⁺); the β band head 0β⁺ is at 22.51 (theory) vs. 22.56 (experiment).
- The spectral ratios R₄/₂, R₀/₂, and R₂γ/₂ are defined as E(4⁺)/E(2⁺), E(0β⁺)/E(2⁺), and E(2γ⁺)/E(2⁺), respectively; in the γ-rigid limit χ→1, the γ band head ratio R₂γ/₂ approaches 1 regardless of β₀.

## Context

This work extends the hybrid γ-rigid/γ-stable model previously introduced by the same authors (Budaca and Budaca, 2015) with an infinite square well in β, by replacing it with a Davidson potential to improve versatility for transitional nuclei. The model builds on the ES-D (exactly separable Davidson) solution of Bonatsos et al. (2004) and the X(3) γ-rigid model of Bonatsos et al. (2006), and it competes with the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) descriptions of rare-earth shape phase transitions. The identification of ¹⁶⁰Gd and ¹⁶²Dy as critical nuclei through a discontinuous behavior in χ enables future systematic studies of γ-rigidity transitions in neutron-rich isotopes.
