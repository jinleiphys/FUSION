# Statistical Model for a Complete Supernova Equation of State

> A statistical model for the equation of state (EOS) and the composition of supernova matter is presented with focus on the liquid-gas phase transition of nuclear matter. It consists of an ensemble of nuclei and interacting nucleons in nuclear statistical equilibrium. A relativistic mean field model is applied for the nucleons. The masses of the nuclei are taken from nuclear structure calculations which are based on the same nuclear Lagrangian. For known nuclei experimental data is used directly. Excluded volume effects are implemented in a thermodynamic consistent way so that the transition to uniform nuclear matter at large densities can be described. Thus the model can be applied at all densities relevant for supernova simulations, i.e. rho=10^5 - 10^15 g/cm^3, and it is possible to calculate a complete supernova EOS table. The model allows to investigate the role of shell effects, which lead to narrow-peaked distributions around the neutron magic numbers for low temperatures. At larger temperatures the distributions become broad. The significance of the statistical treatment and the nuclear distributions for the composition is shown. We find that the contribution of light clusters is very important and is only poorly represented by alpha-particles alone. The results for the EOS are systematically compared to two commonly used models for supernova matter which are based on the single nucleus approximation. Apart from the composition, in general only small differences of the different EOSs are found. The differences are most pronounced around the (low-density) liquid-gas phase transition line where the distribution of light and intermediate clusters has an important effect. Possible extensions and improvements of the model are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper presents a nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) model for the supernova equation of state that can be applied across the full density range relevant for supernova simulations (10⁵–10¹⁵ g/cm³), but the authors acknowledge that the NSE description becomes questionable at densities close to saturation density where they cannot control all effects of nuclear interactions, and they aim to explore the limits of such a model rather than claim it is fully reliable in that regime.

## Method

The model treats matter as an ensemble of nuclei (using experimental masses where available and theoretical masses from the same relativistic mean field (RMF) model otherwise) plus interacting nucleons described by the TMA RMF parameter set, all in nuclear statistical equilibrium. Excluded volume effects are implemented thermodynamically consistently by treating baryons as hard spheres of volume 1/n_B⁰, with nuclei occupying volume A/n_B⁰ and unbound nucleons excluded from the interior of nuclei. The Coulomb energy is included in the Wigner-Seitz approximation, and the model covers the density range ρ = 10⁵–10¹⁵ g/cm³, temperature 0–100 MeV, and proton fraction 0–0.6, with uniform matter assumed above 20 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the line of NSE models (e.g., Clifford (1965), Ishizuka et al. (2003), Blinnikov et al. (2009)) by implementing excluded volume effects in a thermodynamically consistent way to bridge the gap to uniform nuclear matter, and systematically compares results to the two commonly used supernova EOSs: Shen et al. (1998) and Lattimer and Swesty (1991), which both use the single nucleus approximation. The model enables future investigation of how the full distribution of nuclei (including light clusters beyond α-particles) affects supernova dynamics, particularly neutrino opacities and electron capture rates, building on work by Caballero et al. (2006) and Arcones et al. (2008).
