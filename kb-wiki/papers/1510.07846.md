# Fission dynamics within time-dependent Hartree-Fock: boost-induced fission

> Background: Nuclear fission is a complex large-amplitude collective decay mode in heavy nuclei. Microscopic density functional studies of fission have previously concentrated on adiabatic approaches based on constrained static calculations ignoring dynamical excitations of the fissioning nucleus, and the daughter products. Purpose: To explore the ability of dynamic mean-field methods to describe induced fission processes, using quadrupole boosts in the nuclide $^{240}$Pu as an example. Methods: Quadrupole constrained Hartree-Fock calculations are used to create a potential energy surface. An isomeric state and a state beyond the second barrier peak are excited by means of instantaneous as well as temporally extended gauge boosts with quadrupole shapes. The subsequent deexcitation is studied in a time-dependent Hartree-Fock simulation, with emphasis on fissioned final states. The corresponding fission fragment mass numbers are studied. Results: In general, the energy deposited by the quadrupole boost is quickly absorbed by the nucleus. In instantaneous boosts, this leads to fast shape rearrangements and violent dynamics that can ultimately lead to fission. This is a qualitatively different process than the deformation-induced fission. Boosts induced within a finite time window excite the system in a relatively gentler way, and do induce fission but with a smaller energy deposition. Conclusions: The fission products obtained using boost-induced fission in time-dependent Hartree-Fock are more asymmetric than the fragments obtained in deformation-induced fission, or the corresponding adiabatic approaches.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that boost-induced fission (BIF) within time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) can induce fission in configurations where deformation-induced fission (DIF) is forbidden or inhibited, but only when the deposited collective kinetic energy is an order of magnitude larger than the static fission barrier (approximately 175-225 MeV versus ~10 MeV), because the gauge-invariant boost removes the system from the static potential energy surface.

## Method

Quadrupole-constrained Hartree-Fock calculations with the SkM* effective interaction and BCS pairing were used to create a potential energy surface for 240Pu. Instantaneous and temporally extended quadrupole gauge boosts were applied to an isomeric state (β20=0.682) and a state beyond the second barrier peak (β20=0.890), and the subsequent time evolution was simulated using the TDHF code sky3d on a 48×48×160 grid. The fission fragment mass numbers and collective kinetic energies were analyzed using a comoving spatial mask to identify post-scission products.

## Key numbers

- Threshold energy for instantaneous BIF on the isomeric state: between 175 and 200 MeV; scission occurs between 950 and 1000 fm/c for the 200 MeV boost
- Threshold energy for instantaneous BIF on the state at β20=0.890: between 200 and 225 MeV; scission occurs around 1700 fm/c
- Instantaneous BIF on the isomeric state produces symmetric fragments: 120Ag47 (mass 120.00(5), charge 47.00(5)) and 120Ag47, with extrapolated collective kinetic energy of 218(8) MeV
- Instantaneous BIF on the β20=0.890 state produces asymmetric fragments: 151Pr59 (mass 150.50(5), charge 58.78(5)) and 89Br35 (mass 89.49(5), charge 35.23(5)), with extrapolated collective kinetic energy of 189(6) MeV
- Particle number loss during postscission evolution is of order 0.1-0.2 particles; postscission fragment fluctuations in particle number are below 0.05

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous deformation-induced fission (DIF) study of 240Pu (Goddard 2015) by exploring dynamical excitation methods for configurations where fission is forbidden or inhibited within TDHF time scales. The BIF approach produces more asymmetric fragments than DIF or adiabatic approaches, and the instantaneous boost mechanism is qualitatively different from deformation-induced fission, involving violent "sloshing" dynamics with ~100 fm/c period oscillations. The results enable future studies of induced fission processes using time-dependent mean-field methods, though the authors note that pairing correlations beyond frozen-occupation approximation and collective tunneling are not included.
