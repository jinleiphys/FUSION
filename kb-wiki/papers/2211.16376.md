# The 3D structure of anisotropic flow in small collision systems at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider

> We present (3+1)D dynamical simulations of asymmetric nuclear collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC). Employing a dynamical initial state model coupled to (3+1)D viscous relativistic hydrodynamics, we explore the rapidity dependence of anisotropic flow in the RHIC small system scan at 200 GeV center of mass energy. We calibrate parameters to describe central $^3$He+Au collisions and make extrapolations to d+Au and p+Au collisions. Our calculations demonstrate that approximately 50% of the $v_3(p_T)$ difference between the measurements by the STAR and PHENIX Collaborations can be explained by the use of reference flow vectors from different rapidity regions. This emphasizes the importance of longitudinal flow decorrelation for anisotropic flow measurements in asymmetric nuclear collisions, and the need for (3+1)D simulations. We also present results for the beam energy dependence of particle spectra and anisotropic flow in d+Au collisions.

# The 3D structure of anisotropic flow in small collision systems at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider

## Key claim
Approximately 50% of the difference in triangular flow $v_3(p_T)$ measurements between the STAR and PHENIX Collaborations in small asymmetric collision systems can be explained by the different rapidity regions used for reference flow vectors, but the remaining discrepancy suggests that longitudinal flow decorrelation alone does not fully account for the experimental differences.

## Method
The authors employed a (3+1)D hybrid framework combining a 3D Monte-Carlo Glauber dynamical initial state model with (3+1)D viscous relativistic hydrodynamics (MUSIC) and the UrQMD hadronic cascade. They calibrated model parameters to reproduce observables in central $^3$He+Au collisions at 200 GeV center-of-mass energy, then made extrapolations to d+Au and p+Au systems without further parameter adjustment. The framework included pre-hydrodynamic flow, temperature-dependent shear and bulk viscosities, and a crossover equation of state (NEOS-BQS) with finite chemical potentials.

## Key numbers
- Approximately 50% of the $v_3(p_T)$ difference between STAR and PHENIX measurements can be explained by different rapidity regions used for reference flow vectors
- Elliptic flow vector correlation drops to approximately 0.6 in the $|\eta| < 0.35$ bin for central p+Au collisions, compared to approximately 0.9 for d+Au and $^3$He+Au collisions
- The model underestimates p+Au $v_n(p_T)$ by 20-30% for elliptic flow and by 40% for $v_2(p_T)$ in the $3\times2$PC method
- The model underestimates p+Au $v_2(p_T)$ by 40% across all centrality bins in the $3\times2$PC comparison
- Triangular flow correlations are significantly below unity for all three collision systems
- The nucleon width parameter was set to $B_G = 5$ GeV$^{-2}$, corresponding to a nucleon width of 0.44 fm
- The hotspot size parameter was $w_q = 0.2$ fm
- The pre-hydrodynamic flow parameter was $\alpha^{\rm pre-flow} = 0.15$
- The specific shear viscosity parameters: $(\eta/s)_{\rm norm} = 0.13$, $(\eta/s)_{\rm slope} = 1.0$, $\mu_{B, \rm scale} = 0.6$ GeV, $\alpha = 1.5$
- The bulk viscosity parameters: $(\zeta/s)_{\rm norm} = 0.08$, $T_{\rm peak} = 0.17$ GeV
- The switching energy density for particlization was $e_{\rm sw} = 0.50$ GeV/fm$^3$

## Context
This work extends previous (2+1)D hydrodynamic calculations by Habich (2014) and Shen (2016) that reproduced PHENIX flow data but could not capture longitudinal decorrelation effects. It addresses the discrepancy between PHENIX measurements (PHENIX Collaboration, 2018) and STAR measurements (Lacey, 2020; STAR Collaboration, 2022) of triangular flow in small systems. The framework builds on the 3D-Glauber+MUSIC+UrQMD model developed by Shen (2022) and Zhao (2022), which previously described hadron production across multiple collision systems. The results demonstrate that full (3+1)D simulations are essential for quantitative comparisons to experimental data from asymmetric collision systems, and enable future Bayesian calibration studies to disentangle flow decorrelation from non-flow contributions.
