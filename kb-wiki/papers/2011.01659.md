# Nuclear Moments of Germanium Isotopes around $N$ = 40

> Collinear laser spectroscopy measurements were performed on $^{69,71,73}$Ge isotopes ($Z = 32$) at ISOLDE-CERN. The hyperfine structure of the $4s^2 4p^2 \, ^3P_1 \rightarrow 4s^2 4p 5s \, ^3P_1^o$ transition of the germanium atom was probed with laser light of 269 nm, produced by combining the frequency-mixing and frequency-doubling techniques. The hyperfine fields for both atomic levels were calculated using state-of-the-art atomic relativistic Fock-space coupled-cluster calculations. A new $^{73}$Ge quadrupole moment was determined from these calculations and previously measured precision hyperfine parameters, yielding $Q_{\rm s}$ = $-$0.198(4) b, in excellent agreement with the literature value from molecular calculations. The moments of $^{69}$Ge have been revised: $μ$ = +0.920(5) $μ_{N}$ and $Q_{\rm s}$= +0.114(8) b, and those of $^{71}$Ge have been confirmed. The experimental moments around $N = 40$ are interpreted with large-scale shell-model calculations using the JUN45 interaction, revealing rather mixed wave function configurations, although their $g$-factors are lying close to the effective single-particle values. Through a comparison with neighboring isotones, the structural change from the single-particle nature of nickel to deformation in germanium is further investigated around $N = 40$.

## Key claim

Collinear laser spectroscopy measurements on $^{69,71,73}$Ge reveal that previously reported nuclear moments for $^{69}$Ge from atomic beam magnetic resonance studies were incorrect, requiring revision of both the magnetic dipole and electric quadrupole moments; the new values are consistent with state-of-the-art atomic relativistic Fock-space coupled-cluster calculations and large-scale shell-model calculations using the JUN45 interaction.

## Method

Collinear laser spectroscopy was performed at the COLLAPS setup at ISOLDE-CERN on $^{69,71,73}$Ge isotopes produced by a 1.4-GeV proton beam on a ZrO$_2$ target with a sulphur leak, forming volatile GeS molecules. The hyperfine structure of the $4s^2 4p^2 \, ^3P_1 \rightarrow 4s^2 4p 5s \, ^3P_1^o$ transition at 269 nm was probed using frequency-mixing and frequency-doubling laser techniques, with a new time-resolved data acquisition system to reduce background from molecular contamination. Atomic hyperfine fields were calculated using relativistic Fock-space coupled-cluster (FSCC) methods to extract nuclear moments from the measured hyperfine constants.

## Key numbers

* $^{69}$Ge magnetic dipole moment revised to $\mu = +0.920(5) \, \mu_N$ (previously 0.735(7) $\mu_N$ from Oluwole (1970))
* $^{69}$Ge electric quadrupole moment revised to $Q_{\rm s} = +0.114(8)$ b (previously +0.027(5) b from Oluwole (1970))
* $^{73}$Ge quadrupole moment determined as $Q_{\rm s} = -0.198(4)$ b, in agreement with literature value -0.196(1) b from molecular calculations (Kello 1999)
* $^{71}$Ge magnetic dipole moment confirmed as $\mu = +0.547(5) \, \mu_N$ (literature +0.54606(7) $\mu_N$ from Childs (1966))
* $^{73}$Ge magnetic dipole moment determined as $\mu = -0.904(21) \, \mu_N$ (literature -0.87824(5) $\mu_N$ from Makulski (2006))
* Atomic FSCC calculations achieved agreement with experimental hyperfine fields within less than 5% for all three atomic states studied

## Context

This work extends collinear laser spectroscopy to the germanium isotopic chain for the first time, filling a gap after measurements on nickel (Flanagan 2009, Cheal 2010), copper (Vingerhoets 2010), zinc (Wraith 2017, Yang 2018), and gallium (Xie 2019) around $N = 40$. The revised moments for $^{69}$Ge correct earlier atomic beam magnetic resonance results (Oluwole 1970) and, combined with shell-model calculations using the JUN45 interaction, reveal mixed wave function configurations with g-factors near effective single-particle values. Comparison with neighboring isotones (zinc and selenium) enables investigation of the structural transition from single-particle nature in nickel to deformation in germanium as protons are added above $Z = 28$.
