# Effects of Pycnonuclear Reactions on White Dwarf Stellar Evolution

> This document presents a numerical study of the effects of high density nuclear reactions, pycnonuclear reactions, on the evolution of white dwarfs that accrete matter from a companion star. Primarily we seek observable properties that might be different when we include these nuclear reactions with varying microphysical assumptions. These different assumptions include effective nucleon-nucleon interactions, lattice structures and crystal polarization characteristics. Our results indicate that, although we find some significant differences in pycnonuclear reaction rates, the differences do not result in observable differences in the white dwarf evolution. Secondarily we examine the differences in observable properties when pycnonuclear reactions are assumed to happen and when they are assumed to not happen. In this case, our calculations indicate there can be observable differences in the radius, $R$, and the effective temperature, $T_{eff}$, at the stopping condition we use for our simulations, $L_{nuc} > 10^{8}\thinspace \rm{L}_{\odot}$, that is the total rate of energy released by nuclear fusion is greater than $10^{8}$ solar luminosities. Thirdly we explore the often neglected effect of temperature enhancement to pycnonuclear rates.

## Key claim

Although variations in microphysical assumptions (nucleon-nucleon interactions, lattice structures, and crystal polarization) produce significant differences in pycnonuclear reaction rates, these differences do not lead to observable differences in white dwarf evolution; however, the presence versus absence of pycnonuclear reactions altogether can produce observable differences in radius and effective temperature at the stopping condition where total nuclear energy release exceeds 10^8 solar luminosities.

## Method

The author used the Fortran application PycnoCalc to calculate pycnonuclear reaction rates for carbon-12 under varying microphysical assumptions: three nucleon-nucleon interactions (M3Y, Sao Paulo, and Relativistic Mean Field), two lattice structures (bcc and fcc), and three crystal polarization approximations (static, relaxed, and Wigner-Seitz). These rates were then incorporated into the stellar evolution code MESA to model accreting white dwarfs with a constant accretion rate of 10^-9 solar masses per year, assuming accreted material composed of 25% carbon and 75% oxygen with no hydrogen or helium. Simulations were run until the stopping condition of total nuclear energy generation exceeding 10^8 solar luminosities was reached.

## Key numbers

* Final radii for all pycnonuclear models ranged from 1337 km to 1354 km, with percent deviations from the mean of less than 1% (e.g., SK with RMF, bcc, WS gave 1354 km at +0.73% from mean; SK with Sao Paulo, bcc, static gave 1337 km at -0.58% from mean)
* Final effective temperatures for all pycnonuclear models ranged from 105836 K to 106926 K, with percent deviations from the mean of less than 1% (e.g., SK with RMF, bcc, static gave 106926 K at +0.53% from mean; SK with M3Y, bcc, relaxed gave 105836 K at -0.49% from mean)
* The model without pycnonuclear reactions produced a final radius of 1505 km and effective temperature of 96153 K
* The stopping condition for simulations was total nuclear energy generation L_nuc > 10^8 solar luminosities
* Accretion rate used was 10^-9 solar masses per year
* Pycnonuclear reaction rates were calculated for densities up to 10^11 g/cm^3, with the maximum density in the G05 reference model being slightly less than 10^10 g/cm^3
* The threshold below which reaction rates and derivatives were set to zero to resolve convergence issues was 10^-200

## Context

This work extends the pycnonuclear reaction rate calculations of Salpeter and Van Horn (1969), Schramm and Koonin (1991), and Gasques et al. (2005) by systematically comparing multiple microphysical assumptions within a consistent stellar evolution framework using MESA. The study enables future work to focus on the conditions under which pycnonuclear reactions produce observable signatures, particularly the temperature enhancement effects that proved numerically challenging and did not achieve convergence in this study. The finding that microphysical variations in reaction rates do not produce observable differences simplifies the modeling of accreting white dwarfs for Type Ia supernova progenitor studies.
