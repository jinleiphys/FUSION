# Manipulating a Neutrino Spectrum to Maximize the Physics Potential from a Low Energy Beta Beam

> Proposed low energy beta beam facilities would be capable of producing intense beams of neutrinos (anti-neutrinos) with well defined spectra. We present analytic expressions and numerical results which accurately show how the total neutrino flux reaching the detector depends on the geometry of the source and the detector. Several authors have proposed measurements which require using different flux shapes. We show that detectors of different sizes and shapes will receive neutrino fluxes with different spectral shapes, and that the spectral shape will also be different in different regions of the same detector. Our findings also show that for certain measurements systematic uncertainties and run time can be reduced.

## Key claim

The paper shows that different neutrino flux shapes can be obtained not only by changing the boost factor (gamma) of the parent ions, but also by using detectors of different dimensions or by considering different spatial regions within a single detector, potentially reducing systematic uncertainties compared to running the beam at multiple energies.

## Method

The authors derived an analytic expression for the fraction of neutrinos emitted from a point on the straight section of a beta beam storage ring that are contained within a given lab angle, using a monoenergetic rest-frame spectrum as a special case. They then calculated the neutrino flux reaching cylindrical detectors of fixed volume but varying radius and thickness, and computed neutrino-nucleus elastic scattering event rates for inner and outer regions of a cylindrical detector using a liquid argon target, folding the average flux with the differential cross section.

## Key numbers

- For a beam run at gamma = 7, only 50% of neutrinos emitted from any point on the straight section are contained within an angle of 1/gamma.
- For a detector with radius 6 m and thickness 0.5 m located 10 m from a 50 m straight section, the opening angle from point B to the detector is 16.7 degrees, containing 80% of neutrinos emitted from that point; from point A the opening angle is 6.24 degrees, containing 37%.
- For a detector with radius 6 m and thickness 14 m located 10 m from the straight section, the opening angle from point C to detector slice D is 14 degrees, containing 75% of neutrinos; to slice E the angle is 10 degrees, containing 60%.
- The example detector volume used throughout is 71.2 m^3, with five (radius, thickness) pairs in meters: (2.13, 5), (2.75, 3), (3.89, 1.5), (4.77, 1), and (6.74, 0.5).
- The storage ring parameters used are total length L = 450 m, straight section length S = 150 m, detector distance d = 10 m, and injection rate omega = 5 x 10^11 nuclei/sec.
- The example ion is Ne-18 with half-life 1.67 seconds and maximum neutrino energy 3.42 MeV.
- For the 100 tonne fiducial volume (71 m^3) with radius 2.13 m and thickness 5 m, dividing at boundary r = 1 m gives inner region mass 22 tonnes and outer region 78 tonnes; dividing at r = 1.5 m gives each region 50 tonnes.

## Context

This work extends the study of low energy beta beam facilities proposed by Zucchelli and studied by Autin and others, and builds on detailed calculations by Serreau and Volpe (2004) and McLaughlin (2005) for ring dimensions and neutrino fluxes. The paper enables new experimental strategies where a beta beam can be run at a single fixed energy while different flux shapes are obtained by selecting events from different detector regions, potentially reducing systematic uncertainties compared to methods that require running the beam at multiple gamma factors as proposed by McLaughlin (2005), Barger et al. (2006), and Jachowicz and McLaughlin (2006).
