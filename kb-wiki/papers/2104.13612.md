# Impact of massive neutron star radii on the nature of phase transitions in dense matter

> The last few years have seen tremendous progress in the observation of the global properties of neutron stars (NSs), e.g. masses, radii and tidal deformabilities. Such properties provide information about possible phase transitions in the inner cores of NSs, provided the connection between observed masses and radii and the equation of state (EoS) is well understood. We focus the present study on first-order phase transitions, which often softens the EoS and consequently reduces the maximum mass as well as the radii of NSs. Here, we challenge this conventional expectation by constructing explicit examples of EoSs undergoing a first-order phase transition, but which are much stiffer that their purely hadronic counterparts. We also provide comparisons with the recently proposed quarkyonic EoS which suggests a strong repulsion in the core of NSs, and we show that their stiffness can be realistically masqueraded by first-order phase transitions to exotic matter.

# Impact of massive neutron star radii on the nature of phase transitions in dense matter

## Key claim
First-order phase transitions in dense matter, which are conventionally expected to soften the equation of state and reduce neutron star radii, can instead produce stiff equations of state that predict radii comparable to or larger than purely nucleonic and quarkyonic models, provided the sound speed in the exotic phase exceeds the conformal limit (c² ≈ 1/3) and the transition occurs at relatively low densities (n_fo ≲ 2.0 n_sat).

## Method
The authors construct equations of state with a first-order phase transition using a parametric model where the hadronic phase is described by a nuclear meta-model (MM) based on nuclear empirical parameters, and the exotic phase is characterized by three parameters: transition density n_fo, density gap δn_fo, and sound speed squared c²_fo. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations and compare these to purely nucleonic equations of state and to quarkyonic matter models with parameters Λ_qyc = 250-332 MeV and κ_qyc ≈ 0.3.

## Key numbers
- Transition densities explored: n_fo = (1.5 - 2.5) n_sat, where n_sat is saturation density
- Density gaps explored: δn_fo = (0.05 - 1.50) n_sat
- Sound speeds explored: c²_fo = 0.15 - 1.00
- For the stiff case, parameters used: n_fo ≤ 1.8 n_sat, δn_fo = 0.2 - 0.4 n_sat, c²_fo = 1/3, 2/3, 1
- Quarkyonic model parameters: Λ_qyc ≈ 250-300 MeV, κ_qyc ≈ 0.3; onset densities of 0.14 fm⁻³ (Λ_qyc = 250 MeV) and 0.33 fm⁻³ (Λ_qyc = 332 MeV)
- First-order phase transitions can reach maximum masses M_TOV ≈ 3 M_⊙ with c²_fo = 1
- For M_TOV > 2.1 M_⊙, R_1.6 > Rⁿᵘᶜ_1.6 requires c²_fo ≳ 0.37
- For M_TOV > 2.3 M_⊙, R_1.6 > Rⁿᵘᶜ_1.6 requires c²_fo ≳ 0.45
- For 2.1 M_⊙ < M_TOV < 2.3 M_⊙, R_1.6 > Rⁿᵘᶜ_1.6 requires δn_fo ≲ 0.23 n_sat
- For 2.1 M_⊙ < M_TOV < 2.3 M_⊙, R_1.6 > Rⁿᵘᶜ_1.6 requires n_fo ≲ 2.3 n_sat

## Context
This work challenges the conventional expectation from studies by Zdunik (2013) and Alford (2013) that first-order phase transitions generically soften the equation of state and reduce neutron star radii. It extends the parametric approach of those works by exploring parameter ranges (low transition densities, high sound speeds) that produce stiff equations of state, motivated by a Bayesian analysis from Xie (2020) finding most probable values of n_fo ≈ 1.6 n_sat and c²_fo ≈ 0.95. The results show that first-order phase transitions can mimic the mass-radius relations of quarkyonic matter models (McLerran and Reddy, 2018; Margueron, in preparation), moderating the claim by Tan (2020) that massive neutron stars and stiff equations of state require non-trivial sound speed structures. This work enables future differentiation between phase transition scenarios through observations of very low mass neutron stars (≈ 1 M_⊙) or through maximum mass constraints from gravitational wave events.
