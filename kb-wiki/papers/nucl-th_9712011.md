# Frozen motion model and dilepton production from 200A GeV S+W collisions at CERN SPS

> We have analysed the invariant mass distribution of dileptons in S+W collisions at 200A GeV as measured by the HELIOS-3 collaboration at CERN SPS. Three scenarios were considered where the collision lead to formation of (i) the quark-gluon plasma, (ii) the ideal hot hadron gas and (iii) the viscous hot hadron gas. The space-time evolution was governed by the minimal extension of the Bjorken hydrodynamics. All the three scenarios indicate excess dileptons in the experiment.

## Key claim

In the frozen motion model, all three scenarios (quark-gluon plasma, ideal hot hadron gas, and viscous hot hadron gas) indicate excess dileptons in the experiment, but contrary to prior work by Srivastava et al. (1996), the no-phase-transition scenarios give a better description of the HELIOS-3 data than the phase-transition scenario, though the ideal hadron gas requires implausibly high initial temperatures above 300 MeV.

## Method

The author analyzed the invariant mass distribution of dileptons from 200A GeV S+W collisions measured by the HELIOS-3 collaboration using the frozen motion model, a minimal extension of Bjorken hydrodynamics that applies a local fluid approximation at each rapidity. Three initial-state scenarios were considered: a quark-gluon plasma with a bag model equation of state, an ideal hot hadron gas, and a viscous hot hadron gas with shear and bulk viscosity coefficients taken from Chaudhuri (1995). The space-time evolution was solved at each fluid rapidity using the experimental charged-particle pseudorapidity distribution from WA80 for S+Au collisions, scaled to S+W, to determine initial temperatures and freeze-out times.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature of the ideal hadron gas varies between 337 MeV (at pseudorapidity 3.7) and 226 MeV (at pseudorapidity 5.2), while for the viscous hadron gas it varies between 291 MeV and 161 MeV.
- Initial hadron density for the ideal hadron gas varies between 4.2 hadrons/fm³ and 1.2 hadrons/fm³ over the rapidity range considered; for the viscous hadron gas it varies between 2.7 hadrons/fm³ and 0.4 hadrons/fm³.
- In the invariant mass range 1-2 GeV, the ratio of experimental data to theory is 10.0 for the QGP scenario, 2.2 for the ideal hadron gas scenario, and 4.2 for the viscous hadron gas scenario.
- For invariant masses above 2 GeV, the QGP scenario underpredicts data by a factor of approximately 40, while the ideal hadron gas scenario overpredicts by a factor of 5.
- The Gaussian fit to the charged-particle pseudorapidity distribution has width sigma = 1.51, mean = 2.63, and amplitude = 161.48.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Srivastava et al. (1996), who used the treatment of Sinyukov et al. (1991) and found that the HELIOS-3 data favored the QGP scenario, while the frozen motion model here finds the opposite conclusion. The analysis also incorporates viscous hadronic gas, previously shown by Chaudhuri (1995) to describe WA80 single photon data, as an alternative to the ideal hadron gas. The results indicate that the frozen motion model predicts dilepton yields roughly a factor of 10 lower than the Sinyukov treatment, and that the viscous hadron gas gives the best description of high-mass dileptons, suggesting it as a viable alternative to the ideal hadron gas.
