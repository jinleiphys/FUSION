# Possible scenarios for single, double, or multiple kinetic freeze-out in high energy collisions

> Transverse momentum spectra of different types of particles produced in mid-rapidity interval in central and peripheral gold-gold (Au-Au) collisions, central and peripheral deuteron-gold ($d$-Au) collisions, and inelastic (INEL) or non-single-diffractive (NSD) proton-proton ($pp$) collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC), as well as in central and peripheral lead-lead (Pb-Pb) collisions, central and peripheral proton-lead ($p$-Pb) collisions, and INEL or NSD $pp$ collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) are analyzed by the blast-wave model with Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics. The model results are largely consist with the experimental data in special transverse momentum ranges measured by the PHENIX, STAR, ALICE, and CMS Collaborations. It is showed that the kinetic freeze-out temperature of emission source is dependent on particle mass, which reveals the scenario for multiple kinetic freeze-out in collisions at the RHIC and LHC. The scenario for single or double kinetic freeze-out is not observed in this study.

## Key claim

The kinetic freeze-out temperature extracted from transverse momentum spectra depends on particle mass across all collision systems and energies studied, which reveals a scenario of multiple kinetic freeze-out; the scenarios for single or double kinetic freeze-out are not observed in this study, contrary to some prior claims in the literature.

## Method

The authors used the blast-wave model with Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics to fit transverse momentum spectra of pions, kaons, protons, phi mesons, and Xi baryons produced at mid-rapidity in Au-Au, d-Au, and pp collisions at RHIC (200 GeV) and in Pb-Pb, p-Pb, and pp collisions at LHC (2.76 TeV and 7 TeV). The model was applied to both central and peripheral collisions, using a self-similar flow profile with n0=2, and the fitting was performed in the low transverse momentum region (typically 0-5 GeV/c) using the soft excitation component only, with the Hagedorn function reserved for the hard scattering contribution.

## Key numbers

- In central Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV: T0 for pions = 0.152 ± 0.001 GeV, for kaons = 0.120 ± 0.002 GeV, for protons = 0.220 ± 0.002 GeV
- In peripheral Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV: T0 for pions = 0.115 ± 0.003 GeV, for kaons = 0.090 ± 0.002 GeV, for protons = 0.130 ± 0.002 GeV
- In central Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV: T0 for pions = 0.135 ± 0.003 GeV, for kaons = 0.289 ± 0.003 GeV, for protons = 0.443 ± 0.002 GeV
- In peripheral Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV: T0 for pions = 0.133 ± 0.002 GeV, for kaons = 0.210 ± 0.002 GeV, for protons = 0.222 ± 0.001 GeV
- In pp collisions at 200 GeV: T0 for pions = 0.114 ± 0.002 GeV, for kaons = 0.200 ± 0.002 GeV, for protons = 0.145 ± 0.002 GeV
- In pp collisions at 7 TeV: T0 for pions = 0.130 ± 0.002 GeV, for kaons = 0.120 ± 0.003 GeV, for protons = 0.204 ± 0.003 GeV
- Average T0 in central nuclear collisions is approximately 148 MeV, which is less than the chemical freeze-out temperature of about 160 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Waqas and Liu, 2018) on kinetic freeze-out temperatures in nuclear collisions, using the blast-wave model with Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics rather than Tsallis statistics. The finding of mass-dependent kinetic freeze-out temperatures contradicts the single freeze-out scenario proposed by some earlier works and the double freeze-out scenario (separate parameters for strange and non-strange particles) suggested by others. The results enable future systematic studies of the excitation function of kinetic freeze-out temperature across collision energies and system sizes, and provide a framework for quantifying soft versus hard contributions to particle production as a function of centrality and rapidity.
