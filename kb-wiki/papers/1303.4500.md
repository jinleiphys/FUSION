# Possible Constraints on the Density Dependence of the Nuclear Symmetry Energy from Quasiperiodic Oscillations in Soft Gamma Repeaters

> We systematically examine the fundamental frequencies of shear torsional oscillations in neutron star crusts in a manner that is dependent on the parameter $L$ characterizing the poorly known density dependence of the symmetry energy. The identification of the lowest quasiperiodic oscillation (QPO) among the observed QPOs from giant flares in soft-gamma repeaters as the $/ell=2$ fundamental torsional oscillations enables us to constrain the parameter $L$ as $L/ge 47.4$ MeV, which is the most conservative restriction on $L$ obtained in the present work that assumes that the mass and radius of the flaring neutron stars range 1.4-1.8 $M_{/odot}$ and 10-14 km. Next, we identify one by one a set of the low-lying frequencies observed in giant flares as the fundamental torsional oscillations. The values of $L$ that can reproduce all the observed frequencies in terms of the torsional oscillations coupled with a part of dripped neutrons via entrainment effects are then constrained as 101.1 MeV $/le L /le$ 131.0 MeV. Alternatively, if only the second lowest frequency observed in SGR 1806$-$20 has a different origin, one obtains relatively low $L$ values ranging 58.0 MeV $/le L /le$ 85.3 MeV, which seem more consistent with other empirical constraints despite large uncertainties.

## Key claim

The parameter $L$ characterizing the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy can be constrained by identifying observed quasiperiodic oscillations (QPOs) from soft gamma repeater giant flares as fundamental crustal torsional oscillations, but the resulting constraint depends strongly on which observed frequencies are assigned to which oscillation modes and on whether entrainment effects from dripped neutrons are included.

## Method

The authors systematically calculated the fundamental frequencies of shear torsional oscillations in neutron star crusts for 11 different equation-of-state parameter sets that reproduce stable nuclear data, using a relativistic Cowling approximation and assuming zero shear modulus in pasta phases. They constructed equilibrium crust configurations by integrating the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations from the surface inward for stellar masses 1.4-1.8 solar masses and radii 10-14 km, then solved the eigenvalue problem for torsional oscillations with zero-traction boundary conditions at the surface and at the base of the spherical nuclei region, incorporating neutron superfluidity via an effective enthalpy density that subtracts the superfluid neutron component.

## Key numbers

* The most conservative lower limit on $L$ from identifying the lowest QPO (18 Hz in SGR 1806-20) as the $\ell=2$ fundamental torsional oscillation is $L \ge 47.4$ MeV, assuming neutron star masses 1.4-1.8 $M_\odot$ and radii 10-14 km.
* If the oscillating neutron star has $R=10$ km and $M=1.4 M_\odot$, the constraint becomes $L \ge 76.2$ MeV.
* When all low-lying QPO frequencies (18, 26, 30, 92.5 Hz in SGR 1806-20 and 28, 54, 84 Hz in SGR 1900+14) are identified as fundamental torsional oscillations with entrainment effects included, the allowed range is $101.1$ MeV $\le L \le 131.0$ MeV.
* If the second lowest frequency (26 Hz) in SGR 1806-20 has a different origin, the allowed range becomes $58.0$ MeV $\le L \le 85.3$ MeV.
* The best-fit value of $L$ for a typical neutron star with $M=1.4 M_\odot$ and $R=12$ km, fitting the SGR 1806-20 QPOs as $\ell=3,4,5,15$ fundamentals, is $L=128.0$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by Sotani (2011) and Sotani et al. (2012) that first linked crustal torsional oscillations to the symmetry energy parameter $L$, and it incorporates the entrainment effect from band-structure calculations by Chamel (2012) that was not included in those earlier studies. The analysis provides a complementary constraint on $L$ to those from nuclear mass and radius measurements (Oyamatsu and Iida, 2003) and from neutron star mass-radius observations, and it enables future work to refine the nuclear symmetry energy by using additional QPO detections or improved calculations of the superfluid fraction of dripped neutrons.
