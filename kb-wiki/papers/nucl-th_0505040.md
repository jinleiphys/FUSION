# A short remark on the velocities of neutrinos

To estimate it, the experimental values of absorption cross sections $\mathbfσ% _{a}(\widetildeν_{e})$ and $\mathbfσ_{a}(n)$ for electron antineutrinos and neutrons by nuclei are compared. # A short remark on the velocities of neutrinos

## Key claim
By comparing experimental absorption cross sections of electron antineutrinos and neutrons by nuclei, the author estimates that the electron antineutrino velocity may be vastly larger than the speed of light, which would be inconsistent with the theory of relativity. The claim is hedged by the conditional statement that this conclusion follows only if the proposed analogy between neutron and antineutrino absorption cross sections is valid.

## Method
The author applies the quantum theory of nuclear reactions to electron antineutrinos, treating them analogously to slow neutrons with angular momentum l=0. Two estimation approaches are used: first, comparing the measured antineutrino absorption cross section (10⁻⁴³–10⁻⁴⁵ cm²) with a calculated cross section derived from the de Broglie wavelength assuming E ≈ 1 MeV; second, taking the ratio of the antineutrino absorption cross section to the neutron absorption cross section and solving for the antineutrino velocity.

## Key numbers
- Measured electron antineutrino absorption cross section: (10⁻⁴³–10⁻⁴⁵) cm² (references [2,3]).
- Estimated antineutrino cross section from wavelength method: ≈ 1.5 × 10⁻²² cm², giving a discrepancy of 21–23 orders of magnitude with the measured value.
- Neutron absorption cross section used: ≈ 10⁻²⁴ cm².
- Neutron mass: ≈ 939 MeV; neutron velocity: ≈ 10⁸ cm/s.
- Estimated antineutrino velocity from the cross-section ratio method: ≈ 10³⁰ cm/s.
- Antineutrino flight-time through a nucleus: ≈ 10⁻⁴² s; characteristic nuclear time: ≈ 10⁻²³ s; their ratio ≈ 10⁻¹⁹.

## Context
This paper extends the quantum theory of nuclear reactions (reference [1]) to neutral particles beyond neutrons, specifically to electron antineutrinos. The author notes that no direct measurements of antineutrino velocity have been performed, and the result contradicts the theory of relativity. The work suggests that weak interactions might permit a maximum velocity greater than c, which would have implications for physics, astronomy, and cosmology, but the author acknowledges the need for experimental measurements of antineutrino velocity to resolve the question.
