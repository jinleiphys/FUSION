# Sensitivity of Isotopic Fission Yields in Actinides to the Macroscopic Liquid-Drop Model: LSD vs ISOLDA

> The impact of the macroscopic liquid-drop prescription on isotope-resolved fission-fragment yields in the actinide region is assessed by comparing two alternative parameterizations: the Lublin--Strasbourg Drop (LSD) model and the ISOscalar Liquid Drop Approximation (ISOLDA). The two prescriptions differ primarily in the treatment of isospin dependence in the volume and surface terms; in ISOLDA, an explicit dependence on the isospin square $T(T+1)$, where $T=|N-Z|/2$, is introduced in both coefficients. Using an identical set of fragment-yield observables and the same experimental reference (fission of $^{250}$Cf$^*$ at low and high energies), the propagation of the macroscopic-energy choice into the predicted yields is quantified in terms of (i) the location of the most probable post-neutron isotopes along elemental chains, (ii) the widths and asymmetries of the isotopic distributions, and (iii) the population of neighboring nuclides on the distribution tails. A comparable description of the gross properties of the isotopic yield pattern is obtained with both prescriptions, particularly for light and intermediate fragments, where peak positions and near-maximum curvatures are reproduced similarly. The most discriminating differences are found for heavy-fragment chains, for which the ridge location and isotopic centroids are rendered more sensitive to macroscopic isospin terms. Overall, a closer average agreement with the evaluated data is obtained with LSD, while the LSD--ISOLDA spread is shown to provide a practical estimate of the macroscopic-model uncertainty in isotope-resolved yields.

## Key claim

The paper assesses how the choice of macroscopic liquid-drop prescription (LSD versus ISOLDA) propagates into isotope-resolved fission-fragment yields for actinides, finding that both prescriptions give comparable descriptions of gross yield patterns but that LSD provides a slightly closer average agreement with evaluated data, while the spread between the two models offers a practical estimate of macroscopic-model uncertainty.

## Method

The authors used a four-dimensional Langevin-plus-Master-Equation (4D LM) framework with a Fourier-over-Spheroid (FoS) shape parametrization to model dissipative saddle-to-scission dynamics, coupled with a statistical model for post-scission neutron evaporation. They calculated isotopic fission-fragment yields for excited $^{250}$Cf at low energy (thermal-neutron capture on $^{249}$Cf) and high energy ($E^* = 46$ MeV, $L = 20\hbar$ from $^{238}$U + $^{12}$C fusion), comparing results obtained with the LSD and ISOLDA macroscopic energy prescriptions while keeping all other model ingredients identical.

## Key numbers

- For the high-energy fission of $^{250}$Cf$^*$, the excitation energy was $E^{*}=46$ MeV with average angular momentum $L=20\hbar$.
- The scission criterion in the FoS collective space was set at $a_4\approx 0.72$, corresponding to a neck radius close to the size of a single nucleon.
- The charge-radius constant used in the Coulomb repulsion calculation was $r_0=1.217$ fm.
- The ISOLDA formula contains only 6 adjustable parameters, fewer than other commonly used macroscopic models.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous 4D Langevin framework (Pomorski 2024) which showed good agreement for isotope-resolved yields in actinide fission but revealed deterioration for highly excited $^{250}$Cf. The study directly compares LSD (Pomorski and Dudek 2003) with the newly developed ISOLDA (Pomorski and Xiao 2025) to isolate the role of the macroscopic energy term. The results enable future systematic sensitivity studies of fluctuation strength, thermal sampling at scission, and coupling to neutron evaporation, with the goal of broadening calculated isotopic distributions while preserving accurately reproduced ridge centroids.
