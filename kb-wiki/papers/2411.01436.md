# Impact of the Brink Axel Hypothesis on Unique First Forbidden \b{eta} transitions for r process nuclei

> Key nuclear inputs for the astrophysical r process simulations are the weak interaction rates. Consequently, the accuracy of these inputs directly affects the reliability of nucleosynthesis modeling. Majority of the stellar rates, used in simulation studies, are calculated invoking the Brink Axel (BA) hypothesis. The BA hypothesis assumes that the strength functions of all parent excited states are the same as for the ground state, only shifted in energies. However, BA hypothesis has to be tested against microscopically calculated state by state rates. In this project we study the impact of the BA hypothesis on calculated stellar \b{eta} decay and electron capture rates. Our investigation include both Unique First Forbidden (U1F) and allowed transitions for 106 neutron rich trans iron nuclei ([27, 77] less than equal to [Z, A] less than equal to [82, 208]). The calculations were performed using the deformed proton-neutron quasiparticle randomphase approximation (pn QRPA) model with a simple plus quadrupole separable and schematic interaction. Waiting-point and several key r process nuclei lie within the considered mass region of the nuclear chart.

## Key claim

The Brink-Axel (BA) hypothesis, which assumes that strength functions for all parent excited states are identical to the ground-state strength function shifted in energy, is tested against microscopically calculated state-by-state rates for both Unique First Forbidden (U1F) and allowed transitions. The authors find that the BA hypothesis is not a reliable approximation, particularly for beta-decay forbidden transitions, with U1F rates overestimated by 4-5 orders of magnitude compared to microscopic rates.

## Method

The authors employed the deformed proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (pn-QRPA) model with a separable plus quadrupole schematic interaction, accessing up to 7 major oscillator shells. They computed electron capture and beta-decay rates for 106 neutron-rich trans-iron nuclei (27 ≤ Z ≤ 82, 70 ≤ A ≤ 208) under stellar density and temperature conditions (10 ≤ ρY_e (g/cm³) ≤ 10¹¹, 1 ≤ T (GK) ≤ 30) using two prescriptions: one invoking the BA hypothesis and the other performing state-by-state calculation of strength functions.

## Key numbers

- U1F beta-decay rates calculated with the BA hypothesis are overestimated by 4-5 orders of magnitude compared to microscopic state-by-state rates
- For electron capture rates, more than 2 orders of magnitude difference was noted when applying the BA hypothesis
- The study covered 106 neutron-rich trans-iron nuclei in the range [27, 77] ≤ [Z, A] ≤ [82, 208]
- Stellar conditions spanned density ρY_e from 10 to 10¹¹ g/cm³ and temperature T from 1 to 30 GK
- The axial-vector to vector coupling ratio g_A/g_V was taken as -1.2694

## Context

This work extends previous tests of the BA hypothesis (Fra97, Lar07, Nab07, Ang12, Mis14, Joh15, Lu18, Nab19, Yuk20, Her22, Nab22, Fak23) which had shown the BA hypothesis to be a poor approximation for stellar weak rate calculations, but had not covered r-process nuclei or tested the validity for forbidden transitions. The study enables more reliable weak interaction rate inputs for astrophysical r-process simulations, particularly for waiting-point nuclei with neutron numbers 50, 82, and 126 where first-forbidden transitions have significant contributions to beta-decay half-lives.
