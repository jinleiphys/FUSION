# On the Deconfinement Phase Transition in Neutron-Star Mergers

> We study in detail the nuclear aspects of a neutron-star merger in which deconfinement to quark matter takes place. For this purpose, we make use of the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model, an effective relativistic model that includes self-consistent chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement to quark matter and, for this reason, predicts the existence of different degrees of freedom depending on the local density/chemical potential and temperature. We then use the out-of-chemical-equilibrium finite-temperature CMF equation of state in full general-relativistic simulations to analyze which regions of different QCD phase diagrams are probed and which conditions, such as strangeness and entropy, are generated when a strong first-order phase transition appears. We also investigate the amount of electrons present in different stages of the merger and discuss how far from chemical equilibrium they can be and, finally, draw some comparisons with matter created in supernova explosions and heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a strong first-order deconfinement phase transition to quark matter can occur in neutron-star mergers, but only under specific conditions: the transition is sharp at zero and low temperatures, becoming smoother at higher temperatures, and the quark phase appears only after the merger when the hypermassive neutron star (HMNS) center crosses the coexistence line at approximately 4.5 ms post-merger.

## Method

The authors performed full general-relativistic hydrodynamical simulations of an equal-mass nonspinning neutron-star binary with total gravitational mass 2.9 solar masses, using the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model as the equation of state. The CMF model is an effective relativistic model that includes self-consistent chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement via an order parameter (the scalar field Phi), with coupling constants fitted to nuclear saturation properties and lattice QCD data. The simulations were conducted using the Frankfurt/IllinoisGRMHD code with fourth-order accurate numerical methods, solving the Einstein equations via the Z4c formulation and evolving the spacetime with fixed-mesh refinement.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass with quarks suppressed: 2.07 solar masses, radius 12 km
- Maximum neutron star mass with mixed phases (quarks allowed): 1.93 solar masses, radius 13 km, with more than 2 km containing quarks
- Canonical 1.4 solar mass star radius: 14 km
- Total binary mass simulated: 2.9 solar masses
- Initial proper separation: 45 km
- Finest grid resolution: 250 m
- Time when coexistence line is crossed and quark matter appears: approximately 4.5 ms after merger
- Temperature in deconfined phase reaches approximately 60 MeV
- Maximum charge fraction achieved: Y_Q approximately 0.12
- Maximum strangeness fraction in HMNS center: Y_S approximately 40%
- Maximum strange-quark fraction in HMNS center: Y_s-quark approximately 40%
- Entropy per baryon before phase transition: S_B approximately 1; after transition: S_B greater than or equal to 2.5
- Critical temperature for first-order line disappearance: T_c = 169 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Most 2018b) which first reported that a strong first-order phase transition to quark matter can produce distinct gravitational-wave signatures, by providing detailed nuclear analysis of the merger conditions. The results are compared with matter generated in core-collapse supernova explosions (where electron fractions reach Y_e approximately 0.4 and entropies S_B approximately 1-2) and heavy-ion collisions (where net strangeness is zero and charge fractions reach Y_Q approximately 0.4), showing that neutron-star mergers probe a distinct region of the QCD phase diagram with lower charge fractions and nonzero net strangeness. The work enables nuclear physicists to use the specific thermodynamic conditions (temperature, entropy, charge fraction, strangeness) found in these simulations as input for equations of state containing exotic degrees of freedom.
