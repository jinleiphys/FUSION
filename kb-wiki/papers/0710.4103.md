# Path Integrals in Lattice Quantum Chromodynamics

> I discuss the use of path integrals to study strong-interaction physics from first principles. The underlying theory is cast into path integrals which are evaluated numerically using Monte Carlo methods on a space-time lattice. Examples are given on progress related to nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Path integrals in lattice QCD, evaluated numerically via Monte Carlo methods, provide the only known way to solve quantum chromodynamics directly from first principles, but the quenched approximation (ignoring the quark determinant) introduces systematic discrepancies that remain under active correction.

## Method

The Euclidean-space path integral over gluon and quark fields is discretized on a space-time lattice, with quarks on sites and gluons on links. The quark part of the integration is performed analytically using Grassmann variable integration, leaving a path integral over gluon fields only, which is then evaluated numerically by Monte Carlo methods. In the quenched approximation, the quark determinant is ignored (suppressing vacuum polarization) to speed calculations. Hadron masses are extracted from the time-ordered two-point correlation function at large Euclidean time, where the ground state dominates the exponential sum over intermediate states.

## Key numbers

- Computed light hadron mass spectrum in the quenched approximation comes within 10% of the observed spectrum.
- High-precision lattice QCD calculations for selected physics quantities are within 3% of observed values when full QCD is used, with the quenched approximation effects clearly demonstrated.
- Magnetic moment calculations for the proton and neutron show good agreement between lattice QCD and experiment, presented as a function of pion mass squared.
- Pion-pion scattering length in the isospin 2 channel shows good agreement between lattice QCD and experiment, using Lüscher's method.
- Direct lattice QCD calculation of the nucleon-nucleon potential in the $^1$S$_0$ and $^3$S$_1$ channels yields features consistent with known phenomenological nuclear force, with the solid lines corresponding to the one-pion exchange potential (Yukawa potential).

## Context

This work reviews the path-integral approach to lattice QCD as the only known method to solve QCD with controlled errors, extending earlier textbook treatments (Rothe; Montvay; DeGrand). The examples shown build on quenched approximation results (CP-PACS Collaboration, 2000) and full QCD calculations (HPQCD Collaboration, 2004), with magnetic moment calculations (Lee, 2005) and strangeness form factors (Derek, 2005, 2006) providing benchmarks. The nucleon-nucleon potential calculation (Aoki, 2007) and pion-pion scattering using Lüscher's method (Lüscher, 1991) demonstrate progress toward nuclear physics from first principles, enabling future calculations of hadron-hadron scattering in channels such as $\pi^+K^+$, $n\Lambda$, and $n\Sigma^-$.
