# Neural Operators as Efficient Function Interpolators

> Neural operators (NOs) are designed to learn maps between infinite-dimensional function spaces. We propose a novel reframing of their use. By introducing an auxiliary base-space, any finite-dimensional function can be viewed as an operator acting by composition on functions of the base-space. Through a range of benchmarks on analytic functions of increasing complexity and dimensionality, we demonstrate that NOs can match or outperform standard multilayer perceptrons and Kolmogorov--Arnold Networks in accuracy while requiring significantly fewer parameters and training time. As a real-world application, we apply a two-dimensional Tensorized Fourier Neural Operator (TFNO) to the nuclear chart, learning a correction to state-of-the-art nuclear mass models as a partially observed residual field. A TFNO ensemble reaches a held-out root-mean-square error of 198.2 keV, placing it among the best recent neural-network approaches while retaining high parameter efficiency and short training times. More broadly, these results introduce NOs as a scalable framework for finite-dimensional function interpolation, from analytic benchmarks to structured scientific data.

## Key claim

Neural operators (NOs), originally designed for learning maps between infinite-dimensional function spaces, can be repurposed as efficient interpolators for finite-dimensional functions by introducing an auxiliary base-space that lifts any function into an operator acting by composition; the authors claim this approach matches or outperforms standard MLPs and Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks in accuracy while requiring significantly fewer parameters and training time, but only when the NO is trained and evaluated using input functions that sample higher-dimensional subspaces rather than individual points.

## Method

The authors introduce an auxiliary base-space B and functions x: B to D_in, then define an operator F that acts by composition with the target function f to produce f(x(s)). Training proceeds by discretizing B on a grid with r points, assigning groups of r values of x_i and f(x_i) to r discrete values of s, and repeating this partition with random selection to create multiple samples covering the full training dataset. For the nuclear physics application, a two-dimensional Tensorized Fourier Neural Operator (TFNO) with Tucker factorization is trained on the (Z,N) grid of the nuclear chart to learn corrections to the Weizsacker-Skyrme version-4 (WS4) mass model residuals, using masked inpainting where each batch randomly hides about 5% of known residuals and computes loss only on the hidden residuals.

## Key numbers

* A 30-member TFNO ensemble reaches a held-out root-mean-square error of 198.2 keV on the nuclear mass prediction task, representing a 30% reduction relative to the raw WS4 baseline of 282.47 keV on the same subset.
* A single TFNO member with 146,929 trainable parameters reaches 208.3 plus or minus 2.7 keV pooled five-fold out-of-fold RMS (mean plus or minus standard deviation over 46 independent seeds).
* The TFNO architecture uses n_modes = (24,32), h = 96 hidden channels, L = 2 spectral layers, and Tucker factorization with rank r = 0.01, for a total of 4.4 million trainable parameters in the 30-member ensemble.
* The nuclear dataset consists of 2,348 nuclei from AME2020 satisfying N greater than 12 or Z greater than 12 with experimental uncertainty below 100 keV.
* On analytic benchmarks, all models were compared with approximately 8,900 parameters: 1D-TFNO (8,917), 0D-TFNO (8,917), MLP (8,746), and KAN (9,120).

## Context

This work extends the line of neural operator research by Li (2021) and Kossaifi (2024) on Fourier Neural Operators and Tensorized Fourier Neural Operators, which were originally designed for solving parametric partial differential equations. The authors compare their approach against standard multilayer perceptrons and Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (Liu 2025) on analytic benchmarks, and against prior nuclear mass ML approaches including NuCLR (Kitouni 2023), BNN+WS4 (Niu 2018), CNN+WS4 (Lu 2024), and various physics-informed models (Bentley 2024, Gao 2021, Wu 2022). The nuclear application enables future work on mass predictions for astrophysical reaction-network calculations, particularly for the rapid-neutron-capture process where uncertainties of a few hundred keV are significant.
