# CN Neutrinos and the Sun's Primordial Core Metalicity

> I discuss the use of neutrinos from the CN cycle and pp chain to constrain the primordial solar core abundances of C and N at an interesting level of precision. A comparison of the Sun's deep interior and surface compositions would test a key assumption of the standard solar model (SSM), a homogeneous zero-age Sun. It would also provide a cross-check on recent photospheric abundance determinations that have altered the once excellent agreement between the SSM and helioseismology. Motivated by the discrepancy between convective-zone abundances and helioseismology, I discuss the possibility that a two-zone Sun could emerge from late-stage metal differentiation in the solar nebula connected with formation of the gaseous giant planets.

## Key claim

A measurement of the CN-cycle solar neutrino flux, combined with the precisely measured $^8$B neutrino flux as a "thermometer" to calibr out environmental uncertainties, could constrain the primordial solar core abundances of carbon and nitrogen to better than 30% accuracy, testing the standard solar model assumption of a homogeneous zero-age Sun. This test is motivated by the possibility that a two-zone Sun could result from late-stage metal differentiation in the solar nebula connected with formation of the gaseous giant planets.

## Method

The author uses standard solar model (SSM) partial derivatives $\alpha(i,j)$ that relate neutrino fluxes $\phi_i$ to 19 input parameters $\beta_j$ (including solar parameters, nuclear S-factors, and elemental abundances) to decompose the uncertainty in CN neutrino flux predictions into "environmental" effects (opacity, temperature) and the direct dependence on primordial C and N abundances. By forming the ratio $\phi(^{15}\mathrm{O})/\phi(^8\mathrm{B})^{0.828}$, the environmental uncertainties are reduced to 2.6% residual, allowing the measured $^8$B flux from Super-Kamiokande to serve as a calibrated thermometer for the solar core temperature, isolating the CN flux dependence on core C+N content.

## Key numbers

- Predicted $^{13}$N neutrino flux from BPS08(GS) SSM: $(2.93^{+0.91}_{-0.82}) \times 10^8/\mathrm{cm^2s}$ with maximum energy 1.199 MeV
- Predicted $^{15}$O neutrino flux from BPS08(GS) SSM: $(2.20^{+0.73}_{-0.63}) \times 10^8/\mathrm{cm^2s}$ with maximum energy 1.732 MeV
- Predicted $^8$B flux with GS abundances: $5.95 \times 10^6/\mathrm{cm^2s}$; with AGS abundances: $4.72 \times 10^6/\mathrm{cm^2s}$
- Measured $^8$B flux from SNO NCD-phase: $[5.54 ^{+0.33}_{-0.31} (\mathrm{stat}) ^{+0.36}_{-0.34} (\mathrm{sys})] \times 10^6/\mathrm{cm^2s}$
- SK I measured rate: $4.935 \pm 0.05 (\mathrm{stat}) ^{+0.17}_{-0.16} (\mathrm{sys}) \times 10^{-38} \mathrm{electron}^{-1} \mathrm{s}^{-1}$ (combined error $\sim \pm 3.6\%$)
- Residual environmental uncertainty after $^8$B calibration: 2.6% for $^{15}$O, 2.8% for $^{13}$N
- Nuclear S-factor uncertainty contribution: 7.6%
- LMA oscillation parameter uncertainty contribution: 4.9%
- Overall theoretical uncertainty in relating CN flux to core C/N metals: 9.6%
- SNO+ simulated CN-neutrino event rate: 2300 counts/year above 0.8 MeV, with projected 10% accuracy after three years
- Metal excess in gaseous giant planets: $\sim 40-90 M_\oplus$

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Serenelli and Haxton (2008) to show how a CN neutrino measurement could test the SSM homogeneity assumption, motivated by the discrepancy between the revised photospheric abundances of Asplund et al. (2005) (AGS, Z=0.0122) and the older Grevesse and Sauval (1998) abundances (GS, Z=0.0169) that previously gave excellent agreement with helioseismology. The approach uses the $^8$B flux as a calibrated thermometer, building on the partial derivative methodology of Bahcall and Serenelli (2005), and enables a quantitative comparison between solar core and surface metal abundances that could also inform models of giant planet formation (Guillot, 2005) and chemical evolution of the solar system.
