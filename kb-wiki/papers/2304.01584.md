# The proto-neutron star inner crust in the liquid phase

> The crust of a neutron star is known to melt at a temperature that increases with increasing matter density, up to about $10^{10}$ K. At such high temperatures and beyond, the crustal ions are put into collective motion and the associated entropy contribution can affect both the thermodynamic properties and the composition of matter. We studied the importance of this effect in different thermodynamic conditions relevant to the inner crust of the proto-neutron star, both at beta equilibrium and in the fixed-proton-fraction regime. To this aim, we solved the hydrodynamic equations for an ion moving in an incompressible, irrotational, and non-viscous fluid, with different boundary conditions, thus leading to different prescriptions for the ion effective mass. We then employed a compressible liquid-drop approach in the one-component plasma approximation, including the renormalisation of the ion mass to account for the influence of the surrounding medium. We show that the cluster size is determined by the competition between the ion centre-of-mass motion and the interface properties, namely the Coulomb, surface, and curvature energies. In particular, including the translational free energy in the minimisation procedure can significantly reduce the optimal number of nucleons in the clusters and lead to an early dissolution of clusters in dense beta-equilibrated matter. On the other hand, we find that the impact of translational motion is reduced in scenarios where the proton fraction is assumed constant and is almost negligible on the inner-crust equation of state. Our results show that the translational degrees of freedom affect the equilibrium composition of beta-equilibrated matter and the density and pressure of the crust-core transition in a non-negligible way, highlighting the importance of its inclusion when modelling the finite-temperature inner crust of the (proto-)neutron star.

## Key claim

Including the translational free energy of ions in the variational minimization procedure can significantly reduce the optimal number of nucleons in clusters and lead to an early dissolution of clusters in dense beta-equilibrated matter, but this effect is reduced in scenarios where the proton fraction is assumed constant and is almost negligible on the inner-crust equation of state.

## Method

The authors solved the hydrodynamic equations for an ion moving in an incompressible, irrotational, and non-viscous fluid with different boundary conditions (hard impenetrable sphere versus permeable sphere) to derive expressions for the ion effective mass. They then employed a compressible liquid-drop approach in the one-component plasma approximation, including the renormalisation of the ion mass to account for the influence of the surrounding medium, and minimized the total free-energy density under baryon number conservation and charge neutrality constraints.

## Key numbers

- The crust melts at a temperature that increases with increasing matter density, up to about 10^10 K
- The crystallisation transition temperature is approximately k_B T_m ≈ 0.1-1 MeV
- The effective mass for the hard-sphere model is M_i^* = M_i (1 + 1/2 γ), where γ = ρ_b,g / ρ_b,i
- For the permeable sphere model with δ^f = 1 (all neutrons in the cluster participate in the flow), the effective mass is M_i^* = M_i (1-γ)^2 / (1+2γ)
- For the permeable sphere model with δ^f_min ≈ n_gn / n_i (only continuum neutrons participate), the effective mass is approximately M_i^* ≈ M_i (1 - n_gn / n_i)

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Carreau (2019, 2020), Dinh Thi (2021), and Grams (2022) to include translational degrees of freedom in the liquid phase of the proto-neutron star inner crust. It contrasts with earlier approaches that neglected the translational term (Avancini 2009, Shen 2011, Avancini 2017) or excluded it from the variational equation with respect to cluster size (Lattimer 1991, Schneider). The results enable more accurate modelling of the finite-temperature inner crust for proto-neutron star cooling and neutron star merger simulations.
