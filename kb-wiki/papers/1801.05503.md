# Shell-model-like approach based on cranking covariant density functional theory: bandcrossing and shape evolution in $^{60}$Fe

> The shell-model-like approach is implemented to treat the cranking many-body Hamiltonian based on the covariant density functional theory including pairing correlations with exact particle number conservation. The self-consistency is achieved by iterating the single-particle occupation probabilities back to the densities and currents. As an example, the rotational structures observed in the neutron-rich nucleus $^{60}$Fe are investigated and analyzed. Without introducing any \emph{ad hoc} parameters, the bandheads, the rotational spectra, and the relations between the angular momentum and rotational frequency for the positive parity band A, and negative parity bands B and C are well reproduced. The essential role of the pairing correlations is revealed. It is found that for band A, the bandcrossing is due to the change of the last two occupied neutrons from the $1f_{5/2}$ signature partners to the $1g_{9/2}$ signature partners. For the two negative parity signature partner bands B and C, the bandcrossings are due to the pseudo-crossing between the $1f_{7/2,~5/2}$ and the $1f_{5/2,~1/2}$ orbitals. Generally speaking, the deformation parameters $β$ for bands A, B, and C decrease with rotational frequency. For band A, the deformation jumps from $β\sim0.19$ to $β\sim0.29$ around the bandcrossing. In comparison with its signature partner band C, band B exhibits appreciable triaxial deformation.

## Key claim

Without introducing any *ad hoc* parameters, the cranking CDFT-SLAP reproduces the bandheads, rotational spectra, and angular-momentum-versus-frequency relations for three rotational bands in \(^{60}\)Fe, but the agreement depends on including pairing correlations; switching off pairing produces deviations in the low-spin region, particularly for band A.

## Method

The authors implemented the shell-model-like approach (SLAP) to diagonalize the cranking many-body Hamiltonian built from covariant density functional theory (CDFT) with point-coupling functional PC-PK1, using a monopole pairing interaction. The single-particle Routhians were obtained by expanding Dirac spinors in a three-dimensional harmonic oscillator basis with 10 major shells, and the many-particle configuration (MPC) space was truncated to 800 dimensions for both neutrons and protons. Occupation probabilities from the diagonalization were iterated back into the densities and currents to achieve self-consistency, and the effective pairing strength (0.8 MeV for both neutrons and protons) was fixed by reproducing experimental odd-even mass differences.

## Key numbers

* The deformation parameter \(\beta\) for band A jumps from \(\beta \sim 0.19\) to \(\beta \sim 0.29\) around the bandcrossing.
* The neutron pairing energy in band A changes rapidly from \(\sim -5.0\) MeV near the bandhead to \(\sim -1.5\) MeV at \(\hbar\omega \approx 0.75\) MeV, where the bandcrossing occurs.
* Increasing the number of major shells from 10 to 12 changes the total energy by less than 0.1%; increasing the MPC space from 800 to 1200 and adjusting the pairing strength accordingly also changes the total energy by less than 0.1%.
* The cranking CDFT-SLAP results agree with TAC-CDFT calculations (pairing switched off) to within differences less than \(10^{-4}\) MeV for neutron single-particle Routhians and \(10^{-4}\hbar\) for \(J_x\).
* The contribution from the \(1g_{9/2}\) orbitals to the angular momentum in band A after bandcrossing produces a dramatic change around \(6\hbar\).

## Context

This work extends the shell-model-like approach (previously combined with the phenomenological cranking Nilsson model by Zeng (1983) and with CDFT for ground states by Meng (2006)) to the cranking regime, enabling fully microscopic, particle-number-conserving treatment of rotating superfluid nuclei. The calculations for \(^{60}\)Fe reproduce experimental data from Deacon (2007) and are consistent with configuration assignments from the projected shell model by Sun (2009) and large-scale shell-model calculations by Togashi (2015). The method can now be applied to study bandcrossing mechanisms and shape evolution in other neutron-rich nuclei where pairing correlations are essential.
