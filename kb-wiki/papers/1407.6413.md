# Self-consistent Cooper-Frye freeze-out of a viscous fluid to particles

> Comparing hydrodynamic simulations to heavy-ion data inevitably requires the conversion of the fluid to particles. This conversion, typically done in the Cooper-Frye formalism, is ambiguous for viscous fluids. We compute self-consistent phase space corrections by solving the linearized Boltzmann equation and contrast the solutions to those obtained using the ad-hoc "democratic Grad" ansatz typically employed in the literature where coefficients are independent of particle dynamics. Solutions are calculated analytically for a massless gas and numerically for both a pion-nucleon gas and for the general case of a hadron resonance gas. We find that the momentum dependence of the corrections in all systems investigated is best fit by a power close to 3/2 rather than the typically used quadratic ansatz. The effects on harmonic flow coefficients $v_2$ and $v_4$ are substantial, and should be taken into account when extracting medium properties from experimental data.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the commonly used "democratic Grad" ansatz for viscous phase-space corrections in Cooper-Frye freeze-out, which assumes corrections are quadratic in momentum and independent of particle species, is inaccurate. The authors find that self-consistent corrections from linearized kinetic theory are best fit by a momentum power close to 3/2 rather than quadratic, and that species-dependent corrections produce substantial differences in identified-particle elliptic flow that should be detectable in experiment.

## Method

The authors solve the linearized Boltzmann equation for the viscous correction function δf_i by extremizing a functional that incorporates collision terms with elastic two-body interactions. They compute the shear viscosity and the momentum-dependent correction coefficients χ_i(|p̃|) analytically for a massless two-component gas, numerically for a pion-nucleon gas with constant effective cross sections (σ_ππ = 30 mb, σ_πN = 50 mb, σ_NN = 20 mb), and for a full hadron resonance gas with 49 effective species up to mass 1.672 GeV using both constant 30 mb cross sections and additive quark model (AQM) cross sections (σ_MM:σ_MB:σ_BB = 30:45:67.5 mb). The resulting corrections are then used in the Cooper-Frye formalism with AZHYDRO 2+1D hydrodynamic simulations of Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV with impact parameter b = 7 fm, using the s95-p1 equation of state.

## Key numbers

- For the massless two-component system undergoing 0+1D Bjorken expansion, the linear transport prediction for the ratio of species-dependent correction coefficients c_B/c_A is accurate within 10% at late times, while the democratic Grad ansatz (c_B/c_A = 1) fails.
- For the pion-nucleon gas at temperatures 120 MeV ≤ T ≤ 165 MeV, the ratio of pion to proton correction coefficients c_π/c_N ∼ 2, meaning nucleons are about twice as close to equilibrium as pions of the same momentum.
- In the AZHYDRO simulation with the pion-nucleon gas at T_conv = 165 MeV, self-consistent freeze-out produces proton v_2 approximately 30% higher than pion v_2 at p_T around 2 GeV, after resonance decays.
- For the full hadron gas with AQM cross sections at T_conv = 165 MeV, the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/s falls close to the proposed lower bound of 1/(4π).
- The best-fit momentum power for the viscous correction function across all systems investigated is close to 3/2, not the quadratic (power 2) ansatz typically used.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Denicol (2012) by computing self-consistent species-dependent viscous corrections from linearized kinetic theory, in contrast to the ad-hoc "democratic Grad" ansatz that ignores particle dynamics. The authors use the functional extremization method from Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2003) and compare their results to fully nonlinear transport simulations using Molnar's Parton Cascade (MPC). The findings enable more accurate extraction of medium properties such as shear viscosity from identified-particle flow coefficients v_2 and v_4 in heavy-ion experiments at RHIC and LHC, and motivate future work with realistic viscous hydrodynamics and energy-dependent hadron cross sections.
