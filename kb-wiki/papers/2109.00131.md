# Search for the Chiral Magnetic Effect with Isobar Collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV by the STAR Collaboration at RHIC

> The chiral magnetic effect (CME) is predicted to occur as a consequence of a local violation of $\cal P$ and $\cal CP$ symmetries of the strong interaction amidst a strong electro-magnetic field generated in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Experimental manifestation of the CME involves a separation of positively and negatively charged hadrons along the direction of the magnetic field. Previous measurements of the CME-sensitive charge-separation observables remain inconclusive because of large background contributions. In order to better control the influence of signal and backgrounds, the STAR Collaboration performed a blind analysis of a large data sample of approximately 3.8 billion isobar collisions of $^{96}_{44}$Ru+$^{96}_{44}$Ru and $^{96}_{40}$Zr+$^{96}_{40}$Zr at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=200$ GeV. Prior to the blind analysis, the CME signatures are predefined as a significant excess of the CME-sensitive observables in Ru+Ru collisions over those in Zr+Zr collisions, owing to a larger magnetic field in the former. A precision down to 0.4% is achieved, as anticipated, in the relative magnitudes of the pertinent observables between the two isobar systems. Observed differences in the multiplicity and flow harmonics at the matching centrality indicate that the magnitude of the CME background is different between the two species. No CME signature that satisfies the predefined criteria has been observed in isobar collisions in this blind analysis.

## Key claim

The STAR Collaboration performed a blind analysis of approximately 3.8 billion isobar collisions (Ru+Ru and Zr+Zr at 200 GeV) searching for the chiral magnetic effect, but found no CME signature that satisfies the predefined criteria of a significant excess in Ru+Ru over Zr+Zr collisions, despite achieving the anticipated 0.4% precision in relative observable magnitudes; observed differences in multiplicity and flow harmonics between the two species indicate that the CME background magnitude differs between the two systems.

## Method

The STAR Collaboration collected and analyzed approximately 1.8 billion minimum-bias Ru+Ru events and 2.0 billion Zr+Zr events at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV using the Time Projection Chamber, Time-of-Flight detector, Event Plane Detector, and Zero-Degree Calorimeters. A blind analysis procedure was implemented with three steps: isobar-mixed analysis (mixed species samples), isobar-blind analysis (single-species files with disguised identity), and isobar-unblind analysis (full species information revealed), with frozen analysis codes and automated quality assurance algorithms to eliminate unconscious biases. The CME-sensitive observables studied include the gamma correlator (Delta-gamma) and the ratio Delta-gamma/v_2, with comparisons made between the two isobar systems.

## Key numbers

* Approximately 1.8 billion minimum-bias events for Ru+Ru and 2.0 billion for Zr+Zr collisions were analyzed after event selection cuts
* A precision of 0.4% was achieved in the relative magnitudes of CME-sensitive observables between the two isobar systems
* The magnetic field squared was expected to be about 15% larger in Ru+Ru collisions compared to Zr+Zr collisions due to the larger atomic number
* The difference in Delta-gamma background between the two isobars was estimated to be less than 1% in peripheral to mid-central collisions, but larger than 2% in 0-20% centrality collisions
* Nuclear structure calculations suggested eccentricities may differ by 2-3% in mid-central collisions between the two isobars
* Hydrodynamic simulations found approximately 4% difference in flow-driven background between the two systems
* Less than 4% of the data was discarded based on predefined quality assurance criteria
* The TPC tracking efficiency ranged from 85% to 90% as determined by GEANT Monte Carlo simulations

## Context

This work extends previous CME searches by STAR in Au+Au and Cu+Cu collisions at 200 GeV (Abelev 2009, Adamczyk 2013, 2014) and by CMS and ALICE at LHC energies (Khachatryan 2016, Sirunyan 2017, Acharya 2017), which remained inconclusive due to large background contributions. The isobar collision approach was proposed by Voloshin (2010) to exploit the similar nuclear masses but different atomic numbers of Ru and Zr to control backgrounds while maintaining sensitivity to CME signals. The results enable future comparisons with theoretical calculations of CME signals and backgrounds, and inform the design of next-generation measurements at RHIC and future facilities.
