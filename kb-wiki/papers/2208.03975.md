# Fate of the first-order chiral phase transition in QCD: Implications for dark QCD studied via a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model

> The first-order nature of the chiral phase transition in QCD-like theories can play crucial roles to address a dark side of the Universe, where the created out-of equilibrium is essential to serve as cosmological and astrophysical probes such as gravitational wave productions, which have extensively been explored. This interdisciplinary physics is built based on a widely-accepted conjecture that the thermal chiral phase transition in QCD-like theories with massless (light) three flavors is of first order. We find that such a first order feature may not hold, when ordinary or dark quarks are externally coupled to a weak enough background field of photon or dark photon (which we collectively call a ``magnetic" field). We assume that a weak ``magnetic" background field could be originated from some ``magnetogenesis" in the early Universe. We work on a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model which can describe the chiral phase transition in a wide class of QCD-like theories. We show that in the case with massless (light) three flavors, the first-order feature goes away when $2 f_π^2 \lesssim eB ( \ll (4 πf_π)^2)$, where $eB$ is the ``magnetic" field strength and $f_π$ the pion decay constant at the vacuum. This disappearance is the generic consequence of the presence of the ``magnetically" induced scale anomaly and the ``magnetic" catalysis for the chiral symmetry breaking, and would impact or constrain modeling dark QCD coupled to an external ``magnetic" field.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the widely-accepted first-order chiral phase transition in massless (light) three-flavor QCD-like theories may not hold when ordinary or dark quarks are coupled to a weak enough background "magnetic" field (photon or dark photon). The first-order feature disappears when the magnetic field strength satisfies \(2 f_\pi^2 \lesssim eB \ll (4\pi f_\pi)^2\), where \(f_\pi\) is the pion decay constant at the vacuum.

## Method

The authors employ a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with three light flavors (up, down, strange) in the mean field approximation, incorporating a constant external magnetic field along the \(z\)-direction. They include the contribution from the magnetically induced scale anomaly via a tadpole potential term for the chiral singlet scalar meson, which is prominent only in the weak field regime, and evaluate the thermomagnetic potential using the imaginary time formalism and Landau level decomposition. The order of the chiral phase transition is determined by analyzing the temperature dependence of the constituent quark mass \(M_u\) and the sharpness of its derivative \(d M_u/dT\) at the pseudocritical temperature.

## Key numbers

*   The critical magnetic field strength for the disappearance of the first-order transition is estimated as \(eB_{\rm cr}/f_\pi^2 \sim 2\), above which (but still at weak \(eB \ll (4\pi f_\pi)^2\)) the first-order feature goes away.
*   The NJL model parameters are fixed using hadronic observables at \(T=eB=0\): \(f_\pi = 92.4\) MeV, \(m_\pi \simeq 135.0\) MeV, \(m_K \simeq 497.7\) MeV, and \(m_{\eta'} \simeq 957.8\) MeV, with \(m_0 = 5.5\) MeV and \(m_s = 140.7\) MeV.
*   The dimensionless coupling parameters are set as \(G\Lambda^2 \simeq 1.835\) and \(K\Lambda^5 \simeq 12.36\), with \(\Lambda/f_\pi \simeq 6.518\) (yielding \(\Lambda = 602.3\) MeV).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the chiral phase transition in QCD-like theories, originally studied by Pisarski and Wilczek using renormalization group methods and by the Columbia group via lattice QCD (summarized in the Columbia plot), to include the effects of a weak background magnetic field. The finding challenges the common assumption that the first-order transition in massless three-flavor QCD is robust, and it has implications for modeling dark QCD scenarios where a primordial magnetic field from "magnetogenesis" may be present. The result would impact or constrain models of dark QCD coupled to an external magnetic field, which are used to address cosmological and astrophysical probes such as gravitational wave production and dark matter candidates like SIMPs (Strongly Interacting Massive Particles).
