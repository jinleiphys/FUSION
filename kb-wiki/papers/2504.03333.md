# NucleiML: A machine learning framework of ground-state properties of finite nuclei for accelerated Bayesian exploration

> The global behavior of the nuclear equation of state (EoS) is commonly studied using data from finite nuclei (FN), heavy-ion collisions, and astrophysical observations of neutron stars (NS). The constraints derived from FN such as binding energies and charge radii play the most crucial role in shaping the EoS up to saturation density. The computational cost associated with explicitly incorporating these constraints presents a significant challenge especially when the aim is to explore the model uncertainties rather than optimizing a single model. We address this by introducing NucleiML (NML), a machine learning framework trained on ground-state properties of a few finite nuclei generated by a relativistic mean-field model. NML allows us to integrate FN and NS properties within a Bayesian inference framework in an efficient manner. The results demonstrate reasonable accuracy and a speedup of $\sim 10^4$ times for calculation of FN properties for a single parameter set, yielding roughly $\sim 10^3 \times$ speed up in the Bayesian framework. The present study makes the case for extending the work to a larger set of nuclei, potentially enabling future studies of NS properties to incorporate the whole nuclear chart.

## Key claim

NucleiML (NML) achieves a speedup of approximately 10^4 times for calculating finite-nucleus properties for a single parameter set and roughly 10^3 times speedup within a Bayesian inference framework, while maintaining reasonable accuracy compared to the underlying relativistic mean-field model. The framework is demonstrated only for a limited set of five closed-shell spherical nuclei, and the authors note that extending to a larger set of nuclei is needed for future studies.

## Method

The authors trained a two-component neural network framework on ground-state properties (binding energy and charge radius) of five closed-shell spherical nuclei (16O, 40Ca, 48Ca, 132Sn, 208Pb) computed using a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model across randomly sampled nuclear matter parameters. The first component is a classifier that determines whether a given set of nuclear matter parameters yields admissible (convergent and physically reasonable) results, and the second is a regressor that predicts the binding energy and charge radius for admissible inputs. The neural networks were trained using TensorFlow and Keras with hyperparameters optimized via grid search, using a 70%-15%-15% split for training, validation, and testing of the classifier, and a 70%-21%-9% split for the regressor.

## Key numbers

* Classifier accuracy: 95% total accuracy, with precision of 0.93 and recall of 0.97 for the admissible class, and precision of 0.97 and recall of 0.94 for the non-admissible class.
* Regressor training loss (MAPE) stabilized at approximately 0.5% for binding energy and charge radius predictions.
* Global R² score of 0.998 across the complete test dataset aggregating predictions for all nuclei and both observables.
* Per-nucleus R² scores ranged from approximately 0.81 (charge radius of 40Ca) to approximately 0.91 (binding energy of 208Pb).
* Binding energy deviations showed a standard deviation of approximately 0.05 (5%), while charge radius deviations showed a standard deviation of approximately 0.02 (2%).
* Out of approximately 120,000 input parameter samples, only about 4000 (approximately 4%) exceeded a 5% deviation threshold.
* Speedup of approximately 10^4 times for calculation of finite-nucleus properties for a single parameter set, yielding roughly 10^3 times speedup in the Bayesian framework.

## Context

This work extends the explicit incorporation of finite-nucleus constraints from binding energies and charge radii of 40Ca and 208Pb into Bayesian inference, as previously done by Venneti et al. (2024) using a full RMF model. It builds on prior efforts to accelerate finite-nucleus calculations using reduced-order methods (e.g., reduced-basis methods from 2022) and data-driven surrogates (e.g., Higdon et al., 2015), but adopts artificial neural networks to enable rapid evaluations without reformulating the underlying physics. The framework enables future studies to incorporate constraints from a larger set of nuclei across the nuclear chart into Bayesian analyses of neutron star properties, potentially improving constraints on the nuclear equation of state up to saturation density.
