# Saturation and geometrical scaling in small systems

> Saturation and geometrical scaling (GS) of gluon distributions are a consequence of the non-linear evolution equations of QCD. We argue that in pp GS holds for the inelastic cross-section rather than for the multiplicity distributions. We also discuss possible fluctuations of the proton saturation scale in pA collisions at the LHC.

# Saturation and geometrical scaling in small systems

## Key claim
Geometrical scaling (GS) in proton-proton collisions holds for the inelastic cross-section rather than for multiplicity distributions, because the minimum-bias cross-section is energy-dependent and not equal to the transverse overlap area. For proton-nucleus collisions, fluctuations of the proton saturation scale with a large width (σ ~ 1.55) are required to describe ALICE multiplicity data.

## Method
The author tests geometrical scaling by plotting ALICE pp data at √s = 0.9, 2.76, and 7 TeV in terms of the scaling variable τ = pT²/Qs²(x), where the saturation scale Qs²(x) = Q0² (x/x0)^(-λ) with λ determined by minimizing the spread of the scaled cross-section ratios. For pA collisions, the Kharzeev-Levin formula for charged particle multiplicity is modified by introducing log-Gaussian fluctuations of the proton saturation scale, with the fluctuation width σ treated as a free parameter.

## Key numbers
- The exponent λ = 0.32 gives the best geometrical scaling for pp inelastic cross-sections, compatible with model-independent DIS analysis by Praszalowicz (2012).
- For multiplicity distributions in pp, the best scaling exponent is λ = 0.22–0.24, but with worse quality and over a smaller τ interval than for cross-sections.
- Geometrical scaling for pp cross-sections holds up to pT = 4.25 GeV/c.
- For pA collisions, the fluctuation width required to describe ALICE data is σ ≈ 1.55.
- The normalization S⊥ for pA is fitted as S⊥ = (0.88 + 0.47 ln Npart)².

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of geometrical scaling in DIS by Stasto, Golec-Biernat, and Kwiecinski (2000) and in pp multiplicity distributions by McLerran and Praszalowicz (2010). It contradicts the assumption that GS applies directly to multiplicities in pp, showing instead that the inelastic cross-section is the proper observable. For pA collisions, it addresses the failure of the simple Kharzeev-Levin (2004) formula to describe ALICE data (Adam et al., 2014) by incorporating saturation scale fluctuations following the log-Gaussian distribution introduced by Iancu, Itakura, and McLerran (2004). This enables improved modeling of centrality-dependent multiplicity distributions in small collision systems at LHC energies.
