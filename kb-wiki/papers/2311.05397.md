# Spreading widths of giant monopole resonance in the lead region: Random matrix approach

> The microscopic calculation of the decay width of giant monopole resonance (GMR) anticipates the mixing of one-phonon states with configurations of increasing complexity. To this aim we develop the effective approach for description of monopole excited states that are obtained in the quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA), with regard of the coupling between one- and two-phonon states. Based on the QRPA one-phonon states, we generate the coupling and two-phonon states by means of the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) distribution. Within our approach the spreading width of the GMRs in $^{204,206,208}$Pb are described by means of a random matrix approach on two energy scales. It is demonstrated that the main contribution into the decay of the GMR is determined by a small number of two-phonon states strongly coupled to low-energy surface vibrations. While a vast majority of the coupling matrix elements (that are small in value and following the GOE distribution) are responsible for the fine structure of the GMR spreading width. A remarkable agreement between the results of the full microscopic calculations (based on QRPA phonons coupled by means of the microscopic coupling matrix elements with calculated two-phonon states) with those of the developed approach confirms the vitality of the proposed ideas.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the spreading width of the giant monopole resonance (GMR) in lead isotopes can be effectively described using a random matrix approach on two energy scales, where a small number of two-phonon states strongly coupled to low-energy surface vibrations determine the main decay contribution, while the majority of weakly coupled matrix elements following a Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) distribution account for the fine structure.

## Method

The authors first perform quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) calculations using the Skyrme SLy4 interaction to obtain one-phonon monopole states for 204,206,208Pb. They then construct a Hamiltonian where these one-phonon doorway states are coupled to two-phonon background states, but replace the microscopic coupling matrix elements with random Gaussian-distributed matrix elements. The two-phonon space is decomposed into two subspaces: one with strong coupling (σ1 ≈ 600 keV, density ~1 state/MeV) representing couplings to low-lying surface vibrations, and one with weak coupling (σ2 ≈ 30 keV) representing the remaining background states.

## Key numbers

- The rms value of the ten strongest coupling matrix elements is σ1 = 588 keV for 208Pb, and σ1 = 560 keV and 590 keV for 204Pb and 206Pb respectively
- The weak coupling strength is σ2 ≈ 30 keV for all three isotopes 204,206,208Pb
- The number of one-phonon QRPA states in the 8-20 MeV interval is 27, 28, and 15 for 204Pb, 206Pb, and 208Pb respectively
- The number of two-phonon states coupled to 0+ is 2126, 2210, and 914 for 204Pb, 206Pb, and 208Pb respectively
- The RMT approach yields centroid energies Ec = 14.4, 14.5, and 14.7 MeV for 204Pb, 206Pb, and 208Pb, compared to experimental values of 13.8 ± 0.1 MeV for all three isotopes
- The RMT approach yields widths Γ = 4.3, 4.2, and 4.0 MeV for 204Pb, 206Pb, and 208Pb, compared to experimental values of 3.3 ± 0.2, 2.8 ± 0.2, and 3.3 ± 0.2 MeV respectively
- The strongest coupling matrix element in 208Pb is 1.37 MeV for the [3−1 ⊗ 3−1]0+ two-phonon state at 7.1 MeV

## Context

This work extends the random matrix theory approach previously applied to isovector dipole giant resonances by Severyukhin et al. (2014, 2016) to the case of giant monopole resonances in the lead region. The approach is motivated by wavelet analysis of isoscalar quadrupole giant resonances by Shevchenko et al. (2004), which demonstrated the presence of different energy scales responsible for fluctuations in resonance cross sections. The method enables effective treatment of the coupling hierarchy that would be numerically prohibitive in full microscopic calculations, and can be readily extended to include three-phonon states, which would be a laborious task in microscopic calculations.
