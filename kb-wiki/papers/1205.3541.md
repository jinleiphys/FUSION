# Dark matter transport properties and rapidly rotating neutron stars

> Neutron stars are attractive places to look for dark matter because their high densities allow repeated interactions. Weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs) may scatter efficiently in the core or in the crust of a neutron star. In this paper we focus on WIMP contributions to transport properties, such as shear viscosity or thermal conductivity, because these can be greatly enhanced by long mean free paths. We speculate that WIMPs increase the shear viscosity of neutron star matter and help stabilize r-mode oscillations. These are collective oscillations where the restoring force is the Coriolis force. At present r-modes are thought to be unstable in many observed rapidly rotating stars. If WIMPs stabilize the r-modes, this would allow neutron stars to spin rapidly. This likely requires WIMP-nucleon cross sections near present experimental limits and an appropriate density of WIMPs in neutron stars.

# Dark matter transport properties and rapidly rotating neutron stars

## Key claim
WIMPs may dominate the shear viscosity of dense neutron-rich matter and thereby stabilize r-mode oscillations in rapidly rotating neutron stars, but this requires WIMP-nucleon cross sections near present experimental limits and a WIMP number fraction of approximately 10^{-10} relative to nucleons in the stellar core.

## Method
The author calculates the WIMP mean free path in the neutron star core using the relation λ_W = 1/(σ_{Wn} n_n) and estimates the required cross section for a mean free path of about 10 km. The WIMP contribution to shear viscosity is computed from kinetic theory as η_W = (1/3)(3kTM_W)^{1/2} λ_W n_W, and this is compared to the calculated shear viscosity of neutron-rich matter without WIMPs from Shternin et al. (2007). The optical depth for WIMP scattering in the inner crust is also estimated using coherent WIMP-nucleus scattering cross sections with static structure factor and form factor corrections.

## Key numbers
- WIMP mean free path of λ_W ≈ 10 km requires σ_{Wn} ≈ 3 × 10^{-45} cm² for a neutron star core density of n_n ≈ 0.32 fm^{-3}
- The shear viscosity of neutron-rich matter without WIMPs at density 2n_0 = 0.32 fm^{-3} and temperature T ≈ 10^8 K is η_n = 2 × 10^{19} g cm^{-1} s^{-1} (Shternin et al., 2007)
- For M_W = 50 GeV, the WIMP shear viscosity η_W equals η_n when the WIMP number density is n_W ≈ 3 × 10^{28} cm^{-3}
- This corresponds to a WIMP-to-nucleon number fraction of X_W = n_W/n_n ≈ 10^{-10}
- The inner crust optical depth estimate gives τ ≈ σ_0 × 2.5 × 10^{18} fm^{-2}, requiring σ_0 ≈ 4 × 10^{-45} cm² for optical depth of order unity
- For M_W = 50 GeV, efficient crust scattering requires temperature T ≥ 7 × 10^7 K; for M_W = 10 GeV, T ≥ 3.5 × 10^8 K
- The inner crust thickness is Δr ≈ 500 m with typical nucleon density ⟨n(r)⟩ ≈ 0.05 fm^{-3}

## Context
This work extends prior studies of WIMP interactions with neutron stars, which have considered WIMP capture leading to black hole formation (Kouvaris, 2008; Lavallaz and Fairbairn, 2010), conversion of hadronic neutron stars to strange stars (Perez-Garcia et al., 2010), and WIMP annihilation heating (Kouvaris, 2008). The paper specifically addresses the long-standing puzzle that observed rapidly rotating neutron stars appear unstable to r-mode oscillations (Bildsten, 1998; Andersson et al., 1999), where calculations of shear viscosity from standard neutron matter (Benhar et al., 2004), neutron-rich matter with leptons (Shternin et al., 2007), nuclear pasta (Horowitz and Berry, 2008), and crust-core boundary layers (Rieutord, 2001) have found insufficient damping. If confirmed, this mechanism would allow neutron stars to spin rapidly and could explain why old neutron stars (which have captured more dark matter) are observed as millisecond pulsars, while young rapidly rotating stars are not seen.
