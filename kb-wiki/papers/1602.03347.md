# Can one measure the Cosmic Neutrino Background?

> The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) yields information about our Universe at around 380 000 years after the Big Bang (BB). Due to the weak interaction of the neutrinos with matter the Cosmic Neutrino Background (CNB) should give information about a much earlier time of our Universe, around one second after the Big Bang. Probably the most promising method to `see' the Cosmic Neutrino Background is the capture of the electron neutrinos from the Background by Tritium, which then decays into 3He and an electron with the energy of the the Q-value = 18.562 keV plus the electron neutrino rest mass. The `KArlsruhe TRItium Neutrino' (KATRIN) experiment, which is in preparation, seems presently the most sensitive proposed method for measuring the electron antineutrino mass. At the same time KATRIN can also look by the reaction: electron neutrino (~1.95 Kelvin) + 3H --> 3He + e- (with the energy Q = 18.6 keV + electron neutrino mass). The capture of the Cosmic Background Neutrinos (CNB) should show in the electron spectrum as a peak by the electron neutrino rest mass above Q. Here the possibility to see the CNB with KATRIN is studied. A detection of the CNB by KATRIN seems not to be possible at the moment. But KATRIN should be able to determine an upper limit for the local electron neutrino density of the CNB.

## Key claim

The KATRIN experiment, in its current configuration, cannot detect the Cosmic Neutrino Background (CNB) via neutrino capture on tritium, but it should be able to set an upper limit on the local electron neutrino density of the CNB.

## Method

The authors calculate the capture rate of relic electron neutrinos from the CNB by tritium via the reaction ν_e + ³H → ³He + e⁻, using the same Fermi and Gamow-Teller matrix elements as for tritium beta decay. They then scale this rate to the KATRIN experimental parameters (20 μg tritium source, 0.93 eV energy resolution) and consider gravitational clustering enhancements to estimate the observable signal.

## Key numbers

- Average relic electron neutrino density in the Universe: ⟨n_{ν,e}⟩ = 56 cm⁻³
- Capture rate for one tritium atom per year: 4.2 × 10⁻²⁵ × (n_{ν,e}/⟨n_{ν,e}⟩) per year
- KATRIN capture rate with average density: 1.7 × 10⁻⁶ per year, corresponding to one count every 590,000 years
- With optimistic gravitational overdensity of 10⁶: 1.7 counts per year at KATRIN
- With a 2 mg tritium source and overdensity of 10⁶: 170 counts per year
- KATRIN energy resolution: ΔE = 0.93 eV
- KATRIN expected upper limit on electron antineutrino mass: m_{ν,e} ≤ 0.2 eV at 90% C.L.
- Tritium Q-value: Q = 18.562 keV

## Context

This work extends the original proposal by Weinberg (1962) to detect relic neutrinos via induced beta decay, and builds on the KATRIN design report and discussions with Drexlin and Weinheimer. The authors compare with alternative approaches: the Project 8 cyclotron radiation method (Kaboth, 2013) and the PTOLEMY project (Princeton Tritium Observatory for Light, Early-Universe, Massive-Neutrino Yield), which proposes a 100-gram tritium source on graphene layers but faces extreme technical challenges. The paper concludes that while direct detection is not currently feasible with KATRIN, the experiment can provide the first upper limit on the local relic neutrino overdensity in our Galaxy.
