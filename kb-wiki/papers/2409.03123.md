# High Energy Physics from Low Energy Physics

> The separation between physics at low and high energies is essential for physics to have any utility; the details of quantum gravity are not necessary to calculate the trajectory of a cannon ball. However, physics at low and high energies are not completely independent, and this thesis explores two ways that they are related. The first is through a UV/IR symmetry that relates scattering processes at low and high energies. This UV/IR symmetry manifests in geometrical properties of the $S$-matrix, and of the RG flow of the coupling constants in the corresponding effective field theory. Low energy nuclear physics nearly realizes this UV/IR symmetry, providing an explanation for the smallness of shape parameters in the effective range expansion of nucleon-nucleon scattering, and inspiring a new way to organize the interactions between neutrons and protons. The second is through the use of quantum computers to simulate lattice gauge theories. Quantum simulations rely on the universality of the rules of quantum mechanics, which can be applied equally well to describe a (low energy) transmon qubit at 15 milli-Kelvin as a (high energy) 1 TeV quark. This thesis presents the first simulations of one dimensional lattice quantum chromodynamics on a quantum computer, culminating in a real-time simulation of beta-decay. Results from the first simulations of a lattice gauge theory on 100+ qubits of a quantum computer are also presented. The methods developed in this thesis for quantum simulation are ``physics-aware", and are guided by the symmetries and hierarchies in length scales of the systems being studied. Without these physics-aware methods, 100+ qubit simulations of lattice gauge theories would not have been possible on the noisy quantum computers that are presently available.

## Key claim

This thesis explores two connections between low-energy and high-energy physics: a UV/IR symmetry in scattering processes that manifests in the S-matrix and RG flow, and the use of quantum computers to simulate lattice gauge theories. The author claims that "physics-aware" methods guided by symmetries and hierarchies in length scales enabled 100+ qubit simulations of lattice gauge theories that would not have been possible on noisy quantum computers otherwise.

## Method

The author formulates a 1+1 dimensional lattice gauge theory Hamiltonian for quantum chromodynamics with beta-decay interactions, using the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian formulation and Jordan-Wigner mapping to encode the theory onto qubits. Quantum simulations of single-baryon beta-decay (specifically Delta-minus to Delta-zero plus electron and antineutrino) were performed on a single spatial lattice site using Quantinuum's H1-1 20-qubit trapped ion quantum computer, with state preparation via a Variational Quantum Eigensolver circuit and Trotterized time evolution. The simulations used post-selection on physical states with baryon number B=1 and lepton number L=0 for error mitigation, without more advanced techniques like Pauli twirling or dynamical decoupling.

## Key numbers

- Fermi's coupling constant: G_F = 1.1663787(6) × 10^{-5} GeV^{-2}
- A single generation of the Standard Model maps onto 16 qubits per spatial lattice site using the Jordan-Wigner mapping
- The L=1 system requires 16 qubits total (12 quark and 4 lepton qubits)
- State preparation for the Delta-minus baryon requires 9 CNOT gates
- One Trotter step requires 59 ZZ gates (after transpilation to native gates), two Trotter steps require 212 ZZ gates
- Results at approximately 5% level were obtained with minimal error mitigation on the quantum computer
- Post-selection retained approximately 90% of events for one Trotter step and 50% for two Trotter steps
- The Delta-minus baryon energy gap above vacuum is 6.409 (in lattice units with m_u=0.9, m_d=2.1, g=2)

## Context

This work extends previous quantum simulations of lattice gauge theories by Farrell (2022) to include flavor-changing weak interactions via four-Fermi operators, enabling the first quantum simulations of beta-decay. The UV/IR symmetry analysis builds on the effective field theory framework for nuclear beta-decays developed by Kaplan (1998) and others, and the quantum simulation methods follow the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian formulation of lattice gauge theory from Kogut (1974) and Banks (1975). The results enable future real-time simulations of hadronic decays and nuclear reaction dynamics that lie beyond the capabilities of experiment, potentially providing insight into strong-interaction processes and offering first-principles constraints beyond Euclidean-space lattice QCD calculations.
