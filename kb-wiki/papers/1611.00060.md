# Understanding emergent collectivity and clustering in nuclei from a symmetry-based no-core shell-model perspective

> We present a detailed discussion of the structure of the low-lying positive-parity energy spectrum of $^{12}$C from a no-core shell-model perspective. The approach utilizes a fraction of the usual shell-model space and extends its multi-shell reach via the symmetry-based no-core symplectic shell model (NCSpM) with a simple, physically-informed effective interaction. We focus on the ground-state rotational band, the Hoyle state and its $2^+$ and $4^+$ excitations, as well as the giant monopole $0^+$ resonance, which is a vibrational breathing mode of the ground state. This, in turn, allows us to address the open question about the structure of the Hoyle state and its rotational band. In particular, we find that the Hoyle state is best described through deformed prolate collective modes rather than vibrational modes, while we show that the higher-lying giant monopole $0^+$ resonance resembles the oblate deformation of the $^{12}$C ground state. In addition, we identify the giant monopole $0^+$ and quadrupole $2^+$ resonances of selected light and intermediate-mass nuclei, along with other observables of $^{12}$C, including matter rms radii, electric quadrupole moments, as well as $E2$ and $E0$ transition rates.

## Key claim

The Hoyle state in \(^{12}\)C is best described through deformed prolate collective modes rather than vibrational modes, while the higher-lying giant monopole \(0^+\) resonance resembles the oblate deformation of the \(^{12}\)C ground state; this conclusion is drawn from a no-core shell-model approach that uses a fraction of the usual shell-model space and a schematic interaction with one adjustable parameter \(\gamma\).

## Method

The authors employed the no-core symplectic shell model (NCSpM), which uses a symmetry-guided down-selection of the model space to physically relevant symplectic irreps (bandheads of highest quadrupole deformation and lowest intrinsic spin), vertically extended to high \(N_{\max}\) and then expanded horizontally until convergence. The effective Hamiltonian includes a harmonic oscillator potential, a spin-orbit term, and a quadrupole-quadrupole interaction with a single adjustable parameter \(\gamma\) that controls the presence of many-body interactions; the spin-orbit term is applied only to the symplectic bandheads. Calculations were performed for \(^{12}\)C in model spaces up to \(N_{\max}=20\) with total dimensionalities ranging from \(4.5\times10^3\) to larger spaces, and the same \(\gamma\) value was also applied to other nuclei including \(^{8}\)Be and \(sd\)-shell nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The Hoyle state in \(^{12}\)C is the 7.65-MeV second \(0_2^+\) state.
- The model uses \(\hbar\Omega = 18\) MeV and \(\kappa = 3.8\) MeV for \(A=12\).
- The adjustable parameter \(\gamma = 1.71\times 10^{-4}\) was used for all nuclei studied.
- Model space \(\mathfrak{C}\)-1 has total dimensionality \(4.5\times10^3\); model space \(\mathfrak{C}\)-2 has total dimensionality \(6.6\times10^3\).
- The spin-orbit mixing of the \((1\,2)\) irrep with the \((6\,2)\) irrep is estimated to be about 0.2% for the \(^{12}\)C ground state, and with the \((12\,0)\) irrep about \(4\times 10^{-4}\)%.
- The bandheads of the \((0\,4)\) and \((1\,2)\) irreps constitute approximately 70% of the \(0^+_{gs}\), \(2^+_1\), and \(4^+_1\) wave functions.

## Context

This work extends the NCSpM approach previously applied to \(^{8}\)Be and \(sd\)-shell nuclei (Launey et al., 2013; Tobin et al., 2014) and addresses the long-standing question of whether the Hoyle state is a vibrational or shape-coexistence mode, corroborating earlier findings of a large prolate deformation for the Hoyle state (Dreyfuss et al., 2013). The results enable future studies of giant monopole and quadrupole resonances in light and intermediate-mass nuclei, and the approach provides a framework for understanding \(\alpha\)-clustering and highly-deformed spatial configurations from a microscopic many-particle perspective, complementing ab initio studies such as lattice EFT (Epelbaum et al., 2011, 2012) and Green's function Monte Carlo (Wiringa, 2012).
