# Level Densities from 0-30 MeV

> Photon strength, $f(E_γ)$, measured in photonuclear reactions, is the product of the average level density per MeV, $ρ(E_x)$, and the average reduced level width, $Γ_γ/E_γ^3$ for levels populated primarily by E1 transitions at an excitation energy $E_x=E_γ$. It can be calculated with the Brink-Axel (BA) formulation modified to include contributions from the Giant Dipole Resonance (GDR) and higher lying resonances. Level densities and reduced widths have been calculated for 17 nuclei with atomic numbers between Z=14-92. Level densities below the GDR energy were calculated with the CT-JPI model and combined with the BA photon strength to determine the associated reduced widths. The reduced widths varied exponentially with level energy and could be extrapolated up to higher energies. The extrapolated widths were then combined with the BA photon strength to determine the level densities at higher energies. The level densities are found to increase exponentially at low energies, peak near the GDR energy due to the appearance of new states at the $2\hbarω$ shell closure, and continue to increase less rapidly up to at least 30 MeV. The average level densities have been compared with the Fermi Gas Level Density (FGLD), Back-Shifted Fermi Gas (BSFG), and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) models. Good agreement is found with the nearly identical FGLD and BDFG models, while the HFB models gives substantially lower level densities. A universal set of FGLD model parameters were determined as a function of mass and temperature that are applicable to all nuclei.

# Level Densities from 0-30 MeV

## Key claim
Level densities for nuclei populated by E1 transitions in photonuclear reactions can be determined up to 30 MeV by combining the Brink-Axel photon strength function with low-energy level densities from the CT-JPI model, then extrapolating the reduced level widths exponentially to higher energies. The level densities peak near the Giant Dipole Resonance energy due to the appearance of new states at the 2ħω shell closure, not due to collective enhancement of transition probability.

## Method
The author calculated level densities and reduced widths for 17 nuclei with atomic numbers Z=14-92. Level densities below the GDR energy were computed using the CT-JPI constant-temperature model with separate energy cutoffs for each spin and parity, then combined with the Brink-Axel photon strength function to extract reduced level widths. These widths were fit to an exponential function and extrapolated to higher energies, then recombined with the BA photon strength to determine level densities up to 30 MeV. The resulting level densities were compared with the Fermi Gas Level Density (FGLD), Back-Shifted Fermi Gas (BSFG), and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) models.

## Key numbers
- Level densities calculated for 17 nuclei with atomic numbers between Z=14-92
- Reduced widths fit to exponential function with uncertainty less than 2% and coefficient of determination R² > 0.994 for all cases
- For ²⁸Si, the exponential fit constants were A=1.07×10⁻⁶ and B=-0.539
- FGLD model parameters for ²⁸Si: α=2.38, c=-2.97, a=7.44, with normalization factor N_FG=2×10⁻⁵
- Average FGLD parameters across all nuclei: α=2.383(14), c=-2.96(4)
- Level density parameter a fit by relation a=78(3)/(T²√Z) or a=119(3)/(T²√A)
- Temperatures follow empirical relation T=0.80(4)/√A with approximately 15% accuracy
- For ²⁰⁸Pb, the GDR centroid energy is 13.04 MeV (peak 1) and 13.64 MeV (peak 2)
- For ²⁸Si, the GDR energies are 19.00 MeV and 23.01 MeV

## Context
This work extends the Brink-Axel formulation (Brink 1955, Axel 1962) by including contributions from higher shell closures (3ħω, 4ħω, 5ħω) beyond the GDR, and combines it with the CT-JPI level density model (Firestone 2021) which corrects deficiencies in the conventional Constant Temperature model (Ericson 1960) and Back-Shifted Fermi Gas model (Bethe 1936, 1937). The results enable calculation of level densities for nuclei with little experimental data using a universal set of FGLD model parameters as a function of mass and temperature, and provide evidence that the GDR peak is a shell effect rather than collective behavior. The approach mirrors the Gilbert-Cameron method (Gilbert 1965) but with proper spin-parity dependence.
