# ToMCCA-3: A realistic 3-body coalescence model

> The formation of light nuclei in high-energy collisions provides valuable insights into the underlying dynamics of the strong interaction and the structure of the particle-emitting source. Understanding this process is crucial not only for nuclear physics but also for astrophysical studies, where the production of rare antinuclei could serve as a probe for new physics. This work presents a three-body coalescence model based on the Wigner function formalism, offering a refined description of light-nucleus production. By incorporating realistic two- and three-body nuclear interaction potentials constrained by modern scattering and femtoscopic correlation data, our approach improves on traditional coalescence models. The framework is validated using event generators applied to proton-proton collisions at $\sqrt{s}=13$ TeV to predict the momentum spectra of light (anti) nuclear nuclei with mass number $A=3$, which are then compared with the experimental data from ALICE. Our results demonstrate the sensitivity of light nucleus yields to the choice of nuclear wave functions, emphasizing the importance of an accurate description of the coalescence process. This model lays the foundation for the extension of coalescence studies of $A=3$ light nuclei to a wider range of collision systems and energies.

## Key claim

The three-body coalescence model ToMCCA-3, based on the Wigner function formalism with realistic two- and three-body nuclear interaction potentials (AV18+UIX and Minnesota), can describe ALICE measurements of A=3 light nuclei and hypernuclei yields in proton-proton collisions at √s=13 TeV, but the results show sensitivity to the choice of nuclear wave functions and a slight tension for minimum bias collisions that remains within 2 standard deviations.

## Method

The authors extended the ToMCCA event-by-event coalescence Monte Carlo generator to A=3 systems (³H, ³He, and ³ΛH) by implementing the Wigner function formalism with three-dimensional reduction from the four-dimensional Bethe-Salpeter formalism using low-energy and equal-time approximations. The trinucleon bound-state wave functions were calculated using the pair-correlated hyperspherical harmonics (PHH) method with the AV18 NN potential augmented by the UIX three-body potential, as well as the Minnesota NN potential, while the hypertriton wave function used the Congleton approach with a deuteron core and a Λ binding energy of 128 keV. The model was validated against ALICE data for proton-proton collisions at √s=13 TeV across multiple multiplicity classes.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy of ³H with AV18+UIX: 8.479 MeV (proton radius 1.582 fm, matter radius 1.683 fm)
- Binding energy of ³He with AV18+UIX: 7.750 MeV (proton radius 1.771 fm, matter radius 1.715 fm)
- Binding energy of ³H with Minnesota potential: 8.386 MeV (proton radius 1.486 fm, matter radius 1.706 fm)
- Binding energy of ³He with Minnesota potential: 7.711 MeV (proton radius 1.708 fm, matter radius 1.736 fm)
- Λ binding energy in hypertriton model: B_Λ = 128 keV
- B_3 parameter evaluated at p_T/A = 0.73 GeV/c for A=3
- Multiplicity classes between ⟨dN_ch/dη⟩_|η|<0.5 = 2.5 and 25.75 for Λ spectra in pp collisions at √s=13 TeV
- Deuteron spectra uncertainty of 4.6% from experimental uncertainty of proton spectra

## Context

This work extends the ToMCCA coalescence model (Mahlein 2024) from deuterons to A=3 systems, improving on traditional coalescence models that use simple momentum cutoffs (p_0) by incorporating realistic nuclear wave functions from the PHH method (Kievsky 2008). The model addresses discrepancies between statistical hadronization models (Andronic 2010, 2017; Vovchenko 2018) and coalescence approaches for heavier systems like helium, triton, and hypertriton, particularly the hypertriton measurement in p-Pb collisions (ALargeIonColliderExperiment 2021) that supports coalescence over thermal production. The framework enables future extension to a wider range of collision systems and energies, and has applications for interpreting potential AMS-02 antinuclei cosmic ray measurements as dark matter signatures.
