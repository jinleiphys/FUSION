# Internal conversion of nuclear transition in the ${}^{235m}U@C_{60-n}X_n$ molecules and related compounds

> The internal conversion of nuclear transition in the ${}^{235}U$ atom is considered. The low-energy $γ-$quanta ($E_γ \approx$ 76.8 $eV$) are emitted during the $E3-$transition from the excited state ($I = (\frac12)^+$) of the ${}^{235}U$ nucleus to its ground state ($I = (\frac72)^-$). The decay rate of this ${}^{235m}U$ isomer ($E \approx 76.8$ $eV$) noticeably depends upon the chemical composition and actual physical conditions (i.e. temperature $T$ and pressure $p$). By varying such a composition and physical conditions one can change the life-time of the ${}^{235m}U$ isomer to relatively large/small values. A specific attention is given to the fullerene molecules containing the central ${}^{235}U$ atom. It is shown that the decay rate $λ$ of the ${}^{235m}U$ isomer in the ${}^{235m}U@C_{60-n}X_n$ molecules and related compounds can differ significantly from the values obtained for isolated ${}^{235}U$ atoms. Some applications of this effect are considered.

# Internal conversion of nuclear transition in the ${}^{235m}U@C_{60-n}X_n$ molecules and related compounds

## Key claim
The decay rate of the ${}^{235m}U$ isomer (excitation energy ≈ 76.8 eV) can be significantly altered by embedding the uranium atom in fullerene-based molecular structures such as ${}^{235m}U@C_{60-n}X_n$, because the occupation numbers of the $6p_{1/2}$ and $6p_{3/2}$ electron shells, the main contributors to the internal conversion process, change when the uranium atom is bound into a molecule. The effect is conditional on the molecule having at least 40–45 atoms to avoid instant fragmentation from the ≈ 77 eV excitation energy.

## Method
The internal conversion probability for the $E3$ nuclear transition (isomer spin $I = (1/2)^+$ to ground state $I = (7/2)^-$) was computed using the formalism of Grechukhin and Soldatov (1976), where the partial conversion probability per electron is expressed as $W = 2.3955 \times 10^{-10} \times w_e$ sec$^{-1}$, with $w_e$ being a pure atomic matrix element. The single-electron $w_e$ factors for all contributing shells of the uranium atom were calculated using the MOLFDIR relativistic quantum chemistry package. For molecular systems, the decay rate was modified by replacing atomic occupation numbers $N$ with molecular occupation numbers $N^{(M)}$ and adding a molecular correction term $\Delta w_e^{(M)}$ to the electronic factor, derived under a cluster approximation where the central uranium atom is well separated from surrounding atoms.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear transition energy: $E_\gamma \approx 76.8$ eV (with uncertainty $\pm 0.5$ eV from Zhudov et al (1979))
- Half-life of isolated ${}^{235m}U$ isomer: ≈ 26 min (Zhudov et al (1979))
- Computed decay rate constant for isolated ${}^{235}U$ atom with $(6s_{1/2})^2 (6p_{1/2})^2 (6p_{3/2})^4 (5f_{5/2})^3 (6d_{3/2})^1 (7s_{1/2})^2$ configuration: $\lambda \approx 2193.18$ sec = 36.55 min (≈ 40% above experimental value)
- Reduced nuclear matrix element for the $E3$ transition: bounded between $1.12 \times 10^{-2}$ and $1.26 \times 10^{-2}$; assumed value $1.20 \times 10^{-2}$
- Single-electron $w_e$ factors (dimensionless): $w_e(6p_{1/2}) = 4.95 \times 10^5$, $w_e(6p_{3/2}) = 2.17 \times 10^5$, $w_e(6d_{5/2}) = 4.45 \times 10^4$, $w_e(6d_{3/2}) = 4.09 \times 10^4$, $w_e(5f_{5/2}) = 6.87 \times 10^2$, $w_e(6s_{1/2}) = 6.58 \times 10^2$, $w_e(5f_{7/2}) = 3.01 \times 10^2$, $w_e(7s_{1/2}) = 7.05 \times 10^1$
- Excitation of one electron from $6p_{1/2}$ shell to $5f$ shell decreases $\lambda$ by ≈ 21%; from $6p_{3/2}$ shell decreases $\lambda$ by ≈ 9.5%
- Two-electron excitation from $6p_{1/2}$ shell increases half-life to ≈ 40 min; from $6p_{3/2}$ shell to ≈ 31 min
- Minimal number of atoms in molecule to avoid fragmentation from 77 eV excitation: $N_{min} \approx 38$–40 (based on ≈ 2 eV per atom fragmentation threshold)
- Critical energy for instant fragmentation of $C_{60}$: $E_{crit} \ge 2.7 \times 60 \approx 162$ eV (Mowrey et al (1991))
- Energies of $5d$ electron shells in uranium: $5d_{5/2} \approx 109.9857$ eV, $5d_{3/2} \approx 118.41311$ eV
- Energies of $6p$ electrons in uranium: $6p_{1/2} \approx 36.55$ eV, $6p_{3/2} \approx 26.80$ eV
- Probability of inverse internal conversion (nuclear excitation via molecular excitation): ≈ 1%

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of Grechukhin and Soldatov (1976) for internal conversion in isolated ${}^{235}U$ atoms to molecular environments, building on earlier experimental demonstrations by Mevergnies (1969, 1972) and Zhudov et al (1979) who measured the isomer half-life in various metallic foils (Au, Pt, Cu, Ni, V) and found maximal deviation of ≈ 5% between different metals. The paper proposes that fullerene-based molecules (${}^{235m}U@C_{60-n}X_n$) can produce much larger changes in the decay rate (up to tens of percent) by exploiting the overlap between uranium $6p$ orbitals and molecular orbitals with energies ≈ 18–37 eV. This enables two applications: (1) controlling neutron criticality in fissionable materials by varying the isomer concentration $x(t)$ in a ${}^{235}U$/${}^{235m}U$ mixture, using the one-velocity diffusion model of Weinberg and Wigner (1958); and (2) potential isotope separation of ${}^{235}U$ from other uranium isotopes, and possibly of ${}^{247}Cm$ and ${}^{251}Cf$ if similar low-energy isomers exist. The work also discusses the possibility of nuclear pumping via molecular excitations, though the author notes that the current understanding of atom-molecular interactions in these systems is far from complete and plans to apply more accurate relativistic methods (Bagus et al (2000), Graaf et al (1998)) in future work.
