# Supernarrow Dibaryons and Exotic Baryons with Small Masses

> A searche for supernarrow dibaryons (SND) and exotic baryons with small masses is reviewed. As a result of the study of the reaction pd->p+pX_1, three narrow peaks at M_{pX_1}=1904, 1926, and 1942 MeV have been observed. The analysis of the angular distributions of the protons from the decay of the pX_1 states showed that the peaks found can be explained as a manifestation of the isovector SNDs, the decay of which into two nucleons is forbidden by the Pauli exclusion principle. The observation of the peaks in the missing mass M_{X_1} spectra at 966, 986, and 1003 MeV is an additional indication that the dibaryons found are the SNDs. The possible interpretation of these peaks in the M_{X_1} mass spectra and peaks observed in the reaction pp->pi^+pX at M_X=1004, 1044, and 1094 MeV as new exotic baryon states with small masses is discussed. The mass equation has been constructed which is used to calculate the masses and determine parities of the exotic baryons. The obtained values of the masses are in a good agreement with the experimental data. Two new exotic baryon states bellow the πproduction threshold have been predicted.

# Supernarrow Dibaryons and Exotic Baryons with Small Masses

## Key claim
Three narrow peaks observed in the missing mass spectra of the reaction pd → p + pX₁ at 1904, 1926, and 1942 MeV are most likely isovector supernarrow dibaryons (SNDs) whose decay into two nucleons is forbidden by the Pauli exclusion principle, provided that the observed missing mass M_X₁ peaks at 966, 986, and 1003 MeV (which differ from the neutron mass) indeed indicate decay via the γ + n channel rather than background.

## Method
The experiment used a 305 MeV proton beam from the INR Proton Linear Accelerator on CD₂ and ¹²C targets, with the two-arm spectrometer TAMS detecting scattered protons at θ_L = 70° in coincidence with a second charged particle (proton or deuteron) at θ_R = 34°, 36°, and 38°. Software cuts were applied to select proton energies consistent with the SND decay into the γNN channel, suppressing background. Missing mass spectra M_{pX₁} and M_{X₁} were constructed, and angular distributions of decay protons were analyzed to distinguish SNDs from ordinary dibaryons. A dispersion relation with two subtractions for the mass operator, assuming only nucleon-plus-pion intermediate states with opposite parities, was used to calculate exotic baryon masses.

## Key numbers
- Three narrow peaks in M_{pX₁} spectra at 1904 ± 2, 1926 ± 2, and 1942 ± 2 MeV, with widths equal to the experimental resolution (~5 MeV) and statistical significances of 6.0, 7.0, and 6.3 standard deviations, respectively.
- Three peaks in M_{X₁} spectra at 966 ± 2, 986 ± 2, and 1003 ± 2 MeV, coinciding with simulation predictions for SND decay via γ + ³¹S₀ → γ pn.
- The neutron mass is 939.6 MeV, so the observed M_{X₁} values differ essentially from m_n, supporting the SND interpretation.
- Model-predicted exotic baryon masses: 963.4 MeV (J^P = 1/2⁻), 987 MeV (1/2⁺), 1010 MeV (1/2⁻), 1033 MeV (1/2⁺), 1056 MeV (1/2⁻), and 1079 MeV (1/2⁺).
- Experimental masses from Tatischeff et al. (2000) for the reaction pp → π⁺pX: 1004, 1044, and 1094 MeV, with widths 5–8 MeV and statistical significance between 2 and 17 standard deviations.
- Upper limit from Tamii et al. (2002) for NN-decoupled dibaryon production cross section: ~2 μb/sr if Γ_D << 1 MeV, and ~3.5 μb/sr if Γ_D ≈ 3 MeV, compared to the revised cross section of 8 ± 4 μb/sr from Fil'kov et al. (2001) (later estimated to be 2–3 times smaller).

## Context
This work extends the search for supernarrow dibaryons (SNDs) first proposed by Fil'kov and collaborators (1999, 2001) and the d' dibaryon postulated by Bilger et al. (1993) to explain pionic double charge exchange data, though that interpretation was challenged by Nussenzveig (1995). The observed peaks at 966, 986, and 1003 MeV are interpreted as either SND decay products or as new exotic baryon states with masses below the πN threshold, consistent with peaks seen by Tatischeff et al. (2000) in pp → π⁺pX at 1004, 1044, and 1094 MeV. The mass formula derived from dispersion relations predicts two new exotic baryon states at 1033 and 1079 MeV not yet observed, and the parity assignments (odd for states seen by Tatischeff et al.) disagree with the SU(6)ₓₛ 20-plet assignment proposed by Kobushkin (2000). The results contradict the null result of Tamii et al. (2002), attributed to their larger acceptance and insufficient energy cuts, and are constrained by Compton scattering data from L'vov and Workman (1998) which exclude such states as intermediate states in γp → γp.
