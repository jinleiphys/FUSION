# Sensitivity of Pion versus Parton-Jet Nuclear Modification Factors to the Path-Length Dependence of Jet-Energy Loss at RHIC and LHC

> We compare the jet-path length and beam-energy dependence of the pion nuclear modification factor and a parton-jet nuclear modification factor at RHIC and LHC. We contrast predictions based on a linear pQCD and a highly non-linear hybrid-AdS holographic model of jet-energy loss. We find that both models require a reduction of the jet-medium coupling from RHIC to LHC to account for the measured pion nuclear modification factor. In case of the parton-jet nuclear modification factor, however, which serves as a lower bound for the LO jet nuclear modification factor of reconstructed jets, the extracted data can be characterized without a reduced jet-medium coupling at LHC energies. We conclude that while reconstructed jets are sensitive to both quarks and gluons and thus provide more information than the pion nuclear modification factor, their information regarding the jet-medium coupling is limited due to the superimposition with NLO and medium effects. Hence, a detailed description of the underlying physics requires both the leading hadron and the reconstructed jet nuclear modification factor. Unfortunately, the results for both the pion and the parton-jet nuclear modification factor are insensitive to the jet-path dependence of the models considered.

# Sensitivity of Pion versus Parton-Jet Nuclear Modification Factors to the Path-Length Dependence of Jet-Energy Loss at RHIC and LHC

## Key claim

Both a linear pQCD model and a highly non-linear hybrid-AdS holographic model of jet-energy loss require a reduction of the jet-medium coupling from RHIC to LHC to account for the measured pion nuclear modification factor, but the parton-jet nuclear modification factor (a lower bound for the LO jet nuclear modification factor) can be characterized without a reduced coupling at LHC energies. However, the results for both observables are insensitive to the path-length dependence of the models considered, so the path-length exponent cannot be constrained narrower than z = [0,2].

## Method

The authors compared predictions from a radiative pQCD energy-loss model (with parameters a=0, z=1, c=3, q=0) and a hybrid strong/weak AdS string energy-loss model featuring a Bragg peak, both integrated into a common framework using (2+1)d viscous hydrodynamic bulk evolution from VISH2+1 as the background medium. They computed the pion nuclear modification factor using KKP fragmentation functions and an idealized LO parton-jet nuclear modification factor from weighted quark and gluon contributions with pQCD cross-sections from WHDG, at both RHIC (200 GeV) and LHC (2.76 TeV) energies.

## Key numbers

- For pQCD at LHC, a jet-medium coupling of κ = 1.16 describes the measured Jet R_AA within error bars; the same coupling at RHIC produces a Jet R_AA that lies on top of the measured pion nuclear modification factor.
- For pQCD at RHIC, a larger coupling of κ = 2.13 describes the pion nuclear modification factor but underpredicts it at LHC.
- For Hybrid AdS at LHC, a jet-medium coupling of κ = 0.059 describes the measured Jet R_AA within error bars.
- For Hybrid AdS at RHIC, a larger coupling of κ = 0.075 describes the pion nuclear modification factor.
- The path-length dependence for jet-energy loss cannot be constrained narrower than z = [0,2], confirming the earlier result of Betz (2014).

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of Betz (2014) on constraining the path-length dependence of jet-energy loss, and directly compares with the hybrid strong/weak approach of Casalderrey-Solana (2014) which uses falling strings with a Bragg peak. The results contradict the suggestion that the hybrid AdS approach favors a conformal (same coupling at RHIC and LHC) prescription; instead, both pQCD and non-conformal AdS/CFT approaches can describe the data. The study enables future work to focus on combining leading hadron and reconstructed jet nuclear modification factors to disentangle jet-medium coupling from NLO and medium effects, rather than relying on path-length dependence as a discriminator.
