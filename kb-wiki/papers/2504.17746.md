# Flux tube clustering from magnetic coupling of adjacent type-I and -II superconductors in a neutron star: persistent gravitational radiation

> Adjacent type-I and -II proton superconductors in a rotation-powered pulsar are predicted to exist in a metastable state containing macroscopic and quantized flux tubes, respectively. Previous studies show that the type-I and -II regions are coupled magnetically, when macroscopic flux tubes divide dendritically into quantized flux tubes near the type-I-II interface, through a process known as flux branching. The studies assume that the normal-superconducting boundary is sharp, and the quantized flux tubes do not repel mutually. Here the sharp-interface approximation is refined by accounting for magnetic repulsion. It is found that flux tubes in the same flux tree cluster with a minimum-energy separation two to seven times less than that of isolated flux tubes. Neutron vortices pin and cluster about flux trees. We find that the maximum characteristic wave strain $h_0$ of the current quadrupole gravitational radiation emitted by a rectilinear array of clustered vortices exceeds by $(1+N_{\rm v,t})^{1/2}$ the strain $h_0 \sim 10^{-32}(f/30 {\rm Hz})^{5/2} (D/1 {\rm kpc})^{-1}$ emitted by uniformly distributed vortices, where $N_{\rm v,t}$ is the mean number of pinned vortices per flux tree, $f$ is the star's spin frequency, and $D$ is the star's distance from Earth. The factor $(1 + N_{\rm v,t})^{1/2}$ brings $h_0$ close to the sensitivity limit of the current generation of interferometric gravitational wave detectors under certain circumstances, specifically when flux branching forms relatively few (and hence relatively large) flux trees.

## Key claim

By accounting for magnetic repulsion between quantized flux tubes in a neutron star's proton superconductor, the authors find that flux tubes in the same flux tree cluster with a minimum-energy separation two to seven times less than that of isolated flux tubes, and that the resulting gravitational wave strain from pinned neutron vortices may approach the sensitivity limit of current detectors under specific circumstances.

## Method

The authors refined the sharp-interface approximation of previous flux branching studies by incorporating magnetic repulsion between quantized flux tubes in a type-II superconductor region adjacent to a type-I superconductor region in a neutron star. They calculated the equilibrium configuration of flux tubes within a flux tree under the influence of mutual repulsion, then determined the resulting gravitational radiation from neutron vortices pinned to these clustered flux tubes.

## Key numbers

* The maximum characteristic wave strain \(h_0\) of current quadrupole gravitational radiation from a rectilinear array of clustered vortices exceeds the strain from uniformly distributed vortices by a factor of \((1+N_{\rm v,t})^{1/2}\), where \(N_{\rm v,t}\) is the mean number of pinned vortices per flux tree.
* The baseline strain for uniformly distributed vortices is \(h_0 \sim 10^{-32}(f/30 {\rm Hz})^{5/2} (D/1 {\rm kpc})^{-1}\), where \(f\) is the star's spin frequency and \(D\) is the star's distance from Earth.
* Flux tubes in the same flux tree cluster with a minimum-energy separation two to seven times less than that of isolated flux tubes.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of flux branching in neutron star proton superconductors, which assumed a sharp normal-superconducting boundary and neglected mutual repulsion of quantized flux tubes. The refined model predicts enhanced gravitational wave emission that could bring the signal closer to detection by current interferometric gravitational wave detectors, particularly when flux branching produces relatively few and therefore relatively large flux trees.
