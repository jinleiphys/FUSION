# Negative heat capacities and first order phase transitions in nuclei

> Anomalous negative heat capacities have been claimed as indicators of first order phase transitions in finite systems in general, and fornuclear systems in particular. A thermodynamic approach allowing for all Q value terms is used to evaluate heat capacities in finitevan der Waals fluids and finite lattice systems in the coexistence region. Fictitious large effects and negative heat capacities are observed in lattice systems when periodic boundary conditions are introduced. Small anomalous effects are predicted for small drops and for finite lattice systems. A straightforward application of the analysis to nuclei shows that negative heat capacities cannot be observed for A>60.

## Key claim

Negative heat capacities in nuclei are not observable for systems with mass number A > 60, contrary to earlier claims; the effect is only possible for A < 60, and even then only as a slight effect in finite systems.

## Method

The authors apply the Clapeyron equation to finite van der Waals drops and lattice gas models, deriving the molar vaporization enthalpy as a function of drop size including surface, Coulomb, and symmetry energy terms. They then compute the isobaric heat capacity from the derivative of the binding energy per nucleon with respect to mass number, using the liquid drop model to evaluate the relevant terms.

## Key numbers

- For a droplet of A ~ 200 evaporating to A ~ 100, the temperature decreases by approximately 4% (a slight effect).
- Negative heat capacities are possible only for nuclei with A < 60; for A > 60, positive heat capacities are predicted.
- The volume energy coefficient a_v is approximately 15 MeV.
- The surface energy coefficient a_s is comparable in magnitude to a_v (|a_v| ≈ |a_s|).

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic analysis of phase transitions in finite systems by Moretto (1996) and Elliott (2000), and directly contradicts claims of negative heat capacities in nuclear systems reported by D'Agostino (2000, 2002). The authors show that large negative heat capacities previously reported in lattice gas, Ising, and Potts model calculations (Hill 1955, Gross 1997, Chomaz 2000) arise from the artificial use of periodic boundary conditions, which create bubble formation rather than surface evaporation. The analysis enables a clear prediction that experimental searches for negative heat capacities should focus on light nuclei with A < 60, while ruling out such signals in heavier systems.
