# Level-density parameters in superheavy nuclei

> We systematically study the nuclear level densities of superheavy nuclei, including odd systems, using the single-particle energies obtained with the Woods-Saxon potential diagonalization. Minimization over many deformation parameters for the global minima - ground states and the "imaginary water flow" technique on many deformation energy grids for the saddle points, including nonaxial shapes has been applied. The level density parameters are calculated by fitting the obtained results with the standard Fermi gas expression. The total potential energy and shell correction dependencies of the level-density parameter are analyzed and compared at the ground state and saddle point. These parameters are compared with the results of the phenomenological expression. As shown, this expression should be modified for the saddle points, especially for small excitation energy. The ratio of the level-density parameter at the saddle point to that at the ground state is shown to be crucial for the survival probability of the heavy nucleus.

## Key claim

The level-density parameter at the saddle point is systematically larger (by about 10–30%) than at the ground state for superheavy nuclei, and the phenomenological expression of Ignatyuk (1975) must be modified for saddle points by replacing the pure shell correction with the quantity (δE_sh − Δ_N − Δ_Z) to achieve agreement with microscopic BCS calculations, especially at low excitation energy.

## Method

The authors used the macroscopic-microscopic method with a deformed Woods-Saxon potential to calculate single-particle spectra for superheavy nuclei with Z = 112, 114, 116, 117, 118, and 120. Ground states were found by multidimensional minimization over 7 axial deformation parameters (β₂₀ through β₈₀), while first saddle points were located using the Imaginary Water Flow technique on a 5D deformation grid (β₂₀, β₂₂, β₄₀, β₆₀, β₈₀) with 29,250 initial points interpolated to over 50 million points. Level-density parameters were then obtained by fitting the intrinsic level densities from BCS calculations at finite temperature to the back-shifted Fermi gas expression.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperatures for neutrons and protons are up to 0.42 MeV at the ground state and 0.52 MeV at the saddle point; corresponding total excitation energies are U_cr ≈ 5.14 MeV at ground state and U_cr ≈ 11.27 MeV at saddle point.
- For the ground state, the asymptotic level-density parameter follows ᾰ = αA + βA² with α = 0.09 MeV⁻¹ and β = 2.89 × 10⁻⁵ MeV⁻¹; the damping parameter is E_D ≈ 15 MeV on average, with γ₀ = 0.423 MeV⁻¹ in E_D = A¹/³/γ₀.
- For the saddle point, the asymptotic parameters are α = 0.122 MeV⁻¹ and β = −7.3 × 10⁻⁵ MeV⁻¹; damping parameters are close to E_D ≈ 17 MeV for A < 290, with γ₀ = 0.381 MeV⁻¹.
- The ratio a_f/a_n (saddle-point to ground-state level-density parameter) reaches asymptotic values of about 1.1 at high excitation energy, with a maximum of about 1.35 for ²⁸⁴Fl near 20 MeV excitation.

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of level-density parameters in superheavy nuclei beyond earlier work by Bezbakh (2014) and provides microscopic input for the survival probability calculations essential to superheavy element synthesis. The results contradict the topographic theorem of Swiatecki, which states that shell effects disappear at the saddle point; the authors show that shell corrections at the saddle point, though small, are not negligible for superheavy nuclei. The calculated fission probabilities for ²³⁶U and ²⁴⁰Pu agree with experimental data from Cheifetz (1981), validating the method for predictions in the superheavy region. The energy-dependent ratio a_f/a_n enables more reliable estimates of neutron emission versus fission competition in hot fusion reactions, which is critical for planning experiments at the JINR Superheavy Factory.
