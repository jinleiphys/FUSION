# Decoding Neutron Star Observations: Revealing Composition through Bayesian Neural Networks

> We exploit the great potential offered by Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) to directly decipher the internal composition of neutron stars (NSs) based on their macroscopic properties. By analyzing a set of simulated observations, namely NS radius and tidal deformability, we leverage BNNs as effective tools for inferring the proton fraction and sound speed within NS interiors. To achieve this, several BNNs models were developed upon a dataset of $\sim$ 25K nuclear EoS within a relativistic mean-field framework, obtained through Bayesian inference that adheres to minimal low-density constraints. Unlike conventional neural networks, BNNs possess an exceptional quality: they provide a prediction uncertainty measure. To simulate the inherent imperfections present in real-world observations, we have generated four distinct training and testing datasets that replicate specific observational uncertainties. Our initial results demonstrate that BNNs successfully recover the composition with reasonable levels of uncertainty. Furthermore, using mock data prepared with the DD2, a different class of relativistic mean-field model utilized during training, the BNN model effectively retrieves the proton fraction and speed of sound for neutron star matter.

## Key claim

Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) can infer the internal composition of neutron stars (proton fraction and speed of sound) from simulated observations of radius and tidal deformability, but only within the specific family of relativistic mean-field models used to generate the training dataset; the method successfully recovers composition for a different RMF model (DD2) not seen during training, but the generalization to truly unknown physics remains untested.

## Method

The authors generated a dataset of approximately 25,000 nuclear equations of state within a relativistic mean-field framework using Bayesian inference with constraints from nuclear saturation properties, chiral effective field theory calculations of pure neutron matter pressure, and the requirement that maximum neutron star mass exceeds 2.0 solar masses. They trained Bayesian Neural Networks using variational inference to map simulated neutron star observations (mass-radius pairs and tidal deformabilities) to the proton fraction and speed of sound at 15 fixed baryonic densities between 0.15 fm^-3 and 1.0 fm^-3. Four synthetic datasets were created with different levels of observational uncertainty (sigma_R from 0.15 km to 0.3 km, sigma_M from 0.05 to 0.1 solar masses) to test the impact of measurement precision on prediction uncertainty.

## Key numbers

* Dataset contains 25,287 equations of state generated from Bayesian posterior sampling of 7 RMF parameters (g_sigma, g_omega, g_rho, B, C, xi, Lambda_omega) with uniform priors
* Nuclear saturation constraints used: rho_0 = 0.153 +/- 0.005 fm^-3, epsilon_0 = -16.1 +/- 0.2 MeV, K_0 = 230 +/- 40 MeV, J_sym,0 = 32.5 +/- 1.8 MeV
* Pure neutron matter pressure constraints from N^3LO chiral EFT at densities 0.08, 0.12, and 0.16 fm^-3 with 2x N^3LO uncertainty
* Maximum neutron star mass constraint: M_max > 2.0 solar masses
* Training/test split: 80%/20% (22,758 EoS for training, 2,529 for testing)
* Output space: 15 fixed baryonic density points equally spaced from 0.15 fm^-3 to 1.0 fm^-3
* Input space: 10-dimensional (5 mass-radius pairs) or 20-dimensional (5 mass-radius pairs plus 5 mass-tidal deformability pairs)
* Training uses n_s = 60 mock observations per EoS; testing uses n_s = 1 mock observation per EoS
* Dataset 1: sigma_M = 0.05 M_sun, sigma_R = 0.15 km; Dataset 2: sigma_M = 0.1 M_sun, sigma_R = 0.3 km
* Dataset 3: sigma_Lambda = 0.5 x hat_sigma(M_j); Dataset 4: sigma_Lambda = 2 x hat_sigma(M_j)

## Context

This work extends the approach of Fujimoto et al. (2018, 2020, 2021) who used neural networks to map neutron star mass-radius observations to the speed of sound, but adds Bayesian uncertainty quantification that previous neural network studies lacked. It contrasts with the parametric EoS inference methods (e.g., polytropic parametrizations) by using a specific family of microscopic nuclear models that can track individual degrees of freedom like proton fraction, which generic EoS parametrizations cannot do. The study addresses the limitation noted by Tovar (2021), Imam (2021), and Mondal (2021) that nuclear matter properties cannot be recovered from the beta-equilibrium EoS alone without composition information, and demonstrates that BNNs can directly infer composition from macroscopic observables. Future work could apply this framework to real observational data from NICER, LIGO/Virgo, and upcoming missions like eXTP and STROBE-X.
