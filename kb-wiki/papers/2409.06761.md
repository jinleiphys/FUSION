# Alpha decay law of excited nuclei and its role in stellar decay rates

> $α$ decay is one of the prominent decay modes in the nucleosynthesis of heavy and super-heavy elements synthesized at temperatures of the order of Giga Kelvin. To facilitate the investigation of the role played by the $α$ decay half-lives of thermally excited nuclei in nucleosynthesis calculations, an empirical formula based on a model for the $α$ decay of nuclei in their ground and excited states to daughter nuclei in their ground or excited states is presented. Constants appearing in the analytical expression for the $α$ decay half-life obtained within the model are treated as adjustable parameters and fitted to experimental data on 342 $α$ decays in the range of 82 $\le Z_p \le$ 94, to obtain an excitation energy-dependent decay law. Under the assumption that thermal equilibrium has been reached between nuclear states, temperature ($T$)-dependent half-lives, $t_{1/2}(T)$, for several of the experimentally studied $α$ emitters with 65 $\le Z_p \le$ 94 are presented using available data on the half-lives of excited nuclei. Though the general trend is a decrease in $t_{1/2}(T)$ at elevated temperatures, exceptional cases with increased half-lives are found in the case of some isomeric states. A list of such isomers provided in this work motivates future work involving considerations of their thermal equilibration and role in shaping kilonova light curves.

## Key claim

The authors present an excitation-energy-dependent empirical formula for alpha decay half-lives of excited nuclei, fitted to 342 decays in the range 82 ≤ Z_p ≤ 94, which can be used to compute temperature-dependent stellar decay rates. The abstract's claim of a "decrease in t_{1/2}(T) at elevated temperatures" is qualified by the finding that some isomeric states produce increased half-lives at certain temperatures.

## Method

The authors derive an analytical expression for the alpha decay half-life based on a tunneling model through a Coulomb barrier, treating the nuclear potential as a rectangular well. The constants in the expression are treated as adjustable parameters and fitted via ordinary least squares linear regression to experimental data from the National Nuclear Data Center for 342 alpha decays where the parent, daughter, or both are in excited states. The fit uses five predictors: χ', χ'ΔE*/Q_α, ρ', ℓ(ℓ+1)/ρ', and a blocking term δ_oe for odd-even effects, with the constraint |χ'ΔE*/Q_α| ≤ 15.2 MeV^{-1/2}. Temperature-dependent half-lives are then computed using the stellar rate formula under the assumption of thermal equilibrium between nuclear states.

## Key numbers

- The fit uses 342 alpha decays in the parent atomic number range 82 ≤ Z_p ≤ 94
- The optimal constraint value for |χ'ΔE*/Q_α| is approximately 15.2 MeV^{-1/2}
- The fitted model achieves R² = 0.980 and MSE = 1.04
- The coefficient for χ' is β₁ = 7.4737 ± 0.095 MeV^{1/2}
- The coefficient for χ'ΔE*/Q_α is β₂ = -1.1852 ± 0.058 MeV^{1/2}
- The coefficient for ρ' is β₃ = -0.6099 ± 0.094
- The coefficient for ℓ(ℓ+1)/ρ' is β₄ = 0.6414 ± 0.056
- The coefficient for δ_oe is β₅ = 0.6050 ± 0.068
- The constant term is β₀ = 7.1991 ± 0.055
- The isomeric state 9⁻ of ²¹⁰Bi has a total half-life of 3.06 × 10⁶ years, compared to 5.012 days for the 1⁻ ground state
- ²⁴²Am has an isomeric 5⁻ state with half-life 141 years (ground state 16.02 h) at excitation energy 0.0486 MeV, and a state (2⁺, 3⁻) at 2.2 MeV with half-life 13.9 ms

## Context

This work extends the Geiger-Nuttall law (Geiger and Nuttall, 1911) and the Viola-Seaborg formula (Viola and Seaborg, 1966) to include excitation energy dependence, building on the authors' previous work on cluster radioactivity of excited nuclei (Rojas and Kelkar, 2022). It generalizes the approach of Delion (2015), who gave a generalized VS formula depending on daughter excitation energy, and extends the temperature-dependent half-life calculations of Jhoan (2023) from 5 nuclei to a larger set of alpha emitters with 65 ≤ Z_p ≤ 94. The work enables more accurate nucleosynthesis calculations for heavy and super-heavy elements at Giga-Kelvin temperatures and motivates further study of isomeric states' role in shaping kilonova light curves.
