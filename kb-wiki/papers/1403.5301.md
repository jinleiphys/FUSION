# Revealing the high-density equation of state through binary neutron star mergers

> We present a novel method for revealing the equation of state of high-density neutron star matter through gravitational waves emitted during the postmerger phase of a binary neutron star system. The method relies on a small number of detections of the peak frequency in the postmerger phase for binaries of different (relatively low) masses, in the most likely range of expected detections. From such observations, one can construct the derivative of the peak frequency versus the binary mass, in this mass range. Through a detailed study of binary neutron star mergers for a large sample of equations of state, we show that one can extrapolate the above information to the highest possible mass (the threshold mass for black hole formation in a binary neutron star merger). In turn, this allows for an empirical determination of the maximum mass of cold, nonrotating neutron stars to within 0.1 M_sun, while the corresponding radius is determined to within a few percent. Combining this with the determination of the radius of cold, nonrotating neutron stars of 1.6 M_sun (to within a few percent, as was demonstrated in Bauswein et al., PRD, 86, 063001, 2012), allows for a clear distinction of a particular candidate equation of state among a large set of other candidates. Our method is particularly appealing because it reveals simultaneously the moderate and very high-density parts of the equation of state, enabling the distinction of mass-radius relations even if they are similar at typical neutron star masses. Furthermore, our method also allows to deduce the maximum central energy density and maximum central rest-mass density of cold, nonrotating neutron stars with an accuracy of a few per cent.

## Key claim

The paper claims that by measuring the dominant gravitational-wave peak frequency from at least two binary neutron star mergers with slightly different total masses around 2.7 solar masses, one can extrapolate to determine the threshold mass for black hole formation and thereby infer the maximum mass of cold, nonrotating neutron stars to within 0.1 solar masses. This claim is hedged by the fact that the extrapolation procedure relies on an empirically determined "stability line" and a shift parameter that depends on the measured slope and frequency, with the method described as a "proof of principle" that could be refined with more equations of state.

## Method

The authors performed relativistic smoothed particle hydrodynamics simulations of binary neutron star mergers for a sample of 14 microphysical, temperature-dependent equations of state, systematically varying the total binary mass for equal-mass systems. For each simulation, they extracted the dominant postmerger gravitational-wave peak frequency using a fixed 10 ms analysis window after merger with a Hann window applied. They then constructed an empirical "stability line" from the threshold masses and corresponding peak frequencies across all equations of state, and developed a linear extrapolation procedure with a frequency-dependent shift to estimate the threshold mass from measurements at 2.7 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- The threshold mass for black hole collapse can be estimated with an accuracy of 2% or better from extrapolations of gravitational-wave detections of lower-mass binaries with total mass around 2.7 solar masses.
- The maximum mass of cold, nonrotating neutron stars is determined to within 0.1 solar masses using the extrapolation method combined with the empirical relation M_thres = (-3.606 * G M_max / (c^2 R_1.6) + 2.380) M_max - 0.05 M_sun.
- The radius of the maximum-mass configuration is determined with an accuracy of typically 4% or better, with only the NL3 equation of state deviating by 5%.
- The dominant postmerger gravitational-wave peak frequency for binaries with total mass 2.7 solar masses ranges between 2 and 3.5 kHz across the equations of state studied.
- The stability line is fit by two linear segments: f_peak^thres = -0.328 * M_thres + 4.093 for M_thres less than 3.32 solar masses, and f_peak^thres = -1.546 * M_thres + 8.140 for M_thres greater than 3.32 solar masses, with frequencies in kHz and masses in solar masses.
- The radius of a 1.6 solar mass neutron star is determined from the peak frequency at 2.7 solar masses via the quadratic fit R_1.6 = 1.099 * f_peak^2 - 8.574 * f_peak + 28.07, with radii in km and frequencies in kHz.

## Context

This work extends the method of Bauswein et al. (2012) which showed that the dominant postmerger gravitational-wave frequency for 2.7 solar mass binaries determines the radius of a 1.6 solar mass neutron star to within a few percent. It also builds on Bauswein et al. (2013) which demonstrated that the threshold binary mass for black hole collapse constrains the maximum mass of cold, nonrotating neutron stars. The new method enables simultaneous determination of both the moderate-density equation of state (via the 1.6 solar mass radius) and the high-density equation of state (via the maximum mass and corresponding radius), allowing distinction between candidate equations of state even when their mass-radius relations are similar at typical neutron star masses. Future gravitational-wave detections by Advanced LIGO, Advanced Virgo, and the Einstein Telescope could apply this method to empirically determine the neutron star maximum mass and radius.
