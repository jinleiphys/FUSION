# Double DVCS as a window to the complete mapping of GPDs

> Double deeply virtual Compton scattering (DDVCS) is the process where an electron scatters off a nucleon and produces a lepton pair. The main advantage of this process in contrast with deeply virtual and timelike Compton scatterings (DVCS and TCS) is the possibility of directly measuring GPDs for $x\neq\pmξ$ at leading order in $α_s$ (LO). We present a new calculation of the DDVCS amplitude based on spinor techniques which produce expressions for amplitudes that are perfectly suited for their implementation in numerical simulations. Elements of impact studies, including predictions for experiments at JLab12, JLab20+ and the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), are studied by means of the PARTONS software and the EpIC Monte Carlo event generator.

## Key claim

Double deeply virtual Compton scattering (DDVCS) allows direct measurement of generalized parton distributions (GPDs) for \(x \neq \pm \xi\) at leading order in \(\alpha_s\), unlike deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS) or timelike Compton scattering (TCS) which restrict LO studies to the line \(x = \xi\). The authors present a new calculation of the DDVCS amplitude using Kleiss-Stirling spinor techniques, producing expressions suited for numerical simulation, and show that asymmetries at LO are large enough for DDVCS to be a relevant part of GPD programs at current and future facilities.

## Method

The authors computed the DDVCS amplitude using Kleiss-Stirling spinor techniques, which express amplitudes in terms of spinor products \(s(a,b)\) and \(t(a,b)\) for light-like vectors, avoiding the usual trace-over-Dirac-matrices approach. They implemented the resulting cross-section formulae in the PARTONS software framework and the EpIC Monte Carlo event generator, then performed impact studies for JLab12, JLab20+, and the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) using the GK, VGG, and MMS GPD models. The Monte Carlo study generated 10000 events per experimental configuration and compared the event distribution in the inelasticity variable \(y\) against direct seven-fold integration of the cross-section.

## Key numbers

- Total cross-section for JLab12 (electron 10.6 GeV, proton at rest): \(\sigma = 0.14\) pb for \(y \in (0,1)\), requiring 70 fb\(^{-1}\) integrated luminosity for 10000 events.
- Total cross-section for JLab20+ (electron 22 GeV, proton at rest): \(\sigma = 0.46\) pb for \(y \in (0,1)\), requiring 22 fb\(^{-1}\) for 10000 events.
- Total cross-section for EIC 5\(\times\)41 (electron 5 GeV, proton 41 GeV): \(\sigma = 3.9\) pb for \(y \in (0,1)\), requiring 2.6 fb\(^{-1}\) for 10000 events; fraction of events retained after \(y > 0.05\) cut is 0.73.
- Total cross-section for EIC 10\(\times\)100 (electron 10 GeV, proton 100 GeV): \(\sigma = 4.7\) pb for \(y \in (0,1)\), requiring 2.1 fb\(^{-1}\) for 10000 events; fraction of events retained after \(y > 0.05\) cut is 0.32.
- Asymmetries \(A_{LU}\) at LO are of order 15-20% for JLab and 3-7% for EIC.
- Kinematic cuts used: \(Q^2 \in (0.15, 5)\) GeV\(^2\), \(Q^{\prime 2} \in (2.25, 9)\) GeV\(^2\), \(|t| \in (0.1, 0.8)\) GeV\(^2\) for JLab and \((0.05, 1)\) GeV\(^2\) for EIC.

## Context

This work extends earlier detailed phenomenological studies of DDVCS by Belitsky et al. (2003) and provides new amplitude expressions using Kleiss-Stirling techniques, which are more suitable for numerical implementation than previous trace-based methods. The results enable experimental analyses at JLab12, JLab20+, and the EIC, as the implementation in PARTONS and EpIC makes the calculations directly applicable for experimental studies. The authors note that inclusion of NLO corrections, as studied by Pire et al. (2011), should not change the conclusion that DDVCS asymmetries are large enough for GPD programs.
