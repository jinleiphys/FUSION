# Entrance channel dependence and isospin dependence of preequilibrium nucleon emission in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions

> Using isospin dependent quantum molecular dynamical model, the studies of the isospin effect on preequilibrium nucleon emission in heavy ion collisions under different entrance channel conditions show that the ratio of preequilibrium neutron number to proton number depends strongly on symmetry potential, beam energy, and the ratio of neutron to proton of the colliding system, but weakly on isospin dependent in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections, impact parameter, Pauli potential, and momentum dependent interaction in the energy region from 45MeV/u up to 150 MeV/u where the dynamics is dominated by nucleon-nucleon collisions. In addition, the ratio of preequilibrium neutron number to proton number for a neutron-rich colliding system is larger than the initial value of the ratio of the colliding system, but the ratio for a neutron-deficient system is less than the initial value.

# Entrance channel dependence and isospin dependence of preequilibrium nucleon emission in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
The ratio of preequilibrium neutron number to proton number depends strongly on symmetry potential, beam energy, and the initial neutron-to-proton ratio of the colliding system, but only weakly on isospin-dependent in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections, impact parameter, Pauli potential, and momentum dependent interaction in the energy region from 45 MeV/u to 150 MeV/u where the dynamics is dominated by nucleon-nucleon collisions. The abstract's claim that the ratio depends "weakly" on these factors is qualified by the finding that the effects on neutron and proton emission are nearly equal, so the ratio itself remains largely unchanged even when individual emission rates vary.

## Method
The authors used an isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model incorporating Skyrme, Coulomb, Yukawa, symmetry, Pauli, and momentum-dependent interactions to simulate heavy ion collisions. They studied three systems, two neutron-rich (76Zn+76Zn with N/Z=1.53 and 80Zn+80Zn with N/Z=1.67) and one neutron-deficient (76Kr+76Kr with N/Z=1.11), at beam energies from 45 MeV/u to 150 MeV/u and impact parameters of 1.0 fm and 5.0 fm. Preequilibrium nucleon emission was defined as all neutrons and protons emitted before thermal equilibrium was reached, identified by nucleons not correlated with others within a spatial distance of 3 fm and a momentum distance of 300 MeV/c.

## Key numbers
- The ratio of preequilibrium neutrons to protons for 80Zn+80Zn (initial N/Z=1.67) at E=100 MeV/u and b=1.0 fm was 1.83, larger than the initial value.
- The ratio for 76Zn+76Zn (initial N/Z=1.53) at E=100 MeV/u and b=1.0 fm was 1.62, larger than the initial value.
- The ratio for 76Kr+76Kr (initial N/Z=1.11) at E=100 MeV/u and b=1.0 fm was 1.06, less than the initial value of 1.11.
- The study covered beam energies from 45 MeV/u up to 150 MeV/u.
- The symmetry potential strength C was set to either 0 or 32 MeV.
- The in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section used a density dependence parameter alpha = -0.2.
- The neutron-proton cross section was about three times larger than the neutron-neutron or proton-proton cross section below about 500 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the suggestion by Bao-An Li et al. (using BUU calculations) that the ratio of preequilibrium neutrons to protons can be used as a probe to extract information on the symmetry potential, extending the applicable beam energy range from below 100 MeV/u up to 150 MeV/u. The study systematically investigates entrance channel dependence using IQMD, complementing earlier work by R. Pak and Bao-An Li et al. on extracting in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section information from isospin dependences of collective flow and balance energy, and the authors' own prior work on isospin effects of multifragmentation. The results enable using preequilibrium neutron-proton ratios as a probe for symmetry potential across a broader energy range in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions.
