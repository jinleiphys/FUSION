# The origin of elements: the need for UV spectra

> Thanks to the long-term collaborations between nuclear and astrophysics, we have good understanding on stellar nucleosynthesis, except for the elements around Ti and some neutron-capture elements. From the comparison between observations and Galactic chemical evolution models, it is necessary to have the rapid neutron-capture process associated with core-collapse supernovae, although the explosion mechanism is unknown. The impact of rotating massive stars is also shown in this paper. Many of the key elements can be exclusively obtained in the UV, and therefore without UV spectra it would not be possible to fully understand the origin of elements in the universe.

## Key claim

While long-term nuclear-astrophysics collaborations have produced a good understanding of stellar nucleosynthesis, the origin of elements around titanium and several neutron-capture elements remains uncertain, and many key elements can only be measured with UV spectra, meaning that without such observations a full understanding of element origins is impossible.

## Method

The paper compares Galactic chemical evolution (GCE) models, using the best available nucleosynthesis yields from core-collapse supernovae, hypernovae, neutron-star mergers, and magneto-rotational supernovae, against observational stellar abundance data for elements including phosphorus, copper, zinc, and neutron-capture elements. The GCE models adopt specific yield sets (e.g., 25 solar mass 3D yields from Nishimura et al. 2015 and 1.3+1.3 solar mass 3D/GR yields from Wanajo et al. 2014) and include contributions from asymptotic giant branch stars, electron-capture supernovae, and rotating massive stars, with the relative event rates summarized in a table.

## Key numbers

- Phosphorus: the [P/Fe] ratio increases by 0.24 dex from [Fe/H] = -3 to -1 in the model, then decreases due to delayed Type Ia supernova enrichment.
- Copper: Cu II lines at 2126 Angstroms taken by HST STIS confirm a decreasing [Cu/Fe] trend toward low metallicity, consistent with GCE models.
- Zinc: Zn II lines at 2139 Angstroms confirm [Zn/Fe] ~ 0 over a wide metallicity range, reproduced only with hypernovae included.
- Neutron-capture elements: the predicted gold abundance is more than ten times lower than observed, even with both magneto-rotational supernovae and neutron-star mergers included.
- Event rates: in the fiducial model, 50% of stars above 20 solar masses explode as hypernovae, and 1.5% of stars above 25 solar masses produce r-process elements, meaning magneto-rotational supernovae/hypernovae occur in about 1 out of ~1000 supernovae.

## Context

This work extends the GCE models of Kobayashi et al. (2020) by incorporating new UV observations of phosphorus, copper, and zinc, and by testing the contributions of rotating massive stars and various r-process sites. The paper contradicts the need for a separate light element primary process from rotating massive stars, finding that electron-capture supernovae from a narrow mass range (~0.15-0.2 solar masses) can produce enough first-peak neutron-capture elements when combined with AGB stars. The results enable future UV spectroscopy (e.g., with HST, CUBES on VLT) to constrain the explosion mechanism of core-collapse supernovae, the astronomical sites of the r-process, and the effects of stellar rotation, magnetic fields, and binary evolution.
