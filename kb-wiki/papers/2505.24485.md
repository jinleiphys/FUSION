# Neutrino emission from neutron star matter

> The temperature of a newly formed neutron star is believed to be as high as $10^{11}$~K, corresponding to a thermal energy of about $10$ MeV. After a time $t \sim 50 \ {\rm s}$, the neutrino mean free path in nuclear matter exceeds the typical star radius, R~$\sim$~10 Km, and neutrino emission becomes the dominant mechanism of energy loss, eventually bringing the temperature down to $\sim10^{8}$~K. Neutrinos also play a critical role in determining the composition of matter in the star interior, consisting primarily of a charge-neutral mixture of neutrons, protons and leptons in $β$-equilibrium. This article provides an introduction to the weak interactions of nucleons in nuclear matter, as well as a concise review of the neutrino emission reactions taking place in the neutron star core. The approximations involved in the standard theoretical treatment of thermal and dynamical effects are analysed in the light of the recent progress of the field, and the prospects for future developments are outlined.

## Key claim

This article provides an introductory review of neutrino emission from neutron star matter, focusing on the approximations used in standard theoretical treatments of thermal and dynamical effects. The authors argue that while the fundamental theories of strong and weak interactions predict exotic forms of matter at high density, Bayesian inference analyses of recent astronomical data strongly support the validity of a description in terms of nucleons, indicating that a phase transition is unlikely even in the most massive neutron star observed.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for weak interactions in nuclear matter, starting from the Fermi theory of neutron β-decay and extending to the many-body treatment of nucleons in dense matter. It describes the mean-field approximation (MFA) and the correlated basis function (CBF) formalism, which accounts for short-range correlations (SRC) through a density-dependent effective interaction derived from realistic nuclear Hamiltonians including two-nucleon (NN) and three-nucleon (NNN) potentials. The authors then apply this formalism to compute the transition amplitudes for direct Urca (dUrca) and modified Urca processes in charge-neutral, β-stable npe matter.

## Key numbers

- The temperature of a newly formed neutron star is as high as 10^11 K, corresponding to a thermal energy of about 10 MeV; after t ~ 50 s, the neutrino mean free path exceeds the typical star radius R ~ 10 km, and neutrino emission becomes the dominant energy loss mechanism, eventually bringing the temperature down to ~10^8 K.
- The Fermi coupling constant is G_F = 1.166 × 10^{-5} GeV^{-2} and the Cabibbo mixing angle is θ_C = 13.02°.
- The threshold proton fraction for activation of the direct Urca process is x_thr = 1/9, corresponding to a threshold baryon density ρ_thr ≈ 0.4 fm^{-3}, which exceeds nuclear saturation density (ρ_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}) by a factor of about 2.5.
- The effective interaction employed in the CBF formalism is independent of temperature by construction, and the temperature dependence of correlation functions remains negligibly small for temperatures as high as ~20 MeV.
- The squared transition matrix element of the effective weak current in cold symmetric nuclear matter at equilibrium density, for fixed momentum transfer q = 0.3 fm^{-1}, shows significant departure from the independent-particle model predictions.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Friman and Maxwell (1979) on modified Urca processes, which employed low-temperature approximations and a one-pion-exchange potential model, and the subsequent generalizations by Yakovlev and collaborators. It builds upon the improved description of nuclear dynamics using realistic Hamiltonians and the G-matrix formalism by Shternin and collaborators (2018), and the recent efforts by Suleiman (2023), Alford (2024), and Sedrakian (2024) to go beyond the Friman-Maxwell approximations with detailed thermal effects and realistic nuclear dynamics. The paper enables future unified treatments of neutrino emission that consistently account for temperature and nuclear dynamics in all relevant nuclear matter properties, from the equation of state to nucleon effective masses and chemical potentials.
