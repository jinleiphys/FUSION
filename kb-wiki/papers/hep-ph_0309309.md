# Why is CPT fundamental?

> G. Lüders and W. Pauli proved the $\mathcal{CPT}$ theorem based on Lagrangian quantum field theory almost half a century ago. R. Jost gave a more general proof based on ``axiomatic'' field theory nearly as long ago. The axiomatic point of view has two advantages over the Lagrangian one. First, the axiomatic point of view makes clear why $\mathcal{CPT}$ is fundamental--because it is intimately related to Lorentz invariance. Secondly, the axiomatic proof gives a simple way to calculate the $\mathcal{CPT}$ transform of any relativistic field without calculating $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{T}$ separately and then multiplying them. The purpose of this pedagogical paper is to ``deaxiomatize'' the $\mathcal{CPT}$ theorem by explaining it in a few simple steps. We use theorems of distribution theory and of several complex variables without proof to make the exposition elementary.

## Key claim

CPT symmetry is fundamental because it is intimately related to Lorentz invariance, specifically, because spacetime inversion (x → -x) is connected to the identity in the complex Lorentz group, even though it is not connected in the real Lorentz group. The proof requires assuming weak local commutativity at Jost points (a condition weaker than full spin-statistics), not just Lorentz covariance alone.

## Method

The paper presents Jost's axiomatic CPT theorem in a pedagogical, "deaxiomatized" form using Wightman functions (vacuum expectation values of products of fields). It shows that these functions can be analytically continued from the real Lorentz group to the complex Lorentz group via the Bargmann-Hall-Wightman theorem, enlarging the domain of analyticity to include real "Jost points." By combining spacetime inversion in the complex Lorentz group with weak local commutativity at Jost points, the general CPT transformation law for any relativistic field is derived without separately computing C, P, and T.

## Key numbers

- The CPT transformation for a field φ^(k,l)(x) (with k undotted and l dotted spinor indices) is: Θ φ^(k,l)(x) Θ† = (-1)^l (±i)^f φ^(k,l)†(-x), where f = 0 for Bose fields (k+l even) and f = 1 for Fermi fields (k+l odd).
- For the Dirac spin field: the φ^(1,0) part maps to i φ^(1,0) and the φ^(0,1) part maps to -i φ^(0,1).
- For vector and axial vector fields (both φ^(1,1)): both get phase (-1) under Θ.
- For scalar and pseudoscalar fields (both φ^(0,0)): both get phase 1 under Θ.
- For the antisymmetric rank-two tensor T^(μν) (φ^(2,0) + φ^(0,2)): gets phase 1.
- For the traceless symmetric rank-two tensor (φ^(2,2)): gets phase 1.
- Θ² commutes with Bose fields and anticommutes with Fermi fields, with phase (-1)^f.

## Context

This work extends the original Lagrangian CPT proof by Lüders and Pauli (1950s) and the more general axiomatic proof by Jost (1950s-1960s). It contrasts with standard textbook treatments that compute C, P, and T separately and then multiply them, which obscures why CPT is fundamental while the individual symmetries are not. The paper enables a simple, direct calculation of the CPT transform for any relativistic field. It notes that Lorentz covariance alone is insufficient for CPT: free or generalized free fields with different particle and antiparticle masses can violate CPT, as shown by Greenberg (2002), because weak local commutativity fails at Jost points for such fields.
