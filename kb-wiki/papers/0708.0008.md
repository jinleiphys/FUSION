# Ovrview: The Shape of Hadrons

> In this article we address the physical basis of the deviation of hadron shapes from spherical symmetry (non-spherical amplitudes) with focus on the nucleon and $Δ$. An overview of both the experimental methods and results and the current theoretical understanding of the issue is presented. At the present time the most quantitative method is the $γ^{*} p \to Δ$ reaction for which significant non-spherical electric (E2) and Coulomb quadrupole (C2) amplitudes have been observed with good precision as a function of Q^{2} from the photon point through 6 GeV^{2}. Quark model calculations for these quadrupole amplitudes are at least an order of magnitude too small and even have the wrong sign. Lattice QCD, chiral effective field theory, and dynamic model calculations which include the effects of the pion-cloud are in approximate agreement with experiment. This is expected due to the spontaneous breaking of chiral symmetry in QCD and the resulting, long range (low Q^{2}) effects of the pion-cloud. Other observables such as nucleon form factors and virtual Compton scattering experiments indicate that the pion-cloud is playing a significant role in nucleon structure. Semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering experiments with transverse polarized beam and target also show the effect of non-zero quark angular momentum.

## Key claim

The paper argues that deviations of hadron shapes from spherical symmetry are primarily driven by the pion cloud, which arises from the spontaneous breaking of chiral symmetry in QCD, rather than by the color hyperfine interaction between quarks as originally proposed. The authors hedge that while quark model calculations for quadrupole amplitudes in the γ*p → Δ reaction are at least an order of magnitude too small and have the wrong sign, lattice QCD, chiral effective field theory, and dynamic model calculations that include pion-cloud effects are in approximate agreement with experiment.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental measurements of the γ*p → Δ reaction using real and virtual photons, where the dominant magnetic dipole (M1), electric quadrupole (E2), and Coulomb quadrupole (C2) multipoles are extracted from cross section data as a function of Q² from the photon point through 6 GeV². The authors compare these experimental results to predictions from non-relativistic and relativized quark models, chiral effective field theory calculations using both the epsilon expansion and delta expansion schemes, lattice QCD simulations, and dynamic models that incorporate pion-cloud effects. They also examine related observables including nucleon form factors, virtual Compton scattering, and semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering with transverse polarization.

## Key numbers

- The experimental E2/M1 ratio (EMR) at the photon point is approximately -2.5%, compared to quark model predictions of approximately -0.4% (non-relativistic) and approximately -0.2% (relativized)
- The static quadrupole moment of the Δ⁺ is estimated as Q_Δ⁺ = -(0.048 ± 0.002) fm² using large N_c relations
- The transition quadrupole moment Q_γp→Δ⁺ is related to the neutron charge radius as Q_γp→Δ = r_n²/√2 = -(0.084 ± 0.002) fm², with the experimental value being -(0.085 ± 0.003) fm²
- Lattice QCD calculations were performed for pion masses in the range 410-560 MeV (quenched) and 380-690 MeV (unquenched)
- The M1 transition amplitude is underestimated by approximately 30% in quark models
- The neutron RMS charge radius r_n² = -(0.119 ± 0.003) fm²

## Context

This review extends the line of work initiated by Glashow (1979) on non-central tensor interactions between quarks and the subsequent work of Isgur, Karl, and Koniuk (1980s) on D waves in the nucleon as a test of color magnetism. The paper contrasts quark model calculations (Capstick and Karl, 1990s) with more recent chiral effective field theory calculations (Pascalutsa and Vanderhaeghen, Gail and Hemmert) and lattice QCD simulations (Alexandrou, 2000s) that include pion-cloud effects. The work enables future quantitative comparisons between QCD predictions and experimental data through next-order chiral calculations and lattice simulations with lower pion masses (as low as 250 MeV becoming available).
