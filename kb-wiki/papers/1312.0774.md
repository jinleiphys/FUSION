# A study of self-consistent Hartree-Fock plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer calculations with finite-range interactions

> In this work we test the validity of a Hartree-Fock plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer model in which a finite-range interaction is used in the two steps of the calculation by comparing the results obtained to those found in a fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations using the same interaction.Specifically, we consider the Gogny-type D1S and D1M forces. We study a wide range of spherical nuclei, far from the stability line, in various regions of the nuclear chart, from oxygen to tin isotopes. We calculate various quantities related to the ground state properties of these nuclei, such as binding energies, radii, charge and density distributions and elastic electron scattering cross sections. The pairing effects are studied by direct comparison with the Hartree-Fock results. Despite of its relative simplicity, in most of the cases, our model provides results very close to those of the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations, and it reproduces rather well the empirical evidences of pairing effects in the nuclei investigated.

## Key claim

The authors test whether a computationally simpler Hartree-Fock plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (HF+BCS) model using a finite-range interaction in both steps can reproduce results from a fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculation with the same interaction, finding that the HF+BCS approach generally yields very similar results but systematically generates less pairing correlation than HFB, particularly when single-particle levels are well separated in energy.

## Method

The authors performed HF+BCS calculations for spherical even-even nuclei across oxygen, calcium, nickel, zirconium, and tin isotope chains, as well as N=40 and N=50 isotone chains, using the finite-range Gogny D1S and D1M interactions. The HF equations were solved first to obtain single-particle wave functions and energies, then the BCS equations were solved using only the central finite-range part of the same Gogny interaction, with the continuum discretized by a box of radius R_max = 4 r_0 A^(1/3) with r_0 = 1.2 fm and all states below 10 MeV included. Results were compared to HFB calculations using the same interactions and to experimental data for binding energies, radii, charge and density distributions, and elastic electron scattering cross sections.

## Key numbers

- Maximum relative difference between HF+BCS and HFB binding energies is 2% across all studied nuclei
- Maximum relative difference between HF+BCS and HFB proton rms radii is 1.5% (in 18O)
- Maximum relative difference between HF+BCS and HFB neutron rms radii is 1.9% (in 26O)
- Maximum relative difference between HF+BCS and experimental binding energies is about 3%
- Maximum relative difference between HF+BCS and HFB binding energies in open-shell chains is 1.7% (for 74Se)
- Differences between HF+BCS and HF binding energies are below 0.5% for open-shell nuclei
- Pairing effects on rms radii produce relative differences smaller than 0.8% between HF+BCS and HF
- Charge radius relative differences between HF+BCS and experiment are within 2% for all nuclei except 16O (2.2% for D1M, 3.4% for D1S)
- Pairing effects on charge rms radii produce relative differences smaller than 0.3% between HF+BCS and HF

## Context

This work extends the limited literature on HF+BCS calculations with finite-range interactions, following earlier work by Robledo et al. (1987) using the D1 interaction for actinide octupole excitations and Than et al. (2011) using D1S and M3Y interactions for neutron star crust studies. The approach contrasts with the more common use of zero-range Skyrme interactions in HF+BCS (Robledo 1987, Bonche 2005, Sarriguren 2008, Bender 2009, Bender 2012), which require a different zero-range pairing interaction and are not self-consistent. The results enable future applications of the computationally simpler HF+BCS method with finite-range interactions for studying pairing correlations in nuclei far from stability, particularly where the reduced pairing compared to HFB is acceptable.
