# Manifestation of the cyclo-toroid nuclear moment in anomalous conversion and Lamb shift

> We offer the hypothesis that atomic nuclei, nucleons, and atoms possess a new type of electromagnetic moment, that we call a ``cyclo-toroid moment''. In nuclei, this moment arises when the toroid dipole (anapole) moments are arrayed in the form of a ring, or, equivalently, when the magnetic moments of the nucleons are arranged in the form of rings which, in turn, constitute the surface of a torus. We establish theoretically that the cyclo-toroid moment plays a role in the processes of the atomic shell--nucleus interaction. The existence of this moment would explain known anomalies in the internal conversion coefficients for $M1$ transitions in nuclei. We show also that the static cyclo-toroid nuclear moment interacts locally inside the nucleus with the vortex part of the atomic electron currents and this leads to an energy shift in atomic $s_{1/2}$ states. For the hydrogen atom the value of this shift may be comparable in order of magnitude to the present accuracy of measurements of the Lamb shift for the $1s_{1/2}$ level.

# Manifestation of the cyclo-toroid nuclear moment in anomalous conversion and Lamb shift

## Key claim
The paper hypothesizes that atomic nuclei possess a new type of electromagnetic moment called a "cyclo-toroid moment," which arises when toroid dipole (anapole) moments are arrayed in a ring. The authors claim this moment could explain known anomalies in internal conversion coefficients for M1 transitions in nuclei, and that for the hydrogen atom the energy shift from this moment may be comparable in order of magnitude to the present accuracy of measurements of the Lamb shift for the 1s_{1/2} level.

## Method
The authors derive the operator for the cyclo-toroid moment density as c_t = 1/2[r × t], where t is the toroid dipole moment density, and show that the cyclo-toroid interacts locally inside the nucleus with the vortex part of penetrating electron currents via the Hamiltonian H_int = 4π ∫ d³r c_t · curl j_ext. They analyze the electron current for s_{1/2} → S_{1/2} transitions in the Dirac formalism, demonstrating that curl j ≠ 0 for these transitions, and derive an expression for the reduced matrix element of the cyclo-toroid moment in terms of experimentally measured parameters. For the static case, they estimate the energy shift of atomic s_{1/2} levels using perturbation theory.

## Key numbers
- For the 482 keV M1 transition in ¹⁸¹Ta, the experimental K-shell conversion coefficient is 3 times greater than the normal theoretical magnitude, and the penetration parameter λ^(0) equals 150 (Firestone 1991).
- The reduced probability of the M1 transition in ¹⁸¹Ta is B_{W.u.} = 6.21 × 10⁻⁷ in Weisskopf units (Firestone 1991).
- The "unit" for measurement of the cyclo-toroid moment for ¹⁸¹Ta is c_t^(0) = 0.52 fm²/GeV, using r_0 = 1.2A^{1/3} fm.
- The estimated reduced matrix element for the cyclo-toroid moment in the ¹⁸¹Ta anomalous conversion M1 transition is ⟨‖c_t‖⟩ ≈ 0.3 c_t^(0).
- For the 6s level in ¹³³Cs, the estimated energy shift is |ΔE_{6s}| = 0.7 × 10⁻⁷ c_t eV, with c_{6s} = 8.7 and f^(0)_{6s} = -1.2 × 10³.
- For hydrogen-like ions: |ΔE_{1s}(¹³³Cs⁵⁴⁺)| ∼ 1 × 10⁻³ c_t eV and |ΔE_{1s}(²³³,²³⁵U⁹¹⁺)| ∼ 1 × 10⁻¹ c_t eV.
- For the hydrogen atom 1s level, the shift is |ΔE_{1s}| ≈ 1 × 10⁻¹¹ c_t eV, with c_{1s}=2, f^(0)_{1s} = -223.6, and r_0 = 0.862 fm.

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of toroid moments, originally introduced by Zel'dovich (1957) for the anapole moment and developed by Dubovik (1974, 1990), to a new hierarchical moment called the cyclo-toroid. The paper addresses anomalies in internal conversion coefficients for M1 transitions that were previously observed by Church (1956), Green (1958), Nilsson (1958), and Voikhanskii (1959), and which Listengarten (1981) attributed to toroid dipole moments for E1 transitions. The results suggest that the cyclo-toroid moment could be the single non-vanishing electromagnetic moment of a quantum object, enabling experimental investigation through precise measurements of atomic s_{1/2} level shifts, particularly in hydrogen-like ions of heavy nuclei and in the hydrogen Lamb shift.
