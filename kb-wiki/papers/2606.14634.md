# The unintuitive SU(3) flavor and chiral limits of hadron resonances

> Contrary to naive expectations, poles used to define hadron resonances rigorously in the physical world may not evolve continuously to become degenerate in the SU(3)$_F$ and chiral limits of QCD. Instead, other shadow poles, usually ignored, may be the ones that degenerate and characterize the resonances in these limits. This feature is general, and we illustrate it first with the simple and familiar light-vector mesons, followed by the much-discussed light-scalar case. Their shadow poles and their degeneracy are found using the QCD low-energy effective theory unitarized to one loop.

# The unintuitive SU(3) flavor and chiral limits of hadron resonances

## Key claim

Contrary to naive expectations, the poles that rigorously define hadron resonances in the physical world do not necessarily evolve continuously to become degenerate in the SU(3)_F and chiral limits of QCD; instead, other "shadow" poles, usually ignored in phenomenology, may be the ones that degenerate and characterize the resonances in these symmetric limits. This behavior is generic whenever a physical resonance lies between two thresholds that become degenerate below its pole mass in the symmetric limit.

## Method

The authors use next-to-leading order (NLO) chiral perturbation theory (ChPT) amplitudes for meson-meson scattering, unitarized with the coupled-channel inverse amplitude method (IAM), to generate light vector and scalar resonances without a priori assumptions on their existence or nature. They study the SU(3)_F limit by continuously decreasing the strange quark mass in the NLO ChPT meson-mass expansion until m_K = m_eta = m_pi^phys, and then extend to the chiral limit m_pi -> 0. Pole trajectories are tracked in different Riemann sheets (second, third, and fifth sheets depending on the number of thresholds) to identify which poles become degenerate in the symmetric limit.

## Key numbers

- Physical m_K/m_pi ~ 3.5 for the familiar rho(770) and K*(892) peaks; the SU(3)_F limit corresponds to m_K/m_pi = 1
- The rho(770) and K*(892) poles in the second Riemann sheet do not degenerate when m_K/m_pi = 1; instead, two distinct poles remain, with the K* pole sitting on the real axis
- The third-sheet (shadow) poles of the rho(770) and K*(892) coalesce into a single pole in the SU(3)_F limit
- In the chiral limit (m_pi -> 0) with flavor symmetry maintained, the common multiplet mass reduces by about 50 MeV, while the two-Nambu-Goldstone-boson threshold is approximately 280 MeV lower, increasing the resonance width
- For light scalars, the f_0(500)/sigma and f_0(980) poles appear simultaneously in both octet and singlet amplitudes even in the flavor-symmetric limit when tracked in the physically contiguous sheet

## Context

This work addresses the Oakes-Yang problem (Oakes and Yang, 1963), which predates the quark model, concerning whether resonance poles belong to the same multiplet in the flavor-symmetric limit. It extends prior studies that approached the symmetric limit by increasing m_pi (Wali, 1964; Oller, 2003; Roca, 2005; Jido, 2003; Bruns, 2021; Yamaguchi, 2016), which precludes studying the chiral limit and can hide a discontinuous jump from physical poles to shadow poles. The results challenge the common assumption that the full dynamical and symmetry-breaking information of a resonance is encoded in a single pole, and must be taken into account for rigorous spectroscopic classification of hadrons, particularly for the controversial light-scalar meson multiplet (f_0(500)/sigma, K_0*(700)/kappa, f_0(980), a_0(980)).
