# Applications of semiempirical shell model masses based on proton magic number Z = 126 to superheavy nuclei

> A recently published highly extrapolatable semiempirical shell model mass equation is shown to describe rather well the energies of several seemingly well identified alpha-decay chains with known end product nuclei observed in superheavy elements research. The equation is also applied to the interpretation problem of some recent hot fusion-evaporation experiments with unknown end products and several conceivable reaction channels. Some plausible interpretations are indicated.

# Applications of semiempirical shell model masses based on proton magic number Z = 126 to superheavy nuclei

## Key claim
The semiempirical shell model mass equation (SSME) based on proton magic number Z = 126 describes alpha-decay energies of several well-identified superheavy element decay chains rather well, but the equation is oversmooth and does not account for fine structure effects near deformed doubly submagic nuclei such as $^{270}$Hs (N = 162, Z = 108). For hot fusion experiments with unknown end products, the equation can help discriminate between possible reaction channels, but the interpretations remain plausible rather than definitive.

## Method
The authors applied a previously published semiempirical shell model mass equation (eq. (1) of Liran, Marinov, Zeldes (2000)) to predict Q_alpha values for alpha-decay chains observed in cold fusion experiments producing elements 107-112 and in hot fusion experiments with $^{248}$Cm and $^{244}$Pu targets bombarded by $^{48}$Ca ions. For well-identified chains, they compared predicted and measured Q_alpha values and computed average and rms deviations. For experiments with unknown end products, they considered multiple conceivable evaporation channels (xn, p, alpha) and compared the predicted Q_alpha values from each channel to the observed data, along with estimated excitation energies of the evaporation residue.

## Key numbers
- For 57 experimental Q_alpha values measured in region B after the equation was adjusted, the average deviation (delta_av) is -8 keV and the rms deviation (delta_rms) is 220 keV.
- For all 115 data points shown in figure 1 (both experimental and estimated from systematics), delta_av = -33 keV and delta_rms = 214 keV.
- For 32 data points from the cold fusion chains in figures 2-5, the overall delta_av = 86 keV and delta_rms = 337 keV.
- The largest individual deviations in figure 5 are 659 keV for Z = 107, 712 keV for Z = 108, and -605 keV for Z = 110, attributed to the submagic number effect at N = 162, Z = 108.
- For the $^{248}$Cm + $^{48}$Ca experiment, the assigned 4n channel (parent $^{292}$116) gives delta_rms = 758 keV and estimated excitation energy less than 2 MeV; the 2n channel (parent $^{294}$116) gives delta_rms = 466 keV and excitation energy less than or equal to 14 MeV.
- For the $^{244}$Pu + $^{48}$Ca experiment, the assigned 3n channel (parent $^{289}$114) gives delta_rms = 905 keV; the p channel (parent $^{291}$113) gives delta_rms = 414 keV; the alpha channel (parent $^{288}$112) gives delta_rms = 363 keV.

## Context
This work extends the SSME approach of Liran, Marinov, Zeldes (2000, 2001) which was based on proton magic number Z = 126, replacing an earlier SSME based on Z = 114 that stopped at that region. The equation is compared to experimental data from cold fusion experiments by Muenzenberg et al. (1981-1988) and Hofmann et al. (1995-2000), and to hot fusion experiments by Oganessian et al. (1999-2001). The oversmoothness of the model relative to submagic number effects near $^{252}$Fm (N = 152, Z = 100) and $^{270}$Hs (N = 162, Z = 108) is noted, referencing Moller et al. (1994), Sobiczewski et al. (1996), and Smolanczuk et al. (1997). The work enables reinterpretation of reaction channels in hot fusion experiments where decay chains do not connect to known daughters, and suggests that 2n or charged-particle evaporation may be more consistent with the data than the originally assigned xn channels.
