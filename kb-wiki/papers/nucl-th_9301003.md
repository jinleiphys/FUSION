# Neutron matter - Quark matter phase transition and Quark star

> We consider the neutron matter quark matter phase transition along with possible existence of hybrid quark stars. The equation of state for neutron matter is obtained using a nonperturbative method with pion dressing of the neutron matter and an analysis similar to that of symmetric nuclear matter. The quark matter sector is treated perturbatively in the small distance domain. For bag constant $B^{1/4}$=148 MeV, a first order phase transition is seen. In the context of neutron quark hybrid stars, Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are solved using the equations of state for quark matter and for neutron matter with a phase transition as noted earlier. Stable solutions for such stars are obtained with the Chandrasekhar limit as 1.58 $M_\odot$ and radius around 10 km. The bulk of the star is quark matter with a thin crust of neutron matter of less than a kilometer.

# Neutron matter - Quark matter phase transition and Quark star

## Key claim
Using a nonperturbative equation of state for neutron matter (with parameters fixed to reproduce symmetric nuclear matter properties) and a perturbative QCD equation of state for quark matter, a first-order phase transition occurs at a bag constant of B^{1/4}=148 MeV, leading to stable hybrid stars where the bulk of the star is quark matter with only a thin neutron matter crust of less than a kilometer.

## Method
The neutron matter equation of state was derived using a nonperturbative method with pion dressing of neutron matter, employing thermofield dynamics at finite temperature and the same parameters as for symmetric nuclear matter from Mishra et al. (Int. J. Mod. Phys. E, 1993). The quark matter sector was treated perturbatively to order alpha_s in the small-distance domain, including u, d, and s quarks with electrons, following the approach of Kapusta (Finite Temperature Field Theory). The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations were solved using the combined equations of state, with a first-order phase transition at the interface between quark matter and neutron matter, to obtain stellar mass, radius, and related properties.

## Key numbers
- Bag constant: B^{1/4}=148 MeV, used for the quark matter equation of state.
- Critical pressure for phase transition: P_cr=4 MeV/fm^3 at critical baryon chemical potential (mu_B)_cr=960 MeV.
- Critical energy densities: epsilon_cr^{nm}=226 MeV/fm^3 for neutron matter and epsilon_cr^{qm}=284 MeV/fm^3 for quark matter.
- Critical baryon number densities: rho_B^{nm}=0.24/fm^3 and rho_B^{qm}=0.33/fm^3.
- Chandrasekhar limit for hybrid stars: 1.58 solar masses (M_sun).
- Stable hybrid star radii: approximately 9.4 to 9.85 km for masses 1.49 to 1.58 M_sun.
- Neutron matter crust thickness: less than 1 km, ranging from 0.5 km (for central density 1500 MeV/fm^3) to 2.2 km (for central density 350 MeV/fm^3).
- Quark matter core radius: approximately 8.9 to 9 km for central densities 900-1500 MeV/fm^3.
- Nuclear matter saturation properties used for parameter fixing: single particle energy -15.03 MeV at saturation density 0.19 fm^{-3}, with parameters lambda=0.54 fm^2, R_pi=1.18 fm, and a=0.12 GeV.

## Context
This work extends the nonperturbative treatment of symmetric nuclear matter from Mishra et al. (Int. J. Mod. Phys. A, 1990) and Mishra et al. (Int. J. Mod. Phys. E, 1993) to pure neutron matter, and applies it to hybrid star calculations following the approach of Glendenning (1991) for phase transitions in stellar interiors. The results are similar to those obtained by Rosenhauer et al. (Nucl. Phys. A, 1992) for hybrid star properties. The paper enables further study of pulsar timing anomalies and neutrino emissivity from quark matter cores, though the authors note that distinguishing hybrid stars from pure neutron stars via surface gravitational redshift alone is not possible.
