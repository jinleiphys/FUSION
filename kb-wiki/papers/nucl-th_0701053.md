# Correlation and Fluctuation in Multiparticle Production: Some Closing Remarks

## Key claim
The workshop series on multiparticle production has evolved from a field lacking first principles and collective wisdom in 1987 to one where relativistic hydrodynamics provides a successful theoretical basis for heavy-ion collisions, yet the key open question is not how to describe the collision process but what can be learned from hydrodynamical parameters and how to understand features that cannot be explained by hydrodynamics.

## Method
The author provides a historical perspective on the eleven workshops held over approximately twenty years, comparing the state of the field in 1987 (Jinan workshop) with 2006 (Hangzhou workshop). He uses a metaphor of dragons in different cultures to illustrate three main research areas: Bose-Einstein correlation (observing the quark-gluon plasma from afar), event-by-event fluctuation (approaching critical behavior), and jet tomography (probing the medium with hard partons). The talk does not attempt to summarize all presentations but instead offers overarching commentary on the workshop's themes.

## Key numbers
- In 1987 there were only four major experimental talks (from NA22, NA9/EMC & WA21, UA1, and UA5); in 2006 there were 19 experimental talks, all but one on RHIC data.
- The correlation length ξ extracted from PHENIX data exhibits universal scaling and decreases with Npart, but the values of ξ are so small at any Npart that there is no hint of critical behavior.
- At LHC, for pT < 20 GeV/c, so many jets will be produced that correlations examined at RHIC become meaningless in the presence of a sea of associated particles.

## Context
This closing remark extends the series of workshops on multiparticle production that began in Aspen (1986) and Jinan (1987), organized by Q.-B. Xie and the author. The author contrasts the earlier era, when multiparticle production was regarded as too complicated and lacked a workable theory, with the current situation where relativistic hydrodynamics (following Landau and Bjorken) provides a successful framework. The talk identifies three active areas, Bose-Einstein correlation, event-by-event fluctuation, and jet tomography, as natural extensions of earlier concerns such as intermittency (1990s) and critical fluctuation (mid-1990s). The work enables future investigation of how to disentangle correlations from different emission times in evolving systems and how to adapt jet studies for LHC conditions where jet production will be abundant.
