# Effects of Neutron Spatial Distributions on Atomic Parity Nonconservation in Cesium

> We have examined modifications to the nuclear weak charge due to small differences between the spatial distributions of neutrons and protons in the Cs nucleus. We derive approximate formulae to estimate the value and uncertainty of this modification based only on nuclear rms neutron and proton radii. Present uncertainties in neutron distributions in Cs are difficult to quantify, but we conclude that they should not be neglected when using atomic parity nonconservation experiments as a means to test the Standard Model.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the difference between neutron and proton spatial distributions in the cesium nucleus produces a small correction to the nuclear weak charge, which should no longer be neglected in atomic parity nonconservation tests of the Standard Model because experimental and atomic theory uncertainties have recently been reduced to a level comparable to this nuclear correction.

## Method

The authors derived approximate analytic formulas for the correction to the weak charge based on the root-mean-square (rms) neutron and proton radii, assuming a uniform nuclear charge distribution and expanding the Dirac equation for the electron axial matrix elements in powers of the fine structure constant. They then performed full numerical calculations of the electron wave functions using the experimental charge distribution of Cs and various theoretical neutron density distributions from Skyrme (SLy4, SkM*, SkIII) and Gogny nuclear structure models, computing the weak charge correction directly.

## Key numbers

- The latest experimental weak charge for Cs is \(Q_w^{\rm expt} = -72.06(28)_{\rm expt}\ (34)_{\rm atomic\ theory}\), compared to the Standard Model prediction \(Q_w^{\rm St.\ Mod.} = -73.20(13)_{\rm theory}\), a \(2.5\sigma\) discrepancy.
- Using spherical Skyrme SLy4 distributions, the correction is \(\Delta Q_w^{n-p} = +0.14\); using spherical Gogny distributions, \(\Delta Q_w^{n-p} = +0.11\).
- The earlier estimate from Johnson (1990s) gave \(\Delta Q_w^{n-p} \approx 0.06\), based on a Skyrme SkM* model where the neutron rms radius differed by only 0.9% from the proton rms radius.
- Chen and Vogel (1990s) estimated an uncertainty \(\delta \Delta Q_w^{n-p} = \pm 0.13\) corresponding to \(\delta R_n^2 \approx \pm 1\ {\rm fm}^2\), and a total theoretical uncertainty in \(Q_w\) of \(\pm 0.18\).
- Attributing the full experimental discrepancy to nuclear effects would require \(R_n = (1.18 \pm 0.07) R_p\), which is far outside any theoretical or experimental nuclear structure predictions.
- For nearby nuclei, model spreads in \(R_n^2/R_p^2\) range from 1.03 (Skyrme) to over 1.08 (relativistic models) for \(^{138}\)Ba, and from 1.04 to 1.09 for \(^{136}\)Xe.

## Context

This work extends earlier atomic theory calculations by Johnson (1990s) that dismissed the neutron distribution effect as extremely small, and by Chen and Vogel (1990s) who considered two Skyrme models. The paper shows that the uncertainty in the neutron distribution correction is comparable to the Standard Model radiative correction uncertainty and could partially explain the \(2.5\sigma\) discrepancy between experiment and the Standard Model. It enables future work by motivating direct measurements of neutron distributions via parity-violating electron scattering at facilities such as Jefferson Lab, and by highlighting the need for improved nuclear structure calculations in deformed, odd-Z nuclei like \(^{133}\)Cs.
