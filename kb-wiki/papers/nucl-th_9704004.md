# A General Signal of a Phase Transition from Single-Particle Momentum Distributions

> A two-particle space correlation function is derived from the single-particle momentum distribution of the emission source. A signal of a first order phase transition is obtained from this correlation function if density fluctuations are large.

## Key claim

A first-order phase transition can be signaled by a long-range two-particle space correlation function derived from the single-particle momentum distribution, provided that density fluctuations are large; the signal is sensitive to the shape of the single-particle momentum distribution, not just its average transverse momentum.

## Method

The author derives the two-particle space correlation function from the single-particle momentum distribution using a second-quantization formalism, then compares it to the correlation function obtained from a Landau free-energy expansion near the critical point. The calculation assumes a static source at freeze-out with particles moving freely, and uses Boltzmann, Bose, and Fermi statistics to relate the momentum-space occupation numbers to the spatial correlation function.

## Key numbers

- Temperature \( T = 160 \) MeV is chosen for the calculations shown in Figure 1.
- For the first-order phase transition case, the parameter \( a = 0 \) at \( T = T_c \), yielding a correlation function proportional to \( T_c / (4\pi b r) \).
- For the case without phase transition, the ratio \( a/b \approx 4 \) is used.
- A 30% fluctuation in the fitted single-particle momentum distribution is assumed for momenta less than 30 MeV/c to test experimental robustness.

## Context

This work extends the Ornstein-Zernike (1914) treatment of density fluctuations near a critical point to heavy-ion collisions, proposing a new observable for first-order phase transitions in both QGP and liquid-to-gas nuclear transitions. It contrasts with earlier signals based on average transverse momentum (van Hove, 1982) and Bose-Einstein correlation radii (HBT phase transition signal), which are not sensitive to the shape of the single-particle momentum distribution. The method enables event-by-event searches for phase transitions using single-particle momentum distributions, and is applicable to searches for disoriented chiral condensates as discussed by Gavin and Bjorken et al.
