# Size Matters: Origin of Binomial Scaling in Nuclear Fragmentation Experiments

> The relationship between measured transverse energy, total charge recovered in the detector, and size of the emitting system is investigated. Using only very simple assumptions, we are able to reproduce the observed binomial emission probabilities and their dependences on the transverse energy.

## Key claim

The observed binomial scaling of intermediate-mass-fragment (IMF) multiplicity distributions and the linear dependence of ln(p^{-1}) on 1/sqrt(E_t) arise primarily from the finite size of the emitting system and the experimental correlation between transverse energy and system size, not from a fundamental thermal barrier-penetration mechanism as previously claimed.

## Method

The authors generated Monte Carlo fragmentation events with fragment charges drawn from a power-law distribution p(Z) proportional to Z^{-tau}, constrained to exactly Z_sys total charges per event. They then folded the individual fragment charge distributions to construct combined probability distributions for IMFs (charges 3 to k, with k up to 30) and extracted binomial parameters p_k and m_k as functions of system size and upper charge limit. Finally, they integrated these results over the experimentally measured distribution of Z_sys as a function of transverse energy for the Kr+Au system at 55 A MeV, using three different values of tau and one tau(E_t) parameterization.

## Key numbers

- For a system of 100 total charges, individual fragment probability distributions have p values less than or equal to 3 x 10^{-2}
- The mean Z_sys as a function of transverse energy follows: <Z_sys(E_t)> approximately 2 + 0.092 x E_t/MeV for E_t below 0.7 GeV
- The width of the Z_sys(E_t) distribution is on the order of 10 units of charge
- The tau(E_t) parameterization used for agreement with experiment: tau(E_t) = 3.5 - E_t/(0.5 GeV)
- Calculations used 10^4 events per system size for figure 1 and 2 x 10^4 events per point for figure 4

## Context

This work directly addresses the claims of Moretto et al. (1993-1998) that binomial IMF multiplicity distributions imply a simple barrier-penetration mechanism for fragment emission. It extends criticisms by Toke et al. (1997) and Tsang and Danielewicz (1998) regarding autocorrelations and thermal energy transformations. The paper shows that finite-size effects alone reproduce the observed scaling, and suggests that once these size correlations are removed, percolation-model predictions of sub- to super-Poissonian fluctuations near the percolation threshold (Gharib, Bauer, Pratt, 1998) could provide insight into the nuclear fragmentation phase transition.
