# Mass Probe of Tetrahedral Symmetry in Atomic Nuclei

> Tetrahedral symmetry has long been predicted as an exotic shape degree of freedom in atomic nuclei, yet clear experimental manifestations remain elusive. We show that the triple binding energy difference $δV_{pn}^{(3)}$ can isolate a structural effect of tetrahedral symmetry in $^{80}$Zr. Using relativistic density functional theory solved on a three-dimensional lattice without symmetry restrictions, the experimental $δV_{pn}^{(3)}$ values for even-even $^{80\text{-}90}$Zr isotopes are well reproduced without adjustable parameters. While an enhancement of $δV_{pn}^{(3)}$ near $N\simeq Z$ is commonly attributed to proton-neutron correlations beyond the mean field, the pronounced nonmonotonic peak at $N=40$ emerges at the mean-field level only when the tetrahedral degree of freedom is included. Constraining the tetrahedral deformation to zero removes the peak and leads to clear deviations from experiment. The anomaly is traced to a well-localized tetrahedral minimum in $^{80}$Zr, supported by potential energy surfaces and characteristic single-particle level splittings. Calculations restricted to quadrupole and triaxial shapes fail to reproduce the localized enhancement, indicating that the effect is not a generic proton-neutron correlation but a symmetry-selective increase of proton-neutron binding associated with tetrahedral geometry. We therefore identify the $δV_{pn}^{(3)}$ anomaly in $^{80}$Zr as a structural mechanism distinct from the conventional Wigner-type enhancement and show that nuclear masses constitute a sensitive probe of tetrahedral symmetry.

## Key claim

The triple binding energy difference $\delta V_{pn}^{(3)}$ can isolate a structural effect of tetrahedral symmetry in $^{80}$Zr, but the experimental observable alone does not constitute a unique or exclusive proof of tetrahedral symmetry; rather, it is the combination with density functional theory analysis that provides strong evidence for tetrahedral correlations.

## Method

Relativistic density functional theory with the PC-PK1 functional was solved on a three-dimensional lattice without symmetry restrictions, using a 1 fm lattice spacing and 30 grid points along each axis. The Dirac equation was solved directly on the lattice to allow all deformation degrees of freedom, and pairing correlations were treated in the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) approximation with neutron pairing strength $G_n = -330$ MeV fm$^3$ and proton pairing strength $G_p = -430$ MeV fm$^3$. The triple binding energy difference $\delta V_{pn}^{(3)}$ was computed from calculated ground-state binding energies of even-even Zr and Sr isotopes and compared with experimental values from AME2020 and a recent high-precision mass measurement of $^{80}$Zr.

## Key numbers

- $\delta V_{pn}^{(3)}$ for $^{80}$Zr reaches 0.99 MeV at $N=40$, drops sharply to $-0.19$ MeV at $N=42$, and recovers to about 0.05 MeV at $N=44$, showing a pronounced nonmonotonic anomaly.
- Constraining tetrahedral deformation to zero in $^{80}$Zr removes the peak and restores the reflection-symmetric result, identifying tetrahedral deformation as the key origin.
- The potential energy surface of $^{80}$Zr shows a well-localized tetrahedral minimum at $a_{20}=0$ and $a_{32}=0.14$, with an energy gain of approximately 0.84 MeV relative to the tetrahedral-constrained configuration.
- The potential energy surface of $^{76}$Sr exhibits a prolate minimum at $\beta=0.49$ and $\gamma=0^\circ$ with an energy gain of approximately 2.84 MeV.
- For $N=44$ to $N=50$, $\delta V_{pn}^{(3)}$ remains nearly constant at 0.01 to 0.05 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical studies of tetrahedral symmetry using macroscopic-microscopic models (Dudek 2002), nonrelativistic density functional theories (Schunck 2004, Zberecki 2009, Tagami 2013, Miyahara and Nakada 2018, Wang 2019), and relativistic DFTs (Zhao 2017, Rong 2023, Xu 2024), which had predicted tetrahedral shapes but lacked conclusive experimental evidence. The paper identifies $\delta V_{pn}^{(3)}$ as a mass-based observable that is more selective and sensitive than previously proposed signatures such as negative-parity rotational bands with suppressed $E2$ transitions (Bark 2010, Jentschel 2010). Future precision mass measurements of neutron-deficient Zr isotopes, particularly $^{76}$Zr, will provide a decisive test of the prediction and open a new pathway for exploring exotic nonaxial shapes in atomic nuclei.
