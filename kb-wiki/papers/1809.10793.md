# Weak-Interaction Tests via Precision Superallowed $β$-Decay Studies at TRIUMF

> Superallowed $β$-decay studies provide some of the best constraints on the possibility of additional quark generations, as well as limits on exotic currents in the weak interaction. The three experimental quantities that are required for performing these tests using $0^+\rightarrow0^+$ nuclear decays (branching ratio, half-life, and $Q$-value) can all be measured to high-precision with rare-isotope beams at the TRIUMF-ISAC facility in Vancouver, Canada. This proceeding presents a brief outline of the general experimental techniques used at TRIUMF over the past 15 years, as well as recent theoretical advances towards {\it ab-initio} isospin-symmetry-breaking corrections to superallowed nuclear decays.

## Key claim

Superallowed $0^+ \rightarrow 0^+$ nuclear beta-decay data provide the most precise determination of the vector coupling strength $G_V$ and the CKM matrix element $V_{ud}$, but the uncertainty on these extractions is dominated by theoretical corrections, specifically the transition-independent radiative correction $\Delta_R$ and the nucleus-dependent isospin-symmetry-breaking (ISB) correction $\delta_C$, rather than by experimental precision.

## Method

The authors review the experimental program at TRIUMF-ISAC that measures the three quantities required for superallowed decay tests: branching ratios using the 8$\pi$ and GRIFFIN HPGe gamma-ray spectrometers with SCEPTAR beta-particle detectors; half-lives using a 4$\pi$ continuous-flow gas proportional counter (GPS) with nearly 100% detection efficiency; and $Q_{EC}$-values using the TITAN Penning trap mass spectrometer via the time-of-flight ion-cyclotron-resonance technique. They also present preliminary theoretical work using the valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) method, based on chiral effective field theory two- and three-nucleon forces, to calculate ISB corrections $\delta_C$ directly from the Fermi matrix element without the separation of terms used in the semi-phenomenological shell-model approach of Towner and Hardy.

## Key numbers

- The up-down element of the CKM matrix, $V_{ud}$, is known to $0.021\%$ precision, relying nearly entirely on high-precision superallowed beta-decay $ft$ values.
- The 8$\pi$ spectrometer provides photo-peak efficiencies for single events of $\sim1\%$ at 1.3 MeV.
- GRIFFIN is 17 times more efficient than the 8$\pi$ for 1 MeV gamma rays and 37 times more efficient by 10 MeV.
- Half-life measurements with the GPS system typically measure activity for 25 half-lives of the respective radioactive species.
- TITAN uses a 3.7 T high-precision Penning trap; only cycles with 1 detected ion/cycle are typically used to reduce ion-ion interaction effects, which are the largest systematic uncertainty.
- The 1.8/2.0 (EM) NN+3N interaction used in the VS-IMSRG calculations has been shown to reproduce ground-state energies from the $p$-shell to the tin region.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing superallowed beta-decay program at TRIUMF-ISAC, which has been running for over 15 years and has measured nuclei from $^{10}$C to $^{74}$Rb. The experimental results feed into the extraction of $V_{ud}$ that currently relies on the ISB corrections of Towner and Hardy (2015, 2008), calculated within a semi-phenomenological shell-model framework. The authors present preliminary VS-IMSRG calculations of $\delta_C$ as a consistency check on the Towner-Hardy formalism and as a path toward quantifying systematic uncertainties; they compare their results to the shell-model Woods-Saxon values of Towner and Hardy (2015) and the density functional theory approach of Satula et al. (2016). The work enables future tests of the isobaric-multiplet-mass-equation coefficients and, ultimately, more precise constraints on possible scalar currents and additional quark generations.
