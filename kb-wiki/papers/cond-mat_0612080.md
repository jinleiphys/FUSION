# The concept of correlated density and its application

> The correlated density appears in many physical systems ranging from dense interacting gases up to Fermi liquids which develop a coherent state at low temperatures, the superconductivity. One consequence of the correlated density is the Bernoulli potential in superconductors which compensates forces from dielectric currents. This Bernoulli potential allows to access material parameters. Though within the surface potential these contributions are largely canceled, the bulk measurements with NMR can access this potential. Recent experiments are explained and new ones suggested. The underlying quantum statistical theory in nonequilibrium is the nonlocal kinetic theory developed earlier.

# The concept of correlated density and its application

## Key claim

The correlated density, which distinguishes free particles from those in bound or coherent states, leads to a Bernoulli electrostatic potential in superconductors that compensates forces from diamagnetic currents; this potential can in principle provide direct access to material parameters like the superconducting gap, but surface measurements have failed to detect the thermodynamic corrections because the Budd-Vannimenus theorem shows that surface dipoles exactly cancel those contributions, leaving only the quasiparticle-screening term observable at the surface.

## Method

The authors derive the correlated density from the BCS Wigner distribution function and expand it in the zero-temperature limit to obtain an expression for the electrostatic potential that maintains charge neutrality. They extend the Ginzburg-Landau theory to lower temperatures using Bardeen's approach, incorporating condensation energy from the Gorter-Casimir two-fluid model, and solve the resulting equations numerically for Nb to obtain the condensate fraction, magnetic field profile, and charge density. The surface potential is then analyzed via a modification of the Budd-Vannimenus theorem, and the bulk charge profile is used to interpret NMR quadrupole resonance measurements in YBCO.

## Key numbers

- The correlated density in the BCS ground state is given by \( n_{\rm corr} \approx \frac{\partial h}{\partial \mu} \frac{\Delta^2}{4\pi} \ln\left( \frac{2\omega_D}{\sqrt{e}\Delta} \right) \) for \( \omega_D \gg \Delta \), where \( h \) is the density of states, \( \Delta \) is the superconducting gap, and \( \omega_D \) is the Debye frequency.
- The resulting electrostatic potential is \( e\varphi = -\frac{m}{2}v^2 + \frac{\partial \ln h}{\partial \mu} \frac{\Delta^2}{2} \ln\left( \frac{2\omega_D}{\sqrt{e}\Delta} \right) \), combining a Bernoulli term from supercurrent velocity \( v \) and a thermodynamic correction from the gap.
- For the surface potential near \( T_c \), the full theory gives \( \langle e\phi_0 \rangle = \frac{\varepsilon_{\rm con}}{n \beta_{\rm A}} (1-t^2)^2 (1-b)^2 \), while Blatter's approximation gives \( \langle e\phi_{\rm Bl} \rangle = \frac{\varepsilon_{\rm con}}{n \beta_{\rm A}} \frac{\partial\ln T_c}{\partial\ln n} \, 2\,(1-t^4)(1-b) \), where \( t = T/T_c \) and \( b = B/B_{c2} \).
- The BCS estimate of charge per vortex length is \( Q \approx \frac{2 e k_F}{\pi^3} \left( \frac{\lambda_{\rm TF}}{\xi} \right)^2 \left( \frac{d\ln T_c}{d\ln \mu} \right) \), giving \( 10^{-5} e \) per vortex, while the NMR experiment in YBCO yielded a value three orders of magnitude larger.
- The width of the surface charge layer is \( w = \frac{\lambda}{2} \frac{\ln(\kappa/\sqrt{2})}{\kappa/\sqrt{2} - 1} \) for type-II superconductors with GL parameter \( \kappa > \sqrt{2} \), where \( \lambda \) is the London penetration depth.

## Context

This work extends the nonlocal kinetic theory developed by the authors (Morawetz et al., 1997) which unifies the Landau quasiparticle picture with the Beth-Uhlenbeck equation of state, resolving the apparent contradiction between the Luttinger theorem and correlated density approaches. The paper explains why surface measurements by Bok and Klein (1968) and Morris and Brown (1971) on Pb at 7 K failed to detect thermodynamic corrections, resolving a puzzle that persisted for nearly 30 years. The theory enables interpretation of NMR quadrupole resonance measurements by Kumagai et al. (2001) on YBCO, explaining the observed charge of vortices which was three orders of magnitude larger than BCS estimates, and predicts that the surface potential scales as \( (1-t)^2 \) near \( T_c \) and \( (1-b)^2 \) near \( B_{c2} \), in contrast to the linear scaling predicted by Blatter (1996) and Khomskii and Kusmartsev (1992).
