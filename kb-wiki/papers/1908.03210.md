# Towards analog quantum simulations of lattice gauge theories with trapped ions

> Gauge field theories play a central role in modern physics and are at the heart of the Standard Model of elementary particles and interactions. Despite significant progress in applying classical computational techniques to simulate gauge theories, it has remained a challenging task to compute the real-time dynamics of systems described by gauge theories. An exciting possibility that has been explored in recent years is the use of highly-controlled quantum systems to simulate, in an analog fashion, properties of a target system whose dynamics are difficult to compute. Engineered atom-laser interactions in a linear crystal of trapped ions offer a wide range of possibilities for quantum simulations of complex physical systems. Here, we devise practical proposals for analog simulation of simple lattice gauge theories whose dynamics can be mapped onto spin-spin interactions in any dimension. These include 1+1D quantum electrodynamics, 2+1D Abelian Chern-Simons theory coupled to fermions, and 2+1D pure Z2 gauge theory. The scheme proposed, along with the optimization protocol applied, will have applications beyond the examples presented in this work, and will enable scalable analog quantum simulation of Heisenberg spin models in any number of dimensions and with arbitrary interaction strengths.

## Key claim

The paper proposes practical, near-term experimental protocols for analog quantum simulation of simple lattice gauge theories (1+1D QED, 2+1D Abelian Chern-Simons theory coupled to fermions, and 2+1D pure Z2 gauge theory) using trapped ions, but the feasibility depends on achieving individual ion addressing and satisfying specific conditions on laser detunings and Rabi frequencies to suppress spin-phonon entanglement and higher-order sideband transitions.

## Method

The authors devise a scheme using a linear chain of trapped ions with individual addressing by multiple pairs of Raman laser beams, where three sets of beam pairs are configured to produce effective spin-spin interactions along orthogonal Bloch-sphere directions (sigma_x, sigma_y, sigma_z) by coupling to independent sets of motional normal modes. The lattice gauge theories are first mapped to spin-1/2 Hamiltonians via Jordan-Wigner transformations and gauge-field elimination, then the engineered ion-laser Hamiltonian is tuned to reproduce the desired Heisenberg spin model through a Magnus expansion that yields effective spin-spin coupling matrices J_{i,j}^{(xx)}, J_{i,j}^{(yy)}, and J_{i,j}^{(zz)}. An optimization procedure minimizes residual couplings to motional excitations and maximizes closeness to the target Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

* For the 1+1D Schwinger model, the lattice Hamiltonian is written in units of ag^2/2, where a is the lattice spacing and g is the fermion-gauge field coupling; the dimensionless parameters are x = 1/(ag)^2 and mu = 2m/(ag^2)
* The effective spin-spin coupling matrices are given by J_{i,j}^{(xx)} = Omega_I^{(i)} Omega_I^{(j)} R_I sum_m b_m^{(i)} b_m^{(j)} / (mu_I^2 - omega_m^T^2), with R_L = (Delta k_L)^2/(2M) being the recoil frequency
* The condition for suppressing spin-phonon entanglement requires |eta_{I,m}^{(i)} Omega_I^{(i)}| << |mu_I - omega_m^T|, and similarly for the other beam pairs
* Higher-order sideband suppression requires |(eta_{I,m}^{(i)})^(2p-2) (mu_I - omega_m^T) / (mu_I - p omega_m^T)| << 1 for integer p >= 2

## Context

This work extends prior proposals for analog quantum simulation of lattice gauge theories with trapped ions (Hauke 2013) and digital implementations of 1+1D QED (Martinez 2016), where the digital approach was limited by Trotterization noise and short coherence times. The paper builds on the Molmer-Sorenson scheme for spin-spin interactions and the individual-addressing approach of Korenblit (2012), enabling simulation of higher-dimensional gauge theories beyond the 1D chains typically associated with trapped ions. The protocols enable near-term study of real-time dynamics in gauge theories that are intractable with classical Monte Carlo methods, particularly for finite-density systems and out-of-equilibrium phenomena.
