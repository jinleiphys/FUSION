# Quantifying the uncertainties on spinodal instability in stellar matter through meta-modeling

> The influence of the uncertainties of the equation of state empirical parameters on the neutron stars crust-core phase transition is explored within a meta-modeling approach, in which the energy per particle is expanded as a Taylor series in density and asymmetry around the saturation point. The phase transition point is estimated from the intersection of the spinodal instability region for dynamical fluctuations with the chemical equilibrium curve. Special attention is paid to the inclusion of high-order parameters of the Taylor series and their influence on the transition point. An uncorrelated prior distribution is considered for the empirical parameters, with bulk properties constrained through effective field theory predictions, while the surface parameters are controlled from a fit of nuclear masses using the extended Thomas Fermi approximation. The results show that the isovector compressibility $K_{sym}$ and skewness $Q_{sym}$ have the most significant correlations with the transition point, along with the previously observed influence of the $L_{sym}$ parameter. The estimated density and pressure of the crust-core transition are $n_t = (0.071 \pm 0.011) fm^{-3}$ and $P_t = (0.294 \pm 0.102) MeV fm^{-3}$.

## Key claim

The paper quantifies how uncertainties in the equation of state empirical parameters affect the neutron star crust-core transition point, finding that the isovector compressibility \(K_{sym}\) and skewness \(Q_{sym}\) show the most significant correlations with the transition point alongside the previously known influence of the slope parameter \(L_{sym}\), but these conclusions depend on including higher-order terms (up to \(N=4\)) in the Taylor expansion and on the specific choice of the low-density correction parameter \(b\).

## Method

The authors employ a meta-modeling approach where the energy per particle of nuclear matter is expanded as a Taylor series in density and asymmetry around the saturation point, including terms up to fourth order. They compute the crust-core transition point by finding the intersection of the dynamical spinodal instability region (which accounts for finite-size density fluctuations via surface parameters \(C_{fin}\) and \(D_{fin}\)) with the \(\beta\)-equilibrium chemical equilibrium curve. An uncorrelated prior distribution is used for the empirical parameters, with bulk properties constrained by effective field theory predictions and surface parameters fitted to nuclear masses from the AME2012 mass table using the extended Thomas Fermi approximation.

## Key numbers

- Transition density: \(n_t = (0.071 \pm 0.011) \, \text{fm}^{-3}\)
- Transition pressure: \(P_t = (0.294 \pm 0.102) \, \text{MeV} \, \text{fm}^{-3}\)
- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter: \(n_{sat} \sim 0.16 \, \text{fm}^{-3}\)
- Best-fit low-density correction parameter: \(b \sim 2.31\), corresponding to fixing the correction at \(n_m \sim 0.048 \, \text{fm}^{-3}\) (0.3 \(n_{sat}\))
- Surface parameter values for Set 1: \(C_{fin} = 52 \, \text{MeV} \, \text{fm}^{-5}\), \(D_{fin} = -30 \, \text{MeV} \, \text{fm}^{-5}\)
- Surface parameter values for Set 2: \(C_{fin} = 46 \, \text{MeV} \, \text{fm}^{-5}\), \(D_{fin} = -70 \, \text{MeV} \, \text{fm}^{-5}\)
- Variation of the \(b\) parameter changes the transition density by at most \(n_t \pm 0.001 \, \text{fm}^{-3}\) when higher-order parameters (\(N=3,4\)) are included, compared to \(n_t \pm 0.01 \, \text{fm}^{-3}\) for \(N=2\) only

## Context

This work extends the meta-modeling framework previously applied to neutron stars by Margueron et al. (2018) and to finite nuclei by Chatterjee et al. (2017) to the specific problem of the crust-core phase transition. It confirms the dominant role of \(L_{sym}\) reported by Ducoin et al. (2011) while newly identifying \(K_{sym}\) and \(Q_{sym}\) as important parameters. The results enable more precise predictions for neutron star radii (to be measured by NICER) and crustal moment of inertia (linked to pulsar glitches), and provide a systematic uncertainty quantification that can guide future nuclear experiments targeting the symmetry energy and its higher-order derivatives.
