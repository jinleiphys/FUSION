# Neural Ordinary Differential Equations for Mapping the Magnetic QCD Phase Diagram via Holography

> The QCD phase diagram is crucial for understanding strongly interacting matter under extreme conditions, with major implications for cosmology, neutron stars, and heavy-ion collisions. We present a novel holographic QCD model utilizing neural ordinary differential equations (ODEs) to map the QCD phase diagram under magnetic field $B$, baryon chemical potential $μ_B$, and temperature $T$. By solving the inverse problem of constructing a gravitational theory from Lattice QCD data, we reveal an unprecedentedly rich phase structure at finite $B$, including multiple critical endpoints (CEPs) in strong magnetic fields. Specifically, for {$B = 1.618 \, \mathrm{GeV}^2=2.592 \times 10^{19}$ Gauss}, we identify two distinct CEPs at $(T_C = 87.3 \, \mathrm{MeV}, \, μ_C = 115.9 \, \mathrm{MeV})$ and $(T_C = 78.9 \, \mathrm{MeV}, \, μ_C = 244.0 \, \mathrm{MeV})$. Notably, the critical exponents vary depending on the CEP's location, and the conventional scaling relations can be violated in the presence of strong magnetic fields. These findings significantly advance our understanding of the QCD phase structure and provide concrete predictions for experimental validation at upcoming facilities such as FAIR, JPARC-HI, and NICA.

## Key claim
The paper claims that by using neural ordinary differential equations (ODEs) to solve the inverse problem of constructing a holographic gravitational theory from Lattice QCD data, they reveal an unprecedentedly rich phase structure at finite magnetic field, including multiple critical endpoints (CEPs) in strong magnetic fields. The hedge is that this result is obtained specifically for a magnetic field of \(B = 1.618 \, \mathrm{GeV}^2 = 2.592 \times 10^{19}\) Gauss, and the existence of multiple CEPs is a feature that "exceeds prior expectations" and may depend on the specific model assumptions.

## Method
The authors employ a five-dimensional holographic QCD model with a metric, scalar field, and two Maxwell fields (one for baryon chemical potential and one for magnetic field). They use neural ODEs to determine the magnetic coupling function \(\hat{Z}(\phi)\) by minimizing the difference between the holographic predictions and lattice QCD data for thermodynamic quantities such as magnetic susceptibility, magnetization, entropy density, and longitudinal pressure. The equations of motion are solved numerically with boundary conditions at the black hole horizon and the AdS boundary, and the free energy density is computed to map the phase diagram.

## Key numbers
- For \(B = 1.618 \, \mathrm{GeV}^2 = 2.592 \times 10^{19}\) Gauss, two CEPs are identified: one at \((T_C = 87.3 \, \mathrm{MeV}, \mu_C = 115.9 \, \mathrm{MeV})\) and another at \((T_C = 78.9 \, \mathrm{MeV}, \mu_C = 244.0 \, \mathrm{MeV})\).
- At \(B = 0\), the CEP is located at \((T_C = 105 \, \mathrm{MeV}, \mu_C = 555 \, \mathrm{MeV})\).
- In the \(T\)-\(B\) plane at \(\mu_B = 0\), the CEP is at \((T_C = 89.6 \, \mathrm{MeV}, B = 1.6 \, \mathrm{GeV}^2)\).
- Critical exponents for CEP III (at \(\mu_B = 0, B = 1.6 \, \mathrm{GeV}^2\)) are \(\alpha = 0.00917\), \(\beta = 0.3944\), \(\gamma = 1.05175\), and \(\delta = 3.9878\), which violate the scaling relations \(\alpha + 2\beta + \gamma = 2\) and \(\delta - \gamma/\beta = 1\) (giving 1.8498 and 1.3212, respectively).

## Context
This work extends prior holographic QCD models that struggled to quantitatively describe lattice QCD data across the full phase diagram, such as those by Cai (2012), He (2013), and Alho (2013). It also addresses the limitations of Lattice QCD at finite baryon density (the sign problem) and high magnetic fields, as noted by Philipsen (2012) and Andersen (2014). The neural ODE approach enables a data-driven determination of the coupling functions, going beyond manual parameter tuning used in earlier works like Finazzo (2016) and Gursoy (2017). The findings provide concrete predictions for experimental validation at facilities such as FAIR, JPARC-HI, and NICA, and suggest that strong magnetic fields can induce multiple CEPs and violate conventional scaling relations, which could be tested in heavy-ion collision experiments.
