# Superfluidity in Two-Dimensional Imbalanced Fermi Gases

> We study the zero temperature ground state of a two-dimensional atomic Fermi gas with chemical potential and population imbalance in the mean-field approximation. All calculations are performed in terms of the two-body binding energy $ε_B$, whose variation allows to investigate the evolution from the BEC to the BCS regimes. By means of analytical and exact expressions we show that, similarly to what is found in three dimensions, at fixed chemical potentials, BCS is the ground state until the critical imbalance $h_c$ after which there is a first-order phase transition to the normal state. We find that $h_c$, the Chandrasekhar-Clogston limit of superfluidity, has the same value as in three dimensional systems. We show that for a fixed ratio $ε_B/ε_F$, where $ε_F$ is the two-dimensional Fermi energy, as the density imbalance $m$ is increased from zero, the ground state evolves from BCS to phase separation to the normal state. At the critical imbalance $m_c$ phase separation is not supported and the normal phase is energetically preferable. The BCS-BEC crossover is discussed in balanced and imbalanced configurations. Possible pictures of what may be found experimentally in these systems are also shown. We also investigate the necessary conditions for the existence of bound states in the balanced and imbalanced normal phase.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in a two-dimensional imbalanced Fermi gas at zero temperature, the Chandrasekhar-Clogston limit of superfluidity (the critical chemical potential imbalance \(h_c\) beyond which the BCS phase gives way to the normal phase) has the same value as in three-dimensional systems, namely \(h_c = \Delta_0 / \sqrt{2}\) in the BCS limit, where \(\Delta_0\) is the balanced-system gap. This result is derived within the mean-field approximation, neglecting Berezinskii-Kosterlitz-Thouless corrections, which are argued to be irrelevant at zero temperature.

## Method

The authors use a mean-field approximation to the pairing Hamiltonian for a two-dimensional nonrelativistic Fermi gas with attractive s-wave interactions and chemical potential imbalance. They derive an analytical expression for the zero-temperature grand canonical thermodynamic potential, regularizing the ultraviolet divergence using the two-body binding energy \(\epsilon_B\). They then analyze the stability of the BCS, Sarma (breached-pair), phase-separated, and normal states in both the grand canonical ensemble (fixed chemical potentials) and the canonical ensemble (fixed number densities), solving the gap and number equations self-consistently.

## Key numbers

*   The critical chemical potential imbalance (Chandrasekhar-Clogston limit) in the BCS limit is \(h_c = \Delta_0 / \sqrt{2}\), where \(\Delta_0\) is the gap of the balanced system.
*   The balanced-system gap is \(\Delta_0 = \sqrt{2 \epsilon_F |\epsilon_B|}\), where \(\epsilon_F\) is the two-dimensional Fermi energy and \(\epsilon_B\) is the two-body binding energy.
*   The balanced-system chemical potential is \(\mu_0 = \epsilon_F - |\epsilon_B|/2\).
*   The maximum density imbalance for the Sarma phase is \(m_{max} = M \Delta_0 / (2\pi)\), where \(M\) is the atomic mass.
*   The condition for bound states in the imbalanced normal phase is \(2\mu + |\epsilon_B| > 2h\), where \(\mu\) is the average chemical potential and \(h\) is the chemical potential imbalance.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of imbalanced Fermi gases from three dimensions (as studied by Caldas (2004) and Caldas (2005)) to two dimensions, finding that the Chandrasekhar-Clogston limit is dimension-independent in the mean-field approximation. The results are consistent with experimental observations of phase separation in three-dimensional trapped gases (Exp4, Exp5, Exp6, Exp7) and provide a theoretical framework for interpreting future experiments on two-dimensional imbalanced Fermi gases, such as those realized by confining gases in pancake potentials (Exp8, Exp9, Exp10, Exp11). The paper also discusses the BCS-BEC crossover in imbalanced systems, showing that the crossover path is destroyed above a critical density imbalance.
