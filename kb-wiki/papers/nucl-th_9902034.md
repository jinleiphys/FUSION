# Relativistic Treatment of Hypernuclear Decay

> We compute for the first time the decay width of lambda-hypernuclei in a relativistic mean-field approximation to the Walecka model. Due to the small mass difference between the lambda-hyperon and its decay products---a nucleon and a pion---the mesonic component of the decay is strongly Pauli blocked in the nuclear medium. Thus, the in-medium decay becomes dominated by the non-mesonic, or two-body, component of the decay. For this mode, the lambda-hyperon decays into a nucleon and a spacelike nuclear excitation. In this work we concentrate exclusively on the pion-like modes. By relying on the analytic structure of the nucleon and pion propagators, we express the non-mesonic component of the decay in terms of the spin-longitudinal response function. This response has been constrained from precise quasielastic (p,n) measurements done at LAMPF. We compute the spin-longitudinal response in a relativistic random-phase-approximation model that reproduces accurately the quasielastic data. By doing so, we obtain hypernuclear decay widths that are considerably smaller---by factors of two or three---relative to existing nonrelativistic calculations.

# Relativistic Treatment of Hypernuclear Decay

## Key claim

The authors compute hypernuclear decay widths in a relativistic mean-field model and find values that are two to three times smaller than existing nonrelativistic calculations, attributing this reduction to a dynamical quenching of the effective πNN coupling in the nuclear medium. This result depends critically on using self-consistent in-medium nucleon and lambda masses rather than free-space values.

## Method

The decay width of Λ-hypernuclei is computed in a relativistic mean-field approximation to the Walecka model, using the analytic structure of nucleon and pion propagators to express the non-mesonic decay component in terms of the spin-longitudinal response function. Ground-state properties of closed-shell nuclei are computed self-consistently, then the Dirac equation for a Λ-hyperon is solved with scalar and vector potentials scaled by 2/3 from the nucleon values, assuming the strange quark does not couple to sigma or omega mesons. The spin-longitudinal response is calculated in a relativistic random-phase-approximation (RPA) model that was previously used to reproduce quasielastic (p,n) measurements from LAMPF, and the nuclear-matter results are folded with the momentum distribution of the lowest Λ orbital to obtain finite-hypernuclear widths.

## Key numbers

- Free-space Λ decay width: Γ₀ = 2.490 × 10⁻¹² MeV
- For ⁴⁰Ca, the mesonic component relative to free-space value: 0.014 (strongly suppressed by Pauli blocking)
- Non-mesonic decay in ⁴⁰Ca with free-space masses (nonrelativistic approximation): Hartree contribution 0.834 Γ₀, RPA contribution 1.053 Γ₀
- Non-mesonic decay in ⁴⁰Ca with self-consistent in-medium masses (relativistic calculation): Hartree contribution 0.444 Γ₀, RPA contribution 0.544 Γ₀
- Total relativistic RPA width for ⁴⁰Ca: Γ/Γ₀ = 0.567, more than a factor of three smaller than the nonrelativistic value reported by Ramos, Oset, and Salcedo (1995)
- Weak ΛNπ coupling constant: g_w = 2.35 × 10⁻⁷
- Ratio of parity conserving to parity violating coupling: κ = -6.7
- Pseudovector πNN coupling strength: f_π²/4π = 0.0778
- Walecka model parameters: m_s = 520 MeV, g_s²/4π = 8.724, g_v²/4π = 15.154

## Context

This work extends earlier nonrelativistic calculations of hypernuclear decay by Ramos, Oset, and Salcedo (1995) and Alberico et al. (1999) into a relativistic framework, using the same RPA model that Horowitz and Piekarewicz (1993) successfully applied to describe the spin-longitudinal response extracted from quasielastic (p,n) measurements at LAMPF by McClelland et al. (1992) and Chen et al. (1993). The results indicate that existing nonrelativistic calculations overestimate the decay width by factors of two to three, and the computed widths are considerably smaller than experimental measurements by Szymanski et al. (1991), suggesting the need for inclusion of additional nuclear excitations beyond pion-like modes as previously advocated by Dubach et al. (1986, 1996) and Ramos et al. (1995).
