# Faster spectral density calculation using energy moments

> Accurate predictions of inclusive scattering cross sections in the linear response regime require efficient and controllable methods to calculate the spectral density in a strongly-correlated many-body system. In this work we reformulate the recently proposed Gaussian Integral Transform technique in terms of Fourier moments of the system Hamiltonian which can be computed efficiently on a quantum computer. One of the main advantages of this framework is that it allows for an important reduction of the computational cost by exploiting previous knowledge about the energy moments of the spectral density. For a simple model of medium mass nucleus like $^{40}$Ca and target energy resolution of $1$ MeV we find an expected speed-up of $\approx 125$ times for the calculation of the giant dipole response and of $\approx 50$ times for the simulation of quasi-elastic electron scattering at typical momentum transfers.

## Key claim

The authors reformulate the Gaussian Integral Transform (GIT) technique using a Fourier basis expansion, which allows prior knowledge of energy moments (especially the variance) of the spectral density to reduce the number of time-evolution expectation values needed on a quantum computer, achieving a speed-up of approximately 125 times for the giant dipole response in a model of ⁴⁰Ca and approximately 50 times for quasi-elastic electron scattering at a target energy resolution of 1 MeV.

## Method

The authors express the Gaussian integral kernel as a Fourier series with period \(P = \chi \|\hat{H}\|\), where \(\|\hat{H}\|\) is the spectral norm of the Hamiltonian, and truncate the series to a finite number of terms \(N\). They bound the periodic extension error \(\epsilon_P\) using Chebyshev's inequality applied to the known energy variance \(\sigma^2\) of the response function, which allows a smaller period \(P\) and thus fewer Fourier moments \(N\) than the general Shannon-Nyquist-based choice. The total error is decomposed into periodic extension error, truncation error, and statistical sampling error, each bounded separately.

## Key numbers

- For a model of ⁴⁰Ca with target energy resolution of 1 MeV, expected speed-up of ≈125 times for the giant dipole response and ≈50 times for quasi-elastic electron scattering at typical momentum transfers.
- The required number of Fourier moments scales as \(N = \mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{\Delta}\left(\frac{\Omega^{1/3}\sigma^{2/3}}{\varepsilon^{1/3}} + \delta\nu\right)\sqrt{\log(1/\Sigma)\log(\Omega/(\varepsilon\Delta))}\right)\) when using the first two energy moments, where \(\Delta\) is the energy resolution, \(\sigma\) is the energy variance, \(\varepsilon\) is the dimensionless error, \(\Omega\) is an energy scale, \(\Sigma\) is the kernel tail parameter, and \(\delta\nu\) is the frequency range width.
- For the general case without moment information, the number of terms scales as \(N = \widetilde{\mathcal{O}}\left(\frac{\|\hat{H}\|}{\Delta}\sqrt{\log(1/\Sigma)\log(\Omega/(\varepsilon\Delta))}\right)\).
- In numerical tests with \(\|\hat{H}\|=1\), \(\Delta=0.02\), \(\Sigma=0.01\), and target error \(\epsilon_P=0.01\), the improved bound on the period parameter \(\chi\) is \(\chi = \frac{2.7}{\varepsilon_P^{1/3}} \frac{\Omega^{1/3}\sigma^{2/3}}{\|\hat{H}\|} + \frac{\delta\nu}{\|\hat{H}\|}\), valid for \(\Lambda \leq 2\sigma\).

## Context

This work extends the Gaussian Integral Transform method (GIT) introduced by Roggero et al. (2020, 2022) by reformulating it in a Fourier basis rather than a Chebyshev polynomial basis, enabling the incorporation of prior knowledge of energy moments. The approach contrasts with the Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) method of Efros (1994, 2007) and the Kernel Polynomial Method of Weiße et al. (2006), and it provides rigorous error bounds unlike heuristic smoothing procedures such as the Maximum Entropy Method of Silver et al. (1990) and Burnier et al. (2013). The results enable more efficient quantum computation of inclusive scattering cross sections in nuclear physics, particularly for giant dipole resonances and quasi-elastic electron scattering, by reducing the number of time-evolution steps required when the spectral variance is much smaller than the spectral norm.
