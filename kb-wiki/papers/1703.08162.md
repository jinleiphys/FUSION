# Effects of magnetic field on the plasma evolution in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Very strong magnetic fields can arise in non-central heavy-ion collisions at ultrarelativistic energies, which may not decay quickly in a conducting plasma. We carry out relativistic magnetohydrodynamics (RMHD) simulations to study the effects of this magnetic field on the evolution of the plasma and on resulting flow fluctuations in the ideal RMHD limit. Our results show that magnetic field leads to enhancement in elliptic flow for small impact parameters while it suppresses it for large impact parameters (which may provide a signal for initial stage magnetic field). Interestingly, we find that magnetic field in localized regions can temporarily increase in time as evolving plasma energy density fluctuations lead to reorganization of magnetic flux. This can have important effects on chiral magnetic effect. Magnetic field has non-trivial effects on the power spectrum of flow fluctuations. For very strong magnetic field case one sees a pattern of even-odd difference in the power spectrum of flow coefficients arising from reflection symmetry about the magnetic field direction if initial state fluctuations are not dominant. We discuss the situation of nontrivial magnetic field configurations arising from collision of deformed nuclei and show that it can lead to anomalous elliptic flow. Special (crossed body-body) configurations of deformed nuclei collision can lead to presence of quadrupolar magnetic field which can have very important effects on the rapidity dependence of transverse expansion (similar to {\it beam focusing} from quadrupole fields in accelerators).

## Key claim

The paper claims that magnetic fields in relativistic heavy-ion collisions can either enhance or suppress elliptic flow depending on impact parameter, and that localized magnetic field strengths can temporarily increase over time due to energy density fluctuations reorganizing magnetic flux, contrary to the simpler expectation that magnetic fields only decay.

## Method

The authors performed 3+1 dimensional ideal relativistic magnetohydrodynamics (RMHD) simulations on a lattice of size 200 x 200 x 200 (and 150 x 150 x 150 for some cases) using a leapfrog algorithm with second-order accuracy. They used Glauber-like initial conditions for energy density profiles with an ideal gas equation of state for QGP, and initial magnetic field profiles from two oppositely moving uniformly charged spheres representing colliding nuclei, with evolution times up to 3 fm to avoid boundary effects.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field strength at earliest stages: of order 10^15 Tesla (few m_pi^2)
- Initial central temperature range: 160 - 180 MeV
- Magnetic field profile calculated at B_time = 0.4 fm and 0.6 fm after collision
- For very strong magnetic field case, peak value used: of order 15 m_pi^2
- Lattice size: 200 x 200 x 200 (and 150 x 150 x 150 for some cases)
- Evolution time: up to 3 fm
- Nuclear radius used: about 3 fm to 4.5 fm (copper nucleus)
- Impact parameter used for enhancement demonstration: 4 fm
- For low energy collisions: sqrt(s) = 20 GeV
- Maximum velocity assumed for longitudinal expansion: 0.7 at maximum z value

## Context

This work extends earlier arguments by some of the same authors (Das et al., 2015) that magnetic field could enhance elliptic flow by up to about 30% based on directional dependence of sound velocity, and agrees with results from Tuchin (2015) on magnetic field dependence of elliptic flow. It contrasts with other recent RMHD simulations (unnamed authors, 2016) which found no effect of magnetic field on elliptic flow, and the present work reconciles these discrepancies by showing that the effect depends sensitively on impact parameter and magnetic field profile relative to plasma profile. The results enable future searches for signatures of early-stage magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions and suggest that flow pattern understanding requires including magnetic field effects, with implications for the chiral magnetic effect and possible vorticity generation.
