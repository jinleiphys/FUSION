# Barrier penetration in a discrete basis formalism

> A standard way to solve a Schrödinger equation is to discreteize the radial coordinates and apply a numerical method for a differential equation, such as the Runge-Kutta method or the Numerov method. Here I employ a discrete basis formalism based on a finite mesh method as a simpler alternative, with which the numerical computation can be easily implemented by ordinary linear algebra operations. I compare the numerical convergence of the Numerov integration method to the finite mesh method for calculating penetrabilities of a one-dimensional potential barrier, and show that the latter approach has better convergence properties.

# Barrier penetration in a discrete basis formalism

## Key claim
The discrete-basis formalism (finite mesh method) provides better numerical convergence and stability for calculating penetrabilities of a one-dimensional potential barrier compared to the standard Numerov method, even though the discrete-basis formalism uses only a simple 3-point formula for the kinetic energy while the Numerov method uses a more sophisticated formula. However, the mesh spacing Δx in the discrete-basis formalism cannot exceed √(2ħ²/Em) because the wave number k becomes undefined beyond that limit.

## Method
The author discretizes the radial coordinate into mesh points and constructs a Hamiltonian matrix using a 3-point finite difference formula for the kinetic energy. The penetrability is computed by solving the resulting linear algebra problem with appropriate boundary conditions, using the Green's function G = (H̃ - E)⁻¹ where H̃ includes an outgoing wave boundary condition at the rightmost mesh point. The method is applied to a one-dimensional Gaussian barrier and compared with the standard Numerov method and a modified Numerov method.

## Key numbers
- Gaussian barrier parameters: V₀ = 100 MeV, s = 2 fm, with mass m = 29m_N (where m_N is the nucleon mass), chosen to mimic the fusion reaction of ⁵⁸Ni+⁵⁸Ni following Dasso (1983) and Hagino (2004).
- With mesh size Δx = 0.05 fm, both the Numerov method and discrete-basis formalism yield accurate results.
- With mesh size Δx = 0.15 fm, the standard Numerov method fails to reach penetrability of unity even at energies well above the barrier, and the modified Numerov method produces penetrabilities exceeding unity with non-monotonic behavior.
- The discrete-basis formalism with Δx = 0.15 fm produces results nearly identical to those obtained with Δx = 0.05 fm.
- The mesh spacing is bounded by Δx < √(2ħ²/Em) to maintain a real wave number k.

## Context
This work extends the discrete-basis formalism previously applied to induced fission problems by FBA18, BY2019, YBF2020, YBF2021, and BH2023, and tests its applicability to scattering problems. The method is related to the 3D mesh method used in nuclear density functional theory by Davies (1980) and others. The results suggest the method may be useful for obtaining numerically stable solutions of coupled-channels equations, as discussed by Hagino (2022) and Hagino (2012), and for microscopic descriptions of induced fission with absorbing potentials.
