# Spectral functions of heavy quarkonia in a bulk-viscous quark gluon plasma

> We study the properties of quarkonia inside a bulk-viscous quark gluon plasma. The non-equilibrium nature of the medium is encoded in the deformed distribution functions of thermal quarks and gluons, with which we compute the dielectric permittivity within the hard thermal loop approximation at one-loop. The modified dielectric permittivity is used to calculate the in-medium heavy quark potential, and using the potential we compute spectral functions, which reflect the physical properties of heavy quarkonia. We discuss how the bulk viscous effect influences quantities such as binding energies and thermal widths. Based on those properties, we discuss the implications of the bulk viscous effect on the physical observables such as $ ψ' $ to $ J/ψ$ ratio and the nuclear modification factor, $R_{AA}$. In particular, we argue that the nuclear modification factors of excited and ground states show different sensitivities to the bulk viscous nature of a plasma, which is potentially useful for the critical point search.

## Key claim

The paper argues that bulk viscous corrections to the quark-gluon plasma, quantified by a parameter Φ, modify the in-medium heavy quark potential and spectral functions of quarkonia, and that the nuclear modification factors of excited and ground states show different sensitivities to bulk viscosity, which could be useful for the critical point search in the beam energy scan program.

## Method

The authors incorporate bulk viscous corrections by deforming the quasiparticle distribution functions of thermal quarks and gluons, then compute the dielectric permittivity within the hard thermal loop (HTL) approximation at one-loop using a quasiparticle model with temperature- and chemical potential-dependent masses. The modified dielectric permittivity is used to calculate the in-medium heavy quark potential via linear response theory from the Cornell potential, and spectral functions are computed by solving a Schrödinger equation with the complex potential using the Fourier space method of Burnier (2007).

## Key numbers

- Debye masses are computed for temperatures from approximately 160 MeV to 500 MeV, with the critical temperature taken as T_c = 160 MeV; the quasiparticle mass parameters are λ = 4.17 and T_s/T_c = -2.96
- The Cornell potential parameters used are α = 0.513 and string tension σ = (0.412 GeV)^2, with a constant shift c = -0.161 GeV
- The coupling constant uses Λ = 176 MeV and M ≈ 3.7 T in the one-loop expression
- Spectral functions are shown for temperatures T = 170, 200, and 350 MeV for different values of the bulk viscous parameter Φ (0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3)
- The continuum threshold energy is defined as E_cont = 2m_Q + ReV(r → ∞), and resonance masses of Υ(1S), Υ'(2S), Υ'(3S), J/ψ(1S), and ψ'(2S) are extracted as functions of temperature and Φ

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Thakur 2020) by using a quasiparticle model with temperature-dependent masses instead of massless thermal particles, and by computing spectral functions via the Fourier space method of Burnier (2007) rather than solving the Schrödinger equation with only the real part of the potential. The results are compared with lattice QCD data from Lafferty (2019) for Debye masses, and the approach builds on prior work on momentum-space anisotropies from viscous effects by Strickland (2011, 2013, 2014) and others. The study enables future investigation of how bulk viscosity affects experimental observables such as the ψ'/J/ψ ratio and R_AA, which are relevant for the critical point search in heavy ion collisions.
