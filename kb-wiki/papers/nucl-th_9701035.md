# The Deconfinement Phase Transition in Asymmetric Matter

> We study the phase transition of asymmetric hadronic matter to a quark-gluon plasma within the framework of a simple two-phase model. The analysis is performed in a system with two conserved charges (baryon number and isospin) using the stability conditions on the free energy, the conservation laws and Gibbs' criteria for phase equilibrium. The EOS is obtained in a separate description for the hadronic phase and for the quark-gluon plasma. For the hadrons, a relativistic mean-field model calibrated to the properties of nuclear matter is used, and a bag-model type EOS is used for the quarks and gluons. The model is applied to the deconfinement phase transition that may occur in matter created in ultra-relativistic collisions of heavy ions. Based on the two-dimensional coexistence surface (binodal), various phase separation scenarios and the Maxwell construction through the mixed phase are discussed. In the framework of the two-phase model the phase transition in asymmetric matter is continuous (second-order by Ehrenfest's definition) in contrast to the discontinuous (first-order) transition of symmetric systems.

# The Deconfinement Phase Transition in Asymmetric Matter

## Key claim

Within a simple two-phase model using separate equations of state for hadronic matter and quark-gluon plasma, the deconfinement phase transition in asymmetric matter (with nonzero isospin) is continuous and second-order by Ehrenfest's definition, in contrast to the discontinuous first-order transition found in symmetric systems. This result is conditional on the two-phase model framework and the specific choice of hadronic and quark-gluon equations of state, and the authors note that the actual nature of the transition can only be established by full QCD lattice calculations.

## Method

The authors construct a two-phase model with two conserved charges (baryon number and isospin), using Gibbs' criteria for phase equilibrium and stability conditions on the free energy. The hadronic phase is described by a relativistic mean-field model with Dirac nucleons, isoscalar scalar and vector meson fields, isovector rho meson and pion fields, calibrated to nuclear matter properties; the quark-gluon phase uses a bag-model equation of state with two massless flavors including first-order corrections in the strong coupling constant alpha_s. The binodal (two-dimensional coexistence surface) is computed in the space of pressure, temperature, and isospin ratio, and Maxwell constructions through the mixed phase are performed for various thermodynamic processes.

## Key numbers

- Bag constant: b = 160 MeV/fm^3
- Transition temperature at zero baryon density: 150 MeV (used for calibration)
- Strong coupling constant alpha_s = 0.349 for zeta = 0, changing by only 0.1% when zeta is varied within the natural range (0 to 0.06)
- At T_c = 150 MeV, the two binodal branches coalesce at constant pressure p = 19.08 MeV/fm^3 with isospin ratios ranging from approximately x = -4.5 to x = 0
- The natural range for the quartic vector meson coupling zeta is 0 to 0.06

## Context

This work extends the analysis of phase transitions in asymmetric systems from the liquid-gas phase transition of nuclear matter (studied by BB80 and HMUE95) to the deconfinement phase transition. It builds on earlier studies of quark cores in neutron stars by Collins and Perry (1975) and Baym and Chin (1976), and on Glendenning's (1992) analysis of the transition in systems with two conserved charges at zero temperature for neutron star matter. The authors note that lattice QCD calculations at finite baryon density remain at an early stage without definite conclusions (Kanaya, 1996), and that no lattice calculations are available for asymmetric systems, so this two-phase model provides a first orientation for the qualitative features of the phase diagram at finite baryon density and isospin. The results suggest that the transition region is more likely reached in collisions of very neutron-rich nuclei, since the density at the onset of the transition is always lower in asymmetric matter.
