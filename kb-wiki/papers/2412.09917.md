# Improving the predictive power of empirical shell-model Hamiltonians

> We present two developments which enhance the predictive power of empirical shell-model Hamiltonians for cases in which calibration data are sparse. A recent improvement in the ab initio derivation of effective Hamiltonians leads to a much better starting point for the optimization procedure. In addition, we introduce a protocol to avoid overfitting, enabling a more reliable extrapolation beyond available data. These developments will enable more robust predictions for exotic isotopes produced at rare isotope beam facilities and in astrophysical environments.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that using an improved ab initio starting Hamiltonian from the IMSRG(3f2) method, combined with a training/testing data partitioning scheme to avoid overfitting, yields empirical shell-model Hamiltonians that require fewer phenomenological adjustments and provide more reliable extrapolations when calibration data are sparse.

## Method

The authors start with valence-space Hamiltonians derived using the VS-IMSRG method with the EM 1.8/2.0 NN+3N interaction, comparing the standard IMSRG(2) approximation with the newer IMSRG(3f2) factorized approximation that incorporates intermediate three-body operators. They apply singular-value decomposition (SVD) fitting to optimize linear combinations of single-particle energies and two-body matrix elements against experimental data for nuclei between 78Ni and 100Sn in the πj4 model space (4 SPE and 65 TBME). To assess predictive power, they randomly partition the 189 data points (22 ground-state binding energies and 167 excitation energies) into training (80%) and testing (20%) sets, repeating the sampling 2000 times for each number of varied linear combinations.

## Key numbers

- The IMSRG(3f2) starting point requires approximately 55 varied linear combinations before the TBME-RMSD between fitted and ab initio Hamiltonians reaches 200 keV, compared to only 6 VLC for the IMSRG(2) starting point
- The energy-RMSD for the fitted data set falls substantially after only 1 VLC for IMSRG(3f2), while IMSRG(2) requires nearly 15 VLC to reach the same level of accuracy
- The optimal predictive power is achieved at about 35 VLC for both starting Hamiltonians
- For extrapolation to neutron-rich nuclei (Z ≤ 36) using training data only from Z > 36, the RMSD reaches approximately 300 keV at 35 VLC
- The J=0, T=1 TBME are about 30 percent weaker for IMSRG(3f2) compared to those from IMSRG(2)

## Context

This work extends the SVD-based fitting approach previously used to develop empirical Hamiltonians such as USDA/B (sd model space), GPFX1A (fp model space), and JUN45/jj44b (jj44 model space), which achieve RMSD of 150-200 keV when abundant data are available. The paper addresses the challenge of sparse data for exotic nuclei relevant to rare isotope beam facilities and r-process nucleosynthesis, where overfitting becomes a concern. The improved IMSRG(3f2) method from He (2024) provides a more realistic starting point, and the training/testing validation protocol enables more robust extrapolation to regions where experimental data are limited.
