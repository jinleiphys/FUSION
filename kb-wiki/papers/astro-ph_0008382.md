# On Solar System and Cosmic Rays Nucleosynthesis and Spallation Processes

> A brief survey of nuclide abundances in the solar-system and in cosmic rays and of the believed mechanisms of their synthesis is given. The role of spallation processes in nucleosynthesis is discussed. A short review of recent measurements, compilations, calculations, and evaluations of spallation cross sections relevant to nuclear astrophysics is given as well. It is shown that in some past astrophysical simulations, old experimental nuclear data and theoretical cross sections that are in poor agreement with recent measurements and calculations were used. New astrophysical simulations using recently measured and reliably calculated nuclear cross sections, further researches in obtaining better cross sections, and production of evaluated spallation cross sections libraries for astrophysics are suggested.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that some past astrophysical simulations used old experimental nuclear data and theoretical cross sections that are in poor agreement with recent measurements and calculations, and that new simulations using updated cross sections, along with further research and evaluated spallation cross section libraries, are needed to improve understanding of nucleosynthesis.

## Method

The paper surveys nuclide abundances in the solar system and cosmic rays, reviews the believed mechanisms of their synthesis (Big-Bang nucleosynthesis, stellar nucleosynthesis, and spallation processes), and discusses recent measurements, compilations, calculations, and evaluations of spallation cross sections relevant to nuclear astrophysics. It highlights specific nuclear reaction networks used in BBN calculations, ranging from 12 to over 22 reaction chains, and compares older abundance data (Suess and Urey 1956) with newer data (Lang 1980).

## Key numbers

- Baryon density parameter from BBN: $\Omega_B h^2 = 0.019 \pm 0.0024$ (95% confidence level), derived from deuterium abundance measurements by Burles and Tytler (1998a,b)
- Number of massless neutrino species constrained to $1.7 \le N_{\nu} \le 4.3$ at 95% CL from likelihood analysis on $^4$He and $^7$Li (Cassco 1998); refined to $N_{\nu} \le 3.4$ for $Y_{\rm{p}} = 0.242$ and $N_{\nu} \le 3.8$ for $Y_{\rm{p}} = 0.252$ (Copi, Schramm, and Turner 1997)
- Cosmic ray abundances shown for 70-280 MeV/nucleon energies (Simpson 1983)
- BBN begins at $t \simeq 15$ s after the Big Bang when temperature decreases to $T \simeq 3 \times 10^9$ K
- Deuterium binding energy: 2.2 MeV, corresponding to $T \sim 2.5 \times 10^{10}$ K
- Neutron-proton balance at BBN: 13% neutrons and 87% protons
- Carbon burning occurs at temperatures greater than $8 \times 10^8$ K; oxygen burning at about $2 \times 10^9$ K
- Silicon photodisintegration begins at temperatures greater than $3 \times 10^9$ K
- Baryon density relation: $\rho_B = 6.88 \eta \times 10^{-22}$ g cm$^{-3}$ (Burles et al. 1999)
- Critical density: $\rho_c = 1.88 \times 10^{-29} h^2$ g cm$^{-3}$

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957) (the "B$^2$FH" bible of nuclear astrophysics) and updates the understanding of nucleosynthesis mechanisms using more recent abundance compilations such as Lang (1980) and the NACRE reaction rate compilation (Angulo et al. 1999). The paper identifies open questions including the slope of [Be/H] and [B/H] versus [Fe/H] in halo stars (Crosas and Weisheit 1996; Duncan et al. 1992), uncertainties in resonance parameters in $^{16}$O (Salpeter 1999), and the need for better spallation cross section data for astrophysical simulations. It enables future work by recommending new astrophysical simulations using updated nuclear cross sections and the production of evaluated spallation cross section libraries.
