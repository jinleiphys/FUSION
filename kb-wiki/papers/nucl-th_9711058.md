# Information entropy as a measure of the quality of a nuclear density distribution

> The information entropy of a nuclear density distribution is calculated for a number of nuclei. Various phenomenological models for the density distribution using different geometry are employed. Nuclear densities calculated within various microscopic mean field approaches are also employed. It turns out that the entropy increases on going from crude phenomenological models to more sophisticated (microscopic) ones. It is concluded that the larger the information entropy, the better the quality of the nuclear density distribution. An alternative approach is also examined: the net information content i.e. the sum of information entropies in position and momentum space $S_{r}+S_{k}$. It is indicated that $S_{r}+S_{k}$ is a maximum, when the best fit to experimental data of the density and momentum distributions is attained.

# Information entropy as a measure of the quality of a nuclear density distribution

## Key claim

The information entropy S of a nuclear density distribution increases as one moves from crude phenomenological models to more sophisticated microscopic ones, and the larger the entropy, the better the quality of the density distribution; however, this trend saturates once a certain degree of model sophistication is reached, so the entropy stops increasing and the criterion becomes less discriminating for the most advanced models.

## Method

The authors computed the information entropy S (following the Ghosh-Berkowitz-Parr phase-space formalism) for nuclear matter densities of several nuclei using six types of input: uniform, trapezoidal, two-parameter Fermi, harmonic oscillator shell model, semiphenomenological single-particle densities (SP-D) of Gambhir and Patil, and densities from microscopic mean-field approaches (Skyrme DDHF with SkM* and RMF with NL3). They also computed the sum of position-space and momentum-space entropies S_r + S_k (following the Gadre-Bendale approach) for 4He, 16O, and 40Ca using natural-orbital expansions of the one-body density matrix with three different single-particle basis sets (harmonic oscillator, square well, and modified harmonic oscillator).

## Key numbers

- For 208Pb, the information entropy S increases from 1295.99 (uniform model) to 1304.57 (trapezoidal), 1304.91 (SP-D), 1305.99 (Fermi), 1305.81 (DDHF), and 1306.20 (RMF).
- For 40Ca, S increases from 249.23 (uniform) to 252.59 (trapezoidal), 253.04 (HO), 253.08 (SP-D), 253.18 (Fermi), 253.11 (DDHF), and 253.17 (RMF).
- For 4He, the sum S_r + S_k is 11.17 for the square-well basis (best fit to experiment), compared to 9.33 for harmonic oscillator and 9.38 for modified harmonic oscillator.
- The relative increase in S_r + S_k due to short-range correlations (from prior work) ranges from 2.5% for 4He to 1.2% for 40Ca.

## Context

This work extends to nuclear systems the information-entropy quality criterion previously applied to electronic densities by Ghosh, Berkowitz, and Parr (1984) and by Gadre and Bendale (1985, 1987), who found that entropy increases with wavefunction quality in atoms. It also builds on an earlier nuclear application by Panos (1997) that calculated S_r + S_k for two phenomenological models. The results suggest that the Fermi distribution and the SP-D density (which incorporates separation energies and correct asymptotic behavior) are of comparable quality to Skyrme DDHF and RMF densities, while also indicating that more experimental momentum-distribution data are needed to fully validate the S_r + S_k criterion for heavier nuclei.
