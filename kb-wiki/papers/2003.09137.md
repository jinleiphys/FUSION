# How machine learning conquers the unitary limit

> Machine learning has become a premier tool in physics and other fields of science. It has been shown that the quantum mechanical scattering problem can not only be solved with such techniques, but it was argued that the underlying neural network develops the Born series for shallow potentials. However, classical machine learning algorithms fail in the unitary limit of an infinite scattering length and vanishing effective range parameters. The unitary limit plays an important role in our understanding of bound strongly interacting fermionic systems and can be realized in cold atom experiments. Here, we develop a formalism that explains the unitary limit in terms of what we define as unitary limit surfaces. This not only allows to investigate the unitary limit geometrically in potential space, but also provides a numerically simple approach towards unnaturally large scattering lengths with standard multilayer perceptrons. Its scope is therefore not limited to applications in nuclear and atomic physics, but includes all systems that exhibit an unnaturally large scale.

## Key claim

The paper develops a formalism that explains the unitary limit (infinite scattering length) in terms of "unitary limit surfaces" in potential space, and shows that standard multilayer perceptrons can predict unnaturally large scattering lengths by first learning two regular geometric quantities \(f\) and \(b_0\), rather than attempting to learn the singular scattering length directly.

## Method

The authors discretize spherically symmetric attractive potentials into vectors \(\bm{U} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\) with \(d=64\) and define the first unitary limit surface \(\Sigma_1\) as the set of potentials supporting a zero-energy bound state. They train an ensemble of 100 multilayer perceptrons (each with nine CELU-activated \(64 \times 64\) hidden layers) to predict the scaling factor \(f\) that maps a given potential onto \(\Sigma_1\), and a second ensemble of 100 MLPs (five hidden layers) to predict the regular quantity \(b_0 = a_0(1-f)\). The scattering length is then obtained as the quotient \(\mathcal{A}(\bm{U}) = \mathcal{B}(\bm{U}) / (1 - \mathcal{F}(\bm{U}))\).

## Key numbers

* The ensemble \(\mathcal{F}\) predicting the scaling factor \(f\) achieved a mean average percentage error (MAPE) of \(0.028\%\) on the test set of \(2.9 \times 10^3\) samples.
* The ensemble \(\mathcal{B}\) predicting the regular quantity \(b_0\) achieved a MAPE of \(0.017\%\) on the same test set.
* The final quotient \(\mathcal{A}\) predicting scattering lengths achieved a MAPE of \(0.41\%\) on the test set.
* Training data contained \(3 \times 10^4\) samples; test data contained \(2.9 \times 10^3\) samples.
* The ensemble for \(f\) used \(N_{\mathcal{F}} = 100\) MLPs; the ensemble for \(b_0\) used \(N_{\mathcal{B}} = 100\) MLPs.
* For square-well potentials, relative errors of \(\mathcal{A}\) ranged between \(1\%\) and \(15\%\) in the immediate vicinity of the unitary limit at depth \(u = \pi^2/4\).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Wu (2018), who showed that multilayer perceptrons develop the Born series for shallow potentials but fail at the unitary limit. The formalism of unitary limit surfaces provides a geometric interpretation of the singularity and enables machine-learning prediction of scattering lengths in the strongly interacting regime relevant to cold atom experiments (Grimm) and Efimov physics (Efimov 1970). The method generalizes to higher unitary limit surfaces and to other effective-range expansion parameters, and is not limited to nuclear or atomic physics but applies to any system with an unnaturally large scale.
