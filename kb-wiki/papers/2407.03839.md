# Revealing the nature of yrast states in neutron-rich polonium isotopes

> Polonium isotopes having two protons above the shell closure at $Z=82$ show a wide variety of low-lying high-spin isomeric states across the whole chain. The structure of neutron-deficient isotopes up to $^{210}$Po ($N=126$) is well established as they are easily produced through various methods. However, there is not much information available for the neutron-rich counterparts for which only selective techniques can be used for their production. We report on the first fast-timing measurements of yrast states up to the 8$^+$ level in $^{214,216,218}$Po isotopes produced in the $β^-$ decay of $^{214,216,218}$Bi at ISOLDE, CERN. In particular, our new half-life value of 607(14) ps for the 8$_1^+$ state in $^{214}$Po is nearly 20 times shorter than the one available in literature and comparable with the newly measured half-lives of 409(16) and 628(25) ps for the corresponding 8$_1^+$ states in $^{216,218}$Po, respectively. The measured $B(E2;8_1^+ \to 6_1^+)$ transition probability values follow an increasing trend relative to isotope mass, reaching a maximum for $^{216}$Po. The increase contradicts the previous claims of isomerism for the $8^+$ yrast states in neutron-rich $^{214}$Po and beyond. Together with the other measured yrast transitions, the $B(E2)$ values provide a crucial test of the different theoretical approaches describing the underlying configurations of the yrast band. The new experimental results are compared to shell-model calculations using the KHPE and H208 effective interactions and their pairing modified versions, showing an increase in configuration mixing when moving towards the heavier isotopes.

## Key claim

The new half-life measurement of 607(14) ps for the 8₁⁺ state in ²¹⁴Po is nearly 20 times shorter than the previously reported value of 13(1) ns, contradicting earlier claims of isomerism for the 8⁺ yrast states in neutron-rich ²¹⁴Po and beyond, and showing that the measured B(E2;8₁⁺→6₁⁺) values follow an increasing trend with isotope mass reaching a maximum for ²¹⁶Po.

## Method

Yrast states up to the 8⁺ level in ²¹⁴,²¹⁶,²¹⁸Po were populated via β⁻ decay of ²¹⁴,²¹⁶,²¹⁸Bi produced at ISOLDE, CERN using a 1.4 GeV proton beam on a UCₓ target with RILIS ionization. Half-lives were measured using the βγγ(t) fast-timing method with a plastic scintillator β detector, four HPGe Clover detectors, and two LaBr₃(Ce) detectors, employing both decay slope and centroid-shift methods. The experimental B(E2) values were compared to shell-model calculations using the H208 and KHPE effective interactions and their pairing-modified versions (H208-m and KHPE-m), where the diagonal and off-diagonal 1h₉/₂ proton-pairing matrix elements were reduced by 100 keV (H208-m) or 200 keV (KHPE-m) to improve agreement.

## Key numbers

- Half-life of 8₁⁺ state in ²¹⁴Po: 607(14) ps, compared to previous literature value of 13(1) ns
- Half-life of 8₁⁺ state in ²¹⁶Po: 409(16) ps
- Half-life of 8₁⁺ state in ²¹⁸Po: 628(25) ps
- B(E2;8₁⁺→6₁⁺) in ²¹⁴Po: 11.3(3) W.u., compared to previous literature value of 0.54(4) W.u.
- B(E2;8₁⁺→6₁⁺) in ²¹⁶Po: 24(1) W.u.
- B(E2;8₁⁺→6₁⁺) in ²¹⁸Po: 7.8(3) W.u.
- Half-life of 8₂⁺ state in ²¹⁴Po: 73(7) ps
- Half-life of 8₂⁺ state in ²¹⁶Po: 155(14) ps
- Production yields: ²¹⁴Bi >2×10⁴ ions/μC, ²¹⁶Bi 1.5×10³ ions/μC, ²¹⁸Bi 2×10² ions/μC

## Context

This work extends the study of yrast states in neutron-rich polonium isotopes beyond the well-characterized neutron-deficient isotopes up to ²¹⁰Po (N=126), where previous studies by Astier (2011) had reported a 13(1) ns half-life for the 8⁺ state in ²¹⁴Po and proposed similar isomers in ²¹⁶,²¹⁸Po based on one-neutron-pair breaking. The new fast-timing measurements contradict those isomerism claims and, together with shell-model calculations using the H208 interaction from Naïdja (2019, 2021) and the Kuo-Herling interaction modified by Warburton and Brown (1991), reveal increasing configuration mixing when moving towards heavier isotopes, with the neutron 1g₉/₂ and 0i₁₁/₂ orbitals playing increasingly important roles alongside the proton 1h₉/₂ configuration.
