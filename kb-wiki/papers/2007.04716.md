# Gravitational-wave Signature of a First-order Quantum Chromodynamics Phase Transition in Core-Collapse Supernovae

> A first-order quantum chromodynamics (QCD) phase transition (PT) may take place in the protocompact star (PCS) produced by a core-collapse supernova (CCSN). In this work, we study the consequences of such a PT in a non-rotating CCSN with axisymmetric hydrodynamic simulations. We find that the PT leads to the collapse of the PCS and results in a loud burst of gravitational waves (GWs). The amplitude of this GW burst is $\sim30$ times larger than the post-bounce GW signal normally found for non-rotating CCSN. It shows a broad peak at high frequencies ($\sim2500-4000$ Hz) in the spectrum, has a duration of $\lesssim5 {\rm ms}$, and carries $\sim3$ orders of magnitude more energy than the other episodes. Also, the peak frequency of the PCS oscillation increases dramatically after the PT-induced collapse. In addition to a second neutrino burst, the GW signal, if detected by the ground-based GW detectors, is decisive evidence of the first-order QCD PT inside CCSNe and provides key information about the structure and dynamics of the PCS.

## Key claim

A first-order QCD phase transition in a non-rotating core-collapse supernova produces a gravitational-wave burst with amplitude roughly 30 times larger than the standard post-bounce signal, but this louder burst is not necessarily easier to detect because current ground-based detectors have increasing noise at the burst's high frequencies (approximately 2500–4000 Hz).

## Method

The authors performed two-dimensional axisymmetric hydrodynamic simulations of a 12-solar-mass progenitor using the FLASH code with an M1 neutrino transport scheme. They employed a hybrid equation of state combining the STOS hadronic EOS with the MIT bag model for quarks (bag constant B = (165 MeV)^4) using Gibbs construction, and compared results against simulations using only the STOS hadronic EOS. Gravitational waves were extracted via the standard quadrupole formula, and general-relativistic approximations were included through a lapse function in the Euler equations.

## Key numbers

- GW burst amplitude reaches h_+ = 10^{-20} at a source distance of 10 kpc, approximately 30 times larger than other emission episodes
- GW burst duration is less than 5 ms
- Burst carries approximately 4.6 × 10^{-7} M_sun c^2 of energy, about 3 orders of magnitude more than other episodes
- Burst spectrum shows a broad peak at 2500–4000 Hz
- Central density at second collapse reaches 1.5 × 10^{15} g cm^{-3} (approximately 6 times nuclear saturation density)
- Pure quark phase appears at densities above approximately 3.5 times nuclear saturation density
- Explosion energy after PT-induced bounce is approximately 2.0 × 10^{50} erg
- Single-detector SNR of 8 yields detection radius of 22 kpc including the burst, 12 kpc excluding it
- Second bounce occurs at approximately t_b + 287 ms (t_b ≈ 151 ms after core collapse)

## Context

This work extends spherically-symmetric simulations by Sagert (2009) and Fischer (2018) that studied QCD phase transitions in CCSNe, adding multi-dimensional gravitational-wave predictions. The results contrast with standard non-rotating CCSN simulations using purely hadronic equations of state (such as those by Ott, 2013) which produce much weaker GW signals at lower frequencies. The findings enable future searches for high-frequency GW bursts from galactic CCSNe and motivate improvements in detector sensitivity at several kHz for next-generation instruments like the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer.
