# Isotopic dependence of fusion cross sections in reactions with heavy nuclei

> The dependence of fusion cross section on the isotopic composition of colliding nuclei is analysed within the dinuclear system concept for compound nucleus formation. Probabilities of fusion and surviving probabilities, ingredients of the evaporation residue cross sections, depend decisively on the neutron numbers of the dinuclear system. Evaporation residue cross sections for the production of actinides and superheavy nuclei, listed in table form, are discussed and compared with existing experimental data. Neutron-rich radioactive projectiles are shown to lead to similar fusion cross sections as stable projectiles.

# Isotopic dependence of fusion cross sections in reactions with heavy nuclei

## Key claim
The evaporation residue cross section depends weakly on the isotopic composition of colliding nuclei in Pb-based reactions because the increase in surviving probability with neutron richness is compensated by a decreasing fusion probability; neutron-rich radioactive projectiles lead to similar fusion cross sections as stable projectiles, but the authors note their calculated cross sections are "optimistic estimates" and that using alternative fission barriers yields even smaller values.

## Method
The authors apply the dinuclear system (DNS) concept, where compound nucleus formation proceeds via nucleon transfer in a touching configuration rather than adiabatic melting along the relative distance. They calculate the fusion probability using a two-dimensional Kramers-type expression (quasi-stationary solution of the Fokker-Planck equation) with rates for fusion through the mass-asymmetry barrier and quasi-fission through the radial barrier, and compute surviving probabilities using Möller-Nix microscopic corrections for fission barriers and neutron binding energies.

## Key numbers
- For the reaction $^{70}$Zn+$^{208}$Pb$\to ^{277}$112+$1n$, $\sigma_{ER}\approx 1$ pb, described as "practically on the level of the present experimental possibilities"
- For reactions $^{74,76}$Ge+$^{208}$Pb$\to ^{283,281}$114+$1n$, $\sigma_{ER}$ is expected to be smaller than 0.2 pb
- For $Z=116$ and 118 elements formed in $^{84}$Se, $^{86}$Kr+$^{208}$Pb reactions, $\sigma_{ER}$ values are of the order of 0.01 pb
- Surviving probabilities $W_{sur}$ are about $10^{-3}-10^{-4}$ for nuclei with $Z=104-113$ and about $10^{-2}$ for nuclei with $Z=114, 116, 118$
- For the reaction $^{132}$Sn + $^{132}$Sn$\to ^{261}$Fm+$3n$, the DNS model gives $P_{CN}\approx 10^{-9}$ and $\sigma_{ER}\approx 30$ fb (three orders of magnitude smaller than the adiabatic model prediction of $P_{CN}\approx 10^{-6}$ and $\sigma_{ER}\approx 30$ pb)
- Experimental difference of about 3 orders of magnitude in $\sigma_{ER}$ between $^{86}$Kr+$^{130}$Xe and $^{86}$Kr+$^{136}$Xe reactions
- For the $^{62}$Ni+$^{208}$Pb reaction, the experimental value is $\sigma_{1n}=3.5^{+2.7}_{-1.8}$ pb

## Context
This work extends the DNS concept developed by the authors and collaborators (Adamian et al., 1997; Adamian et al., 1998) to systematically study isotopic trends in fusion cross sections for heavy and superheavy element production. It contradicts adiabatic fusion models (e.g., those of P. Moller and J. Nix, or W. Reisdorf) which treat fusion as motion along the relative distance and incorrectly predict that fusion probability always increases with neutron number. The results enable predictions for planned experiments with radioactive beams at facilities such as GSI and provide guidance for synthesizing superheavy elements with $Z$ up to 118, though the authors note that experimental data on symmetric reactions with stable and radioactive beams could help test the DNS model.
