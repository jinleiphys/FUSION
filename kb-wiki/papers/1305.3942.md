# Density determinations in heavy ion collisions

> The experimental determination of freeze-out temperatures and densities from the yields of light elements emitted in heavy ion collisions is discussed. Results from different experimental approaches are compared with those of model calculations carried out with and without the inclusion of medium effects. Medium effects become of relevance for baryon densities above $\approx 5 \times 10^{-4}$ fm$^{-3}$. A quantum statistical (QS) model incorporating medium effects is in good agreement with the experimentally derived results at higher densities. A densitometer based on calculated chemical equilibrium constants is proposed.

## Key claim

Medium effects (Pauli blocking, Mott effect) become relevant for baryon densities above approximately \(5 \times 10^{-4}\) fm\(^{-3}\), and a quantum statistical (QS) model incorporating these effects agrees well with experimentally derived results at higher densities, whereas the simple nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) model fails in that regime.

## Method

The authors compare four experimental approaches to extracting freeze-out baryon density from yields of light particles (p, d, t, \(^3\)He, \(^4\)He) emitted in near-Fermi-energy heavy ion collisions: (i) NSE-based Albergo relations, (ii) the Mekjian coalescence model, (iii) quantum fluctuation analysis, and (iv) a chemical equilibrium constant (\({\tilde K}_\alpha\)) densitometer. They then compare these experimental results with theoretical calculations from a quantum statistical (QS) model that includes in-medium corrections to binding energies and the Mott effect, and with the standard NSE model that neglects medium effects.

## Key numbers

- Medium effects become relevant for baryon densities above approximately \(5 \times 10^{-4}\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The NSE consistency test ratio \(R_{\text{test}}\) deviates from the NSE prediction of 1.0: data from Kowalski (2006) give \(R_{\text{test}} = 1.22\), data from Natowitz give \(R_{\text{test}} = 1.36\), and data from Aldo give \(R_{\text{test}} = 1.147\).
- The Albergo temperature formula is \(T_{\text{HHe}} = 14.3\ \text{MeV} / \ln[1.59\ (Y_\alpha Y_d)/(Y_t Y_h)]\).
- The NSE-based free proton density formula is \(n_p = 0.62 \times 10^{36}\ T^{3/2} \exp[-19.8/T]\ (Y_\alpha/Y_t)\), with \(T\) in MeV and \(n_p\) in nucleons/cm\(^3\).
- The QS model shows that bound state fractions disappear and the free proton fraction rises near a density of \(10^{-2}\) fm\(^{-3}\) due to the Mott effect.

## Context

This work extends earlier freeze-out analyses of heavy ion collisions, such as those by Bondorf (1995) and Albergo, by demonstrating that the simple NSE model is insufficient for densities above about \(5 \times 10^{-4}\) fm\(^{-3}\). The proposed QS-based densitometer using the chemical equilibrium constant \({\tilde K}_\alpha\) provides a method to extract baryon densities from experimental yields at higher densities than previously possible. The results are consistent with coalescence model analyses by Mekjian and with quantum fluctuation analyses by Aldo, and the data appear to sample the vapor branch of the liquid-gas coexistence curve, enabling determination of critical temperatures for mesoscopic nuclear systems as in treatments by Elliott and by Natowitz.
