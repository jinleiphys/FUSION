# A way forward in the study of the symmetry energy: experiment, theory, and observation

> The symmetry energy describes how the energy of nuclear matter rises as one goes away from equal numbers of neutrons and protons. This is very important to describe neutron rich matter in astrophysics. This article reviews our knowledge of the symmetry energy from theoretical calculations, nuclear structure measurements, heavy ion collisions, and astronomical observations. We then present a roadmap to make progress in areas of relevance to the symmetry energy that promotes collaboration between the astrophysics and the nuclear physics communities.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while heavy-ion collisions provide the only laboratory means to probe the symmetry energy at both sub-saturation and supra-saturation densities, extracting reliable constraints requires overcoming serious theoretical challenges in transport models, particularly regarding cluster formation, momentum dependence of the isovector mean field, and in-medium particle production, and that progress demands coordinated efforts between experimentalists, theorists, and astrophysical observers.

## Method

The paper reviews constraints on the symmetry energy from heavy-ion collisions at low densities (35–150 MeV per nucleon) using isospin diffusion observables and cluster production, and at higher densities (up to 3.5 times saturation density) using elliptic flow ratios, pion ratios, and kaon production. It compares these experimental constraints with those from nuclear-structure observables (isobaric analog resonances, electric dipole polarizability in lead-208, and nuclear binding energies) and with neutron-star observations (masses and radii). The authors then identify specific deficiencies in transport models, including Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU), Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD), and Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD), and propose a roadmap of code comparisons, improved treatment of cluster formation, and consistent implementation of in-medium cross sections and self-energies.

## Key numbers

- Isospin diffusion measurements using tin-112 and tin-124 at 50 MeV per nucleon constrain the symmetry energy for densities in the range of one-third to one times saturation density (Tsang 2009)
- The symmetry energy at density 0.1 inverse femtometers cubed (approximately two-thirds saturation density) is 25.4 ± 0.8 MeV from Skyrme CSkp functionals fitting ground-state properties of double magic nuclei (Brown 2013)
- The symmetry energy at density 0.11 inverse femtometers cubed is 26.65 ± 0.2 MeV from masses of 38 spherical nuclei (Zhang 2013)
- In central gold-on-gold collisions at 1 GeV per nucleon, nuclear matter is compressed to almost 3 times saturation density for a period of about 5 to 10 femtometers per c
- Heavy-ion collisions at incident energies from 35 to 150 MeV per nucleon access the symmetry energy at densities from about 0.1 to 1.5 times saturation density
- In the final state of tin-on-tin collisions at 50 MeV per nucleon, about 90% of the protons in the system are bound in clusters and heavier fragments
- An experiment using indium-109 on a tin-124 target achieves an asymmetry difference of 0.093; a more desirable reaction using tin-132 on tin-112 would give an asymmetry difference of 0.135, a more than 50% increase

## Context

This review extends the program of constraining the symmetry energy from heavy-ion collisions (Tsang 2009, Kohley 2013) and nuclear-structure observables (Danielewicz 2013, Tamii 2011, Roca-Maza 2013, Kortelainen 2010) by identifying specific theoretical bottlenecks that prevent unambiguous extraction of the density dependence. It builds on earlier successful efforts to constrain the symmetric-matter equation of state using flow and kaon production (Danielewicz 2002, Fuchs 2006) and calls for systematic code comparisons, building on workshops at Trento in 2004 and 2009, to resolve discrepancies between transport models that currently yield conflicting conclusions (e.g., whether a stiff or soft symmetry energy better describes pion ratio data). The roadmap enables future experiments at RIKEN and NSCL/FRIB using rare isotope beams (tin-132, calcium-52) to place tighter constraints on the symmetry energy at supra-saturation densities, which is critical for interpreting neutron-star observations of masses (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013) and radii (Guillot 2013).
