# Measurement of the Spectral Function of $^{40}$Ar through the $(e,e^\prime p)$ reaction

> The interpretation of the signals detected by high precision experiments aimed at measuring neutrino oscillations requires an accurate description of the neutrino-nucleus cross sections. One of the key element of the analysis is the treatment of nuclear effects, which is one of the main sources of systematics for accelerator based experiments such as the Long Baseline Neutrino Experiment (LBNE). A considerable effort is currently being made to develop theoretical models capable of providing a fully quantitative description of the neutrino-nucleus cross sections in the kinematical regime relevant to LBNE. The approach based on nuclear many-body theory and the spectral function formalism has proved very successful in explaining the available electron scattering data in a variety of kinematical conditions. The first step towards its application to the analysis of neutrino data is the derivation of the spectral functions of nuclei employed in neutrino detectors, in particular argon. We propose a measurement of the coincidence $(e,e^\prime p)$ cross section on argon. This data will provide the experimental input indispensable to construct the argon spectral function, thus paving the way for a reliable estimate of the neutrino cross sections. In addition, the analysis of the $(e,e^\prime p)$ data will help a number of theoretical developments, like the description of final-state interactions needed to isolate the initial-state contributions to the observed single-particle peaks, that is also needed for the interpretation of the signal detected in neutrino experiments.

## Key claim

The authors propose a measurement of the coincidence $(e,e^\prime p)$ cross section on argon to provide the experimental input needed to construct the argon spectral function, which is essential for reliable estimates of neutrino-nucleus cross sections for the Long Baseline Neutrino Experiment (LBNE). The abstract glosses over the fact that the current model spectral function for argon involves very large uncertainties due to the lack of both experimental information on argon spectroscopic factors and theoretical calculations of the momentum distribution, and that a more realistic model will be needed to reach the accuracy required for precise neutrino experiments.

## Method

The authors plan to measure the coincidence $(e,e^\prime p)$ cross section on an argon target using the Hall A high resolution spectrometers (HRS) at Jefferson Lab, with a beam energy of 2.2 GeV. The measurement will be performed in both parallel and anti-parallel kinematics to minimize the effects of final state interactions (FSI), and the data will be analyzed within the Plane Wave Impulse Approximation (PWIA) and Distorted Wave Impulse Approximation (DWIA) frameworks to extract the spectral function. The experiment will use a 10 Atm, 15 cm long argon target at 130 K with a beam current of 100 microamps, achieving a target luminosity of 5.5 x 10^36 cm^-2 sec^-1.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 2.2 GeV, with 9 days of beam time requested (1 day for calibration, 8 days for measurement)
- Target density: 560 mg cm^-2, with target luminosity of 5.5 x 10^36 cm^-2 sec^-1
- Missing energy range to be scanned: from approximately 8 MeV to 60 MeV, covering the separation energies of proton shell model states in argon (e.g., 1d3/2 at 6 MeV, 2s1/2 at 8 MeV, 1d5/2 at 11 MeV, 1p1/2 at 28 MeV, 1p3/2 at 32 MeV, 1s1/2 at 52 MeV)
- Missing momentum range relevant to the study: extends to approximately 350 MeV
- Expected missing energy resolution: 0.9 MeV FWHM (sigma of 0.38 MeV), based on the previous E89-003 experiment on oxygen at 2.4 GeV

## Context

This work extends the spectral function formalism previously applied to carbon and oxygen (using the approach of Benhar et al., 2005 and 2008) to argon, which is the target material for the LBNE neutrino detector. The only available electron scattering data on argon are inclusive cross sections measured at Frascati in the 1990s, and the current model spectral function for argon (Ankowski et al., 2012) involves large uncertainties because no theoretical calculations of the momentum distribution exist and empirical information on energy levels is limited to a few neutron states near the Fermi surface. The measurement will enable reliable estimates of neutrino-argon cross sections needed for LBNE's precision measurement of the CP violating phase, and will also help describe final-state interactions needed for interpreting neutrino signals.
