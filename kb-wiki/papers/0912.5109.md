# A Field-Theoretic Parametrization of Low-Energy Nucleon Form Factors

> A field-theoretic parametrization is proposed for nucleon electromagnetic form factors at momentum transfer less than 600 MeV. The parametrization is part of a larger effective field theory lagrangian that is Lorentz covariant and chirally symmetric, and that has been used to successfully describe bulk and single-particle properties of medium to heavy mass nuclei. The parametrization is based on vector meson dominance and a derivative expansion of nucleon couplings to the electromagnetic fields. At lowest order in the expansion, it is possible to fit all four parameters to modern data on the rms radii of the nucleon form factors. At next-to-leading order it is possible to fit the form factors to within a few percent up to momentum transfers of 600 MeV. The vector meson dominance contributions are crucial in this fit, since a simple expansion in powers of momentum transfer would require many, many terms to achieve comparable accuracy. The ability to fit single-nucleon form factors up to 600 MeV momentum transfer makes possible the study of two-body electromagnetic exchange currents within this effective field theory framework.

# A Field-Theoretic Parametrization of Low-Energy Nucleon Form Factors

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a field-theoretic parametrization based on vector meson dominance and a derivative expansion can fit nucleon electromagnetic form factors to within a few percent up to momentum transfers of 600 MeV, but only when the next-to-leading order derivative terms are included; the lowest-order parametrization alone is only accurate up to roughly 250 MeV momentum transfer.

## Method

The author uses a Lorentz-covariant, chirally symmetric effective field theory (QHD EFT) with vector meson dominance to parametrize the four Sachs form factors (proton electric, neutron electric, proton magnetic, neutron magnetic). At lowest order, the four parameters are fit to modern data on the rms radii of the nucleon form factors, using Kelly's (2004) analytic fits as the reference data set. At next-to-leading order, four additional parameters (contained in beta' and lambda'') are adjusted numerically to minimize the maximum deviation between the fit and Kelly's results over the range 0 to 0.1 in tau (Q^2/4M^2), corresponding to momentum transfers up to 600 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Momentum transfer range of interest: up to 600 MeV (tau approximately 0.1)
- Lowest-order fit yields proton electric rms radius: 0.862 fm
- Neutron electric mean-square radius from lowest-order fit: -0.127 fm^2, compared to Kelly's value of -0.112 +/- 0.003 fm^2
- Dirac isoscalar rms radius: 0.799 fm; Dirac isovector rms radius: 0.780 fm
- Pauli isoscalar rms radius: 1.30 fm; Pauli isovector rms radius: 0.896 fm
- Proton magnetic mean-square radius: 0.7191 fm^2
- Neutron magnetic mean-square radius: 0.8226 fm^2
- New leading-order parameters from fit: beta^(0) = 0.0670, beta^(1) = -0.2402, f_v = -0.3279, f_rho = 4.4198
- Next-to-leading order constraints: lambda_p' - beta_p' = 2.255, lambda_n' - beta_n' = -2.786
- Next-to-leading order parameters: beta_p' = -0.9438, beta_n' = 0.4688
- Vector meson masses used: m_v (omega) = 782 MeV, m_rho = 770 MeV
- Dipole form factor parameter: Lambda^2 = 0.71 GeV^2

## Context

This work extends the QHD effective field theory approach of Furnstahl, Serot, and Tang (1997) by updating the lowest-order fits with modern high-precision data from the early 2000s and by adding next-to-leading order derivative terms to extend the momentum range. The parametrization is fit to the analytic results of Kelly (2004), who achieved excellent fits to the full data set with a small number of parameters. This enables future studies of two-body electromagnetic exchange currents within the same EFT framework, which were previously impossible because the lowest-order form factors were only accurate up to 250 MeV. The paper demonstrates that a simple power-series expansion in momentum transfer would require many terms to achieve comparable accuracy, making the vector meson dominance contributions critical.
