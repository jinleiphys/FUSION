# Solution of the microscopic gap equation for a slab of nuclear matter with the Paris NN-potential

> The gap equation in the $^1S_0$-channel is solved for a nuclear slab with the separable form of the Paris potential. The gap equation is considered in the model space in terms of the effective pairing interaction which is found in the complementary subspace. The absolute value of the gap $Δ$ turned out to be very sensitive to the cutoff $K_{max}$ in the momentum space in the equation for the effective interaction. It is necessary to take $K_{max}=160-180 fm^{-1}$ to guarantee 1% accuracy for $Δ$. The gap equation itself is solved directly, without any additional approximations. The solution reveals the surface enhancement of the gap $Δ$ which was earlier found with an approximate consideration. A strong surface-volume interplay was found also implying a kind of the proximity effect. The diagonal matrix elements of $Δ$ turned out to be rather close to the empirical values for heavy atomic nuclei.

# Solution of the microscopic gap equation for a slab of nuclear matter with the Paris NN-potential

## Key claim

The absolute value of the pairing gap Δ in a nuclear slab is extremely sensitive to the momentum cutoff K_max used in the equation for the effective pairing interaction, requiring K_max = 160–180 fm⁻¹ to achieve 1% accuracy for Δ, even though the effective pairing interaction itself converges at much lower cutoffs. The direct solution confirms a surface enhancement of the gap and yields diagonal matrix elements close to empirical values for heavy nuclei, but the authors note that the room for additional corrections beyond BCS is only about 20%, contradicting earlier work by Barranco et al. (2004) which found only 50% of the empirical gap for ¹²⁰Sn using the Argonne v14 potential.

## Method

The authors solved the BCS gap equation in the ¹S₀ channel for a one-dimensional nuclear slab (Saxon-Woods potential with depth U₀ = 50 MeV, diffuseness d = 0.65 fm, thickness L = 8 fm to imitate heavy nuclei) using the separable 3×3 representation of the Paris NN-potential. They employed a two-step method: the effective pairing interaction (EPI) was computed in the complementary subspace via a Bethe-Goldstone-type equation, and then the gap equation was solved directly in the model space (E₀ = 20 MeV) by iterating the Bogolyubov equations until the maximum change in Δ components was below 10⁻⁵ MeV, with the chemical potential recalculated at each iteration to fix the particle number per unit surface.

## Key numbers

- Cutoff momentum required for 1% accuracy in Δ: K_max = 160–180 fm⁻¹ (compared to K_max = 60 fm⁻¹ used in earlier EPI calculations).
- Model space separation energy: E₀ = 20 MeV was sufficient for convergence; results changed negligibly when increasing to E₀ = 30 MeV.
- Diagonal matrix elements Δ_nn (k_⊥ = 0) for L = 8 fm slab: values range from 0.26 MeV (for ε_n = −1.09 MeV state) to 1.14 MeV (for ε_n = −49.06 MeV state), with average Δ̄ = 0.85 MeV (or 1.00 MeV excluding the lowest-energy state).
- Chemical potential with pairing: μ = −7.96 MeV, very close to the non-pairing value μ₀ = −8 MeV, with difference δμ = −0.04 MeV.
- Central Fermi-averaged gap Δ_F(X=0) for L = 8 fm: 0.41 MeV; surface maximum Δ_F^max = 0.94 MeV; ratio α = 2.29.
- For L = 6.2 fm (imitating Sn region): Δ_F(0) = 0.45 MeV, Δ_F^max = 1.20 MeV, α = 2.67; average diagonal matrix elements Δ̄ = 1.06 MeV (or 1.26 MeV excluding the small-energy level).
- For L = 7.4 fm (imitating Pb region): Δ_F(0) = 0.46 MeV, Δ_F^max = 1.09 MeV, α = 2.37; average Δ̄ = 1.08 MeV.
- For infinite slab (L → ∞): Δ_F(0) = 0.22 MeV.
- Asymptotic value of the Fermi-averaged EPI outside the slab: −720 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the two-step approach developed by the same group (reviewed in a 2004 report by the authors) for solving the gap equation in semi-infinite nuclear matter and slabs, where previously only an approximate KKC method had been used for the gap equation itself. The direct solution confirms the surface enhancement of the gap found earlier with approximate methods (the authors' 2004 work). The results are compared to Barranco et al. (2004), who solved the gap equation directly for ¹²⁰Sn with the Argonne v14 potential and obtained only about 50% of the empirical gap, attributing the missing 50% to surface vibrations; the present authors argue that the difference arises mainly from their use of m* = m (rather than the density-dependent m* used by Barranco et al.) and suggest the surface vibration contribution may be overestimated in that work. The paper enables future consistent inclusion of non-pole diagrams in the pairing problem, following the approach of Khodel and Saperstein (2001).
