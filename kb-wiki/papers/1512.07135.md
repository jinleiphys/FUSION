# The unreasonable effectiveness of hydrodynamics in heavy ion collisions

> Event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations of AA and pA collisions involve initial energy densities with large spatial gradients. This is associated with the presence of large Knudsen numbers ($K_n\approx 1$) at early times, which may lead one to question the validity of the hydrodynamic approach in these rapidly evolving, largely inhomogeneous systems. A new procedure to smooth out the initial energy densities is employed to show that the initial spatial eccentricities, $\varepsilon_n$, are remarkably robust with respect to variations in the underlying scale of initial energy density spatial gradients, $λ$. For $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV LHC initial conditions generated by the MCKLN code, $\varepsilon_n$ (across centralities) remains nearly constant if the fluctuation scale varies by an order of magnitude, i.e., when $λ$ varies from 0.1 to 1 fm. Given that the local Knudsen number $K_n\approx \frac{1}λ$, the robustness of the initial eccentricities with respect to changes in the fluctuation scale suggests that the vn's cannot be used to distinguish between events with large $K_n$ from events where $K_n$ is in the hydrodynamic regime. We use the 2+1 Lagrangian hydrodynamic code v-USPhydro to show that this is indeed the case: anisotropic flow coefficients computed within event-by-event viscous hydrodynamics are only sensitive to long wavelength scales of order $\frac{1}{Λ_{QCD}}\approx 1$ fm and are incredibly robust with respect to variations in the initial local Knudsen number. This robustness can be used to justify the somewhat unreasonable effectiveness of the nearly perfect fluid paradigm in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the initial spatial eccentricities and final anisotropic flow coefficients in heavy-ion collisions are remarkably insensitive to the scale of initial energy density fluctuations, provided the smoothing scale is larger than the relaxation time (approximately 0.25 fm). This robustness means that flow observables cannot distinguish between events with large local Knudsen numbers (where hydrodynamics might be invalid) and events where the Knudsen number is in the hydrodynamic regime.

## Method

The authors used MCKLN initial conditions for PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV and applied a cubic spline smoothing procedure to systematically vary the fluctuation scale lambda from 0.1 fm to 1.0 fm. They then evolved the smoothed initial conditions using the 2+1 Lagrangian viscous hydrodynamic code v-USPhydro, with a lattice-based equation of state, shear viscosity tuned for each lambda, and freeze-out temperatures between 120 and 130 MeV, to compute charged hadron spectra and integrated flow harmonics v2 through v5.

## Key numbers

- The smoothing scale lambda was varied from 0.1 fm to 1.0 fm, an order of magnitude, while initial spatial eccentricities epsilon_n remained nearly constant across centralities.
- The relaxation time tau_pi is approximately 0.25 fm at LHC, setting the microscale; the gluon saturation scale 1/Q_s is less than 0.1 fm.
- The system size for PbPb collisions at LHC is approximately 7 to 10 fm.
- For lambda = 0.1 fm, which is below the relaxation time, the flow harmonics v2 through v5 are clearly lower than for larger lambda values; for lambda greater than tau_pi, there is very little difference in v2 through v5 between the microscale and the mesoscale.
- The freeze-out temperature was varied between T_FO = 120 and 130 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Noronha-Hostler et al. (2015) on the robustness of eccentricities under smoothing, and qualitatively agrees with Floerchinger et al. (2013) that azimuthal anisotropies are more sensitive to intermediate (mesoscopic) length scales than to deep sub-nucleonic scales. The results justify the use of hydrodynamics even when early-time Knudsen numbers are large, and suggest that future sensitivity to sub-nucleonic fluctuations may require event-by-event correlations rather than ensemble-averaged flow harmonics.
