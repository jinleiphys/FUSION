# A variational study of two-nucleon systems with lattice QCD

> The low-energy spectrum and scattering of two-nucleon systems are studied with lattice quantum chromodynamics using a variational approach. A wide range of interpolating operators are used: dibaryon operators built from products of plane-wave nucleons, hexaquark operators built from six localized quarks, and quasi-local operators inspired by two-nucleon bound-state wavefunctions in low-energy effective theories. Sparsening techniques are used to compute the timeslice-to-all quark propagators required to form correlation-function matrices using products of these operators. Projection of these matrices onto irreducible representations of the cubic group, including spin-orbit coupling, is detailed. Variational methods are applied to constrain the low-energy spectra of two-nucleon systems in a single finite volume with quark masses corresponding to a pion mass of 806 MeV. Results for S- and D-wave phase shifts in the isospin singlet and triplet channels are obtained under the assumption that partial-wave mixing is negligible. Tests of interpolating-operator dependence are used to investigate the reliability of the energy spectra obtained and highlight both the strengths and weaknesses of variational methods. These studies and comparisons to previous studies using the same gauge-field ensemble demonstrate that interpolating-operator dependence can lead to significant effects on the two-nucleon energy spectra obtained using both variational and non-variational methods, including missing energy levels and other discrepancies. While this study is inconclusive regarding the presence of two-nucleon bound states at this quark mass, it provides robust upper bounds on two-nucleon energy levels that can be improved in future calculations using additional interpolating operators and is therefore a step toward reliable nuclear spectroscopy from the underlying Standard Model of particle physics.

## Key claim

This study demonstrates that variational methods applied to two-nucleon systems in lattice QCD can provide robust upper bounds on energy levels, but the extracted spectra are sensitive to the choice of interpolating operators; the study is inconclusive regarding the presence of two-nucleon bound states at the quark mass used (pion mass of 806 MeV), and interpolating-operator dependence can lead to missing energy levels and other discrepancies in both variational and non-variational approaches.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD calculations of two-nucleon systems using a variational approach with a large set of interpolating operators, including dibaryon operators (products of plane-wave nucleons), hexaquark operators (six localized quarks), and quasi-local operators inspired by two-nucleon bound-state wavefunctions from low-energy effective theories. Sparsening techniques were used to compute the timeslice-to-all quark propagators needed to form correlation-function matrices, which were then projected onto irreducible representations of the cubic group including spin-orbit coupling. Variational methods (generalized eigenvalue problem) were applied to extract the low-energy spectra in a single finite volume, and S- and D-wave phase shifts in the isospin singlet and triplet channels were obtained under the assumption that partial-wave mixing is negligible.

## Context

This work extends previous lattice QCD studies of two-nucleon systems by Beane et al. (various years), Yamazaki et al. (2011-2015), and others using the same gauge-field ensemble, and addresses the challenge of excited-state contamination that has been a significant issue in multi-baryon spectroscopy. The study demonstrates that variational methods can provide upper bounds on energy levels, but the interpolating-operator dependence observed highlights the need for more comprehensive operator sets in future calculations. This work enables further progress toward reliable nuclear spectroscopy from lattice QCD by establishing that additional interpolating operators are needed to resolve the full spectrum, and provides a foundation for future studies at lighter quark masses where the signal-to-noise problem is more severe.
