# Angular Momentum Conservation Law in Light-Front Quantum Field Theory

> We prove the Lorentz invariance of the angular momentum conservation law and the helicity sum rule for relativistic composite systems in the light-front formulation. We explicitly show that $j^3$, the $z$-component of the angular momentum remains unchanged under Lorentz transformations generated by the light-front kinematical boost operators. The invariance of $j^3$ under Lorentz transformations is a feature unique to the front form. Applying the Lorentz invariance of the angular quantum number in the front form, we obtain a selection rule for the orbital angular momentum which can be used to eliminate certain interaction vertices in QED and QCD. We also generalize the selection rule to any renormalizable theory and show that there exists an upper bound on the change of orbital angular momentum in scattering processes at any fixed order in perturbation theory.

## Key claim

The paper proves that the $z$-component of angular momentum $j^3$ is Lorentz invariant under light-front kinematical boost operators, a property unique to the front form; this invariance holds for both elementary and composite relativistic systems, and the helicity sum rule is frame-independent in the front form, contrary to the instant form where helicity conservation is not generally valid.

## Method

The authors construct light-front spin states by applying a longitudinal boost along the $z$-direction followed by a light-front transverse boost, using the kinematical generators $M^{+\perp}$ and $M^{+-}$ that leave the $x^+=0$ plane invariant. They compute the expectation value of the spin operator $S^3$ on these states and show it equals the rest-frame spin projection $s^3$ in any Lorentz frame, using the commutation relations of the Poincaré algebra and the property that the expectation values of other Lorentz generators vanish on rest-frame eigenstates. The analysis is performed for both massive and massless particles, and the selection rule for orbital angular momentum is derived by applying the invariance of $j^3$ to interaction vertices in QED, QCD, and general renormalizable theories.

## Key numbers

- For a massive particle moving with momentum $p^\mu = (E, p, 0, 0)$, the light-front spin expectation value is $\langle S^i \rangle_L(p) = \lambda \begin{bmatrix} p^1/p^+ & p^2/p^+ & 1 \end{bmatrix}^T$, where $\lambda$ is the helicity and $p^+$ is the light-front longitudinal momentum.
- The canonical spin expectation value for a massive particle is $\langle S^i \rangle_c(p) = \frac{\lambda}{m(m+p^0)} \begin{bmatrix} -p^1 p^3 & -p^2 p^3 & m(m+p^0) + |p^\perp|^2 \end{bmatrix}^T$.
- The Wick helicity spin expectation value is $\langle S^i \rangle_h(p) = \frac{\lambda}{|\mathbf{p}|} \begin{bmatrix} p^1 & p^2 & p^3 \end{bmatrix}^T$.
- In the non-relativistic regime $|\mathbf{p}|^2 \ll m^2$, both canonical and light-front spin reduce to $\langle S^i \rangle = \lambda \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}^T$.
- The light-front boost parameter for massive particles is $e^\omega = m/p^+$, and for massless particles $e^\omega = 2\bar{p}/p^+$, where $\bar{p}$ is the reference momentum along the $z$-direction.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of angular momentum in relativistic quantum field theory by proving that the light-front formulation, originally developed by Dirac (1949) and further studied by Kogut and Soper (1970), provides a unique frame-independent definition of spin projection along the $z$-direction. The paper contradicts the conventional belief that helicity and spin are not identical in arbitrary frames, as discussed by Leader (2013), and shows that the light-front spin choice avoids the non-uniqueness issues of relativistic spin definitions raised by Bakker (2004) and Carlson (2003). The selection rules derived here enable systematic elimination of certain interaction vertices in QED and QCD, and provide an upper bound on orbital angular momentum changes in scattering processes, building on observations by Cruz-Santiago (2015), Brodsky (2000), and Dixon (1996).
