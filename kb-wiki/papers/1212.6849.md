# Lattice Hadron Structure: Applications within and beyond QCD

> Study of the hadronic matrix elements can provide not only tests of the QCD sector of the Standard Model (in comparing with existing experiments) but also reliable low-energy hadronic quantities applicable to a wide range of beyond-the-Standard Model scenarios where experiments or theoretical calculations are limited or difficult. On the QCD side, progress has been made in the notoriously difficult problem of addressing gluonic structure inside the nucleon, reaching higher-$Q^2$ region of the form factors, and providing a complete picture of the proton spin. However, even further study and improvement of systematic uncertainties are needed. There are also proposed calculations of higher-order operators in the neutron electric dipole moment Lagrangian, which would be useful when combined with effective theory to probe BSM. Lattice isovector tensor and scalar charges can be combined with upcoming neutron beta-decay measurements of the Fierz interference term and neutrino asymmetry parameter to probe new interactions in the effective theory, revealing the scale of potential new TeV particles. Finally, I revisit the systematic uncertainties in recent calculations of $g_A$ and review prospects for future calculations.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD calculations of hadronic matrix elements can provide both tests of the Standard Model and low-energy inputs for beyond-the-Standard-Model searches, but many current results still suffer from uncontrolled systematic uncertainties and require further improvement before they can be used as reliable inputs by experiments or model builders.

## Method

The author reviews recent lattice QCD calculations of nucleon matrix elements, including gluonic structure, form factors, the nucleon axial charge g_A, and quantities relevant to neutron electric dipole moment and neutron beta decay. The review examines systematic uncertainties in g_A calculations including renormalization, lattice discretization artifacts, excited-state contamination, chiral extrapolation, and finite-volume corrections, comparing results from multiple lattice groups using different fermion actions and analysis methods.

## Key numbers

- The Standard Model prediction for the neutron electric dipole moment is 10^{-30} e·cm, far below current experimental sensitivity
- Combined lattice analysis of the neutron electric dipole moment from the leading theta term gives O(30%) statistical error alone
- Lattice QCD values obtained for the tensor charge: g_T = 0.978(35) (statistical error only)
- Lattice QCD values obtained for the scalar charge: g_S = 0.796(79) (statistical error only)
- Model estimates for g_S range between 0.25 and 1, much looser than lattice constraints
- Upper bounds on effective couplings epsilon_S and epsilon_T correspond to lower bounds for new physics scales at 5.6 and 10 TeV respectively
- Upcoming neutron beta-decay experiments at LANL and ORNL plan to measure BSM parameters to 10^{-3} level by 2013, potentially improving to 10^{-4}
- The best g_A lattice calculation achieves 5% statistical error and sits a few standard deviations away from experimental values
- Finite-volume corrections to g_A from chiral perturbation theory at physical pion mass for a 4 fm box are less than 1%
- Lattice data from RBC/UKQCD and QCDSF show finite-volume shifts in g_A that are systematically larger than chiral perturbation theory predictions by factors of 2 to 10
- Volume changes can cause shifts of up to 0.3 in lattice g_A values

## Context

This conference proceeding from Lattice 2012 reviews progress in lattice hadron structure calculations following earlier reviews by Alexandrou (2010), Renner (2010), Zanotti (2008), Hagler (2007), and Orginos (2006). The work extends previous lattice calculations of nucleon matrix elements by examining systematic uncertainties in g_A more carefully than earlier studies, particularly finite-volume effects that may explain the 10-15% discrepancy between lattice results and experimental values. The calculations of tensor and scalar charges enable new probes of TeV-scale physics when combined with upcoming neutron beta-decay experiments, while the nEDM calculations can help constrain supersymmetric models when combined with effective field theory.
