# A deep learning approach for predicting multiple observables in Au+Au collisions at RHIC

> We develop a neural network model, based on the processes of high-energy heavy-ion collisions, to study and predict several experimental observables in Au+Au collisions. We present a data-driven deep learning framework for predicting multiple bulk observables in Au+Au collisions at RHIC energies. A single neural network is trained exclusively on experimental measurements of charged-particle pseudorapidity density distributions, transverse-momentum spectra and elliptic flow coefficients over a broad range of collision energies and centralities. The network architecture is inspired by the stages of a heavy-ion collision, from the quark-gluon plasma to chemical and kinetic freeze-out, and employs locally connected hidden layers and a structured input design that encodes basic geometric and kinematic features of the system. We demonstrate that these physics-motivated choices significantly improve test performance compared to purely fully connected baselines. The trained model is then used to predict the above observables at collision energies not yet explored experimentally at RHIC, and the results are validated using the energy dependence of the total charged-particle multiplicity per participant pair as well as comparisons to a CLVisc hydrodynamic calculation with TRENTo initial conditions. Our findings indicate that such physics-guided neural networks can serve as efficient surrogates to fill critical data gaps at RHIC and to support further phenomenological studies of QGP properties.

## Key claim

A single physics-informed neural network, trained exclusively on experimental measurements of charged-particle pseudorapidity density distributions, transverse-momentum spectra, and elliptic flow coefficients across multiple collision energies and centralities, can simultaneously reproduce and predict all three classes of observables in Au+Au collisions at RHIC, provided the architecture incorporates locally connected hidden layers inspired by the space-time stages of a heavy-ion collision. The authors hedge that the network is an empirical interpolator that reproduces regularities in the data but does not constitute a microscopic theory of QCD matter.

## Method

The authors constructed a feed-forward neural network with six hidden layers (QGP, chemical freeze-out, kinetic freeze-out, detector, and two data processing layers) where neurons in the QGP, chemical freeze-out, and kinetic freeze-out layers are distributed uniformly on spheres of increasing radius using a Fibonacci grid, and each neuron in one layer connects only to its seven nearest neighbors in the adjacent layer. The network takes two inputs: basic geometric and kinematic features of the collision system (atomic masses, collision energy, centrality range) and final-particle kinematics parameters with one-hot encoding; it outputs the corresponding observable value. The model was trained using the ADAM optimizer and Huber loss on experimental data for charged-particle pseudorapidity density distributions at 19.6, 62.4, and 200 GeV, elliptic flow at 7.7, 9.2, 11.5, 14.5, 27, 62.4, 130, and 200 GeV, and transverse-momentum spectra at 7.7, 11.5, 14.5, 27, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV, with test sets at held-out energies.

## Key numbers

- Training loss of the physics-guided model: 1.41 x 10^-3; test loss: 0.71 x 10^-3.
- Model without local connection: training loss 5.72 x 10^-3, test loss 3.77 x 10^-3.
- Model without splitting the input: training loss 3.18 x 10^-3, test loss 1.61 x 10^-3.
- Model without both local connection and input splitting: training loss 17.65 x 10^-3, test loss 4.88 x 10^-3.
- Model replacing local connection with Dropout layers: training loss 3.35 x 10^-3, test loss 1.07 x 10^-3.
- Predictions were made for pseudorapidity density distributions at sqrt(s_NN) = 17.3, 39, and 54.4 GeV; elliptic flow at 13.7, 17.3, and 54.4 GeV; and transverse-momentum distributions at 9.2, 17.3, and 54.4 GeV.
- An error of 10% was added to all neural network predictions based on the typical experimental data error.

## Context

This work extends the application of machine learning to heavy-ion collision phenomenology, building on prior studies that used neural networks for tasks such as jet image generation (Oliveira 2017), elliptic flow estimation (Mallick 2023), and impact parameter determination (Omana Kuttan 2020). Unlike those approaches, which often combine experimental data with synthetic data from hydrodynamic or transport models, this network is trained exclusively on experimental measurements. The predictions fill data gaps at RHIC energies not yet explored experimentally, and the results are validated against a CLVisc hydrodynamic calculation with TRENTo initial conditions and the global systematics of total charged-particle multiplicity per participant pair. The work enables future integration of such physics-guided networks into global analyses as fast emulators for inferring QGP properties from multi-dimensional data sets.
