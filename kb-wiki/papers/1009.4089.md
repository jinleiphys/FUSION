# Lattice QCD at non-zero temperature and baryon density

> Lectures given at the Summer School on "Modern perspectives in lattice QCD", Les Houches, August 3-28, 2009

## Key claim

The paper argues that lattice QCD at finite temperature and baryon density is fundamentally more challenging than vacuum QCD, with perturbation theory breaking down at a finite order (the Linde problem) such that all loop orders contribute to the pressure at order \(g^6\), making the calculation of the magnetic screening mass an entirely non-perturbative problem regardless of how weak the coupling is.

## Method

The author derives the grand canonical partition function for QCD on a Euclidean lattice with compactified temporal direction of extent \(N_\tau\), where temperature is identified as \(T = 1/(a N_\tau)\). The method involves evaluating the path integral for gauge fields with periodic boundary conditions and fermions with anti-periodic boundary conditions, then computing thermodynamic quantities from derivatives of \(\ln Z\). The author also presents the perturbative expansion of the partition function, the ideal gas limit (Stefan-Boltzmann), and the hadron resonance gas model for low temperatures, and discusses the infrared structure of finite-temperature perturbation theory using Matsubara frequency sums.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for the QCD transition: \(T_c \sim 200\) MeV
- Stefan-Boltzmann pressure for one massless bosonic degree of freedom: \(p = \frac{\pi^2}{90} T^4\)
- Stefan-Boltzmann pressure for one massless fermionic degree of freedom: \(p = \frac{7}{8} \frac{\pi^2}{90} T^4\)
- Leading-order electric (Debye) mass: \(m_E^{LO} = \left( \frac{N}{3} + \frac{N_f}{6} \right)^{1/2} g T\)
- String tension scale: \(\sigma \approx 425\) MeV
- Sommer scale definition: \(r^2 \frac{dV(r)}{dr} = 1.65\)
- Lattice correction to free boson pressure: \(\frac{p}{p_{\text{cont}}} = 1 + \frac{8\pi^2}{21} \frac{1}{N_\tau^2} + O\left( \frac{1}{N_\tau^4} \right)\)

## Context

This lecture paper extends the pedagogical tradition of lattice QCD summer schools (Les Houches 2009) and reviews the foundational concepts of finite-temperature field theory applied to QCD, including the Matsubara formalism and the Linde problem. It builds on the textbook treatment by Kapusta (2006) and references the hadron resonance gas model comparison to lattice Monte Carlo simulations by Karsch (2003). The work enables future lattice studies of the QCD phase diagram, heavy-ion collision phenomenology at RHIC, LHC, and FAIR, and connections to early-universe cosmology and compact-star physics.
