# Initial Vorticities of Quark-Gluon Matter in Heavy-ion Collisions

> We calculate four types of initial vorticities in Au+Au collisions at energies $ \sqrt{S_{NN}} $ = 5--200 GeV using a microscopic transport model PACIAE. Our simulation shows the non-monotonic dependence of the initial vorticities on the collision energies. The energy turning point is around 10-15 GeV for different vorticities but not sensitive to impact parameter.

## Key claim

The paper claims that initial vorticities in Au+Au collisions show a non-monotonic dependence on collision energy, with a turning point around 10-15 GeV that is not sensitive to impact parameter; however, this turning point differs from previous studies using UrQMD and IQMD models (Deng 2020).

## Method

The authors used the microscopic parton and hadron cascade model PACIAE to simulate Au+Au collisions at energies from 5 to 200 GeV. They applied a generalized coarse-graining method with cell size 0.5 x 0.5 x 0.5 fm^3 and time slice 0.5 fm/c to construct continuous fluid cells, then calculated four types of vorticities (non-relativistic, kinematic, temperature, and thermal) from the flow velocity and local temperature fields at the partonic stage.

## Key numbers

- Collision energy range studied: sqrt(S_NN) = 5 to 200 GeV for Au+Au collisions
- Cell size used in coarse-graining: 0.5 x 0.5 x 0.5 fm^3 with time slice 0.5 fm/c
- Turning point for non-relativistic, kinematic, and thermal vorticities: around 10 GeV
- Turning point for temperature vorticity: around 15 GeV
- Number of quark flavors used in temperature extraction: N_f = 3
- Impact parameter range studied: b = 0 to 10 fm

## Context

This work extends previous studies of vorticity and polarization in heavy-ion collisions, particularly the non-monotonic energy dependence predicted by Deng (2020) using UrQMD and IQMD models, though with a different turning energy. The results provide complementary information to experimental measurements of global Lambda polarization by STAR (2017, 2021) and HADES (2022), and suggest that further studies in low- and moderate-energy regions are needed to resolve disagreements between model predictions and experimental data.
