# Femtoscopy scales and particle production in the relativistic heavy ion collisions from Au+Au at 200 AGeV to Xe+Xe at 5.44 ATeV within the integrated hydrokinetic model

> The recent results on the main soft observables, including hadron and photon yields and particle number ratios, $p_T$ spectra, flow harmonics, as well as the femtoscopy radii, obtained within the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) for high-energy heavy-ion collisions are reviewed and re-examined. The cases of different nuclei colliding at different energies are considered: Au+Au collisions at the top RHIC energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV, Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC energies $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV and $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.02$ TeV, and the LHC Xe+Xe collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.44$ TeV. The effect of the initial conditions and the model parameters, including the utilized equation of state (EoS) for quark-gluon phase, on the simulation results, as well as the role of the final afterburner stage of the matter evolution are discussed. The possible solution of the so-called ``photon puzzle'' is considered. The attention is also paid to the dependency of the interferometry volume and individual interferometry radii on the initial transverse geometrical size of the system formed in the collision.

## Key claim

The integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) can simultaneously describe bulk observables (hadron yields, pT spectra, flow harmonics, and femtoscopy radii) across multiple collision systems and energies using a single set of model parameters per collision type, but the model's parameters (particularly the binary collision fraction α and initial energy density ε0) must be re-tuned for each system, and the choice of equation of state for the quark-gluon phase can be compensated by afterburner dynamics to yield equally good descriptions of experimental data.

## Method

The iHKM simulates heavy-ion collisions starting at τ0 ≈ 0.1 fm/c with a far-from-equilibrium initial state generated by the Monte Carlo Glauber model (GLISSANDO) with a Color Glass Condensate-inspired momentum distribution. The system evolves through a pre-thermal stage using a relaxation-time approximation that smoothly transitions the energy-momentum tensor to Israel-Stewart viscous hydrodynamics (η/s = 0.08) by τth = 1 fm/c, then continues with hydrodynamical expansion until reaching the particlization temperature Tp, where particles are generated via the Cooper-Frye prescription with viscous corrections and passed to the UrQMD hadron cascade for the afterburner stage.

## Key numbers

- Au+Au at √sNN = 200 GeV: α = 0.18, τ0 = 0.1 fm/c, ε0 = 235 GeV/fm³, using Laine-Schroeder EoS with Tp = 165 MeV and μB = 21 MeV, μS = 5 MeV
- Pb+Pb at √sNN = 2.76 TeV: α = 0.24, τ0 = 0.1 fm/c (Laine-Schroeder EoS, ε0 = 679 GeV/fm³) or τ0 = 0.15 fm/c (HotQCD EoS, ε0 = 495 GeV/fm³)
- Pb+Pb at √sNN = 5.02 TeV: α = 0.24, τ0 = 0.1 fm/c (Laine-Schroeder EoS, ε0 = 1067 GeV/fm³) or τ0 = 0.12 fm/c (HotQCD EoS, ε0 = 870 GeV/fm³)
- Xe+Xe at √sNN = 5.44 TeV: α = 0.44, τ0 = 0.1 fm/c, ε0 = 445 GeV/fm³, using HotQCD EoS with Tp = 156 MeV and xenon deformation parameter β = 0.18
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio fixed at η/s = 0.08 ≈ 1/(4π)
- Initial state momentum anisotropy parameter Λ = λ⊥/λ∥ = 100 for all collision systems
- Inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross-section: σ = 42 mb for RHIC Au+Au, σ = 64 mb for LHC Pb+Pb, σ = 68.4 ± 5.0 mb for LHC Xe+Xe

## Context

This work extends the iHKM framework previously applied to LHC Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV (Shapoval et al., 2019-2020) and RHIC Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV (Shapoval et al., 2019) to include new results for Xe+Xe collisions at 5.44 TeV. The model addresses the "photon puzzle" and demonstrates that different equations of state (Laine-Schroeder with Tp = 165 MeV versus HotQCD with Tp = 156 MeV) can produce equally good descriptions of experimental data when the afterburner stage is included, supporting the hypothesis of continuous chemical and thermal freeze-out. The results enable systematic comparisons of femtoscopy scales across different nuclear sizes and collision energies, and provide a framework for testing initial-state models against bulk observables.
