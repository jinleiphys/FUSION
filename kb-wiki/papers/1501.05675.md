# Neutron Matter from Low to High Density

> Neutron matter is an intriguing nuclear system with multiple connections to other areas of physics. Considerable progress has been made over the last two decades in exploring the properties of pure neutron fluids. Here we begin by reviewing work done to explore the behavior of very low density neutron matter, which forms a strongly paired superfluid and is thus similar to cold Fermi atoms, though at energy scales differing by many orders of magnitude. We then increase the density, discussing work that ties the study of neutron matter with the determination of the properties of neutron-rich nuclei and neutron-star crusts. After this, we review the impact neutron matter at even higher densities has on the mass-radius relation of neutron stars, thereby making contact with astrophysical observations.

# Neutron Matter from Low to High Density

## Key claim

The equation of state of cold neutron matter at low and moderate densities is now tightly constrained from many-body calculations with realistic interactions, but the high-density regime remains uncertain due to the role of three-nucleon forces and the possible appearance of hyperons, which may or may not soften the equation of state enough to conflict with the observed two-solar-mass neutron stars.

## Method

The authors review quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) calculations of neutron matter using the AV8' two-nucleon interaction combined with the Urbana-IX (UIX) three-nucleon force, as well as various chiral effective field theory interactions at N2LO and N3LO orders. They compare results from multiple many-body methods including many-body perturbation theory (MBPT), self-consistent Green's functions (SCGF), coupled-cluster with doubles and triples (CCD(T)), auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFQMC), and particle-particle ladder approximations. The equation of state is then used to solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain neutron star mass-radius relations, and the role of Lambda hyperons is studied by including Lambda-nucleon and Lambda-nucleon-nucleon interactions.

## Key numbers

- The binding energy of symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density is -16.0 ± 0.1 MeV (Moller 1995)
- The AV8' Hamiltonian alone does not support neutron stars with masses of 1.97(4) solar masses (Demorest 2010) or 2.01(4) solar masses (Antoniadis 2013)
- The maximum neutron star radius implied by modern neutron matter EOS is not larger than 13.5 km, unless drastic repulsion sets in just above saturation density (Gandolfi 2012)
- Neutron stars are unlikely to have radii lower than 10 km (Ozel 2010, Guillot 2013) or larger than 15 km (Heinke 2014)
- The symmetry energy at saturation is varied between approximately 16.0 MeV and 17.7 MeV in different three-neutron force models
- The crust of neutron stars is modeled using the EOS for densities above 0.08 fm^{-3}, with lower densities taken from Baym (1971) and Negele (1973)

## Context

This review extends earlier work on neutron matter equations of state, particularly the Akmal-Pandharipande-Ravenhall (APR) results using AV18 plus UIX (Akmal 1998), and connects to cold Fermi atom studies at unitarity (Giorgini 2008). The paper shows that three-nucleon interactions are repulsive in neutron matter (unlike in light and medium-mass nuclei), and that the correlation between the symmetry energy E_sym and its slope L is very strong within the Illinois model of three-neutron forces. The results enable improved density functionals for neutron-rich nuclei, as demonstrated by comparisons with neutron drop calculations in harmonic oscillator and Wood-Saxon traps, and provide constraints for neutron star mass-radius relations that can be tested by future gravitational wave observations from neutron star mergers.
