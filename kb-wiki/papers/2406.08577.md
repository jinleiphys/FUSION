# FastEEC: Fast Evaluation of N-point Energy Correlators

> Energy correlators characterize the asymptotic energy flow in scattering events produced at colliders, from which the microscopic physics of the scattering can be deduced. This view of collisions is akin to analyses of the Cosmic Microwave Background, and a range of promising phenomenological applications of energy correlators have been identified, including the study of hadronization, the deadcone effect, measuring $α_s$ and the top quark mass. While $N$-point energy correlators are interesting to study for larger values of $N$, their evaluation is computationally intensive, scaling like $M^N/N!$, where $M$ is the number of particles. In this Letter, we develop a fast, approximate method for their evaluation exploiting that correlations at a given angular scale are insensitive to effects at other (widely-separated) scales. This implies that the energy correlator can be computed on (sub)jets, effectively reducing M. Furthermore, we utilize a dynamical (sub)jet radius that allows us to obtain reliable results without restricting the angular scales being probed. For concreteness we focus on the projected energy correlator, which projects onto the largest separation between the $N$ directions. E.g.~for $N=7$ we find a speed up of up to four orders of magnitude, depending on the desired accuracy. We also consider the possibility of raising the energy to a power higher than one in the energy correlator, which has been proposed to reduce soft sensitivity, and further cuts back the required computation time. These higher-power correlators are not collinear safe, but as a byproduct our approach suggests a natural method to regularize them, such that they can be described using perturbation theory. This letter is accompanied by a public code that implements our method.

## Key claim

The paper presents a fast, approximate method for evaluating N-point energy correlators that achieves speedups of up to four orders of magnitude for N=7, with the caveat that the accuracy depends on a resolution parameter f and the specific angular region being probed, with relative errors ranging from below 1% to about 5% depending on the chosen settings.

## Method

The method reclusters the jet using the Cambridge/Aachen (C/A) or kT algorithm, then recursively traverses the clustering tree. At each splitting with angular separation ΔR, the two branches are resolved into subjets using a dynamic radius r = ΔR/√f, where f is a resolution parameter; contributions involving particles from both branches are computed using these subjets, while single-branch contributions are obtained recursively. For kT reclustering, the resolution parameter is taken as f = f' kT,min^2, where f' is a dimensionful scale, to achieve per-mille level precision across different jet transverse momentum ranges.

## Key numbers

- For N=7 with C/A reclustering and f=8, the speedup is a factor of about 5000, corresponding to an evaluation time of about 10^-3 seconds per jet
- For N=7 with C/A reclustering and f=64, the speedup is about a factor of 40, with relative error below 1%
- For N=5 with C/A reclustering and f=64, the speedup is about a factor of 20
- Using C/A with f=8, the relative error grows from about 1% at N=2 to about 5% at N=7
- Using kT reclustering with f = kT,min^2, the relative error is at the per-mille level or less over most of the angular range
- For N=3 and N=6 with κ=2 and C/A with f=8, the relative error is about 1.5%, improving to less than 0.5% with f=32

## Context

This work extends the study of energy correlators, which were proposed in e+e- collisions by Basham et al. (1979) and have recently been applied to jets by Dixon et al. (2019) and Chen et al. (2020). Previous calculations using the EnergyEnergyCorrelators package have been restricted to N≤6 due to computational cost scaling as M^N/N! where M is the number of particles. The method enables access to higher-point correlators (N>6) for phenomenological applications including hadronization studies, dead-cone effect measurements, αs determination, and top quark mass extraction, as well as enabling the regularization of collinear-unsafe higher-power correlators.
