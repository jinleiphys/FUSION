# High Baryon Densities in Heavy Ion Collisions at Energies Attainable at the BNL Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the CERN Large Hadron Collider

> In very high energy collisions nuclei are practically transparent to each other but produce very hot, nearly baryon-free, matter in the so-called central rapidity region. The energy in the central rapidity region comes from the kinetic energy of the colliding nuclei. We calculate the energy and rapidity loss of the nuclei using the color glass condensate model. This model also predicts the excitation energy of the nuclear fragments. Using a space-time picture of the collision we calculate the baryon and energy densities of the receding baryonic fireballs. For central collisions of gold nuclei at the highest energy attainable at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider, for example, we find baryon densities more than ten times that of atomic nuclei over a large volume.

## Key claim

Using the color glass condensate model, the authors calculate that in central gold-gold collisions at the top RHIC energy, the receding baryonic fireballs reach proper baryon densities more than ten times that of normal nuclear matter over a large volume, with the result being more robust for baryon density than for energy density because the rapidity loss (and therefore compression) is determined mostly within the first few tenths of a fm/c.

## Method

The authors use the McLerran-Venugopalan model to compute the rapidity loss and excitation energy of nuclear slabs as they pass through each other, solving the equations of motion for the projectile slab's four-momentum per unit area using the energy-momentum tensor of the glasma fields. They then apply a Lorentz-invariant compression formula from Anishetty et al. (1980) to convert the rapidity loss into proper baryon density as a function of transverse coordinate and longitudinal position, evaluated at the transition time of 0.6 fm/c when the glasma is assumed to convert to quark-gluon plasma.

## Key numbers

- Maximum proper baryon density of about 3 baryons/fm^3, which is 20 times greater than normal nuclear matter density of 0.155 nucleons/fm^3, for central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV
- The central core of a gold nucleus loses about three units of rapidity within the first 0.1 to 0.2 fm/c; when averaged over the whole nucleus the baryon rapidity loss is about 2.4
- Extrapolating from hydrodynamic initial conditions at tau = 0.6 fm/c back to tau = 0 gives initial energy density epsilon_0 = 123, 142, and 158 GeV/fm^3 for Q = 3, 4, and 5 GeV respectively
- Assuming thermalization at tau = 0.6 fm/c with energy density 20 GeV/fm^3 and baryon density 3 baryons/fm^3 gives temperature T = 299 MeV and baryon chemical potential mu_B = 1061 MeV
- At r_perp where energy density is 5.5 GeV/fm^3 and baryon density is 1.5 baryons/fm^3, the temperature is T = 205 MeV and mu_B = 1007 MeV

## Context

This work extends the original proposal of Anishetty, Koehler and McLerran (1980), who argued that baryonic fireballs would reach about 3.5 times normal nuclear density, by using the modern McLerran-Venugopalan model to compute the rapidity loss and compression more rigorously. The results contradict the prevailing focus on the central rapidity region (following Bjorken 1983) and suggest that the baryon-rich fireballs at high rapidity may reach densities that allow experimental study of the QCD phase diagram at high baryon chemical potential, potentially passing near a critical point. The authors note that detectors at RHIC and LHC focus on central rapidities, with only BRAHMS having coverage up to y = 3.1, and call for further theoretical and experimental studies to probe the equation of state at the highest baryon densities achievable in a laboratory.
