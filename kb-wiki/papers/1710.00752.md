# Constant temperature description of the nuclear level densities

> The spin and parity dependent nuclear level densities (NLD) are calculated for medium-heavy nuclei using shell model techniques. The NLD are used to calculate cross sections and reaction rates of interest for nuclear astrophysics and nuclear energy applications. We investigate a new approach of describing the shell model NLD via a constant temperature parametrization. This approach provides new information about the effects of symmetries on the temperature of the low-lying nuclear states, and it is shown to be more versatile for applications.

## Key claim

The authors propose that a constant temperature parametrization applied separately to each spin and parity (rather than to the total density of states) provides a more versatile and physically justified description of shell-model nuclear level densities, with the caveat that the temperature is not constant across different angular momenta or parities for the same nucleus.

## Method

The authors used the moments method of statistical spectroscopy to calculate spin- and parity-dependent nuclear level densities for sd-shell and fp-shell nuclei, employing the USDA and GXPF1A shell-model Hamiltonians respectively. They then fitted these calculated level densities to a constant temperature formula (Eq. moroj) that assigns independent temperatures and energy shifts to each spin J and parity pi. The fits were performed for a large set of isotopes, and the resulting temperature and energy shift parameters were analyzed as functions of mass number and angular momentum.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous shell-model level density methodology (Horoi et al., 2004-2016) and the constant temperature parametrization approach of von Egidy and Bucurescu (2009, 2012), which applied constant temperature only to the total density of states. The new spin- and parity-dependent constant temperature formula is intended to enable simpler integration of shell-model level densities into reaction codes such as TALYS, replacing the previous table-based input method (Horoi, 2010). The results also connect to studies of pairing phase transitions in nuclei (Horoi et al., 2007; Arima and Iachello, 1987; Dukelsky et al., 1990).
