# Extension of the J-PARC Hadron Experimental Facility: Third White Paper

> The J-PARC Hadron Experimental Facility was constructed with an aim to explore the origin and evolution of matter in the universe through the experiments with intense particle beams. In the past decade, many results on particle and nuclear physics have been obtained at the present facility. To expand the physics programs to unexplored regions never achieved, the extension project of the Hadron Experimental Facility has been extensively discussed. This white paper presents the physics of the extension of the Hadron Experimental Facility for resolving the issues in the fields of the strangeness nuclear physics, hadron physics, and flavor physics.

## Key claim
The extension of the J-PARC Hadron Experimental Facility will enable high-precision spectroscopy of Λ hypernuclei via the (π⁺, K⁺) and (π⁻, K⁺) reactions with sub-MeV resolution, providing crucial constraints on the hyperon-nucleon interaction needed to resolve the "hyperon puzzle" of neutron star structure, but the paper acknowledges that the effects of three-body forces on hypernuclear binding energies are typically less than 1 MeV, requiring experimental accuracy better than 100 keV to distinguish between theoretical models.

## Method
The paper proposes using the High-Intensity High-Resolution (HIHR) beam line at the extended J-PARC Hadron Facility to perform missing-mass spectroscopy of Λ hypernuclei via the (π⁺, K⁺) reaction for a wide mass range of targets, and the double charge exchange (π⁻, K⁺) reaction for neutron-rich hypernuclei. The dispersion match technique will be applied to both π⁺ and π⁻ beams to achieve an energy resolution of 0.4 MeV (FWHM), a factor of more than three improvement over previous KEK-SKS experiments. Yield estimates are based on known cross sections from prior experiments at KEK-PS and BNL-AGS, with assumed cross sections of 10 nb/sr for the DCX reactions on ⁹Be and ¹⁰B targets.

## Key numbers
- Energy resolution of 0.4 MeV (FWHM) expected for both (π⁺, K⁺) and (π⁻, K⁺) reactions at HIHR, compared to 1.5 MeV at KEK-SKS and 3 MeV at BNL-AGS
- π⁻ intensity at HIHR: 2 × 10⁸ π/spill, which is 50 times stronger than the KEK-PS K6 beam line
- Cross section for ¹⁰B(π⁻, K⁺)¹⁰_ΛLi reaction: 11.3 ± 1.9 nb/sr (measured at KEK-PS)
- Upper limit of cross section for ⁶Li(π⁻, K⁺)X reaction (⁶_ΛH search): 0.56 nb/sr
- Expected yields at HIHR: ⁹_ΛHe at 0.092 h⁻¹ and ¹⁰_ΛLi at 0.078 h⁻¹ (assuming 10 nb/sr cross section)
- Beamtime required for 100 counts: 1091 hours (45 days) for ⁹_ΛHe and 1282 hours (53 days) for ¹⁰_ΛLi with 50 kW proton beam
- Expected precision of Λ binding energy measurements: < 100 keV
- Observed neutron star masses requiring stiff EOS: PSR J1614-2230 at 1.97(4) M⊙, PSR J0348+0432 at 2.01(4) M⊙, and MSP J0740+6620 at 2.14(20) M⊙
- Typical cross sections: (K⁻, π⁻) reaction ~1 mb/sr, (π⁺, K⁺) reaction ~10 μb/sr, (e, e′K⁺) reaction ~0.1 μb/sr

## Context
This white paper extends the physics program of the existing J-PARC Hadron Experimental Facility, where previous experiments have studied double-Λ and Ξ hypernuclei and performed YN scattering measurements. The proposed HIHR beam line will improve upon prior (π⁺, K⁺) spectroscopy performed at BNL-AGS and KEK-PS with the SKS spectrometer (Hasegawa 1996, Hotchi 2001), which achieved resolutions of a few MeV. The paper directly addresses the "hyperon puzzle" identified from neutron star mass measurements by Demorest (2010), Antoniadis (2013), and Cromartie (2020), where existing YN interaction models predict maximum masses below 1.5 M⊙. The work builds on theoretical calculations by Yamamoto (2014) showing that three-body repulsive forces (multi-Pomeron exchange) can stiffen the equation of state, but their effects on hypernuclear binding energies are small (< 1 MeV), necessitating the sub-MeV resolution only achievable at HIHR. This facility will enable systematic study of Λ hypernuclei across a wide mass range (A ≤ 208) to constrain effective YN interactions for extrapolation to infinite nuclear matter, complementing ongoing (e, e′K⁺) spectroscopy at JLab and Mainz.
