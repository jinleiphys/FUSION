# Crust-core transition of a neutron star: effect of the temperature under strong magnetic fields

> The effect of temperature on the crust-core transition of a magnetar is studied. The thermodynamical spinodals are used to calculate the transition region within a relativistic mean-field approach for the equation of state. Magnetic fields with intensities $5\times 10^{16}$ G and $5\times 10 ^{17}$ G are considered. It is shown that the effect on the extension of the crust-core transition is washed away for temperatures above $10^{9}$ K for magnetic field intensities $ \lesssim 5\times 10^{16}$ G but may still persist if a magnetic field as high as $5\times 10 ^{17}$G is considered. For temperatures below that value, the effect of the magnetic field on crust-core transition is noticeable and grows as the temperature decreases and, in particular, it is interesting to identify the existence of disconnected non-homogeneous matter above the $B=0$ crust core transition density. Models with different symmetry energy slopes at saturation show quite different behaviors. In particular, a model with a large slope, as suggested by the recent results of PREX-2, predicts the existence of up to four disconnected regions of non-homogeneous matter above the zero magnetic field crust-core transition density.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the effect of strong magnetic fields on extending the crust-core transition region of a magnetar is washed away for temperatures above 10^9 K for magnetic field intensities less than or equal to 5 x 10^16 G, but may persist for fields as high as 5 x 10^17 G; the existence of disconnected non-homogeneous matter regions above the zero-field crust-core transition density depends sensitively on both temperature and the symmetry energy slope.

## Method

The authors used a relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach with the TM1 and TM1e parametrizations to describe nuclear matter, including the anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) of nucleons and Landau quantization of proton energies due to the magnetic field. They calculated the thermodynamical spinodal surface (the region where the curvature matrix of the free energy density has a negative eigenvalue) in the proton-neutron density plane for magnetized matter at finite temperatures. The crust-core transition was determined from the crossing of the spinodal surface by the beta-equilibrium proton fraction curve and by fixed proton fraction curves (Y_p = 0.1 and Y_p = 0.02).

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field intensities considered: B* = 10^3 (4.4 x 10^16 G) and B* = 10^4 (4.4 x 10^17 G), where B* = B/B_ce with B_ce = 4.4 x 10^13 G the critical electron field.
- Temperatures studied: T = 10, 50, 100, and 500 keV (approximately 1.16 x 10^8 K to 5.8 x 10^9 K).
- For the TM1e model at B* = 10^3 and T = 10 keV, the extension of spinodal bands beyond the B = 0 surface reduced from more than 0.01 fm^-3 at T = 0 to less than 0.005 fm^-3.
- For the TM1 model at B* = 10^3 and T = 10 keV, the beta-equilibrium curve defines five disconnected non-homogeneous regions; at T = 100 keV there are no disconnected unstable regions.
- For the TM1 model at B* = 10^4 and T = 10 keV, at least three disconnected non-homogeneous regions exist for beta-equilibrium matter; this reduces to two at T = 100 keV and one at T = 500 keV.
- The energy difference between Landau levels is approximately eB/M* ≈ 0.5 MeV for B* = 10^3.
- The AMM contribution is approximately |kappa_p + kappa_n| mu_N B ≈ 2 x 10^-5 B* MeV, about one order of magnitude smaller than the Landau quantization effect.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Fang et al. (2016, 2017) and Avancini et al. (2018) that predicted enlarged crust regions and disconnected non-homogeneous matter in magnetars at zero temperature using dynamical and thermodynamical spinodal approaches. It also builds on Rabhi et al. (2008, 2009) and Chen et al. (2017) who used thermodynamical spinodals. The paper specifically addresses the competition between Landau quantization and temperature, following the initial finite-temperature study by Fang et al. (2017a) that considered temperatures below 1 MeV for fields up to 5 x 10^16 G. The results enable predictions for the crust structure of cooling magnetars and inform whether solid regions can form near the crust-core transition during thermal evolution, with implications for understanding magnetar activity such as crust fractures.
