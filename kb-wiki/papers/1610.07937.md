# Unexpected properties of interactions of high energy protons

> Experimental data on proton-proton interactions in high energy collisions show quite a special and unexpected behaviour of the proportion of elastic scattering compared to inelastic processes with increasing energy. It decreases at the beginning (at comparatively low energies) but then starts increasing. From Intersecting Storage Rings (ISR) energies of 23.5 - 62.5 GeV up to higher energies 7 - 13 TeV at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) it increases by a factor more than 1.5! According to intuitive classical ideas we would expect a stable tendency with increasing proportion of the break-down of protons compared to their survival probability. One can assume that either the asymptotic freedom or the extremely short time of flight of high energy protons through each other are in charge of such a surprising effect. The unquestionable principle of unitarity combined with the available experimental data on elastic scattering is used to get new conclusions about the shape of the interaction region of colliding protons. Its evolution at present energies is considered. Some predictions about its behaviour at even higher energies are described with different assumptions on relative roles of elastic scattering and inelastic processes. The shape can transform rather drastically if the proportion of elastic processes keeps rising. This unexpected property leads to an unexpected corollary. The possible origin of the effect and its interrelation to the strong interaction dynamics are speculated.

## Key claim

The proportion of elastic scattering relative to inelastic processes in proton-proton collisions, which decreases at lower energies, unexpectedly begins increasing from ISR energies (23.5–62.5 GeV) to LHC energies (7–13 TeV) by a factor more than 1.5, contradicting classical intuition that higher-energy collisions should favor proton breakup.

## Method

The author applies the unitarity condition of the S-matrix, which relates elastic and inelastic scattering amplitudes, to experimental data on elastic proton-proton scattering. Using the Fourier–Bessel transform to convert momentum-space data into impact-parameter space, the transverse spatial profiles of the inelastic interaction region are derived from the measured differential cross sections and total cross sections. The analysis relies on the exponential parameterization of the diffraction cone and neglects the small contribution of the real part of the elastic amplitude, justified by experimental data showing its ratio to the imaginary part is about 0.12–0.145 at t=0 and even smaller elsewhere.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of inelastic to elastic cross sections decreases from about 5 at ISR energies (62.5 GeV) to about 3 at LHC energies (7 TeV).
- The parameter ζ (proportional to the share of elastic processes) decreases from about 0.98 at √s = 4.11 GeV to about 0.67 at ISR energy 62.5 GeV, then increases to 1.00–1.04 at LHC energy 7 TeV.
- The absorption at central collisions G(s,0) reaches 1.00 at LHC 7 TeV, indicating full absorption for head-on collisions.
- The ratio of the real to imaginary parts of the forward elastic amplitude ρ₀ is measured at LHC energies in the range 0.12–0.145.
- The contribution of the real part to the differential cross section at t=0 is only about 1–2%.
- The upper limit of the real-to-imaginary ratio at the dip is ≤ 0.006.
- The tail region beyond the diffraction cone contributes less than 3.9% to ζ and less than 2×10⁻³ correction to G(7 TeV,0).

## Context

This work extends the observation that hadronic total cross sections increase with energy, first seen in the "Serpukhov effect" for kaon-proton collisions at Protvino (up to 70 GeV lab energy), and later established for proton-proton collisions. The paper uses the unitarity condition, a principle of conservation of total probability, to derive spatial profiles of the interaction region from elastic scattering data without phenomenological input. The results enable predictions about the transition from the current BEL-scenario (Blacker, Edgier, Larger) to a possible toroidal elastic hollow (TEH) regime at higher energies if the proportion of elastic scattering continues to increase, and have already been used to interpret jet production data at 7 TeV.
