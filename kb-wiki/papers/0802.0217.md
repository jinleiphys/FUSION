# Aspects of Hadron Physics

> Detailed investigations of the structure of hadrons are essential for understanding how matter is constructed from the quarks and gluons of Quantum chromodynamics (QCD), and amongst the questions posed to modern hadron physics, three stand out. What is the rigorous, quantitative mechanism responsible for confinement? What is the connection between confinement and dynamical chiral symmetry breaking? And are these phenomena together sufficient to explain the origin of more than 98% of the mass of the observable universe? Such questions may only be answered using the full machinery of nonperturbative relativistic quantum field theory. This contribution provides a perspective on progress toward answering these key questions. In so doing it will provide an overview of the contemporary application of Dyson-Schwinger equations in Hadron Physics. The presentation assumes that the reader is familiar with the concepts and notation of relativistic quantum mechanics, with the functional integral formulation of quantum field theory and with regularisation and renormalisation in its perturbative formulation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that confinement and dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) are the dominant emergent phenomena of QCD, and that they are likely manifestations of the same nonperturbative mechanism, but it hedges that a rigorous, quantitative mechanism for confinement remains unknown and that the connection between confinement and DCSB is still an open question.

## Method

The authors present a pedagogical overview of the contemporary application of Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) to hadron physics, using a simplified Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with a momentum-independent contact interaction and a gluon mass-scale to illustrate how the quark gap equation can yield a nonzero dressed-quark mass in the chiral limit. They then contrast this with a model that produces a momentum-dependent mass function to discuss the relationship between DCSB and confinement.

## Key numbers

* The proton's magnetic moment is \(\mu_p = (1 + 1.79) \, \mu_D\), where \(\mu_D = e/(2M)\), first measured by Stern (1933).
* The ratio of proton form factors \(\mu_p G_E^p(Q^2)/G_M^p(Q^2)\) shows a marked discrepancy between Rosenbluth separation and polarisation transfer experiments for \(Q^2 \gsim 2\) GeV\(^2\).
* In the NJL model, a nontrivial DCSB solution exists for a gluon mass-scale \(m_G < m_G^{\rm cr} = 1/(\surd{3}\,\pi) \simeq 0.2\) GeV (with \(\Lambda \sim 1\) GeV as the regularisation scale).
* The pion mass is \(\sim 140\) MeV, while a naive constituent-quark estimate would give \(\sim 700\) MeV; the \(\rho\)-meson mass is 770 MeV.
* The ratio \(m_\rho^2 / m_\pi^2 = 30\), and the ratio of radial excitation energies \(m_{\pi_1}^2 / m_\pi^2 = 86\) versus \(m_{\rho_1}^2 / m_\rho^2 = 3.5\).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to understand hadron structure via nonperturbative QCD methods, building on the Dyson-Schwinger equation framework reviewed in earlier works such as Roberts and Williams (1994) and Maris and Roberts (2003). It directly addresses the puzzle raised by the discrepancy between Rosenbluth and polarisation transfer measurements of the proton form factor ratio at Jefferson Lab (Jones 2000, Gayou 2002), and it enables future calculations of hadron observables from continuum strong QCD, including the role of two-photon exchange corrections.
