# Torsional oscillations of strange stars

> Strange stars are one of the hypothetical compact stellar objects that can be formed after a supernova explosion. The existence of these objects relies on the absolute stability of strange {\it collapsed} quark matter with respect to standard nuclear matter. We discuss simple models of strange stars with a bare quark matter surface, thus standard nuclear matter is completely absent. In these models an electric dipole layer a few hundreds Fermi thick should exist close to the star surface. Studying the torsional oscillations of the electrically charged layer we estimate the emitted power, finding that it is of the order of $10^{45}$ erg/s, meaning that these objects would be among the brightest compact sources in the heavens. The associated relaxation times are very uncertain, with values ranging between microseconds and minutes, depending on the crust thickness. Although part of the radiated power should be absorbed by the electrosphere surrounding the strange star, a sizable fraction of photons should escape and be detectable.

# Torsional oscillations of strange stars

## Key claim

The emitted power from torsional oscillations of the electric dipole layer at the surface of a bare strange star is estimated to be of order 10^45 erg/s, making these objects among the brightest compact sources; however, the associated relaxation times are highly uncertain, ranging from microseconds to minutes depending on crust thickness, and a sizable fraction of the radiated power should be absorbed by the surrounding electrosphere.

## Method

The author constructs equilibrium models of bare strange stars using a Taylor-expanded equation of state for quark matter with two parameter sets (Models A and B), solves the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain stellar configurations, and determines the electric charge distribution near the star surface by solving Poisson's equation with a planar interface approximation. The torsional oscillations of the crystalline color superconducting crust are studied in the Newtonian approximation, assuming the lowest-energy nontrivial mode (l=1, n=1) is excited by a stellar glitch, and the emitted electromagnetic power is estimated using a magnetic dipole oscillator model.

## Key numbers

- Emitted power from torsional oscillations: of order 10^45 erg/s (abstract); for a > 1/2, approximated as P(a) ~ 6.4 x 10^40 (1-a)^(-5) (nu/nu_0)^2 (rho_R/rho_0)^(-3) (R/10 km)^(-1) (alpha E_glitch / E_glitch^Vela) (Q_+/Q)^2 erg/s
- Reference shear modulus: nu_0 = 2.47 MeV/fm^3
- Shear modulus scaling: nu ~ nu_0 (Delta/10 MeV)^2 (mu/400 MeV)^2, with Delta ranging between 5 MeV and 25 MeV
- Model A: mass M = 1.27 M_sun, radius R ~ 7.1 km
- Model B: mass M ~ 2.0 M_sun, radius R ~ 10.9 km
- Oscillation frequency for l=1, n=1 mode: omega_11 ~ 0.06 (nu/nu_0)^(1/2) (delta_R/1 km)^(-1) (rho_R/rho_0)^(-1/2) MHz
- Reference glitch energy: E_glitch^Vela = 3 x 10^(-12) M_sun
- Electric dipole layer thickness: a few hundred Fermi
- Relaxation times: ranging between microseconds and minutes

## Context

This work extends the pioneering studies of bare strange stars by Alcock et al. (1986) and Haensel et al. (1986), who first identified the existence of a strong electric dipole layer at the star surface. The author builds on the crystalline color superconducting phase formalism reviewed by Anglani et al. (2013) and the shear modulus calculation by Mannarelli et al. (2007). The results suggest that torsional oscillations of strange stars could explain phenomena such as Rotating Radio Transients (observed by McLaughlin et al. 2005, Lorimer et al. 2007, Thornton et al. 2013) and giant magnetar x-ray flares (observed by Strohmayer et al. 2005), though the standard explanation of magnetar flares involves seismic vibrations of a nuclear crust triggered by starquakes. The work enables future observational searches for strange stars through their electromagnetic emission signatures, though the author notes that no strong evidence for their existence has accumulated to date.
