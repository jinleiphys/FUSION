# Physics with ELFE

> A 15-30 GeV continuous beam electron facility has been proposed by nuclear physicists in Europe to study how color forces build up hadrons from quarks and gluons. This project and its physics case are briefly reviewed. The recommendations of NuPECC, the Nuclear Physics Committee of the European Science Foundation are presented.

# Physics with ELFE

## Key claim

The paper argues that a 15-30 GeV continuous beam electron facility (ELFE) is necessary to study how color forces build up hadrons from quarks and gluons, but explicitly acknowledges that this research lies in the confinement regime where the coupling is strong and only crude theoretical models inspired by QCD currently exist; the authors state that "one does not understand how quarks build up hadrons" despite knowing the microscopic theory.

## Method

The paper reviews the physics case for the proposed ELFE (Electron Laboratory For Europe) facility, a 15-30 GeV high-luminosity continuous beam electron accelerator, and presents the recommendations of NuPECC (Nuclear Physics Committee of the European Science Foundation). The authors describe the experimental program focused on exclusive and semi-inclusive electron scattering from nuclear targets, using the nucleus as a microscopic detector to study the time evolution of quark configurations into hadrons. They discuss specific research topics including exclusive processes, color transparency, hadronization in nuclear medium, heavy flavor production, and short range structure of nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy range: 15 to 30 GeV
- Energy resolution FWHM: 3 × 10⁻⁴ at 15 GeV; 10⁻³ at 30 GeV
- Duty factor: approximately 100%
- Beam current: 10 to 50 μA
- Polarized beams: P > 80%
- Target luminosity for magnetic focusing spectrometers: 10³⁸ nucleons/cm²/s
- Momentum resolution for detectors: 5 × 10⁻⁴
- Characteristic time scale for hadron formation in rest frame: τ₀ ∼ 1 fm/c
- Minimum electron beam energy for charm production: 15 GeV
- SLAC NE-18 experiment explored color transparency in (e,e'p) reaction for H, C, Fe, and Au in Q² range of 1-7 GeV², with no effect observed
- CEBAF reached nominal energy E = 4 GeV at beginning of 1995, with final energy expected to be of order 6 GeV

## Context

This paper extends the discussion of electromagnetic nuclear physics from existing facilities (Amsterdam, Bonn, Mainz, MIT-Bates, and CEBAF at 4-6 GeV) to a proposed higher-energy facility. The authors contrast ELFE with existing accelerators designed for electroweak physics (such as LEP for Z₀ decays) which cannot access exclusive reactions due to the smallness of exclusive amplitudes at large transfers. The paper builds on prior work including the EMC and NMC collaborations' deep inelastic scattering experiments on nuclei, the SLAC NE-18 color transparency experiment, and Brookhaven proton-proton elastic scattering data. The NuPECC recommendations presented in the paper call for proceeding toward construction of a European facility providing electron beams of high duty cycle and brilliance in the 15-30 GeV energy range, with the next steps including advancement of superconducting RF technology and formation of an ELFE coordinating group.
