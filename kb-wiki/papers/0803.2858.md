# Nuclear excitation by electron capture followed by fast x-ray emission

> The resonance strength of the two-step process of nuclear excitation by electron capture followed by $γ$ decay of the nucleus can be significantly increased in highly charged ions if the resonant capture proceeds via an excited electronic state with subsequent fast x-ray emission. For fully ionized $^{238}_{92}\mathrm{U}$ and $^{232}_{90}\mathrm{Th}$, the {x-ray} decay stabilizes the system against internal conversion of the captured electron, with an increase of both nuclear lifetimes and resonance strengths of up to two orders of magnitude compared with the case when occupied atomic orbitals prevent the x-ray de-excitation. Applications of this effect to the measurement of the not yet experimentally observed nuclear excitation by electron capture and to dense astrophysical plasmas are discussed.

## Key claim

The resonance strength of nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) can be significantly increased in highly charged ions if the resonant capture proceeds via an excited electronic state with subsequent fast x-ray emission (NEECX), rather than capture directly into the electronic ground state. This enhancement occurs because the x-ray decay stabilizes the system against internal conversion of the captured electron, but only when the K-shell internal conversion channel is energetically forbidden.

## Method

The authors used a Feshbach projection operator formalism to calculate the total resonance strength (integrated cross section over continuum electron energy) for the three-step process of NEEC into an excited L-shell orbital, followed by fast x-ray decay to the K-shell, and subsequent gamma decay of the nucleus. Electronic widths and transition rates were calculated using the OSCL92 module of the GRASP92 package for fully ionized 238U and 232Th ions, considering capture into the 2s, 2p1/2, and 2p3/2 orbitals.

## Key numbers

- For 238U, the first excited nuclear state energy is 44.910 keV (E2 transition from 2+ to 0+); for 232Th, it is 49.369 keV.
- The K-shell binding energy is -131.815 keV for U91+ and -125.248 keV for Th89+, making K-shell internal conversion energetically forbidden.
- L-shell electronic lifetimes in U91+ are 5 x 10^-15 s (2s), 2 x 10^-17 s (2p1/2), and 3 x 10^-17 s (2p3/2); the nuclear excited state lives approximately 2 x 10^-10 s in neutral atoms and about 10^-7 s in bare ions.
- The gamma decay rates are A_gamma = 5.40 x 10^6 s^-1 for 238U and A_gamma = 6.65 x 10^6 s^-1 for 232Th.
- Partial internal conversion coefficients for one-electron configurations in U91+ are alpha = 4 (2s), 129 (2p1/2), and 67 (2p3/2); in Th89+ they are alpha = 2 (2s), 69 (2p1/2), and 36 (2p3/2).
- The nuclear mean lifetimes for the first excited state in H-like ions (after NEECX) are determined solely by gamma decay, showing a two orders of magnitude increase compared to cases where the x-ray decay does not suppress internal conversion.
- The estimated NEECX event rate at the ESR facility is 4.6 events per minute (273 events per hour) for maximum beam luminosity.

## Context

This work extends theoretical studies of NEEC that previously considered only electron recombination into the electronic ground state (Cue 1989, Kimball 1978, Harston 1998, Palffy 2006). The authors introduce NEECX as analogous to the atomic process of resonant transfer and excitation followed by x-ray emission (RTEX). The proposed experimental scenario using the ESR storage ring at GSI Darmstadt builds on earlier proposals for beam-foil geometry (Cue 1989) but avoids Coulomb excitation background by using an electron target. The results enable a realistic pathway for the first experimental observation of NEEC, which had not been observed in prior attempts (Izawa 1997, GANIL experiments). The work also suggests that NEECX should be considered in astrophysical plasma models, particularly for cosmochronometers such as the 187Re-187Os system (Bosch 1996) and for triggering nuclear isomer decay.
