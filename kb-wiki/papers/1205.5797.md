# Fission modes of mercury isotopes

> Background: Recent experiments on beta-delayed fission in the mercury-lead region and the discovery of asym- metric fission in 180 Hg [1] have stimulated theoretical interest in the mechanism of fission in heavy nuclei. Purpose: We study fission modes and fusion valleys in 180 Hg and 198 Hg to reveal the role of shell effects in pre-scission region and explain the experimentally observed fragment mass asymmetry and its variation with A. Methods: We use the self-consistent nuclear density functional theory employing Skyrme and Gogny energy density functionals. Results: The potential energy surfaces in multi-dimensional space of collective coordinates, including elongation, triaxiality, reflection-asymmetry, and necking, are calculated for 180 Hg and 198 Hg. The asymmetric fission valleys - well separated from fusion valleys associated with nearly spherical fragments - are found in in both cases. The density distributions at scission configurations are studied and related to the experimentally observed mass splits. Conclusions: The energy density functionals SkM\ast and D1S give a very consistent description of the fission process in 180 Hg and 198 Hg. We predict a transition from asymmetric fission in 180 Hg towards more symmetric distribution of fission fragments in 198 Hg. For 180 Hg, both models yield 100 Ru/80 Kr as the most probable split. For 198 Hg, the most likely split is 108 Ru/90 Kr in HFB-D1S and 110 Ru/88 Kr in HFB-SkM\ast.

## Key claim

The paper predicts a transition from asymmetric fission in 180Hg towards a more symmetric fragment mass distribution in 198Hg, based on self-consistent density functional theory calculations. The authors hedge that this transition is governed by shell effects in pre-scission configurations and that the potential energy surface for 198Hg is "fairly soft" in the octupole direction, meaning the symmetric and asymmetric pathways are nearly degenerate rather than one being clearly dominant.

## Method

The authors performed constrained self-consistent nuclear density functional theory (DFT) calculations using the Skyrme SkM* functional (with mixed pairing) and the Gogny D1S functional. They computed potential energy surfaces in a multi-dimensional collective space including elongation (Q20), triaxiality (Q22), reflection-asymmetry (Q30), and necking (Q40 or QN), for 180Hg and 198Hg. Static fission pathways were identified by following energy minima in this collective space, and density distributions at scission configurations were analyzed to determine the most probable fragment mass splits.

## Key numbers

- For 180Hg, both SkM* and D1S models yield 100Ru/80Kr as the most probable split; the static scission point in HFB-D1S gives a mass split of 99/81, consistent with the experimental asymmetry of 100(1)/80(1) from Andreyev (2010).
- For 198Hg, the most likely split is 108Ru/90Kr in HFB-D1S and 110Ru/88Kr in HFB-SkM*; the static scission point in HFB-D1S gives a mass split of 108/90.
- In 180Hg, the asymmetric fission valley (aEF) branches away from the symmetric valley below Q20 = 100 b.
- In 180Hg, the fusion valleys (sFu and aFu) appear very low in energy above Q20 ≈ 200 b.
- The neck in the near-scission configuration of 180Hg (at Q20 = 260 b, QN = 0.3) contains 8 protons and 10 neutrons.
- The neck in the near-scission configuration of 198Hg (at Q20 = 330 b, QN = 0.33) contains 8 protons and 12 neutrons.
- The deformed configuration of 72Ge used to match the lighter fragment in 180Hg has Q20 = 8 b at an excitation energy of 3.4 MeV.
- The ground state of 80Ge used to match the lighter fragment in 198Hg has Q20 = 2.4 b; the deformed 98Zr fragment has Q20 = 12 b.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical analysis of Andreyev (2010), who discovered asymmetric fission in 180Hg via beta-delayed fission experiments, and who offered an explanation using the macroscopic-microscopic model. The present self-consistent DFT calculations confirm that the asymmetric fission valley is well separated from fusion valleys associated with nearly spherical fragments, and explain the observed transition from asymmetric fission in 180Hg to a broader, more symmetric mass distribution in 198Hg as reported by Mulgin (1998) and Itkis (1990, 1991). The results enable future dynamical calculations of fragment mass distributions and highlight the importance of shell effects between the fission saddle and scission in pre-actinide nuclei, as also discussed by Karlsson (2008).
