# Parity Violating Electron Scattering Measurements of Neutron Densities

> Parity violating electron scattering allows model independent measurements of neutron densities that are free from most strong interaction uncertainties. In this paper we present statistical error estimates for a variety of experiments. The neutron radius $R_n$ can be measured in several nuclei, as long as the nuclear excited states are not too low in energy. We present error estimates for $R_n$ measurements in $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca, $^{112}$Sn, $^{120}$Sn, $^{124}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb. In general, we find that the smaller the nucleus, the easier the measurement. This is because smaller nuclei can be measured at higher momentum transfers where the parity violating asymmetry $A_{pv}$ is larger. Also in general, the more neutron rich the isotope, the easier the measurement, because neutron rich isotopes have larger weak charges and larger $A_{pv}$. Measuring $R_n$ in $^{48}$Ca appears very promising because it has a higher figure of merit than $^{208}$Pb. In addition, $R_n(^{48}$Ca) may be more easily related to two nucleon and three nucleon interactions, including very interesting three neutron forces, than $R_n(^{208}$Pb). After measuring $R_n$, one can constrain the surface thickness of the neutron density $a_n$ with a second measurement at somewhat higher momentum transfers. We present statistical error estimates for measuring $a_n$ in $^{48}$Ca, $^{120}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb. Again, we find that $a_n$ is easier to measure in smaller nuclei.

## Key claim

Parity violating electron scattering enables model-independent measurements of neutron densities, with statistical error estimates showing that smaller nuclei and more neutron-rich isotopes are easier to measure because they allow higher momentum transfers where the parity violating asymmetry is larger.

## Method

The authors calculated parity violating asymmetries \(A_{pv}\) by solving the Dirac equation for electrons scattering in both the Coulomb potential and a weak interaction axial vector potential, using model neutron densities from Skyrme Hartree-Fock and relativistic mean field interactions. They then estimated statistical errors for measuring the neutron radius \(R_n\) and surface thickness \(a_n\) by computing figures of merit that combine the differential cross section, the asymmetry, and the sensitivity of the asymmetry to changes in the quantity of interest, assuming experimental parameters appropriate for Hall A at Jefferson Laboratory.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{208}\)Pb at 1.05 GeV and 5 degrees, a 1% statistical error on \(R_n\) (\(\pm 0.05\) fm) can be obtained in 13 days of running.
- For \(^{48}\)Ca at 1.8 GeV and 5 degrees, a 1% statistical error on \(R_n\) requires only 5.5 days.
- For \(^{40}\)Ca at 1.90 GeV and 5 degrees, a 1% statistical error on \(R_n\) is possible in 11 days.
- For \(^{48}\)Ca at 2.2 GeV and 4 degrees, a 1% statistical error on \(R_n\) needs only 3.5 days.
- For \(^{208}\)Pb at 1.8 GeV and 5 degrees, the surface thickness \(a_n\) can be measured to 7.9% statistical error in 30 days, assuming \(a_n \approx 0.55\) fm.
- For \(^{48}\)Ca at 2.15 GeV and 5 degrees, the surface thickness \(a_n\) can be measured to 2.96% statistical error in 30 days, assuming \(a_n \approx 0.50\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the Lead Radius Experiment (PREX), which measured \(A_{pv}\) for 1.05 GeV electrons on \(^{208}\)Pb at five degrees, by providing statistical error estimates for additional nuclei including \(^{40}\)Ca, \(^{48}\)Ca, \(^{112}\)Sn, \(^{120}\)Sn, \(^{124}\)Sn, and \(^{208}\)Pb. The neutron radius of \(^{208}\)Pb has a strong correlation with the pressure of neutron matter near 0.1 fm\(^{-3}\), as shown by Hebeler et al. using chiral perturbation theory, and with the radius of neutron stars as studied by Ozel et al. and Steiner et al. Measuring \(R_n\) in \(^{48}\)Ca may enable more direct connections to microscopic two-nucleon and three-nucleon forces, including three-neutron forces, than is possible with \(^{208}\)Pb, and can test predictions from coupled cluster or no core shell model calculations that are not yet feasible for lead.
