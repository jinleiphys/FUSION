# Temperature effects in the nuclear isoscaling

> The properties of the nuclear isoscaling at finite temperature are investigated and the extent to which its parameter $α$ holds information on the symmetry energy is examined. We show that, although finite temperature effects invalidate the analytical formulas that relate the isoscaling parameter $α$ to those of the mass formula, the symmetry energy remains the main ingredient that dictates the behavior of $α$ at finite temperatures, even for very different sources. This conclusion is not obvious as it is not true in the vanishing temperature limit, where analytical formulas are available. Our results also reveal that different statistical ensembles lead to essentially the same conclusions based on the isoscaling analysis, for the temperatures usually assumed in theoretical calculations in the nuclear multifragmentation process.

## Key claim

Although finite temperature effects invalidate the analytical formulas that relate the isoscaling parameter α to the symmetry energy coefficient of the mass formula, the symmetry energy remains the main ingredient dictating the behavior of α at temperatures above about 5 MeV, even for very different sources; this conclusion is not obvious because it is not true in the vanishing temperature limit where analytical formulas are available.

## Method

The authors used the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) with fragments at saturation density, employing two versions of a liquid-drop mass formula (LDM1 and LDM2) to calculate fragment yields in grand-canonical, canonical, and micro-canonical ensembles. They extracted the isoscaling parameter α by fitting the ratio of yields from different source pairs (e.g., ¹¹²Sn/¹²⁴Sn, ¹⁶⁸Re/¹²⁴Sn) and reconstructed the apparent symmetry energy coefficient C_sym^Unc from α using the standard zero-temperature formula, comparing it to the fixed input value a_v k_v.

## Key numbers

- For the LDM1 formula, the input symmetry energy coefficient is a_v k_v = 22.39 MeV; for LDM2, it is a_v k_v = 27.80 MeV.
- For T > 5.0 MeV, the uncorrected apparent symmetry energy C_sym^Unc becomes the same for all source pairs tested, despite large differences at low temperatures.
- For sources with Δ(Z/A)² = 0, α approaches zero for T ≳ 5.0 MeV, showing that Coulomb and surface terms become negligible at high temperatures.
- A polynomial fit for T > 5.0 MeV gives α = 4(C_sym/T)Δ(Z/A)² × (a_0 + a_1 T + a_2 T² + a_3 T³), with coefficients for LDM1: a_0 = 1.3338, a_1 = -0.131842 MeV⁻¹, a_2 = 0.0156409 MeV⁻², a_3 = -0.000531862 MeV⁻³; for LDM2: a_0 = 2.6431, a_1 = -0.292421 MeV⁻¹, a_2 = 0.0338321 MeV⁻², a_3 = -0.00126698 MeV⁻³.
- Changing the level density parameter ε₀ from 16 MeV to 4 MeV (which changes f*(T) for A=100 at T=6 MeV from -319.4 MeV to -994.4 MeV) had very little effect on C_sym^Unc for T ≳ 5.0 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the isoscaling formalism developed by Tsang et al. (2001) and Botvina et al. (2002) by testing whether the zero-temperature relationship between α and the symmetry energy holds at the finite temperatures (T > 5 MeV) relevant to nuclear multifragmentation. It contradicts the assumption that Eq. (α = 4C_sym/T[(Z₁/A₁)²-(Z₂/A₂)²]) remains accurate at these temperatures, as used in studies by the INDRA collaboration (2005) and Botvina et al. (2006). The results enable more careful interpretation of isoscaling analyses and suggest that α remains a useful probe of the symmetry energy, but quantitative extraction of C_sym requires either numerical modeling or the polynomial correction provided.
