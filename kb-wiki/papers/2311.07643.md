# Isolating perturbative QCD splittings in heavy-ion collisions

> We define a new strategy to scan jet substructure in heavy-ion collisions. The scope is multifold: (i) test the dominance of vacuum jet dynamics at early times, (ii) capture the transition from coherent to incoherent jet energy loss, and (iii) study elastic scatterings in the medium, which are either hard and perturbative or soft and responsible for jet thermalisation. To achieve that, we analyse the angular distribution of the hardest splitting, $θ_{\rm hard}$, above a transverse momentum scale, $k_t^{\rm min}$, in high-$p_t$ jets. Sufficiently high values of $k_t^{\rm min}$ target the regime in which the observable is uniquely determined by vacuum-like splittings and energy loss, leaving the jet substructure unmodified compared to proton-proton collisions. Decreasing $k_t^{\rm min}$ enhances the sensitivity to the relation between energy loss and the intra-jet structure and, in particular, to observe signatures of colour decoherence at small angles. At wider angles it also becomes sensitive to hard elastic scatterings with the medium and, therefore, the perturbative regime of medium response. Choosing $k_t^{\rm min}\approx 0$ leads to order one effects of non-perturbative origin such as hadronisation and, potentially, soft scatterings responsible for jet thermalisation. We perform a comprehensive analysis of this observable with three state-of-the-art jet-quenching Monte Carlo event generators. Our study paves the way for defining jet observables in heavy-ion collisions dominated by perturbative QCD and thus calculable from first principles.

## Key claim

By imposing a sufficiently high transverse momentum cut \(k_t^{\rm min} \gg \sqrt{\hat q L}\) on the hardest splitting in high-\(p_t\) jets, one can isolate a regime where jet substructure in heavy-ion collisions is dominated by vacuum-like QCD splittings and energy loss, leaving the intra-jet angular distribution unmodified compared to proton-proton collisions; lowering \(k_t^{\rm min}\) progressively introduces sensitivity to colour decoherence, hard elastic scatterings, and finally non-perturbative hadronisation.

## Method

The authors analyse the angular distribution \(\theta_{\rm hard}\) of the hardest splitting (maximum \(k_t = \min(p_{t1},p_{t2})\theta\)) in anti-\(k_t\) jets with \(R=0.2\) and \(p_t > 400\) GeV, reclustered with the Cambridge/Aachen algorithm. They impose a transverse momentum threshold \(k_t^{\rm min}\) on the splitting, scanning values of 20, 5, and 1 GeV to slice the radiation phase space, and compare results from three jet-quenching Monte Carlo event generators (JetMed, Hybrid, Jewel) against a proton-proton baseline from Powheg+Pythia8 at NLO.

## Key numbers

- Jet selection: anti-\(k_t\) with \(R=0.2\), \(p_t > 400\) GeV, \(|\eta| < 2.8\).
- Three values of \(k_t^{\rm min}\) are explored: 20 GeV, 5 GeV, and 1 GeV.
- For \(k_t^{\rm min}=20\) GeV, formation times are parametrically \(t_f < 0.4\) fm; hadronisation corrections are within statistical uncertainty, demonstrating pQCD purity.
- For \(k_t^{\rm min}=5\) GeV, hadronisation corrections are less than 20% in a wide angular region.
- For \(k_t^{\rm min}=1\) GeV, hadronisation corrections reach up to 50%.
- In JetMed, the typical relative transverse momentum of medium-induced emissions is \(\langle k_t\rangle = \sqrt{\hat q L} \approx 2.4\) GeV, with \(\hat q = 1.5\) GeV\(^2\)/fm and \(L=4\) fm; the decoherence angle is \(\theta_c = 2/\sqrt{\hat q L^3} = 0.04\).
- In the Hybrid model, a representative resolution length is \(L_{\rm res} \approx 0.5\) fm for central PbPb collisions with \(L \sim 6\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the strategy of using jet substructure to disentangle vacuum and medium effects in heavy-ion collisions, building on prior studies of colour coherence and selection biases (Mehtar-Tani 2010, Casalderrey-Solana 2011, Caucal 2021) and on dynamical grooming observables (Mehtar-Tani 2019, Caucal 2021). It introduces a controlled \(k_t^{\rm min}\) scan to separate perturbative from non-perturbative regimes, enabling future measurements at LHC Runs 3 and 4 that can test factorisation of vacuum and medium physics, the angular dependence of energy loss, and the role of elastic scatterings.
