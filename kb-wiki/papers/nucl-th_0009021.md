# Complex Langevin Equation and the Many-Fermion Problem

> We study the utility of a complex Langevin (CL) equation as an alternative for the Monte Carlo (MC) procedure in the evaluation of expectation values occurring in fermionic many-body problems. We find that a CL approach is natural in cases where non-positive definite probability measures occur, and remains accurate even when the corresponding MC calculation develops a severe ``sign problem''. While the convergence of CL averages cannot be guaranteed in principle, we show how convergent results can be obtained in three examples ranging from simple one-dimensional integrals over quantum mechanical models to a schematic shell model path integral.

## Key claim

The complex Langevin (CL) equation can serve as a viable alternative to Monte Carlo (MC) methods for fermionic many-body problems with non-positive definite probability measures, remaining accurate even when MC calculations develop a severe sign problem; however, convergence of CL averages cannot be guaranteed in principle, and the method works only when fixed points of the Langevin equation lie in the complex plane rather than on the real axis.

## Method

The authors replace the standard Monte Carlo sampling of auxiliary fields in the Auxiliary Field Path-Integral (AFPI) method with a complex Langevin stochastic process, solving coupled real and imaginary Langevin equations for the auxiliary fields using the two-step algorithm of Greenside and Helfand. They apply this approach to three test systems: one-dimensional integrals abstracted from fermionic problems, a quantum mechanical toy model of a single j-shell with variable filling and cranking, and the Lipkin schematic shell model. For cases where fixed points lie on the real axis, they modify the action by extending it into the complex plane (e.g., replacing cosines with exponentials) to shift fixed points away from the real line.

## Key numbers

- For the one-dimensional integral ⟨σ²⟩_{N=1} = 1 − z², the CL method using the extended action reproduces the exact result while the MC calculation deteriorates as the average sign approaches zero.
- For the toy model with a j=5/2 shell, CL calculations remain accurate for cranking frequencies ω up to at least 1.5, where the MC sign essentially disappears (sign → 0 for ω > 3).
- For the Lipkin model with N=3 particles at β=2.0 and Δβ=0.1, the CL calculation maintains accuracy as a function of cranking frequency ω while the MC signal-to-noise ratio deteriorates.
- For the toy model with N=2 particles in a cranked j=5/2 shell at β=1, the MC sign drops quickly with increasing ω and becomes useless beyond ω ≈ 1.5.

## Context

This work extends the AFPI Monte Carlo method of Lang, Johnson, Koonin, and Ormand (1993) and addresses the sign problem that arises for repulsive interactions and odd-particle configurations in nuclear shell model calculations. The CL approach had been previously explored in lattice gauge theory for complex actions (CLREF, 1980s) but was largely abandoned due to concerns about non-stationarity. The authors show that non-stationarity can be avoided for Hamiltonians whose Langevin equations have fixed points in the complex plane, and suggest the method should be tested in lattice gauge calculations at finite chemical potential.
