# Elastic, Inelastic, and Path Length Fluctuations in Jet Tomography

> We propose a possible perturbative QCD solution to the heavy quark tomography problem posed by recent non-photonic single electron data from central Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s} = 200$ AGeV. Jet quenching theory is extended to include (1) elastic as well as (2) inelastic parton energy losses and (3) jet path length fluctuations. The three effects combine to reduce the discrepancy between theory and the data without violating the global entropy bounds from multiplicity and elliptic flow data. We also check for consistency with the pion suppression data out to 20 GeV. Fluctuations of the geometric jet path lengths and the difference between the widths of fluctuations of elastic and inelastic energy loss play essential roles in the proposed solution.

## Key claim

The authors propose that including both elastic and inelastic parton energy losses together with jet path length fluctuations in perturbative QCD jet tomography can significantly reduce the discrepancy between theory and non-photonic single electron data from central Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s}=200$ AGeV, while maintaining consistency with global entropy bounds from multiplicity and elliptic flow data and with pion suppression data out to 20 GeV.

## Method

The authors extend the DGLV (Djordjevic-Gyulassy-Levai-Vitev) opacity expansion formalism to include for the first time both elastic and inelastic energy losses and their fluctuations, combined with geometric path length fluctuations via integration over initial production points and azimuthal directions in a Woods-Saxon nuclear density profile with Glauber profiles. They compute quenched spectra of partons, hadrons, and leptons using a pQCD convolution of initial parton distributions, energy loss probability distributions, fragmentation functions, and decay functions, employing two complementary approaches: a full geometric integration (approach I) and a fixed effective path length approximation (approach II) for computational efficiency.

## Key numbers

- Initial gluon rapidity density fixed at $dN_g/dy = 1000$, consistent with measured multiplicity
- Strong coupling constant held fixed at $\alpha_s = 0.3$
- Effective path lengths determined for different parton flavors: $L_g = 3.5$ fm for gluons, $L_u = 5.0$ fm for light quarks, $L_c = 4.5$ fm for charm, $L_b = 5.5$ fm for bottom
- Elastic energy loss for heavy quarks found to be comparable to radiative energy loss over a wide kinematic range; for bottom quarks at energies up to approximately 15 GeV, elastic energy loss can exceed inelastic energy loss
- For light quarks at 20 GeV, elastic energy loss is only 50% smaller than radiative energy loss
- Gluon quenching reduced by a factor of approximately 2 when geometric fluctuations are included compared to fixed $L=5$ fm case
- Even with path length fluctuations, gluons are quenched by a factor of 10 when elastic energy loss is included in addition to radiative
- The lower yellow band in the electron suppression plot reaches below $R_{AA} \sim 0.5$ while keeping $dN_g/dy = 1000$ and $\alpha_s = 0.3$
- Pion suppression $R_{AA}^{\pi^0} \approx 0.2 \pm 0.1$ observed out to 20 GeV is reproduced
- Bottom quark elastic energy loss uncertainty: $\Delta E/E \approx 0.0 - 0.1$
- Characteristic widths of surviving jet distributions range from $\Delta x \approx 3-6$ fm

## Context

This work extends the DGLV radiative energy loss framework (Djordjevic, Gyulassy, Levai, Vitev, 2003-2005) which had significantly underestimated the suppression of non-photonic single electrons from heavy quark decays observed by PHENIX and STAR at RHIC. The authors build on Mustafa (2004) who found that radiative and elastic average energy losses for heavy quarks were comparable, and on earlier elastic energy loss calculations by Bjorken (1982), Thoma (1990), and Braaten (1991). The geometric path averaging approach is similar to that used by Gyulassy (2000) and Eskola (2004), but the inclusion of elastic energy losses together with path fluctuations in more realistic geometries had not been considered before. The results enable improved tomographic extraction of sQGP properties from jet quenching data, though the authors note that further theoretical work is needed on coherence effects between elastic and inelastic processes, running coupling effects, and full fluctuation spectra.
