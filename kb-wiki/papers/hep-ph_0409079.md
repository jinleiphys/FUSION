# PCAC and Shadowing of Low Energy Neutrinos

> The Adler relation between reactions initiated by neutrinos and pions is easy to misinterpret as a manifestation of the pion pole dominance. An axial current, however, cannot fluctuate into a pion, but only to heavy axial-vector states, since the lepton current is transverse. This is the miracle of the PCAC hypothesis which dictates a specific conspiracy between the heavy fluctuations, so that all together they mock the pion pole. Indeed, the observed Q^2 dependence of the axial form factor is controlled by the effective mass m_A \sim 1 GeV, rather than the pion mass. On the contrary, the onset of nuclear shadowing is governed by the small pion mass, rather than by the large axial mass scale. This is in variance with the conventional wisdom which equates the fluctuation lifetime and the coherence time. For the case of axial current they are different by almost two orders of magnitude. As a result, neutrino interactions are shadowed at very low energies of few hundred MeV, while energy of about 10 GeV is needed to access nuclear shadowing for the vector current. On the contrary to naive expectations, nuclear absorption enhances, rather than suppresses the cross section of coherent neutrino-production of pions which is the strongest channel (half of the total cross section) in the black disc limit.

# PCAC and Shadowing of Low Energy Neutrinos

## Key claim

The Adler relation between neutrino-induced and pion-induced reactions is often misinterpreted as pion pole dominance, but the axial current cannot fluctuate into a pion because the lepton current is transverse; instead, heavy axial-vector states conspire to mimic the pion pole. Nuclear shadowing for neutrinos sets in at energies of a few hundred MeV, almost two orders of magnitude lower than the energy required for vector current shadowing, because the coherence time for the axial current is governed by the small pion mass rather than the large axial mass scale.

## Method

The author analyzes the partial conservation of axial current (PCAC) hypothesis and its implications for neutrino-nucleus interactions, contrasting the fluctuation lifetime (controlled by the effective mass m_A ~ 1 GeV) with the coherence time (controlled by the pion mass). Nuclear shadowing calculations are performed using the Glauber approximation with Gribov's inelastic corrections, applied to the total neutrino-nucleus cross section on neon targets. The cross sections for coherent and incoherent diffractive neutrino-production of pions on nuclei are derived from the Adler relation and compared with experimental data from the BEBC experiment at CERN.

## Key numbers

- The axial mass parameter controlling the Q² dependence is m_A² ~ 1 GeV², about two orders of magnitude larger than the pion mass squared.
- Neutrino interactions are shadowed at energies of a few hundred MeV, while energy of about 10 GeV is needed to access nuclear shadowing for the vector current.
- The coherence time for the axial current is almost two orders of magnitude longer than the fluctuation lifetime given by t_fluct = 2E/M_eff².
- The axial form factor is parametrized as F_N(Q²) = 1/(1 + Q²/m_A²), where m_A² = 2(m_ρ² + m_π²) is close to the a₁ meson mass.
- Coherent neutrino-production of pions reaches half of the total cross section in the black disc limit.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of PCAC beyond the Goldberger-Treiman relation and low-energy tests, addressing the Piketty-Stodolsky puzzle (1970) which showed that a₁ meson dominance cannot explain the observed cross section of neutrino-production of pions. The author's calculations build on prior work by Kopeliovich (2001, 2002) and the Deck model (1967), and contradict the conventional wisdom that equates fluctuation lifetime with coherence time. The results enable improved modeling of low-energy neutrino-nucleus interactions, relevant for neutrino oscillation experiments, and challenge the Rein-Sehgal model (1983) which predicts zero cross section for coherent pion production in the black disc limit.
