# Disentangling the influence of excitation energy and compound nucleus angular momentum on fission fragment angular momentum

> The origin of the large angular momenta observed for fission fragments is still a question under discussion. To address this, we study isomeric yield ratios (IYR), i.e. the relative population of two or more long-lived metastable states with different spins, of fission products. We report on IYR of 17 isotopes produced in the 28 MeV $α$-induced fission of $^{232}$Th at the IGISOL facility of the University of Jyväskylä. The fissioning nuclei in this reaction are $^{233,234,235}$U*. We compare our data to IYR from thermal neutron-induced fission of $^{233}$U and $^{235}$U, and we observe statistically significant larger IYR in the $^{232}$Th($α$,f) reaction, where the average compound nucleus (CN) spin is 7.5 $\hbar$, than in $^{233,235}$U(n$_{th}$,f), with average spins 2.5 and 3.5 $\hbar$, respectively. To assess the influence of the excitation energy, we study literature data of IYR from photon-induced fission reactions, and find that the IYR are independent of the CN excitation energy. We conclude that the different IYR must be explained by the different CN spin alone. This implies that the FF angular momentum only partly comes from the fission process itself, and is in addition influenced by the angular momentum present in the CN.

## Key claim

The authors provide experimental evidence that an increase in the compound nucleus (CN) spin leads to an increase in fission fragment (FF) angular momentum, while the CN excitation energy has no observable effect on FF angular momentum; this conclusion is hedged by noting that the photo-fission data show no dependency "within current uncertainties" and that the reported IYR differences can only serve as "preliminary estimates."

## Method

Isomeric yield ratios (IYR) for 17 fission products from 28 MeV alpha-induced fission of Th-232 were measured using the Phase-Imaging Ion-Cyclotron-Resonance (PI-ICR) technique at the JYFLTRAP double Penning trap mass spectrometer at the IGISOL-4 facility. The measured IYR were compared to literature data from thermal neutron-induced fission of U-233 and U-235 (with average CN spins of 2.6 and 3.6 hbar, respectively) and to literature IYR from photon-induced fission reactions at varying incident photon energies to isolate the effects of CN spin and excitation energy separately.

## Key numbers

* Average CN spin for Th-232(alpha,f) is 7.7 hbar, compared to 2.6 hbar for U-234* from thermal neutrons and 3.6 hbar for U-236* from thermal neutrons.
* Weighted mean difference in IYR between Th-232(alpha,f) and U-235(n_th,f) is Delta IYR = 0.135(8); between Th-232(alpha,f) and U-233(n_th,f) is Delta IYR = 0.156(6).
* Normalized change in IYR per change in average CN spin is Delta IYR / Delta Jbar = 0.032(1) hbar^{-1} for the studied isomeric pairs.
* Average slope of IYR versus incident photon energy from photo-fission data is Delta IYR / Delta E = 0.0001(5) MeV^{-1}, indicating no effect of excitation energy.
* At least 2 hbar out of the additional 4-5 hbar (greater than 40 percent) of CN angular momentum goes to FF angular momenta, based on TALYS calculations.

## Context

This work extends the study of fission fragment angular momentum generation by experimentally disentangling the roles of CN spin and excitation energy, building on the IYR compilation by Sears (2021) and the post-scission model of Wilson (2021). The finding that IYR increases with CN spin but not with excitation energy contradicts the FREYA model assumption by Randrup and Vogt (2021) that FF spin is mainly determined by temperature, but agrees with the microscopic calculations of Bulgac (2022) and the gamma-ray multiplicity measurements of Giha (2023), Laborie (2023), and Qi (2023). The results enable future PI-ICR measurements of IYR from thermal neutron-induced fission of U-233 and U-235 to clarify the situation, and suggest that a larger portion of additional CN angular momentum may be transmitted to FFs in asymmetric fission compared to symmetric fission.
