# Bound State Internal Interactions as a Mechanism for Exponential Decay

> We hypothesize that the binding interactions among the components of bound systems and the background fields, sometimes known as virtual particle exchange, affect the state of the systems as do typical scattering interactions. Then with the assumption that the interior environment of unstable particles is disordered, we derive in the limit of continuous binding both an exactly exponential non-decay probability and Fermi's Golden Rule for the decay rates. The result suggests resolutions to several long-standing theoretical challenges associated with exponential decay in quantum mechanics, without appealing directly to non-Hermitian, approximate Hamiltonians or complex energies. It also contributes to a conceptual understanding of the continuum between controlled interactions that induce deviations from exponential decay, such as those in the Quantum Zeno Effect, and the uncontrolled internal dynamics of excited atoms and nuclei, which exhibit no such deviations. Finally, we examine how the binding interactions responsible for the general exponential character of decay for bound systems differ from the couplings with decay products that control decay rates, providing insight into challenges in quantum computing and information processing.

# Bound State Internal Interactions as a Mechanism for Exponential Decay

## Key claim
The paper hypothesizes that the binding interactions among components of bound systems (virtual particle exchange) act like scattering or measurement interactions, and that with the assumption of a disordered internal environment, the continuous limit of these interactions produces exactly exponential decay and Fermi's Golden Rule without requiring non-Hermitian Hamiltonians or complex energies. The derivation depends critically on the assumption that consecutive internal projections do not commute, which distinguishes uncontrolled bound systems from controlled systems exhibiting the Quantum Zeno Effect.

## Method
The author models a bound system as undergoing a sequence of instantaneous projection operations representing binding interactions, taking the continuous limit where the number of interactions N goes to infinity while Nδ = t remains finite. The density operator formalism is used with the assumption that consecutive metastable states do not commute due to a disordered internal environment. The decay rate is then derived by shifting focus from undecayed projection operators Λ_u to decayed projection operators Λ_d, applying standard scattering theory with Lippmann-Schwinger states and a partitioned Hamiltonian H = H_0 + V.

## Key numbers
- The non-decay probability is derived as P_u(t) = e^{-t/τ}, where τ^{-1} = -d/dδ Tr(Λ_u ρ(δ))|_{δ=0}
- The decay rate from Fermi's Golden Rule is τ^{-1}_{η_i→η_f} = (2π/ħ) |⟨ψ_{E,η_f}|V|φ_{E,η_i}⟩|^2 λ(E,η_f)
- For multiple decay channels, the non-decay probability factorizes as P_u(t) = e^{-t/τ_k} e^{-t/τ_ℓ}

## Context
This work extends the conceptual framework of the Quantum Zeno Effect (Misra and Sudarshan, 1977) by showing that the same continuous-projection limit produces exponential rather than frozen decay when projections are onto non-commuting states. It addresses the long-standing challenge raised by Khalfin (1958) regarding the impossibility of exact exponential decay from Lorentzian energy wave functions, arguing that the Lorentzian line shape is emergent rather than fundamental. The model also connects to observations of decay deviations in dissolved organic molecules (Rothe et al., 2006) and the absence of such deviations in excited nuclei and atoms (Norman et al., 1988; Ramirez-Jimenez, 2019), suggesting a continuum between controlled and uncontrolled decay phenomena.
