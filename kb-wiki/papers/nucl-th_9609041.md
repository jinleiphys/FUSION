# Reaction Rates of neutron capture by Li- and Be-isotopes

> Neutron capture by neutron-rich Li- and Be-isotopes plays a role in big--bang nucleosynthesis, especially in its inhomogeneous version and in the $α$--process occurring in supernovae. New reaction rates for $^{7,8}$Li(n,$γ$)$^{8,9}$Li and $^{9,10,11}$Be(n,$γ$)$^{10,11,12}$Be have been consistently calculated using direct capture for the nonresonant part and the Breit-Wigner formula for the resonant part. The spectroscopic factors, spin/parity assignments and excitation energies of the final bound and initial resonant states have been taken from existing experimental data whenever possible. For unstable nuclei where this information is not experimentally available the shell model was used to determine these quantities.

# Reaction Rates of Neutron Capture by Li- and Be-Isotopes

## Key claim
New reaction rates for neutron capture on neutron-rich Li- and Be-isotopes are calculated using direct capture for nonresonant contributions and the Breit-Wigner formula for resonant contributions, with spectroscopic factors taken from experimental data where available and from shell-model calculations otherwise. The resulting rates differ significantly from previous estimates, particularly for $^8$Li(n,$\gamma$)$^9$Li (a factor of 5 smaller resonance strength) and $^9$Be(n,$\gamma$)$^{10}$Be (higher resonance strengths), which could alter the reaction flow in inhomogeneous big-bang nucleosynthesis and the $\alpha$-process in supernovae.

## Method
The total cross sections were calculated by summing resonant contributions (using the Breit-Wigner formula for isolated resonances) and nonresonant contributions (using the direct capture model). Spectroscopic factors, spin/parity assignments, and excitation energies were taken from existing experimental data when available; for unstable nuclei lacking such data, the shell model code OXBASH was used with the (8-16)POT interaction of Cohen and Kurath for normal parity states and the WBN interaction of Warburton and Brown for nonnormal parity states. Scattering and bound-state wave functions were computed using real folding potentials with the DDM3Y effective nucleon-nucleon interaction and nuclear densities from measured charge distributions or Hartree-Fock calculations.

## Key numbers
- $^7$Li(n,$\gamma$)$^8$Li: resonance at 227 keV neutron energy with $\omega\gamma = 0.061$ eV; calculated neutron width 28.9 keV (experimental $31 \pm 7$ keV); thermal cross section $8.2 \times 10^{-2}$ b from shell model (experimental $4.54 \times 10^{-2}$ b)
- $^8$Li(n,$\gamma$)$^9$Li: resonance at 247 keV with $\omega\gamma = 0.066$ eV; $\gamma$-width $\Gamma_\gamma = 0.11$ eV (previous estimate 0.56 eV); thermal cross section $1.94 \times 10^{-2}$ b (Rauscher et al. value $3.51 \times 10^{-2}$ b)
- $^9$Be(n,$\gamma$)$^{10}$Be: resonances at 559 keV ($\omega\gamma = 0.578$ eV) and 730 keV ($\omega\gamma = 0.509$ eV); thermal cross section $1.06 \times 10^{-2}$ b from shell model (experimental $7.6 \times 10^{-3}$ b)
- Nonresonant cross sections at 30 keV: $^7$Li(n,$\gamma$)$^8$Li: 39.000 $\mu$b; $^8$Li(n,$\gamma$)$^9$Li: 16.795 $\mu$b; $^9$Be(n,$\gamma$)$^{10}$Be: 8.294 $\mu$b; $^{10}$Be(n,$\gamma$)$^{11}$Be: 4.281 $\mu$b; $^{11}$Be(n,$\gamma$)$^{12}$Be: 0.996 $\mu$b

## Context
This work extends earlier reaction rate compilations by Rauscher et al. (1994) for neutron-rich Li and Be isotopes, providing updated rates based on consistent application of direct capture and Breit-Wigner formalisms with improved spectroscopic information. The new rates differ substantially from previous estimates: the $^8$Li(n,$\gamma$)$^9$Li rate is smaller by a factor of 5 due to a revised $\gamma$-width, while the $^9$Be(n,$\gamma$)$^{10}$Be rate is higher due to enhanced resonance strengths. These changes could shift the reaction flow in inhomogeneous big-bang nucleosynthesis toward $^8$Li($\alpha$,n)$^{11}$B rather than neutron capture, and give greater importance to $^9$Be(n,$\gamma$)$^{10}$Be. The authors note that detailed network calculations with the new rates are planned, and that experimental tests using radioactive nuclear beams are becoming feasible.
