# Analysis of Molecular Rotational Spectra

> Rotational spectra of diatomic molecules measured in the high-precision experiments are analyzed. Such a spectrum is usually fitted by an 8th order polynomial in spin. In fact, from the theoretical point of view, the rotational spectrum is expected to be a smooth function of spin. Thus, fitting the rotational spectrum in terms of a low order polynomial in spin seems to be justified. However, this is not what we found in the present work. We show that the measured spectrum is not necessarily a smooth function of the spin, which is quite surprising. Whether or not it indicates the presence of some hidden physics is an open question.

## Key claim

The measured intra-band rotational transition energies of diatomic molecules are not necessarily smooth functions of spin, contrary to the theoretical expectation that the eigenvalue \(E_V(I)\) must be an analytic function of spin \(I\) based on the Born-Oppenheimer approximation; whether this indicates hidden physics remains an open question.

## Method

The authors analyzed high-precision R- and P-branch transition energies from published molecular spectra, extracting intra-band \(\Delta I=2\) transition energies \(\Delta E_V(I) = E_V(I)-E_V(I-2)\) for each vibrational band and spin parity. They subtracted a 7th-order Legendre polynomial fit from \(\Delta E(I)\) to define a "one-point formula" \(\Delta^{(1)}_N E(I)\), and applied a filter that sets the deviation to zero when its absolute value is within twice the experimental error bar. The resulting filtered deviations were examined for patterns, and the observed irregularities were modeled phenomenologically by introducing local "kinks" (discrete energy shifts) into a smooth rotational spectrum.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of electronic to vibrational excitation energy (\(E_{ele}/E_{vib}\)) is typically 100, and the ratio of vibrational to rotational energy (\(E_{vib}/E_{rot}\)) is another 100.
- The rotational spectrum is conventionally fitted by an 8th-order polynomial in spin \(I\) (equation 1.3), so the transition energy \(\Delta E(I)\) is a 7th-order polynomial in \(I\).
- For the YD molecule, three-fold kinks occur at two different places: at spin \(I=18\) and \(I=32\) in a spectrum of the form \(E(I)=AI(I+1)-B[I(I+1)]^2\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of molecular rotational spectra beyond the standard polynomial fitting approach used in prior experimental studies (references 1–8 in the paper). The authors draw an analogy to nuclear physics, where band crossing produces kinks leading to the backbending phenomenon (Hara and Lalazissis, earlier work). The results challenge the assumption that molecular rotational spectra are smooth analytic functions of spin, and the authors suggest that intensive phenomenological studies along this line may eventually reveal the physical origin of the observed irregularities.
