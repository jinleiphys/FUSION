# Variational approach to anharmonic collective motion

> We derive large-amplitude collective equations of motion from the variational principle for the time-dependent Schroedinger equation. These equations reduce to the well-known diabatic formulas for vibrational frequencies in the small amplitude limit. The finite amplitude expression allows departures from harmonic behavior of giant resonances to be simply estimated. The relative shift of the second phonon falls with nuclear mass A as A^(-4/3) in the three modes we consider: monopole, dipole, and quadrupole. Numerically the effect is very small in heavy nuclei, as was found with other approaches.

## Key claim

The anharmonic shift of the second phonon in giant resonances scales with nuclear mass as A^(-4/3) and is numerically very small in heavy nuclei, contradicting earlier expectations that the shift would scale as A^(-2/3).

## Method

The authors derive large-amplitude collective equations of motion from the Dirac variational principle for the time-dependent Schroedinger equation, using a trial wave function constructed from two unitary transformations: one generated by a velocity field Q and one by the commutator Q_1 = m_N[H,Q]. They evaluate the resulting Hamiltonian and inertia for monopole, quadrupole, and dipole modes using a Skyrme-like Hamiltonian density, then compute the anharmonic energy shift via semiclassical Bohr-Sommerfeld requantization.

## Key numbers

- Monopole: anharmonicity parameter E_anh = 40 A MeV; for Pb-208 the double-phonon shift Delta^(2)E = 0.05 MeV
- Quadrupole: anharmonicity parameter E_anh = (288/25) A e_f, where e_f is the Fermi energy (approximately 36 MeV)
- Dipole: anharmonicity parameter |E_anh| > 100 A MeV
- Nuclear matter parameters: v_a = -107 MeV, v_b = 70 MeV, v_tau = 50 MeV, compressibility K = 230 MeV
- The relative shift of the second phonon falls with nuclear mass A as A^(-4/3) for all three modes considered

## Context

This work extends the small-amplitude sum-rule approach of Bohigas, Lane, and Martorell (1979) to finite amplitudes, providing simple analytic formulas for anharmonic effects in giant resonances. The result that anharmonic shifts are extremely small agrees with earlier time-dependent mean-field calculations by Ponomarev et al. (1996) and others, but contradicts the expectation from Bohr and Mottelson (1975) that Delta^(2)E would scale as A^(-2/3). The approach enables simple estimates of anharmonicity without full mean-field calculations, and the authors suggest it could be extended to low-lying collective modes such as the octupole vibration.
