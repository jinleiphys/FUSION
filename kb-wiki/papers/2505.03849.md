# Improved Dimensionality Reduction for Inverse Problems in Nuclear Fusion and High-Energy Astrophysics

> Many inverse problems in nuclear fusion and high-energy astrophysics research, such as the optimization of tokamak reactor geometries or the inference of black hole parameters from interferometric images, necessitate high-dimensional parameter scans and large ensembles of simulations to be performed. Such inverse problems typically involve large uncertainties, both in the measurement parameters being inverted and in the underlying physics models themselves. Monte Carlo sampling, when combined with modern non-linear dimensionality reduction techniques such as autoencoders and manifold learning, can be used to reduce the size of the parameter spaces considerably. However, there is no guarantee that the resulting combinations of parameters will be physically valid, or even mathematically consistent. In this position paper, we advocate adopting a hybrid approach that leverages our recent advances in the development of formal verification methods for numerical algorithms, with the goal of constructing parameter space restrictions with provable mathematical and physical correctness properties, whilst nevertheless respecting both experimental uncertainties and uncertainties in the underlying physical processes.

## Key claim

The authors advocate for a hybrid approach that combines Monte Carlo sampling with non-linear dimensionality reduction techniques (autoencoders, manifold learning) and formal verification methods, in order to construct parameter space restrictions that are provably physically and mathematically valid, while still respecting experimental uncertainties and uncertainties in the underlying physics models. The paper is a position paper, not presenting implemented results; the claim is that such an approach *could* be developed, not that it has been demonstrated.

## Method

The paper reviews the challenges of high-dimensional parameter scans in inverse problems for tokamak reactor geometry optimization and black hole parameter inference from interferometric images. It identifies that classical dimensionality reduction techniques (principal component analysis, autoencoders, manifold learning) can reduce parameter space dimensionality but provide no guarantee that the resulting subspace is physically valid, as parameter combinations may violate known physical limits (e.g., Greenwald density limit, plasma beta limit, causality constraints). The authors propose extending their prior work on formal verification and automated theorem-proving for numerical algorithms to automatically "tag" regions of parameter space corresponding to physically or mathematically disallowed combinations, enabling dimensionality reduction algorithms that certifiably avoid projecting onto invalid subspaces.

## Context

This position paper extends the authors' prior work on formal verification methods for numerical algorithms (Gorard 2025), which allowed construction of formal correctness proofs for computational physics simulations verifying properties such as L² stability, flux conservation, and thermodynamic consistency. The paper identifies limitations in existing dimensionality reduction approaches applied to inverse problems in nuclear fusion (citing Freidberg 2015 on tokamak and stellarator design) and high-energy astrophysics (citing Broderick 2020 on black hole shadow inference and Ferreira 2022 on neutron star equation of state inference). The proposed hybrid approach would enable future development of dimensionality reduction algorithms that provably avoid projecting onto physically invalid subspaces, addressing a gap left by classical techniques that remain agnostic to problem-specific physical constraints.
