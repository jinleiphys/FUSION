# Medium Assisted Low Energy Nuclear Fusion

> We study the process of nuclear fusion at low energies in a medium using the second order time dependent perturbation theory. We consider a specific process which involves fusion of a low energy proton with a Nickel nucleus. The reaction proceeds in two steps or interactions. We refer to the amplitudes corresponding to these two interactions as the the molecular and the nuclear matrix elements. The first amplitude involves Coulomb interaction with another nucleus in the medium while the second corresponds to the nuclear fusion process. It has been shown in earlier papers that such a second order process has negligible amplitude unless it is assisted by special medium effects. In the present paper we show the presence of a special configuration of atoms which greatly enhances the process. We find that if the spacings among the atoms can be tuned, the rate can be sufficiently enhanced so that easily observable. The spacings do not require acute fine tuning, however, if they are significantly off the rate falls sharply to negligible values. This might also explain both the successes and failures experienced by many experimentalists studying this phenomenon. We study only a particular final state which involves emission of one photon. However we show that many final states are possible which need not involve photon emission.

# Medium Assisted Low Energy Nuclear Fusion

## Key claim

The authors claim that nuclear fusion between a low-energy proton and a Nickel nucleus can proceed at an observable rate in a medium, but only under a special configuration where the spacings among atoms can be tuned; if the spacings are significantly off, the rate falls sharply to negligible values, which may explain both successes and failures in experimental cold fusion studies.

## Method

The authors use second-order time-dependent perturbation theory to compute the fusion rate for the reaction p + Ni + Y → Cu + Y + γ, where Y is another Nickel nucleus in the medium. The first perturbation is the screened Coulomb interaction between the proton and the Y nucleus (molecular matrix element), and the second perturbation is the electromagnetic interaction leading to photon emission during fusion (nuclear matrix element). The intermediate state wave functions are assumed to be localized in the transverse direction due to disorder in the medium, with a plane-wave behavior along the direction of large momentum transfer, which avoids the cancellation of amplitudes found under free-space boundary conditions.

## Key numbers

- The total rate for the process is approximately 2 × 10⁻¹⁷ per second, implying that with 10¹⁷ proton-Nickel molecular pairs, roughly two events per second are expected.
- The number density of Y particles is set to 2.65 × 10²⁰ cm⁻³.
- The nuclear potential uses a Woods-Saxon form with V₀ = 36 MeV, r_nuc = 1.25 A¹/³ fm, and r₀ = 0.524 fm.
- The energy eigenvalue of the final state (⁶³Cu with one unpaired proton) is 9.1 MeV.
- The differential rate shows a peak slightly above 9.07 MeV photon energy.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous second-order perturbation theory approach to nuclear fusion (Jain 2020, Jain 2021, Ramkumar 2022), which was applied to proton-deuteron fusion with photon emission. The present paper generalizes the mechanism to fusion of a light nucleus with a heavy nucleus (proton + Nickel), requiring much higher intermediate state energies, and replaces photon emission at the first vertex with Coulomb interaction with a heavy nucleus Y to achieve the necessary momentum transfer. The mechanism relies on localization of eigenstates and discretization of energy eigenvalues in a disordered medium, which differs from Anderson localization (Anderson 1958) since it concerns nuclear wave functions rather than electrons. The results provide a specific prediction of gamma-ray emission near 9.07 MeV that could be tested in future experiments, as previous transmutation experiments (Miley 1996, Rajeev 2017, Kumar 2022) have reported Nickel-to-Copper conversion but without evidence of gamma-ray emission in this energy regime.
