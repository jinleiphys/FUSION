# The phases of deuterium at extreme densities

> We consider deuterium compressed to higher than atomic, but lower than nuclear densities. At such densities deuterium is a superconducting quantum liquid. Generically, two superconducting phases compete, a "ferromagnetic" and a "nematic" one. We provide a power counting argument suggesting that the dominant interactions in the deuteron liquid are perturbative (but screened) Coulomb interactions. At very high densities the ground state is determined by very small nuclear interaction effects that probably favor the ferromagnetic phase. At lower densities the symmetry of the theory is effectively enhanced to SU(3), and the quantum liquid enters a novel phase, neither ferromagnetic nor nematic. Our results can serve as a starting point for investigations of the phase dynamics of deuteron liquids, as well as exploration of the stability and dynamics of the rich variety of topological objects that may occur in phases of the deuteron quantum liquid, which range from Alice strings to spin skyrmions to Z_2 vortices.

# The phases of deuterium at extreme densities

## Key claim
Deuterium compressed to densities between atomic and nuclear scales forms a superconducting quantum liquid whose ground state depends on the density regime. At very high densities, small nuclear interaction effects likely favor a "ferromagnetic" phase, while at lower densities the symmetry is effectively enhanced to SU(3), producing a novel phase that is neither ferromagnetic nor nematic, but the authors note that the ferromagnetic preference depends on uncertain nuclear scattering lengths and that the pressure estimate of ~1800 GPa required to reach these phases should be viewed as only a rough estimate.

## Method
The authors construct a non-relativistic effective field theory for deuterons as charged spin-1 bosons interacting via screened Coulomb and nuclear forces, then compute the one-loop effective potential to determine which condensate phase (ferromagnetic or nematic) is energetically favored. They provide a power-counting argument showing that at leading order in l/a0 (where l is interparticle distance and a0 is the Bohr radius), the dominant contributions come from resummed one-loop diagrams with screened Coulomb photons, and they estimate the size of SU(3)-breaking effects from both electromagnetic dipole interactions and nuclear contact interactions using known deuteron-deuteron scattering lengths from several nuclear potential models.

## Key numbers
- Condensation temperature: T_cond ~ 10^6 K for interparticle distance l ~ 10 a0
- Pressure estimate for deuterium to become a quantum liquid: P0 ~ 1.8 × 10^3 GPa (based on electron degeneracy pressure with l = 2a0)
- Debye screening mass: m_s^2 ~ α m_e / l = 1/(a0 l)
- Deuteron magnetic moment: g ≈ 0.857 in Bohr magnetons
- Deuteron mass: M ~ 2 GeV
- S-wave deuteron-deuteron scattering length in S=0 channel from one model: A0 = 4.91 ± 0.02 + i(-0.0115 ± 0.0001) fm (PhysRevC.58.58)
- Scattering lengths from Deltuva calculations (AV18, CDBonn, INOY04 potentials): A0 = 5.35, 5.13, 4.87 fm and A2 = 3.16, 3.16, 3.18 fm respectively
- Ratio of SU(3)-breaking energy to thermal energy: E_θ/E_T ~ (10^(-6))/(a0 T) × (a0/l)^3, with a0T ~ 1 at T ~ 10^5 K

## Context
This work extends the prediction by Berezhiani (2010) that deuterium becomes a quantum liquid at densities between atomic and nuclear scales, where the crystallization temperature T_crys and condensation temperature T_cond cross. The authors build on the jellium model approach of Foldy (1961) and Brueckner (1967) for charged Bose gases, but incorporate the spin-1 nature of deuterons and the competition between Coulomb and nuclear interactions. The results provide a foundation for studying phase dynamics and topological defects (ranging from Alice strings to Z_2 vortices) in deuteron quantum liquids, with potential applications to brown dwarf interiors and shock-compression experiments. The analysis of SU(3) symmetry enhancement and breaking parallels work on W boson condensation in the early Universe by Dolgov (2010) and on spin-1 Bose-Einstein condensates in cold atom systems.
