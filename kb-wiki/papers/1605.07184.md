# Simulating collisions of thick nuclei in the color glass condensate framework

> We present our work on the simulation of the early stages of heavy-ion collisions with finite longitudinal thickness in the laboratory frame in 3+1 dimensions. In particular we study the effects of nuclear thickness on the production of a glasma state in the McLerran-Venugopalan model within the color glass condensate framework. A finite thickness enables us to describe nuclei at lower energies, but forces us to abandon boost-invariance. As a consequence, random classical color sources within the nuclei have to be included in the simulation, which is achieved by using the colored particle-in-cell (CPIC) method. We show that the description in the laboratory frame agrees with boost-invariant approaches as a limiting case. Furthermore we investigate collisions beyond boost-invariance, in particular the pressure anisotropy in the glasma.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that simulating heavy-ion collisions with finite longitudinal nuclear thickness in the laboratory frame using the colored particle-in-cell (CPIC) method is viable, and that this approach reproduces boost-invariant results in the limit of small thickness, while also enabling studies of collisions at lower energies where boost invariance is broken.

## Method

The authors model two nuclei as sheets of color charges with finite longitudinal thickness, described by Gaussian profiles, moving at the speed of light in the laboratory frame within the McLerran-Venugopalan model. They solve the classical Yang-Mills equations on a 3+1 dimensional lattice using a leap-frog scheme, with the color charges treated as dynamical particles evolved via Wong's equations using the nearest-grid-point interpolation method and a charge refinement algorithm to reduce numerical noise. The initial gauge fields are constructed in Lorenz gauge and then transformed to temporal gauge via Wilson lines, with infrared and ultraviolet regulators applied to the Poisson equation solution.

## Key numbers

- The longitudinal thickness of each nucleus is parameterized by a Gaussian width σ, with the relation between laboratory frame width and light cone frame width given by √2 σ̄ = σ
- The simulation uses a 3D grid with N_L · N_T² cells, with spatial lattice spacing a_s and time step a_t
- The number of particles per cell in the longitudinal direction is set to N_p = a_s / a_t to ensure smooth currents with the NGP algorithm
- Infrared regulation uses a mass parameter m that introduces a finite correlation length on the order of m⁻¹ in the transverse directions
- The UV cutoff Λ is applied such that Fourier modes with |k_T| > Λ are set to zero

## Context

This work extends the boost-invariant CGC framework of Krasnitz (1998), Lappi (2003), and Kovner (1995) by introducing finite nuclear thickness, which forces abandonment of the boost-invariance assumption used in previous 2+1 dimensional simulations. The CPIC method, previously applied to hard-thermal-loop simulations by Hu (1996) and Moore (1997) and to jet energy loss by Schenke (2009), is here adapted for the first time to simulate the collision itself beyond pioneering work by Poschl (1998, 1999, 2000) on very small transverse lattices. This approach enables future studies of heavy-ion collisions at lower energies relevant to the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program and future facilities at GSI FAIR and JINR NICA.
