# Complex conjugate poles and parton distributions

> We calculate parton and generalized parton distributions in Minkowski space using a scalar propagator with a pair of complex conjugate poles. Correct spectral and support properties are obtained only after careful analytic continuation from Euclidean space. Alternately the quark distribution function can be calculated from modified cutting rules, which put the intermediate state on its complex mass shells. Distribution functions agree with those resulting from the model's Euclidean space double distribution which we calculate via non-diagonal matrix elements of twist-two operators. Thus one can use a wide class of analytic parameterizations of the quark propagator to connect Euclidean space Green functions to light-cone dominated amplitudes.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a scalar propagator with a pair of complex conjugate poles can be used to calculate parton and generalized parton distributions (GPDs) in Minkowski space, but only after careful analytic continuation from Euclidean space or by using modified cutting rules; naive Minkowski-space calculation yields distributions with incorrect support and non-real values.

## Method

The authors use a scalar \(\phi^3\) model with a trivial Bethe-Salpeter vertex and a propagator containing one pair of complex conjugate poles, parameterized by a mass \(m^2 = a^2 - b^2\) and a width \(\epsilon = 2ab\). They first compute the double distribution (DD) from non-diagonal matrix elements of twist-two operators in Euclidean space, then derive the quark distribution and GPD via reduction relations. They also calculate the quark distribution in Minkowski space using modified cutting rules that put intermediate states on complex mass shells, and they perform the full analytic continuation of the GPD amplitude from Euclidean to Minkowski space by adding Wick-pole residues.

## Key numbers

- Model parameters chosen arbitrarily: \(M = 0.14\) GeV and \(m = 0.33\) GeV.
- The quark distribution \(q(x)\) is plotted for several values of \(\epsilon\) in GeV\(^2\) (e.g., \(\epsilon = 0.17\) GeV\(^2\) is used in one GPD plot).
- The electromagnetic form factor is plotted as a function of \(-t\) for several values of \(\epsilon\) in GeV\(^2\).
- The GPD is plotted at fixed \(\zeta = 0.5\) and \(t = -1.0\) GeV\(^2\) for several \(\epsilon\) values, and at fixed \(\epsilon = 0.17\) GeV\(^2\) and \(t = -2.0\) GeV\(^2\) for several \(\zeta\) values.

## Context

This work extends Dyson-Schwinger model studies of light-cone amplitudes, specifically addressing the problem that entire-function propagators used in earlier work (Hecht et al., 2000) lack a Kallen-Lehmann representation and have unbounded behavior in the complex plane. The authors show that meromorphic propagators with complex conjugate poles, which appear in solutions to Dyson-Schwinger equations and in fits to lattice data (Bhagwat et al., 2003), can be rigorously used to compute parton distributions and GPDs. This enables future calculations of light-cone dominated amplitudes using propagators and vertices constrained by lattice data and Ward-Takahashi identities, bridging light-cone methods and Dyson-Schwinger studies.
