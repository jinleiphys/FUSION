# CVC in Particle Physics

> We review the hypothesis of the conserved vector current (CVC) within the Standard Model. In addition to the classic tests, such as pion beta decay and neutrino scattering, we mention recent tests involving LEP data. As well as providing a clear indication that the isovector current is not conserved, rho-omega mixing offers a fascinating opportunity to study CP violation at B-factories and we outline these ideas. Finally we briefly touch on a new approach to mass generation in the Standard Model which, for example, leads to the up-down mass difference which breaks CVC.

## Key claim

The conserved vector current (CVC) hypothesis is a good approximation within the Standard Model, but it is not exact because the QCD Hamiltonian contains a term proportional to (m_u - m_d), meaning the isovector current is not strictly conserved; the best particle physics test of CVC (pion beta decay) is consistent at the current experimental limit of about 3%.

## Method

The paper reviews the CVC hypothesis by examining classic tests (pion beta decay, neutrino-nucleon scattering, and the Adler sum-rule) and recent tests using tau decay data from LEP. It also analyzes rho-omega mixing as a manifestation of CVC violation, and outlines a proposal to use rho-omega interference to study CP violation in charged B decays. Finally, it discusses a speculative approach to mass generation in the Standard Model without an elementary Higgs field.

## Key numbers

- Pion beta decay branching ratio: theoretical value (1.0482 ± 0.0048) × 10^(-8), experimental world average (1.025 ± 0.034) × 10^(-8), consistent at about 3% precision.
- Pion mass difference: Δ ~ 4 MeV, which suppresses the pion beta decay rate.
- Neutrino scattering dipole masses from Baker et al.: M_V = 0.86 ± 0.07 GeV, M_A = 1.04 ± 0.14 GeV; from Miller et al.: M_V = 0.96 ± 0.04 GeV, M_A = 0.80 ± 0.10 GeV; from Kitagaki et al.: M_V = 0.89 (+0.04, -0.07) GeV, M_A = 0.97 (+0.14, -0.11) GeV; electron scattering gives M_V = 0.84 GeV.
- Adler sum-rule test from BEBC: 1.01 ± 0.08 (stat.) ± 0.18 (syst.).
- Rho-omega mixing amplitude: Π^(ρω) = -3800 ± 370 MeV^2 from a recent analysis of world data.
- Tau decay branching ratio to π^- π^0 ν_τ: world average 25.24 ± 0.16%; theoretical estimate from Sobie: 24.6 ± 1.4%, differing by about 2.5% with a theoretical error of ~5%.
- Limit on second-class scalar form factor parameter ρ: ρ < 1.8, with M_S = 1 GeV.
- Branching ratios for B^- → ρ^0 h^- decays: ~10^(-8), with possible CP asymmetry as large as 90%.

## Context

This review extends the classic tests of CVC from pion beta decay (McFarlane et al., Depommier et al.) and neutrino scattering (Baker et al., Miller et al., Kitagaki et al.) to modern tests using tau decay data from LEP (Sobie, Enomoto and Tanabashi). The rho-omega mixing analysis follows the work of Renard, Coon and Barrett, and Heath, and the CP violation application follows a suggestion by Lipkin and the analysis of Enomoto and Tanabashi. The paper also discusses a speculative mass-generation mechanism proposed by Bass and Thomas that could explain the up-down mass difference and the breaking of CVC, and notes a recent concern by Maltman et al. about uncertainties in the rho-omega mixing amplitude.
