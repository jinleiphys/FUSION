# Nonlinear Fokker-Planck Acceleration for Forward-Peaked Transport Problems in Slab Geometry

> This paper introduces a nonlinear acceleration technique that accelerates the convergence of solution of transport problems with highly forward-peaked scattering. The technique is similar to a conventional high-order/low-order (HOLO) acceleration scheme. The Fokker-Planck equation, which is an asymptotic limit of the transport equation in highly forward-peaked settings, is modified and used for acceleration; this modified equation preserves the angular flux and moments of the (high order) transport equation. We present numerical results using the Screened Rutherford, Exponential, and Henyey-Greenstein scattering kernels and compare them to established acceleration methods such as diffusion synthetic acceleration (DSA). We observe three to four orders of magnitude speed-up in wall-clock time compared to DSA.

# Nonlinear Fokker-Planck Acceleration for Forward-Peaked Transport Problems in Slab Geometry

## Key claim

The paper introduces Nonlinear Fokker-Planck Acceleration (NFPA), a high-order/low-order acceleration scheme that uses a modified Fokker-Planck equation as the low-order model, which upon convergence preserves all angular moments of the flux from the high-order transport equation. The method achieves three to four orders of magnitude speed-up in wall-clock time compared to diffusion synthetic acceleration (DSA) for problems with highly forward-peaked scattering, though the authors note that a full convergence analysis using Fourier analysis remains to be performed.

## Method

The authors solve the monoenergetic, steady-state transport equation in homogeneous slab geometry using discrete ordinates (S_N) angular discretization and linear discontinuous finite element spatial discretization. The NFPA scheme iterates between a high-order transport sweep and a low-order modified Fokker-Planck equation that includes a consistency term D_F, which forces the angular moments of the low-order solution to match those of the high-order solution. The low-order stiffness matrix is set up and inverted only once, similar to Fokker-Planck Synthetic Acceleration (FPSA), and Picard iteration is used to solve the nonlinear high-order/low-order-plus-consistency system.

## Key numbers

* For the Screened Rutherford Kernel with eta = 10^-7 (Problem 1): NFPA runtime 0.542 s and 10 iterations, compared to DSA runtime 3270 s and 73940 iterations.
* For the Exponential Kernel with Delta = 10^-7 (Problem 1): NFPA runtime 0.318 s and 6 iterations, compared to DSA runtime 3120 s and 70851 iterations.
* For the Henyey-Greenstein Kernel with g = 0.99 (Problem 1): NFPA runtime 3.93 s and 73 iterations, compared to DSA runtime 243 s and 5530 iterations.
* For the Screened Rutherford Kernel with eta = 10^-7 (Problem 2): NFPA runtime 0.484 s and 8 iterations, compared to DSA runtime 1470 s and 33246 iterations.
* All numerical experiments used 200 spatial cells, X = 400, sigma_a = 0, L = 15 Legendre moments, and N = 16 discrete ordinates.

## Context

This work extends the Fokker-Planck Synthetic Acceleration (FPSA) method developed by Japanese researchers (JapanFPSA, japanDiss), which accelerates up to N moments of the angular flux, by introducing a nonlinear HOLO scheme that preserves angular moments between the high-order and low-order equations. The method is tested against established approaches including diffusion synthetic acceleration (Alcouffe), nonlinear diffusion acceleration (Smith et al.), and GMRES. The preservation of moments makes NFPA particularly appealing for multiphysics problems where the low-order Fokker-Planck equation can be coupled with other physics models, similar to how standard HOLO schemes are used in multiphysics frameworks (Patel).
