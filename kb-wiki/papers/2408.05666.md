# Nuclear Burning in Accretion Flow of Helium-rich matter onto Compact Objects

> We investigate the impacts of nuclear burning on the spherically symmetric stationary accretion flow of helium-rich matter on to compact objects. We have already shown the existence of the critical accretion rates for the accretion of CO-rich matter above which the flow truncates in the supersonic region due to nuclear burning in the previous paper \citep{2022ApJ...933...29N}. Here, we show that there are also critical accretion rates for helium-rich matter. While we used empirical formulae for the energy generation rates for carbon burning and oxygen burning without solving the nuclear reaction network in our previous work, we solve a simple nuclear reaction network consisting of 13 elements from $^4$He to $^{56}$Ni to investigate influence of the energy generation from not only triple-$α$ reactions but also the subsequent reactions of synthesized elements. We have also qualitatively confirmed the previous results for CO-rich matter accretion using the revised code with the nuclear reaction network and reported some new findings.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that there exist critical accretion rates for helium-rich matter onto compact objects, above which the accretion flow truncates in the supersonic region due to nuclear burning; this result is conditional on the assumption that nuclear reactions only become significant in the supersonic part of the flow, which may not hold for ambient matter with specific enthalpy above approximately \(10^{16}\) erg g\(^{-1}\).

## Method

The authors solve the steady, spherically symmetric conservation equations for mass, momentum, and energy in an accretion flow, coupled to a nuclear reaction network of 13 nuclides from \(^{4}\)He to \(^{56}\)Ni. They search for the sonic point radius for given compact object mass \(M\), accretion rate \(\dot M\), and ambient specific enthalpy \(h_\infty\), then integrate the equations inward including nuclear burning only in the supersonic region. The critical accretion rate is identified as the value above which the flow decelerates to a second sonic point and truncates rather than reaching the center.

## Key numbers

- For pure He accretion onto a \(1.4\ M_\odot\) compact object with \(h_\infty = 5.19 \times 10^{15}\) erg g\(^{-1}\), a transonic flow reaching the center is obtained at \(|\dot M| = 10^{32}\) g s\(^{-1}\); increasing the rate by 60% to \(1.6 \times 10^{32}\) g s\(^{-1}\) causes truncation.
- The critical accretion rate for pure He matter lies between \(1.0 \times 10^{32}\) g s\(^{-1}\) and \(1.6 \times 10^{32}\) g s\(^{-1}\) for \(M = 1.4\ M_\odot\) and \(h_\infty = 5.19 \times 10^{15}\) erg g\(^{-1}\).
- For He-rich matter with solar-like heavy-element abundances, the critical accretion rates are a few percent smaller than for pure He.
- Varying the \(^{12}\)C(\(\alpha,\gamma\))\(^{16}\)O reaction rate by \(+2\sigma\) changes the critical accretion rate by \(-1.7\%\); a \(-2\sigma\) change gives \(+3.6\%\).
- For CO-rich matter, the critical accretion rates are larger than those in the authors' previous work (Shigeyama and Dohi 2022) by a factor of about 2.
- The critical accretion rate for CO-rich matter scales as \(M^{1.862}\), and for pure He matter as \(M^{1.614}\).
- The truncation radius for CO-rich matter is estimated analytically as \(2.7 \times 10^8 f\) cm for oxygen burning with \(X_i = 0.7\) and \(Q_{ij} = 16\) MeV, where \(f \sim 0.6\) reproduces numerical results.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Shigeyama and Dohi 2022), which used empirical energy generation rates for carbon and oxygen burning in CO-rich accretion, by implementing a full 13-element nuclear reaction network. It confirms the existence of critical accretion rates for helium-rich matter, relevant to scenarios where a neutron star or black hole is engulfed by the helium core of an evolved massive star. The results enable future investigation of accretion flows with high specific enthalpy where nuclear burning occurs in the subsonic region, which may affect heavy-element yields in core-collapse supernovae and the explosion mechanism itself, a possibility previously considered by Colgate (1966).
