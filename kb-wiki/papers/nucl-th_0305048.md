# Comparative Analysis of the Mechanisms of Fast Light Particle Formation in Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions at Low and Intermediate Energies

> The dynamics and the mechanisms of preequilibrium-light-particle formation in nucleus-nucleus collisions at low and intermediate energies are studied on the basis of a classical four-body model. The angular and energy distributions of light particles from such processes are calculated. It is found that, at energies below 50 MeV per nucleon, the hardest section of the energy spectrum is formed owing to the acceleration of light particles from the target by the mean field of the projectile nucleus. Good agreement with available experimental data is obtained.

## Key claim
At beam energies below 50 MeV per nucleon, the hardest section of the energy spectrum of preequilibrium light particles is formed primarily by the acceleration of light particles from the target nucleus by the mean field of the projectile nucleus, not by nucleon-nucleon collisions. This conclusion is drawn from a classical four-body model, and the authors note that nucleon-nucleon collisions become more significant only at higher beam energies above approximately 40 A MeV.

## Method
The authors developed a semi-classical four-body model in which both projectile and target nuclei are represented as two-particle subsystems (heavy core plus light fragment). They solved 24 coupled classical equations of motion with six pair interaction potentials, including Woods-Saxon potentials for core-light particle interactions, Coulomb and nuclear potentials for core-core interactions, and phenomenological friction forces to account for energy dissipation. The model tracks 15 possible output reaction channels and calculates differential cross sections by Monte Carlo sampling over initial conditions (impact parameter, internal configurations, and angular momenta), with evaporation contributions from excited fragments added separately using a Maxwell distribution.

## Key numbers
- At beam energies below 50 MeV per nucleon, the hardest section of the energy spectrum is formed by target-nucleus light particles accelerated by the projectile mean field.
- For neutrons emitted from the projectile, the maximum energy is approximately 3 times the beam energy per nucleon (E_n^max ~ 3E_0) when v_0 ~ v_F.
- For neutrons emitted from the target and accelerated by the projectile mean field, the maximum energy is approximately 8 times the beam energy per nucleon (E_n^max ~ 8E_0) under the same conditions.
- For nucleon-nucleon collisions, the maximum energy of an emitted nucleon is approximately 4 times the beam energy per nucleon (E_i^max ~ 4E_0) when v_0 ~ v_i ~ v_F.
- At beam energies around 20 MeV per nucleon, the Pauli exclusion principle limits the maximum neutron energy from nucleon-nucleon collisions to no more than 3.5 times the beam energy per nucleon.
- The rainbow-scattering angle formula uses a coefficient of 0.7 (rather than 0.56 from the literature) for light particles such as neutrons or protons.

## Context
This work extends the Fermi-jet model and related approaches (e.g., Bondorf et al., the hot-spot model, and the moving-source model) by explicitly comparing the roles of mean nuclear fields versus nucleon-nucleon collisions in preequilibrium light particle formation. The authors show that the mechanism of target-nucleon acceleration by the projectile mean field can explain experimental data from reactions such as 20Ne + 165Ho at 20 A MeV and 40Ar + 51V at 44 A MeV, where the hardest particles come from the target rather than the projectile. The work enables future experimental tests using projectiles of different mass on the same target to distinguish between emission mechanisms.
