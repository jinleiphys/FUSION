# Rare isotope production in statistical multifragmentation

> Producing rare isotopes through statistical multifragmentation is investigated using the Mekjian method for exact solutions of the canonical ensemble. Both the initial fragmentation and the the sequential decay are modeled in such a way as to avoid Monte Carlo and thus provide yields for arbitrarily scarce fragments. The importance of sequential decay, exact particle-number conservation and the sensitivities to parameters such as density and temperature are explored. Recent measurements of isotope ratios from the fragmentation of different Sn isotopes are interpreted within this picture.

# Rare isotope production in statistical multifragmentation

## Key claim
Statistical multifragmentation can produce rare neutron-rich isotopes, but the optimum temperature for production depends strongly on fragment mass: for lighter fragments (a≈40) the best temperature is near 5 MeV, while for heavier fragments (a≈100) the optimum is near 3 MeV, where fission rather than multifragmentation dominates. Sequential decay dramatically narrows isotope distributions and pushes yields toward the valley of stability, qualitatively modifying the naive expectation that higher temperatures always produce more exotic isotopes.

## Method
The authors employed the Mekjian recursion method to obtain exact solutions of the canonical ensemble for statistical multifragmentation, avoiding Monte Carlo sampling and thus enabling calculation of yields for arbitrarily scarce fragments. Initial fragmentation yields were calculated for all levels of every possible isotope using the finite-range liquid-drop model (FRLDM) for ground-state energies and a simplified level density parameter α=10 MeV. Sequential decay was then modeled by apportioning the weight of each particle-unstable level into all energetically accessible states through eight decay modes (proton, neutron, deuteron, dineutron, diproton, triton, ³He, and alpha), using Weisskopf arguments with both classical and s-wave tunneling cross sections.

## Key numbers
- Breakup density default: one sixth of normal nuclear density (ρ₀/6), with reduced volume four times the volume at normal nuclear density
- Level density parameter default: α = 10 MeV, with variations tested at 8 MeV and 12 MeV
- Symmetry energy parameter ξ = 23.4 MeV and Coulomb parameter b_c = 0.7 MeV used in liquid-drop estimates
- For a system of A=200, Z=80 at T=4.5 MeV, the isospin amplification factor from liquid-drop analysis is 2ξ/T, which exceeds 10 for temperatures less than 5 MeV
- The variance of energy per nucleon in canonical calculations: σ_{E/A} ≈ 2T/√A, giving approximately 1.0 MeV per nucleon for A≈200 at T≈6 MeV
- For the fragmentation of ¹¹²Sn and ¹²⁴Sn systems at T=4.5 MeV, the ratio R^{124}_{112}(^aC/¹²C) yields e^{(n-z)Δμ_n/T}, where Δμ_n is the difference of neutron chemical potentials
- Experimental results appear consistent with model predictions if approximately one third of the extra neutrons from ¹²⁴Sn relative to ¹¹²Sn evaporated prior to multifragmentation

## Context
This work extends the statistical multifragmentation approach of Chase and Mekjian (1995) and Majumder and Das Gupta (1999) to rare isotope production, using exact canonical ensemble solutions rather than Monte Carlo methods. It builds on the success of statistical models (SMM, MMMC) in predicting mass yields at excitation energies of a few MeV per nucleon, and addresses the isospin amplification effects observed experimentally by Xu et al. (2000) in Sn isotope fragmentation. The results enable comparison with the EPAX parameterization and abrasion/ablation models (Cugnon, 1987; Schmidt, 1991) that currently provide the preferred scenario for rare isotope production, and suggest that multifragmentation may complement these approaches for producing neutron-rich fragments in the a≈40 region.
