# Skyrme-type nuclear interaction as a tool for calculating the finite nuclear size correction to atomic energy levels and the bound-electron $g$ factor

> A state-of-the-art approach for calculating the finite nuclear size correction to atomic energy levels and the bound-electron $g$ factor is introduced and demonstrated for a series of highly charged hydrogen-like ions. Firstly, self-consistent mean-field calculations based on the Skyrme-type nuclear interaction are employed in order to produce a realistic nuclear proton distribution. In the second step, the obtained nuclear charge density is used to construct the potential of an extended nucleus, and the Dirac equation is solved numerically. The ambiguity in the choice of a Skyrme parametrization is supressed by fine-tuning of only one parameter of the Skyrme force in order to accurately reproduce the experimental values of nuclear radii in each particular case. The homogeneously charged sphere approximation, the two-parameter Fermi distribution and experimental nuclear charge distributions are used for comparison with our approach, and the uncertainties of the presented calculations are estimated. In addition, suppression of the finite nuclear size effect for the specific differences of $g$ factors is demonstrated.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a method that uses Skyrme-Hartree-Fock nuclear-structure calculations to produce realistic nuclear charge densities for computing finite nuclear size corrections to atomic energy levels and bound-electron g factors, but the key hedge is that the ambiguity in choosing a Skyrme parametrization is suppressed only after fine-tuning a single parameter (t0) to reproduce experimental root-mean-square nuclear radii, and the method's advantage over simpler models (like the Fermi distribution) becomes significant only when nuclear radii are known to higher accuracy.

## Method

Self-consistent Hartree-Fock calculations using the Skyrme-type nuclear interaction were performed with the skyrme_rpa program to obtain proton densities, which were then convoluted with a Gaussian proton form factor to produce nuclear charge densities. The resulting charge density was used to construct the nuclear potential, and the Dirac equation was solved numerically using a B-spline basis with dual-kinetic-balance approach to obtain bound-electron energies and g factors. The Skyrme parameter t0 was adjusted in each case to reproduce experimental root-mean-square nuclear radii from Angeli (2013), and results were compared with the homogeneously charged sphere approximation, the two-parameter Fermi distribution, and experimental charge distributions from De Vries (1987).

## Key numbers

- For hydrogen-like lead 208Pb81+, the FNS correction to the 1s1/2 energy using the Skyrme model (with adjusted t0) was 1.3147 × 10−4 in units of electron rest energy, with an uncertainty of 0.0004 × 10−4 from the experimental RMS radius uncertainty of 5.5012(13) fm.
- For hydrogen-like calcium 40Ca19+, the FNS correction to the 1s1/2 energy using the Skyrme model was 2.8502 × 10−8 in units of electron rest energy, with an uncertainty of 0.003 × 10−8 from the experimental RMS radius of 3.4776(19) fm.
- For hydrogen-like tin 116Sn49+, the FNS correction to the 1s1/2 g factor using the Skyrme model was 1.4408 × 10−5, with an uncertainty of 0.001 × 10−5 from the experimental RMS radius of 4.6250(19) fm.
- For hydrogen-like lead 208Pb81+, the FNS correction to the 1s1/2 g factor using the Skyrme model was 4.5291 × 10−4, with an uncertainty of 0.002 × 10−4.
- The specific difference g'FNS[1s1/2, 2s1/2] for 208Pb81+ using the Skyrme model was −2.278 × 10−7, showing suppression of the FNS effect by several orders of magnitude compared to individual g factor corrections.
- For 208Pb81+, using numerically calculated ξs and ξp from the sphere model instead of analytical formulas reduced the Skyrme-model specific difference corrections to −1.1 × 10−9 and 5.4 × 10−10, respectively, which is 2-3 orders of magnitude smaller.

## Context

This work extends the approach of using nuclear charge distributions for FNS corrections beyond the simple Fermi distribution model used by Beier (2000) and the analytical expressions from Shabaev (1993) and Glazov (2004). The method enables more reliable calculations of FNS corrections for high-precision tests of quantum electrodynamics in highly charged ions, particularly for bound-electron g factor measurements as performed by Sturm (2014) and others. The suppression of the FNS effect in specific differences, introduced by Shabaev (2002) and used for fine-structure constant determination proposals, is demonstrated to be further enhanced by using numerically evaluated scaling factors from the best available nuclear model rather than analytical expansions.
