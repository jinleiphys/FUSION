# Nonperturbative effects in QCD at Finite Temperature and Density

> These lecture notes illustrate the application of Dyson-Schwinger equations in QCD. The extensive body of work at zero temperature and chemical potential is represented by a selection of contemporary studies that focus on solving the Bethe-Salpeter equation, deriving an exact mass formula in QCD that describes light and heavy pseudoscalar mesons simultaneously, and the calculation of the electromagnetic pion form factor and the vector meson electroproduction cross sections. These applications emphasise the qualitative importance of the momentum-dependent dressing of elementary Schwinger functions in QCD, which provides a unifying connection between disparate phenomena. They provide a solid foundation for an extension of the approach to nonzero temperature and chemical potential. The essential, formal elements of this application are described and four contemporary studies employed to exemplify the method and its efficacy. They study the demarcation of the phase boundary for deconfinement and chiral symmetry restoration, the calculation of bulk thermodynamic properties of the quark-gluon plasma and the response of pi- and rho-meson observables to T and mu. Along the way a continuum order parameter for deconfinement is introduced, an anticorrelation between the response of masses and decay constants to T and their response to mu elucidated, and a (T,mu)-mirroring of the slow approach of bulk thermodynamic quantities to their ultrarelativistic limit highlighted. These effects too are tied to the momentum-dependent dressing of the elementary Schwinger functions.

## Key claim

The Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) provide a nonperturbative, Poincare invariant continuum framework for studying QCD at finite temperature and chemical potential, with the momentum-dependent dressing of elementary Schwinger functions serving as a unifying mechanism that simultaneously generates confinement, dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB), and the observed properties of hadrons without fine-tuning, provided the truncation scheme preserves global symmetries such as chiral symmetry.

## Method

The author reviews the application of DSEs to QCD at zero temperature and chemical potential as a foundation, then extends the formalism to nonzero temperature (T) and chemical potential (mu). The approach involves solving the quark DSE (the "gap equation") using a dressed-gluon propagator that exhibits infrared enhancement, combined with the Bethe-Salpeter equation for meson bound states, employing truncations (rainbow for the quark DSE, ladder for the scattering kernel) that preserve the axial-vector Ward-Takahashi identity. Four contemporary studies are used to exemplify the method: demarcation of the phase boundary for deconfinement and chiral symmetry restoration, calculation of bulk thermodynamic properties of the quark-gluon plasma, and the response of pi- and rho-meson observables to T and mu.

## Key numbers

* The dressed-quark mass-function M(p^2) for light quarks (u/d, s) rises sharply below p^2 ~ 1 GeV^2, with the boundary at p^2 ~ 1-2 GeV^2 where the enhancement in the dressed-gluon propagator becomes significant.
* Current-quark masses at renormalization point mu ~ 20 GeV: m_{u/d}^mu = 3.7 MeV, m_s^mu = 82 MeV, m_c^mu = 0.59 GeV, m_b^mu = 2.0 GeV.
* Evolved to 1 GeV: m_{u/d}^{1 GeV} = 5.5 MeV, m_s^{1 GeV} = 130 MeV, m_c^{1 GeV} = 1.0 GeV, m_b^{1 GeV} = 3.4 GeV.
* Ratio of Euclidean constituent-quark mass M^E to current-quark mass at mu ~ 20 GeV: for u/d = 150, for s = 10, for c = 2.3, for b = 1.4, for t approaches 1.
* The characteristic mass-scale associated with DCSB, M_chi ~ 0.2 GeV ~ Lambda_QCD.
* In the chiral limit, the pion is a massless composite of quark and antiquark each with effective mass M^E ~ 450 MeV.
* The Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner relation is recovered: f_pi^2 m_pi^2 = -[m_u^mu + m_d^mu] <q qbar>_mu^0 + O(m_q^2), and similarly for the K+.

## Context

These lecture notes extend the extensive body of DSE work at zero temperature and chemical potential (reviewed in Roberts and Williams, 1994) to finite T and mu, providing a continuum complement to lattice-QCD simulations. The approach enables study of the phase boundary for deconfinement and chiral symmetry restoration, bulk thermodynamics of the quark-gluon plasma, and the T- and mu-dependence of hadron properties in regions (such as finite chemical potential) that are presently inaccessible to lattice simulations. A continuum order parameter for deconfinement is introduced, and an anticorrelation between the response of masses and decay constants to T versus mu is elucidated.
