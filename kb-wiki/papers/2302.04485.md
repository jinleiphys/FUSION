# Strong correlation of the neutron star core-crust transition density with the $σ$-meson mass via vacuum polarization

> We study the neutron star core-crust transition density $ρ_t$ with the inclusion of the vacuum polarization in the dielectric function in the nonlinear relativistic Hartree approach (RHAn). It is found that the strong correlation between the $ρ_{t}$ and the scalar meson mass $m_σ$ strikingly overwhelms the uncertainty of the nuclear equation of state in the RHAn models, in contrast to the usual awareness that $ρ_{t}$ is predominantly sensitive to the isovector nuclear potential and symmetry energy. The accurate extraction of $ρ_{t}$ through the future gravitational wave measurements can thus provide a strong constraint on the longstanding uncertainty of $m_σ$, which is of significance to better infer the vacuum property. As an astrophysical implication, it suggests that the correlation between $ρ_t$ and $m_σ$ is very favorable to reconcile the difficulty in reproducing the large crustal moment of inertia for the pulsar glitches with the well constrained symmetry energy.

# Strong correlation of the neutron star core-crust transition density with the σ-meson mass via vacuum polarization

## Key claim
The core-crust transition density ρt in neutron stars is strongly correlated with the scalar meson mass mσ through the inclusion of vacuum polarization in the dielectric function, and this correlation overwhelms the uncertainty of the nuclear equation of state in nonlinear relativistic Hartree approximation (RHAn) models. This contrasts with the usual awareness that ρt is predominantly sensitive to the isovector nuclear potential and symmetry energy.

## Method
The authors employed the nonlinear relativistic Hartree approximation (RHAn) with renormalized vacuum polarization in the dielectric function, treating the density-dependent and Feynman parts of the polarization tensor on equal footing. The transition density ρt was determined as the largest density at which the dielectric function εL becomes less than or equal to zero, using the relativistic random phase approximation (RRPA) with the scalar polarization renormalized at the on-shell point q² = mσ². The coupling constants were adjusted to reproduce nuclear matter saturation properties at ρ0 = 0.16 fm⁻³ with binding energy -16 MeV and vanishing pressure.

## Key numbers
- The transition density ρt ranges from approximately 0.075 to 0.095 fm⁻³ across various RHAn parametrizations with mσ varying from 454 to 580 MeV
- The incompressibility κ ranges from 240 to 300 MeV in the RHAn models (RHAn1: 300 MeV, RHAn2: 270 MeV, RHAn3: 240 MeV)
- The symmetry energy at saturation density is constrained to 31.7 ± 3.2 MeV, with a scatter range from 28.5 to 42.8 MeV across various models
- The slope parameter L of the symmetry energy ranges from about 70 to 120 MeV in the models, consistent with 106 ± 37 MeV from the PREX-2 experiment
- For the Vela pulsar glitch requirement of ΔI/I = 7%, the minimum mσ values range from 454 MeV (RHAn1 with Esym = 42.8 MeV) to 580 MeV (RHAn3 with Esym = 33.0 MeV)
- The maximum neutron star masses range from 1.72 M⊙ (RHAn3) to 2.04 M⊙ (RHAn1)
- The tidal deformability Λg for 1.4 M⊙ neutron stars ranges from 408.5 to 711.1 across the parametrizations

## Context
This work extends the relativistic Hartree approximation (RHA) originally developed by Chin and Walecka (1977) by including nonlinear σ-meson self-interactions to reduce the compression modulus to empirical values. It contrasts with standard relativistic mean-field (RMF) models such as NL3 and FSUGold, which show no significant ρt-mσ correlation because they lack the Dirac sea contribution. The strong correlation enables future gravitational wave measurements from third-generation detectors (Abbott et al., 2017) to constrain the longstanding uncertainty of mσ (400-650 MeV according to PDG 2022), and helps reconcile the difficulty in reproducing the large crustal moment of inertia (7%) required for Vela pulsar glitches (Andersson et al., 2012; Chamel, 2013) with well-constrained symmetry energy.
