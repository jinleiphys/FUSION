# Quantum entanglement and interference from classical statistics

> Quantum mechanics for a four-state-system is derived from classical statistics. Entanglement, interference, the difference between identical fermions or bosons and the unitary time evolution find an interpretation within a classical statistical ensemble. Quantum systems are subsystems of larger classical statistical systems, which include the environment or the vacuum. They are characterized by incomplete statistics in the sense that the classical correlation function cannot be used for sequences of measurements in the subsystem.

## Key claim

Quantum mechanics for a four-state system can be derived from classical statistics, but only if the subsystem is described by "incomplete statistics" where the classical correlation function cannot be used for sequences of measurements in the subsystem, and the quantum system is an isolated subsystem of a larger classical statistical ensemble that includes the environment or vacuum.

## Method

The author constructs a classical statistical ensemble with infinitely many classical states τ, each with probabilities pτ ≥ 0, and defines the quantum subsystem by fifteen expectation values ρk (for a four-state system) that are computed from the classical probability distribution. The pure states of the subsystem are parameterized by the homogeneous space SU(4)/SU(3)×U(1), represented by a 4×4 density matrix ρ = ¼(1 + ρkLk) where Lk are fifteen hermitean matrices. The classical probability distribution is written as p({σk},ζ) = ps({σk})p̄s(ζ) + δpe({σk},ζ), where ps({σk}) = 2⁻¹⁵∏k(1 + σkρk) contains the subsystem information and δpe describes the environment.

## Key numbers

- The pure state manifold is SU(4)/SU(3)×U(1), with dimension 6 (the complex projective space CP³)
- Fifteen numbers fk (or ρk) describe the four-state quantum subsystem, normalized such that ∑k fk² = 3 for pure states
- The purity P = ρkρk ≤ 3, with P = 3 for pure states and P = 4trρ² - 1 = 4∑α pα² - 1
- For the entangled state ψ± = 1/√2(ψ₂ ± ψ₃), the expectation values are f₃ = -1, f₁₂ = ±1, f₁₄ = ∓1, with all other fk vanishing
- For the interference example, ⟨T₂⟩ = cos(Δt) where Δ = ωa - ωb
- The classical probability distribution ps involves 2¹⁵ different possibilities for the σk variables

## Context

This work extends earlier attempts to derive quantum mechanical features from classical statistical ensembles (referenced as "3" and "4" in the text), but claims to be the first full implementation of all features of quantum mechanics within classical statistics. It builds on the functional integral representation of quantum field theory (Zinn-Justin, referenced as "ZJ") and the geometric description of complex projective spaces for pure states (Brody and Hughston, referenced as "BH"). The approach enables a framework to test quantum mechanics against neighboring statistical ensembles where properties like unitary evolution or the purity constraint are not obeyed exactly, potentially describing decoherence or non-linear versions of quantum mechanics.
