# The Solar $Hep$ Process

> The $Hep$ process is a weak-interaction reaction, $He3 + p \to He4 + e^+ + ν_e$, which occurs in the sun. There is renewed interest in $Hep$ owing to current experimental efforts to extract from the observed solar neutrino spectrum information on non-standard physics in the neutrino sector. $Hep$ produces highest-energy solar neutrinos, although their flux is quite modest. This implies that the $Hep$ neutrios can at some level influence the solar neutrino spectrum near its upper end. Therefore, a precise interpretation of the observed solar neutrino spectrum requires an accurate estimate of the $Hep$ rate. This is an interesting but challenging task. We describe the difficulties involved and how the recent theoretical developments in nuclear physics have enabled us to largely overcome these difficulties. A historical survey of $Hep$ calculations is followed by an overview of the latest developments. We compare the results obtained in the conventional nuclear physics approach and those obtained in a newly developed effective field theory approach. We also discuss the current status of the experiments relevant to $Hep$.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the $Hep$ reaction rate can now be calculated with significantly improved reliability, but the result still carries a $\sim$15% uncertainty due to the large cancellation between one-body and two-body contributions, which amplifies the dependence on the momentum-cutoff parameter $\Lambda$ in the effective field theory approach.

## Method

The authors review two complementary theoretical frameworks for calculating the $Hep$ cross section: the Standard Nuclear Physics Approach (SNPA), which uses phenomenological potentials and correlated-hyperspherical-harmonics wave functions for the four-body system, and a hybrid effective field theory (EFT*) approach that combines chiral perturbation theory transition operators with SNPA wave functions. In the EFT* calculation, the unknown low-energy constant $\hat{d}_R$ appearing in the two-body axial-current operator is fixed by fitting to the experimental tritium beta-decay rate, enabling a parameter-free prediction of the $Hep$ $S$-factor.

## Key numbers

- The SNPA calculation gives $S = (10.1 \pm 0.6) \times 10^{-20}$ keV-b at 10 keV (near the Gamow peak), with a threshold value of $S = 9.64 \times 10^{-20}$ keV-b.
- The EFT* calculation gives a threshold $S$-factor of $S = (8.6 \pm 1.3) \times 10^{-20}$ keV-b, where the error reflects the $\Lambda$ dependence.
- The $Hep$ neutrino flux predicted by the standard solar model (BP00) is $\phi_\nu(hep) = 9.4 \times 10^3$ cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$, which is seven orders of magnitude smaller than the $pp$-fusion neutrino flux and three orders of magnitude smaller than the $^8$B neutrino flux.
- The maximum energy of $Hep$ neutrinos is $E_\nu^{max}(hep) = 18.8$ MeV, compared to $E_\nu^{max}(^8{\rm B}) = 17$ MeV for $^8$B neutrinos.
- The tritium beta-decay rate used for normalization is $\Gamma_\beta^{\rm tritium}({\rm exp}) = 12.32 \pm 0.03$ yr.

## Context

This review extends the long history of $Hep$ calculations dating back to Salpeter (1952), who obtained $S=630\times 10^{-20}$ keV-b, through Werntz and Brennan (1967, 1973), Tegner and Bargholtz (1983), and the variational Monte Carlo work of Carlson et al. (1991) and Schiavilla et al. (1992), which brought the $S$-factor down to the range of $1-3\times 10^{-20}$ keV-b. The convergence of the SNPA result of Marcucci et al. (2000, 2001) and the EFT* result of Park et al. provides a reliable basis for interpreting the high-energy tail of the solar neutrino spectrum, which is essential for extracting information on neutrino oscillations and non-standard physics from experiments at Super-Kamiokande and SNO.
