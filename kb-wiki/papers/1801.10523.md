# Determine Arbitrary Feynman Integrals by Vacuum Integrals

> By introducing an auxiliary parameter, we find a new representation for Feynman integrals, which defines a Feynman integral by analytical continuation of a series containing only vacuum integrals. The new representation therefore conceptually translates the problem of computing Feynman integrals to the problem of performing analytical continuations. As an application of the new representation, we use it to construct a novel reduction method for multi-loop Feynman integrals, which is expected to be more efficient than known integration-by-parts reduction method. Using the new method, we successfully reduced all complicated two-loop integrals in $gg\to HH$ process and $gg\to ggg$ process.

## Key claim

By introducing an auxiliary parameter η into all Feynman propagators, any Feynman integral can be defined as the analytical continuation of an asymptotic series that contains only vacuum integrals; this representation conceptually translates the problem of computing Feynman integrals to the problem of performing analytical continuations, with the caveat that the series must be truncated in practice and the continuation is realized by solving ordinary differential equations.

## Method

The authors introduce an auxiliary parameter η into all propagators of a dimensionally regularized L-loop Feynman integral, then expand the integrand in the limit η → ∞, where all loop momenta scale as |η|^(1/2). This expansion yields an asymptotic series in 1/η whose coefficients are vacuum integrals with equal internal squared masses −iη, which are reduced to known vacuum master integrals. Using this series representation, the authors construct a step-by-step reduction algorithm that searches for linear relations among sets of integrals by matching coefficients of vacuum master integrals, η, and kinematic variables; the algorithm iteratively increases the polynomial degree d_max until the desired integrals are expressed in terms of simpler ones, avoiding the large coupled linear systems typical of integration-by-parts reduction.

- For the six-propagator nonplanar sector in gg → HH (Fig. 1c), reducing the set **3⁺**e (56 integrals) required 55 relations with d_max = 1 and 1 relation with d_max = 5.
- For the seven-propagator nonplanar sector in gg → ggg (Fig. 2c), reducing **3⁺**e (84 integrals) required 14 relations with d_max = 0, 64 with d_max = 1, 4 with d_max = 2, and 2 with d_max = 4.
- Reduction relations for all sectors listed in Table 1 were obtained on a laptop (4-core Intel i7-6500U, 16GB RAM) within 1 day.
- Solving the obtained relations for a given phase-space point and spacetime dimension D takes under 0.01 second, compared to several hours needed by the FIRE5 implementation of integration-by-parts reduction.
- The number of L-loop equal-mass vacuum master integrals B_L is given: B_1 = 1, B_2 = 2, B_3 = 5.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous method (Liu and Ma, 2017) that introduced an auxiliary parameter to set up ordinary differential equations for multiloop master integrals. The new representation provides a systematic reduction method that contrasts with the traditional integration-by-parts reduction based on Laporta's algorithm (Chetyrkin and Tkachov, 1981; Laporta, 2001), which generates huge coupled linear systems that have proven insufficient for complete reduction of two-loop integrals in processes such as gg → HH (Borowka et al., 2016). The method successfully reduces all complicated two-loop integrals in gg → HH and gg → ggg, and the authors suggest it should be applicable to even more challenging problems such as two-loop five-gluon scattering with a massive quark loop and gg → tt̅g.
