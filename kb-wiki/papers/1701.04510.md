# Influence of pairing correlations on the radius of neutron-rich nuclei

> The influence of pairing correlations on the neutron root mean square (rms) radius of nuclei is investigated in the framework of self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations. The continuum is treated appropriately by the Green's function techniques. As an example the nucleus $^{124}$Zr is treated for a varying strength of pairing correlations. We find that, as the pairing strength increases, the neutron rms radius first shrinks, reaches a minimum and beyond this point it expands again. The shrinkage is due to the the so-called `pairing anti-halo effect', i. e. due to the decreasing of the asymptotic density distribution with increasing pairing. However, in some cases, increasing pairing correlations can also lead to an expansion of the nucleus due to a growing occupation of so-called `halo' orbits, i.e. weakly bound states and resonances in the continuum with low-$\ell $ values. In this case, the neutron radii are extended just by the influence of pairing correlations, since these `halo' orbits cannot be occupied without pairing. The term `anti-halo effect' is not justified in such cases. For a full understanding of this complicated interplay self-consistent calculations are necessary.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that increasing pairing correlations in neutron-rich nuclei can either shrink or expand the neutron root mean square radius, depending on the competition between the "pairing anti-halo effect" (which reduces asymptotic density) and the growing occupation of low-angular-momentum continuum orbits (which increases the radius). The authors show that the term "anti-halo effect" is not universally justified, as strong pairing can actually produce radius expansion.

## Method
Self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations were performed using the SkI4 functional with a density-dependent delta interaction (DDDI) pairing force. The continuum was treated properly using Green's function techniques to avoid discretization artifacts, with calculations performed for the neutron-rich nucleus 124Zr and compared to 122Zr. The pairing strength was varied artificially by a factor eta from 0.6 to 1.4 to study the systematic effect on neutron radii, with box-discretized HFB calculations also performed for comparison.

## Key numbers
- Neutron rms radius of 124Zr first decreases with increasing pairing strength, reaches a minimum at eta approximately 1.0, then increases for eta greater than 1.0
- The pairing strength factor eta ranges from 0.6 to 1.4, corresponding to neutron pairing energies from -0.7 to -83.7 MeV and average pairing gaps from 0.2 to 2.9 MeV
- The HF calculation without pairing (eta=0) gives a neutron rms radius of 5.08 fm for 124Zr, which is 0.6% larger than the continuum HFB result of 5.05 fm at eta=0.6
- For 122Zr, the neutron rms radius first remains unchanged and then monotonously increases with increasing pairing strength, showing no anti-halo effect

## Context
This work extends the theoretical discussion of the "pairing anti-halo effect" introduced by Bennaceur et al. (2000), who showed that pairing correlations prevent divergence of the rms radius for weakly bound s-wave orbits. The authors go beyond the fixed-potential study of Yamagami (2005) by performing fully self-consistent calculations with proper continuum treatment. The results clarify that the interplay between pairing-induced shrinkage of individual orbit radii and the increased occupation of low-angular-momentum continuum orbits can produce either radius shrinkage or expansion, enabling more nuanced predictions for halo structure in neutron-rich nuclei near the drip line.
