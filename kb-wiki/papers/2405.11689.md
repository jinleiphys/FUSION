# Investigation of suppression of $Υ(nS)$ in relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies

> The primary purpose of studying quarkonium production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is to understand the properties of the quark-gluon plasma. At various collision systems, measurements of quarkonium states of different binding energies, such as $Υ(nS)$, can provide comprehensive information. A model study has been performed to investigate the modification of $Υ(nS)$ production in Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}=$ 5.02 TeV and Au-Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}=$ 200 GeV. The Monte-Carlo simulation study is performed with a publicly available hydrodynamic simulation package for the quark-gluon plasma medium and a theoretical calculation of temperature-dependent thermal width of $Υ(nS)$ considering the gluo-dissociation and inelastic parton scattering for dissociation inside the medium. In addition, we perform a systematic study with different descriptions of initial collision geometry and formation time of $Υ(nS)$ to investigate their impacts on yield modification. The model calculation with a varied parameter set can describe the experimental data of $Υ(nS)$ in Pb-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV and $Υ(2S)$ in Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV but underestimates the modification of $Υ(1S)$ at the lower collision energy. The nuclear absorption mechanism is explored to understand the discrepancy between the data and simulation.

## Key claim

The model can describe the experimental data for Υ(nS) in Pb-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV and Υ(2S) in Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV, but it underestimates the suppression of Υ(1S) at the lower collision energy (200 GeV), suggesting that additional nuclear absorption mechanisms beyond the baseline dissociation model are needed to explain the similar suppression of Υ(1S) at both RHIC and LHC energies.

## Method

The authors performed Monte-Carlo simulations using the TRENTO model for initial collision geometry and the SONIC hydrodynamic simulation package for the quark-gluon plasma medium evolution. The temperature-dependent thermal width of Υ(nS) was calculated from hard-thermal loop perturbation theory considering gluo-dissociation and inelastic parton scattering, and the survival probability was computed by integrating the dissociation rate over the medium evolution. A systematic study was conducted with different initial geometry parameters (reduced thickness parameter p = 0 and p = 1) and varying formation times for each Υ state, and nuclear absorption was added as an additional suppression mechanism for the RHIC energy case.

## Key numbers

- Full dissociation temperatures: 600 MeV for Υ(1S), 240 MeV for Υ(2S), and 190 MeV for Υ(3S)
- Default formation times: 0.5 fm/c for Υ(1S), 1.0 fm/c for Υ(2S), and 1.5 fm/c for Υ(3S)
- Nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross-sections: 42.3 mb for √sNN = 200 GeV and 70.0 mb for √sNN = 5.02 TeV
- Nucleon width parameter: σ = 0.4 fm
- Hydrodynamic simulation time step: Δt = 0.03 fm/c
- Nuclear absorption cross-sections tested: 2 mb to 6 mb for Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV
- Formation time variations: ±0.2 fm/c for Υ(1S), ±0.4 fm/c for Υ(2S), and ±0.6 fm/c for Υ(3S)

## Context

This work extends the SHINCHON simulation framework previously introduced by Kim (2022) for p-Pb, p-O, and O-O collisions to study Υ(nS) modification in heavy-ion collisions at two different energies. The study addresses the puzzling experimental observation from STAR (2022) and CMS (2018, 2023) that Υ(1S) suppression is similar at RHIC and LHC energies despite higher temperatures at the LHC, which contradicts expectations from other models such as the open quantum system approach by Brambilla (2022) and the transport model by Du (2017). The nuclear absorption mechanism, previously used by McGlinchey (2012) to describe J/ψ suppression at backward rapidity in d-Au collisions, is explored to resolve the discrepancy. Future experimental measurements of Υ(nS) in p-A and A-A collisions at different energies could help clarify the relationship between formation time and final-state suppression effects.
