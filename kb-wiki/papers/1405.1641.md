# A Differential Equation for the Transition Probability B(E2) and the Resulting Recursion Relations connecting Even-Even Nuclei

> We obtain here a new relation for the reduced electric quadrupole transition probability B(E2) of a given nucleus in terms of its derivatives with respect to neutron and proton numbers based on a similar local energy relation in the Infinite Nuclear Matter (INM) model of Atomic Nuclei, which is essentially built on the foundation of the Hugenholtz-Van Hove Theorem of many-body theory. Obviously such a relation in the form of a differential equation is expected to be more powerful than the usual algebraic difference equations. Although the relation for B(E2) has been perceived simply on the basis of a corresponding differential equation for the local energy in the INM model, its theoretical foundation otherwise has been clearly demonstrated. We further exploit the differential equation in using the very definitions of the derivatives to obtain two different recursion relations for B(E2) , connecting in each case three neighboring even-even nuclei from lower to higher mass numbers and vice-verse. We demonstrate their numerical validity using available data throughout the nuclear chart and also explore their possible utility in predicting B(E2) values.

## Key claim

The authors propose a new differential equation for the reduced electric quadrupole transition probability B(E2) of a given nucleus in terms of its partial derivatives with respect to neutron and proton numbers, based on an analogy with the local energy relation in the Infinite Nuclear Matter (INM) model. The relation is presented as an ansatz and is not strictly valid at magic numbers or in regions where deformations change sharply.

## Method

The authors start from the local energy differential equation of the INM model, which itself derives from the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem of many-body theory, and postulate an analogous differential equation for B(E2) by replacing the local energy with B(E2). They then justify this equation theoretically by approximating B(E2) as a sum of a function of neutron number and a function of proton number, each taken as linear in N and Z respectively. Using forward and backward finite-difference definitions for the partial derivatives, they convert the differential equation into two recursion relations connecting three neighboring even-even nuclei: one forward relation linking (N,Z), (N-2,Z), and (N,Z-2), and one backward relation linking (N,Z), (N+2,Z), and (N,Z+2).

## Key numbers

- For the forward recursion relation [B(E2)-F], 119 out of 187 cases showed deviations within +/- 25% error when tested against the experimental data set of Raman et al. in the mass range A=10 to 240.
- For the backward recursion relation [B(E2)-B], 124 out of 186 cases showed deviations within +/- 25% error.
- For comparison, the difference equation of Patnaik et al. (connecting four nuclei) showed deviations within +/- 25% error in 117 out of 178 cases.
- The experimental B(E2) value for 142Nd ranges from 0.256 to 0.437 e^2b^2, with an adopted value of 0.265 e^2b^2; for 154Sm it ranges from 3.45 to 6.8 e^2b^2, with an adopted value of 4.36 e^2b^2.
- For 236U, the two recursion relations predict values differing by about 17%, with input data errors of 0.20, 0.70, 0.20, and 0.17 e^2b^2 for the neighboring nuclei.

## Context

This work extends earlier efforts to find symmetry relations in nuclear properties beyond ground-state masses, specifically following the difference equations for B(E2) developed by Ross and Bhaduri and the simpler four-nuclei difference equations of Patnaik et al. (2014). The differential equation approach is claimed to be more powerful than algebraic difference equations, and the recursion relations are tested against the experimental data compilation of Raman et al. and compared with predictions from the Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM) and the Single-Shell Asymptotic Nilsson Model (SSANM). The work enables prediction of unknown B(E2) values for even-even nuclei throughout the nuclear chart, with demonstrated success for isotopes of Cr, Fe, Ni, and Zn when compared to the newer data set of Pritychenko et al.
