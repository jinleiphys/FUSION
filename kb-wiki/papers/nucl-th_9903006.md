# Statistical Models of Nuclear Fragmentation

> A method is presented that allows exact calculations of fragment multiplicity distributions for a canonical ensemble of non-interacting clusters. Fragmentation properties are shown to depend on only a few parameters. Fragments are shown to be copiously produced above the transition temperature. At this transition temperature, the calculated multiplicity distributions broaden and become strongly super-Poissonian. This behavior is compared to predictions from a percolation model. A corresponding microcanonical formalism is also presented.

# Statistical Models of Nuclear Fragmentation

## Key claim
The paper presents an exact method for calculating fragment multiplicity distributions in a canonical ensemble of non-interacting clusters, showing that fragmentation properties depend on only a few parameters (surface term to temperature ratio, intrinsic entropy, and Coulomb term). The authors hedge that the observed sharp transition and spike in the correlation coefficient at the boiling temperature, which they associate with a first-order phase transition, might disappear in a microcanonical treatment because a wide range of energy densities collapses to a narrow range of temperatures.

## Method
The authors use a recursion relation derived by Chase and Mekjian (1995) to exactly calculate the canonical partition function for a system of non-interacting clusters of fixed nucleon number A, then extend this approach to compute factorial moments and multiplicity distributions via additional recursion relations. Individual fragment partition functions are parameterized using a liquid-drop model with bulk, surface, and Coulomb terms, where the surface energy is fixed at 17.2 MeV and the Coulomb term includes a Wigner-Seitz screening correction. The calculations assume a density of one third of nuclear matter density (0.05 fm^-3) and are performed numerically in a fraction of a second of computer time.

## Key numbers
- Surface energy parameter: f_s = 17.2 MeV
- Coulomb parameter: f_c = 0.70 (1 - (rho/rho_0)^(1/3)) MeV, where rho_0 = 0.15 fm^-3
- Density used in calculations: one third of nuclear matter density (0.05 fm^-3)
- Temperatures studied: T = 7.0, 7.8, and 8.6 MeV for mass distributions; T = 7, 8, and 9 MeV for multiplicity distributions
- System sizes: A = 100, 250, 500, and 1000 for various calculations
- IMF definition: fragments with mass between 5 and 40 (or 6 and 40 for percolation comparison)
- Percolation critical bond-breaking probability: p_c = 0.7512
- Number of sites in percolation lattice: 203

## Context
This work extends the exact canonical partition function method of Chase and Mekjian (1995) to include fragmentation observables, providing a numerically efficient alternative to Monte Carlo approaches such as those used by Randrup and Koonin (1981) or the Copenhagen group (Gross, 1990; Bondorf et al., 1995). The results are contrasted with percolation models (Bauer, 1985; Campi, 1986) and with experimental measurements of IMF multiplicity distributions by Moretto and collaborators (1995, 1997), which showed sub-Poissonian behavior describable by binomial distributions. The method enables exact calculation of all fragmentation observables without arbitrary algorithmic choices, and the authors suggest it can be extended to include isospin conservation and microcanonical ensembles, though the latter requires significantly more computational effort (estimated factor of 10^6 increase for 1 GeV excitation energy with 1.0 MeV steps).
