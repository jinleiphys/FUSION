# Relativistic Processes and the Internal Structure of Neutron Stars

> Models for the internal composition of Dense Compact Stars are reviewed as well as macroscopic properties derived by observations of relativistic processes. Modeling of pure neutron matter Neutron Stars is presented and crust properties are studied by means of a two fluid model.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how the internal composition of neutron stars can be inferred from relativistic processes, but the actual crust-core transition point is determined by a two-fluid model that neglects finite-size effects like Coulomb and surface energy, which would lead to pasta phases.

## Method

The authors model neutron star structure by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for static, non-rotating stars using various equations of state (EoS) categorized as hadronic (pure neutron matter or hyperonic matter), strange quark matter, or boson condensates. The crust-core transition is studied via a two-component system where mechanical and chemical equilibrium (Gibbs conditions) determine the density at which homogeneous matter splits into nuclear clusters immersed in pure neutron matter, with the spinodal line marking the instability boundary.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star central densities range from approximately \(10^{14}\) to \(10^{16}\) g/cm\(^3\).
- The symmetry energy at saturation density \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) is \(S(n_0) = 30 \pm 1\) MeV, as determined by isospin diffusion experiments.
- For the PALu model, the crust-core transition occurs at baryon number density \(n_c = 0.0892\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The fastest observed pulsar rotation is 716 MHz.
- Neutron star surface gravity reaches \(10^{14}\) cm/s\(^2\) and magnetic fields reach \(10^{15}\) G.
- The Vela pulsar observations give a lower bound on crustal moment of inertia: \(I/I_{crust} \geq 1.4\%\).
- A 2 \(M_\odot\) neutron star (J1614-2230) has been measured via Shapiro delay.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on neutron star structure by Landau (1932) and Zwicky (1934) and builds on the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff framework from Shapiro (1983). The two-fluid model for the crust-core transition follows the approach of Prakash (1988) and is discussed in detail by Kubis (2011). The paper enables future work by showing how forthcoming observations from new extraterrestrial telescopes can constrain equations of state, particularly the 2 \(M_\odot\) measurement that imposes strong constraints on the EoS.
