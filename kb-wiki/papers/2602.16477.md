# The Crusts of Neutron Stars Revisited: Approximations within a Polytropic Equation of State Approach

> In this work, we revisit several thin-crust approximations presented in the literature and compare them with the exact solutions of the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff (TOV) equations. In addition, we employ three different equations of state (EoSs), including one with a pasta phase, each based on a distinct theoretical framework: the variational method, relativistic Brueckner--Hartree--Fock theory, and relativistic mean-field theory. We emphasize that these approximations require only the TOV solutions for the core and the EoS properties at the core--crust interface; in our approach, only the energy density is needed. Finally, the relativistic approximation, as well as the Newtonian approximation with corrections, shows good agreement with the exact solutions. This indicates that a simple treatment of the crust is sufficient for structural purposes, independently of the uncertainties in the sub-nuclear equation of state, which are not very large. The unified EoS SINPA (relativistic mean-field theory), including the pasta phase, was used to study the thin-crust approximation, while degeneracy in the $M$--$R$ relation is demonstrated through: (i) anisotropic pressure in the modified TOV equations, (ii) the $f(R, L_m, T)$ gravity model, and (iii) dark matter admixture. As demonstrated, modifications to the description of gravitation introduce degeneracies in the mass--radius relation that are challenging to disentangle or quantify precisely.

## Key claim

The paper argues that simple thin-crust approximations (relativistic or Newtonian with corrections) are sufficient for determining neutron star structural properties, because the intrinsic uncertainty from any crust approximation is about 500 m, which is larger than the differences between detailed crust models. The authors qualify that this conclusion holds for catalyzed matter and that the radius is only partially determined by the subnuclear equation of state, with significant dependence on the core radius.

## Method

The authors compared four hierarchical thin-crust approximations (simplest slab, Newtonian thin, relativistic thin using Tolman VII core radius, and relativistic thin using exact TOV core radius) against exact numerical solutions of the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for four equations of state: MPA1 (relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock), AP4 (variational method with AV18+UIX*), MS1 (relativistic mean-field), and the unified SINPA EoS (relativistic mean-field with pasta phase). For each EoS, they computed mass-radius relations and crust thickness for fixed masses of 1.4, 2.0, and 2.08 solar masses, using a polytropic fit to the inner crust EoS and the core-crust transition properties from each model.

## Key numbers

- The intrinsic uncertainty from any crust approximation is approximately 500 m, meaning measurements would need precision of 100 m or less to discriminate subnuclear matter models.
- For the MPA1 EoS, the core-crust transition occurs at pressure p_t = 0.550 MeV/fm^3, energy density epsilon_t = 92.5 MeV/fm^3, and baryon density n_cc = 0.096 fm^-3.
- For the AP4 EoS, the transition values are p_t = 1.040 MeV/fm^3, epsilon_t = 115.0 MeV/fm^3, and n_cc = 0.122 fm^-3.
- For the MS1 EoS, the transition values are p_t = 0.455 MeV/fm^3, epsilon_t = 82.0 MeV/fm^3, and n_cc = 0.087 fm^-3.
- For the SINPA EoS, the transition values are p_t = 0.429 MeV/fm^3, epsilon_t = 72.0 MeV/fm^3, and n_cc = 0.084 fm^-3.
- The crust mass reaches a maximum of about 0.08 solar masses for a 1.0 solar mass neutron star, with much lower values for more massive stars.
- For the SINPA EoS with pasta phase, the radius difference between exact TOV solutions and relativistic thin-crust approximations (using BBP fit without pasta) remains below 400 m.
- The NICER measurements give PSR J0030+0451 with M = 1.34 (+0.15/-0.16) solar masses and R = 12.71 (+1.14/-1.19) km, and PSR J0740+6620 with M = 2.072 (+0.067/-0.066) solar masses and R = 12.39 (+1.30/-0.98) km.

## Context

This work extends the thin-crust approximation framework of Zdunik (2016) by testing it against four modern equations of state including a unified EoS with pasta phase (SINPA), and by quantifying the intrinsic uncertainty in crust modeling. The authors show that the approximations work well because the crust contains only a few percent of the total mass, consistent with earlier work by Lattimer and Prakash (2007) and Zdunik (2016). The results enable future high-precision radius measurements (targeting 100 m accuracy) to distinguish between different subnuclear matter models, and demonstrate that modifications to gravity (anisotropic pressure, f(R,L_m,T) gravity, dark matter admixture) introduce degeneracies in the mass-radius relation that are difficult to disentangle from crust effects.
