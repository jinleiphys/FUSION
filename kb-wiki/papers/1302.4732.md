# Generic conditions for stable hybrid stars

> We study the mass-radius curve of hybrid stars, assuming a single first-order phase transition between nuclear and quark matter, with a sharp interface between the quark matter core and nuclear matter mantle. We use a generic parameterization of the quark matter equation of state, which has a constant, i.e. density-independent, speed of sound ("CSS"). We argue that this parameterization provides a framework for comparison and empirical testing of models of quark matter. We obtain the phase diagram of possible forms of the hybrid star mass-radius relation, where the control parameters are the transition pressure, energy density discontinuity, and the quark matter speed of sound. We find that this diagram is sensitive to the quark matter parameters but fairly insensitive to details of the nuclear matter equation of state. We calculate the maximum hybrid star mass as a function of the parameters of the quark matter EoS, and find that there are reasonable values of those parameters that give rise to hybrid stars with mass above $2\,M_\odot$.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a generic parameterization of quark matter with a constant speed of sound (CSS) provides a framework for comparing and empirically testing models of quark matter, and that there exist reasonable parameter values yielding hybrid stars with mass above 2 solar masses, though the observability of hybrid branches depends strongly on the specific parameter values and is not guaranteed by the simple stability criterion alone.

## Method

The authors model hybrid stars assuming a single first-order phase transition between nuclear and quark matter with a sharp interface (Maxwell construction), using a CSS parameterization of the quark matter equation of state characterized by three parameters: the transition pressure, the energy density discontinuity at the transition, and the constant speed of sound in quark matter. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for two representative nuclear matter equations of state (NL3, a stiff model, and HLPS, a softer model) and map out the phase diagram of possible mass-radius relation topologies as a function of the quark matter parameters.

## Key numbers

- The maximum mass of pure nuclear matter stars is 2.77 solar masses for NL3 and 2.15 solar masses for HLPS
- The radius at 1.4 solar masses is 14.92 km for NL3 and 10.88 km for HLPS
- The symmetry energy derivative parameter L is 118 MeV for NL3 and 33 MeV for HLPS
- The critical energy density discontinuity for a connected hybrid branch is given by destab/etrans = 1/2 + (3/2)(ptrans/etrans)
- For CSS quark matter with c^2 = 1/3, the vertical phase boundary occurs at ptrans/etrans approximately 0.15, where the nuclear speed of sound squared is about 0.5 for both HLPS and NL3
- For CSS quark matter with c^2 = 1, the vertical phase boundary occurs at ptrans/etrans approximately 0.3, where the nuclear speed of sound squared is about 0.75 for HLPS and 0.65 for NL3
- The HLPS equation of state becomes acausal at n = 5.458 times nuclear saturation density (n0 = 0.16 fm^-3)
- The perturbative quark matter equation of state has roughly density-independent c^2 with a value around 0.2 to 0.3
- In the quartic polynomial parameterization with a2 between +/- (150 MeV)^2 and a4 between 0.6 and 1, c^2 is always between 0.3 and 0.36

## Context

This work extends the classic stability criterion of Seidov (1971) and the treatments by Haensel (1983) and Lindblom (1998) for hybrid stars with a sharp interface, providing a complete phase diagram of possible mass-radius topologies. The CSS parameterization generalizes the approach of Zdunik (2012), which considered two first-order transitions, and Chamel (2012), which set the speed of sound to 1. The results enable empirical constraints on quark matter parameters from future neutron star mass and radius measurements, and show that the simple stability criterion is not a reliable guide to the presence of observable hybrid branches, as the connected branch may be too small to detect even well below the critical energy density discontinuity.
