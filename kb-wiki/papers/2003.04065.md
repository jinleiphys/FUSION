# Effective field theory for triaxially deformed odd-mass nuclei

> The effective field theory for collective rotations of triaxially deformed nuclei is generalized to odd-mass nuclei by including the angular momentum of the valence nucleon as an additional degree of freedom. The Hamiltonian is constructed up to next-to-leading order within the effective field theory formalism. The applicability of this Hamiltonian is examined by describing the wobbling bands observed in the lutetium isotopes $^{161,163,165,167}$Lu. It is found that by taking into account the next-to-leading order corrections, quartic in the rotor angular momentum, the wobbling energies $E_{\textrm{wob}}$ and spin-rotational frequency relations $ω(I)$ are better described than with the leading order Hamiltonian.

## Key claim

The effective field theory (EFT) for collective rotations of triaxially deformed nuclei is generalized to odd-mass nuclei by including the valence nucleon angular momentum as an additional degree of freedom, and the Hamiltonian is constructed up to next-to-leading order (NLO). The paper claims that including NLO corrections (quartic in the rotor angular momentum) improves the description of wobbling energies and spin-rotational frequency relations compared to the leading-order (LO) Hamiltonian, but the improvement is conditional on the fitting strategy used for the low-energy constants.

## Method

The authors construct an effective Lagrangian for triaxially deformed odd-mass nuclei using Nambu-Goldstone fields on the coset space SO(3)/D₂, with the valence nucleon angular momentum components j_k coupled to the angular velocity components a_t^k via a Coriolis-type term. The Hamiltonian is derived up to NLO (including terms quartic in the rotor angular momentum components R_k = I_k - j_k) and combined with a single-j shell Nilsson Hamiltonian for the valence nucleon. The total Hamiltonian is diagonalized in a weak-coupling basis, and the parameters (moments of inertia J_k and higher-order coefficients M_k) are fitted to experimental wobbling energies and spin-rotational frequency relations for the lutetium isotopes ¹⁶¹,¹⁶³,¹⁶⁵,¹⁶⁷Lu.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁶³Lu, the LO fit (I) gives J₁ = 65.48, J₂ = 50.51, J₃ = 4.09 (units: ħ²/MeV); the NLO fit (I) gives J₁ = 60.56, J₂ = 44.45, J₃ = 3.85 (ħ²/MeV) and M₁ = -4.93, M₂ = 19.77, M₃ = -0.19, M₄ = -19.97, M₅ = 9.94, M₆ = 19.99 (units: ħ⁴/MeV³).
- For ¹⁶¹Lu, the LO fit gives J₁ = 61.89, J₂ = 49.53, J₃ = 4.23 (ħ²/MeV); the NLO fit gives J₁ = 60.59, J₂ = 44.61, J₃ = 3.38 (ħ²/MeV) and M₁ = 9.99, M₂ = 9.99, M₃ = 0.05, M₄ = -9.99, M₅ = 3.03, M₆ = 9.99 (ħ⁴/MeV³).
- For ¹⁶⁵Lu, the LO fit gives J₁ = 63.11, J₂ = 55.52, J₃ = 7.05 (ħ²/MeV); the NLO fit gives J₁ = 60.90, J₂ = 50.04, J₃ = 5.66 (ħ²/MeV) and M₁ = 9.96, M₂ = 9.99, M₃ = 0.14, M₄ = -9.91, M₅ = 0.92, M₆ = 9.98 (ħ⁴/MeV³).
- For ¹⁶⁷Lu, the LO fit gives J₁ = 64.10, J₂ = 52.10, J₃ = 4.20 (ħ²/MeV); the NLO fit gives J₁ = 59.66, J₂ = 43.17, J₃ = 2.64 (ħ²/MeV) and M₁ = -10.00, M₂ = 10.00, M₃ = -0.38, M₄ = -9.99, M₅ = 8.92, M₆ = 9.99 (ħ⁴/MeV³).
- The deformation parameters used for all Lu isotopes are β₂ = 0.40 and γ₂ = 20° (taken from Odegaard (2001)).
- For ¹⁶³Lu, the NLO fit (II) underestimates the first wobbling band energy by about 0.1 MeV at low spin; the NLO fit (I) overpredicts the second wobbling band energy by about 0.3 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the EFT for triaxially deformed even-even nuclei developed by Chen, Kaiser, Meißner, and Meng (2017, 2018) to odd-mass nuclei, building on the EFT framework for axially deformed nuclei by Papenbrock and collaborators (2011-2016). The paper applies the formalism to transverse wobbling bands in ¹⁶¹,¹⁶³,¹⁶⁵,¹⁶⁷Lu, which were previously studied experimentally by Bringel (2005), Odegaard (2001), Jensen (2002), Schonwasser (2003), and Amro (2003). The NLO Hamiltonian goes beyond the standard particle-rotor model (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975) by including quartic terms in the rotor angular momentum. The work enables future generalization to include proton-neutron coupling for studying chiral doublet bands, as proposed by Frauendorf (1997).
