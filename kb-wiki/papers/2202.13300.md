# A topological realization of spin polarization through vortex formation in collisions of Bose-Einstein condensates

> The global spin polarization of hadrons in heavy ion collisions has been measured in STAR (the Solenoidal Tracker At Relativistic heavy ion collider) experiments, which opens up a new window in the study of the hottest, least viscous and most vortical fluid that has ever been produced in the laboratory. We present a different approach to spin polarization from conventional ones: a topological realization of spin polarization through quantum vortex formation in collisions of Bose-Einstein condensates (BEC). This approach is based on the observation that the vortex is a topological excitation in a superfluid in presence of local orbital angular momentum and is an analogue of spin degrees of freedom. The formation processes of vortices and vortex-antivortex pairs are investigated by solving the Gross-Pitaevskii Equation with a large-scale parallel algorithm on Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) to very high precision. In a rotating environment, the primary vortex with winding number one is stable against perturbation, which has a minimal energy and fixed orbital angular momentum (OAM), but the vortices with larger winding numbers are unstable and will decay into primary vortices through a redistribution of the energy and vorticity. The injection of OAM can also be realized in non-central collisions of self-interacting condensates, part of the OAM of the initial state will induce the formation of vortices through concentration of energy and vorticity density around topological defects. Different from a hydrodynamical description, the interference of the wave function plays an important role in the transport of energy and vorticity, reflecting the quantum nature of the vortex formation process. The study of the vortex formation may shed light on the nature of particle spin and spin-orbit couplings in strong interaction matter produced in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a topological mechanism for spin polarization via quantum vortex formation in Bose-Einstein condensate collisions, as an alternative to conventional hydrodynamic spin-vorticity coupling descriptions. The authors hedge that this approach is an analogue study: vortex formation in BECs may shed light on the nature of particle spin and spin-orbit couplings in strong-interaction matter produced in heavy-ion collisions, rather than directly modeling those collisions.

## Method

The authors solved the Gross-Pitaevskii Equation (GPE) numerically using a large-scale parallel algorithm on Graphics Processing Units (GPU) to simulate vortex dynamics in Bose-Einstein condensates. They studied stationary vortex solutions with different winding numbers by solving the radial profile equation via a shooting method, and then performed time-dependent simulations of single vortex evolution and non-central collisions of two BECs with finite orbital angular momentum.

## Key numbers

- The healing length is defined as \(\xi = \hbar/\sqrt{2m\lambda\rho}\), which sets the scale over which density perturbations relax to bulk values.
- For stationary vortices, the relative energy is \(\Delta E_s = (L\pi \rho_0/m) s^2 \ln(\gamma_s R/\xi)\) with \(\gamma_s = 1.464, 0.572, 0.358\) for winding numbers \(s=1,2,3\) respectively.
- The speed of sound in the BEC is \(c_s = \hbar/(\sqrt{2}m\xi)\).
- The ratio of healing length to interparticle distance is \(\xi/d = 1/(\sqrt{8\pi}(\rho a_s^3)^{1/6})\).

## Context

This work extends the study of spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions beyond conventional hydrodynamic and transport models based on spin-vorticity coupling (Becattini 2013, Csernai 2013, Florkowski 2017). The authors connect to the experimental measurements of global \(\Lambda\) hyperon polarization by STAR (2017, 2018) and \(\rho_{00}\) deviations for \(\phi\) mesons by STAR (2022). The topological approach using BEC vortex formation provides a quantum mechanical perspective that may enable future studies of relativistic superfluids relevant to early-stage gluon dynamics in heavy-ion collisions and pion condensation in dense nuclear matter.
