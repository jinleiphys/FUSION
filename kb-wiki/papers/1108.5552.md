# Fluctuating initial conditions and fluctuations in elliptic and triangular flow

> In heavy ion collisions, event-by-event fluctuations in participating nucleon positions can lead to triangular flow. With fluctuating initial conditions, flow coefficients will also fluctuate. In a hydrodynamic model, we study the fluctuations in elliptic and triangular flow, due to fluctuating initial conditions. Both elliptic and triangular flow fluctuates strongly, triangular flow more strongly than the elliptic flow. Strong fluctuations greatly reduce the sensitivity of elliptic and triangular flow to viscosity.

## Key claim

Event-by-event fluctuations in initial conditions cause both elliptic and triangular flow to fluctuate strongly, with triangular flow fluctuating more strongly than elliptic flow; these strong fluctuations greatly reduce the sensitivity of both flow coefficients to shear viscosity, undermining the possibility of constraining the viscosity-to-entropy ratio from triangular flow measurements.

## Method

The authors simulate Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s}_{NN}=2.76\) TeV using the Israel-Stewart second-order dissipative hydrodynamic code AZHYDRO-KOLKATA, assuming boost-invariant, baryon-free fluid evolution. Fluctuating initial energy density distributions are generated by superimposing Gaussian hot spots (width \(\sigma=1\) fm) at random positions corresponding to participant nucleons from a Glauber model, with the central density \(\varepsilon_0\) tuned to reproduce experimental charged particle yields in 30-40% peripheral collisions. For each event, the hydrodynamic equations are solved with constant \(\eta/s\) values of 0, 0.04, 0.08, 0.12, and 0.16, and the invariant \(\pi^-\) distribution at freeze-out (\(T_F=130\) MeV) is used to compute harmonic flow coefficients \(v_2\) and \(v_3\).

## Key numbers

- In b=8.9 fm Pb+Pb collisions, the spatial eccentricity distribution \(\epsilon_2\) peaks around \(\epsilon_2 \approx 0.2\), while the triangularity parameter \(\epsilon_3\) peaks around \(\epsilon_3 \approx 0.04\).
- Fluctuations in integrated elliptic flow \(v_2\) are \(\sim 20\%\); fluctuations in integrated triangular flow \(v_3\) are \(\sim 50\%\).
- In the \(p_T\) range 1-3 GeV, fluctuations in differential elliptic flow \(v_2(p_T)\) are \(\sim 15-20\%\), while fluctuations in differential triangular flow \(v_3(p_T)\) are \(\sim 70-80\%\).
- Within the fluctuations, differential elliptic flow does not distinguish between ideal fluid and fluid with \(\eta/s=0.08\); triangular flow does not distinguish between \(\eta/s\) varying from 0 to 0.16.
- Reducing the hot spot width from \(\sigma=1\) fm to \(\sigma=0.5\) fm increases the average triangular flow by a factor of \(\sim 2\) in the \(p_T\) range 1-3 GeV, but fluctuations remain largely unaltered.

## Context

This work extends the study of event-by-event fluctuations in initial conditions, previously shown to generate odd harmonic flow (Manly 2005, Mishra 2008, Alver 2010, Teaney 2010), by quantifying how those fluctuations propagate into the flow coefficients themselves in a viscous hydrodynamic model. The results contradict the expectation that triangular flow measurements will constrain \(\eta/s\) more accurately than elliptic flow (Alver 2010, Teaney 2010); instead, the large fluctuations render triangular flow even less sensitive to viscosity than elliptic flow. The findings suggest that if initial-state fluctuations are the sole source of triangular flow, accurate extraction of \(\eta/s\) from \(v_3\) will be difficult, and that other mechanisms such as jet quenching (Chaudhuri 2006) may need to contribute to explain the small experimental uncertainties reported by ALICE (2011).
