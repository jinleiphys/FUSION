# The decline rate of the production cross-section of superheavy nuclei with $Z=114-117$ at high excitation energies

> The production cross sections of superheavy nuclei with charge numbers $114-117$ are predicted in the $(5-9)n$-evaporation channels of the $^{48}$Ca-induced complete fusion reactions for future experiments. The estimates of synthesis capabilities are based on a uniform and consistent set of input nuclear data provided by the multidimensional macroscopic-microscopic approach. The contributions of various factors to the final production cross section are discussed. As shown, the specific interplay between survival and fusion probabilities unexpectedly leads to a relatively slow decline of the total cross-sections with increasing excitation energy. This effect is supported by a favorable arrangement of fission barriers protecting the compound nucleus against splitting concerning energetic thresholds for the emission of successive neutrons. In particular, the probabilities of the formation of superheavy nuclei in the $5n$-, $6n$-, and in some cases even in $7n$-evaporation channels are still promising. This may offer a new opportunity for the future synthesis of unknown neutron-deficient superheavy isotopes.

## Key claim

The production cross sections of superheavy nuclei with Z=114-117 in (5-9)n-evaporation channels decline relatively slowly with increasing excitation energy, contrary to expectations, because the specific interplay between survival and fusion probabilities, supported by a favorable arrangement of fission barriers relative to neutron separation energies, keeps the cross sections in the 5n-, 6n-, and in some cases 7n-channels still promising for future experiments.

## Method

The authors used the dinuclear system model to calculate capture cross sections and compound nucleus formation probabilities, combined with a multidimensional macroscopic-microscopic (MM) approach to compute nuclear properties (masses, fission barriers, neutron separation energies) for all relevant nuclei. The survival probabilities were calculated by modeling the competition between fission and neutron evaporation in the decay cascade, using the MM predictions for fission barrier heights and neutron separation energies. Excitation functions for (5-9)n-evaporation channels were then computed for the complete fusion reactions ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁴²,²⁴⁴Pu, ²⁴³Am, ²⁴⁸Cm, and ²⁴⁹Bk.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁴Pu, as excitation energy increases from 55 MeV to 102 MeV, the fusion probability P_CN increases by about 6 times while the survival probability W_xn drops by about 640 times, resulting in a cross section decrease of more than 2 orders of magnitude.
- For the reaction ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁹Bk, the fusion probability grows by about 13 times while the survival probability decreases by about 10⁴.
- Predicted maximum cross sections for unknown isotopes: ²⁹¹Ts in 6n-channel σ_max_6n ≈ 0.2 pb; ²⁹⁰Ts in 7n-channel σ_max_7n ≈ 0.1 pb; ²⁸⁶Mc in 5n-channel σ_max_5n ≈ 1 pb; ²⁸⁵Mc in 6n-channel σ_max_6n ≈ 0.2 pb; ²⁸⁴Mc in 7n-channel σ_max_7n ≈ 0.1 pb; ²⁸³Fl in 7n-channel σ_max_7n ≈ 0.1 pb.
- The cross section drops from about 1 pb at the 5n-channel to about (1-10) fb at the 9n-channel.
- For the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁴Pu reaction, the total cross section (sum over all xn-channels) reaches a maximum at E*_CN ≈ 35 MeV and then decreases relatively slowly over the range 35-110 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Hong et al., 2020) which successfully described production cross sections for Z=112-118 in (2-5)n-evaporation channels using the same MM mass table from Kowal and Jachimowicz (2017). The present paper is the first systematic prediction of production cross sections for superheavy nuclei in high-multiplicity (5-9)n-evaporation channels, addressing the open question of how rapidly the evaporation residue cross section decreases with increasing beam energy. The results suggest that with planned beam intensity increases (about 10 pμA on target) and the new Dubna gas-filled magnetic recoil separator (DGFS) setup, these extremely low cross sections will soon be measurable, potentially enabling synthesis of new neutron-deficient superheavy isotopes.
