# Constraining and applying a generic high-density equation of state

> We discuss the "constant speed of sound" (CSS) parametrization of the equation of state of high-density matter and its application to the field correlator method (FCM) model of quark matter. We show how observational constraints on the maximum mass and typical radius of neutron stars are expressed as constraints on the CSS parameters. We find that the observation of a $2\,M_{\odot}$ star already severely constrains the CSS parameters, and is particularly difficult to accommodate if the squared speed of sound in the high-density phase is assumed to be around $1/3$ or less. We show that the FCM equation of state can be accurately represented by the CSS parametrization, which assumes a sharp transition to a high-density phase with density-independent speed of sound. We display the mapping between the FCM and CSS parameters, and see that FCM only allows equations of state in a restricted subspace of the CSS parameters.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the "constant speed of sound" (CSS) parametrization can accurately represent the field correlator method (FCM) model of quark matter, but that the FCM model only occupies a restricted subspace of the full CSS parameter space; the observation of a 2 solar mass star already severely constrains the CSS parameters, and is particularly difficult to accommodate if the squared speed of sound in the high-density phase is assumed to be around 1/3 or less.

## Method

The authors used the CSS parametrization, which assumes a sharp first-order phase transition between nuclear matter and a high-density phase with a density-independent speed of sound, to map observational constraints on neutron star maximum mass and radius onto three dimensionless parameters: transition pressure relative to transition energy density, energy density discontinuity at the transition, and squared speed of sound in the high-density phase. They then mapped the FCM quark matter equation of state onto this CSS parameter space and compared the allowed regions with constraints from the observed 2 solar mass star, using both a soft (BHF with Argonne v18 potential and Urbana TBF) and a stiff (DBHF with Bonn-A potential) nuclear matter equation of state.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of purely hadronic star for BHF (soft) nuclear EoS: 2.03 solar masses, with radius 9.92 km for the heaviest star and 11.77 km for a 1.4 solar mass star
- Maximum mass of purely hadronic star for DBHF (stiff) nuclear EoS: 2.31 solar masses, with radius 11.26 km for the heaviest star and 13.41 km for a 1.4 solar mass star
- For squared speed of sound = 1/3 in the high-density phase, the minimum radius of the maximum-mass hybrid star is about 11.5 km for soft hadronic EoS and 11.7 km for stiff hadronic EoS, almost independent of transition pressure
- For squared speed of sound = 1/3, the minimum radius of a 1.4 solar mass hybrid star is 12.2 km for BHF (soft) hadronic EoS and about 12.5 km for DBHF (stiff) hadronic EoS
- If a 2.1 solar mass star were observed, the minimum radius for a 1.4 solar mass star would increase to about 12.7 km (BHF) or 13 km (DBHF)
- For squared speed of sound = 1, the smallest hybrid stars have radii as small as 9 km, occurring for disconnected branch stars with low transition pressure and large energy density discontinuity
- The critical energy density discontinuity below which a stable connected hybrid branch always exists is given by (Delta epsilon_crit)/(epsilon_trans) = 1/2 + (3/2)*(p_trans/epsilon_trans)
- Nuclear saturation density n0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}

## Context

This work extends the CSS parametrization approach introduced by Alford (2013) and applies it to the FCM model of quark matter, building on earlier work by Zdunik (2012) and Chamel (2012). The paper shows how the observation of a 2 solar mass star by Antoniadis (2013) constrains the parameter space, and finds that for squared speed of sound = 1/3 (typical of many quark matter models including perturbative QCD by Kurkela (2010) and NJL-type models), the allowed parameter space is severely restricted to either low-transition-pressure or high-transition-pressure regions. The results are consistent with the lower bound on radius of about 11 km found by Lattimer (2012) for maximally compact stars with squared speed of sound = 1/3, and with the minimum radius of about 13 km found by Bedaque (2014) for transition density of 2n0. The work enables future observational constraints from radius measurements to distinguish between different high-density equations of state and to test whether the speed of sound exceeds the conformal value of 1/3.
