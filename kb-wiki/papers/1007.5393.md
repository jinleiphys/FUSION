# Confinement Phase in Carbon-Nanotubes and the Extended Massive Schwinger Model

> Carbon nanotube with electric fluxes confined in one dimension is studied. We show that a Coulomb interaction \propto |x| leads to a confinement phase with many properties similar to QCD in 4D. Low-energy physics is described by the massive Schwinger model with multi-species fermions labeled by the band and valley indices. We propose two means to detect this state. One is through an optical measurement of the exciton spectrum, which has been calculated via the 't Hooft-Berknoff equation with the light-front field theory. We show that the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner relation is satisfied by a dark exciton. The second is the nonlinear transport which is related to Coleman's "half-asymptotic" state.

## Key claim

Carbon nanotubes with electric fluxes confined to one dimension can host a confinement phase analogous to QCD in 4D, where all excitations are neutral bound states (excitons) and no free charged particles exist. This claim depends on the realization of a one-dimensional Coulomb potential proportional to \(|x|\), which requires shielding of electric fluxes by, for example, metallic outer tubes or embedding in a superconductor.

## Method

The low-energy effective model for electrons in a nanotube is formulated as an extended massive Schwinger model with multi-species fermions labeled by band index \(n\), spin \(\sigma\), and valley index \(\alpha = \text{K}, \text{K}'\). The exciton spectrum is calculated using the light-front quantization scheme and the 't Hooft-Bergknoff equation, which is solved numerically via the basis-function method. Nonlinear transport properties are analyzed in a weak-coupling, nonrelativistic approximation using a one-dimensional Schrödinger equation with a trapping potential \(V(x) = \frac{e^2}{2}|x|e^{-|x|/\xi} + e_0 E x\).

## Key numbers

- Coupling strength: \(e^2/(\pi v_F) = 0.709/\varepsilon_r\), where \(\varepsilon_r\) is the dielectric constant.
- In the limit \(L \to 0\) (pure SU(\(N\)) massless Schwinger model), the lightest bright exciton mass is \(M_\eta = \sqrt{N e^2/\pi}\), giving \(M_\eta = 0.8944\) for metallic (\(\nu=0\), SU(4)) nanotubes and \(M_\eta = 0.6325\) for semiconducting (\(\nu=1\), SU(2)) nanotubes at \(e^2/\pi v_F = 0.2\).
- The lightest dark exciton mass vanishes at specific magnetic flux values: \(\bar{\phi} = 0, 1\) for metallic nanotubes and \(\bar{\phi} = 1/3, 2/3\) for semiconducting ones, with \(M \propto |\phi - \bar{\phi}|^\alpha\) where \(\alpha \simeq 0.5\).
- Critical electric field for the half-asymptotic state: \(E_{\text{cr}} \equiv e_0/2\varepsilon_r\).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical study of confinement in the Schwinger model (Schwinger, 1962) and its extensions (Coleman, 1976; Bergknoff, 1977; Harada, 1994, 1999) to a solid-state realization in carbon nanotubes. It contrasts with the conventional weak-coupling picture of metallic nanotubes with a \(1/r\) potential, where a continuum exists down to zero energy. The proposal builds on experimental evidence of confined electric fluxes in two-dimensional organic crystals (Yamaguchi, 2006) and enables experimental detection of the confinement phase through optical measurement of the exciton spectrum and nonlinear transport, including Coleman's "half-asymptotic" state.
