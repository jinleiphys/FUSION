# Wavelet Notes

> Wavelets are a useful basis for constructing solutions of the integral and differential equations of scattering theory. Wavelet bases efficiently represent functions with smooth structures on different scales, and the matrix representation of operators in a wavelet basis are well-approximated by sparse matrices. The basis functions are related to solutions of a linear renormalization group equation, and the basis functions have structure on all scales. Numerical methods based on this renormalization group equation are discussed. These methods lead to accurate and efficient numerical approximations to the scattering equations. These notes provide a detailed introduction to the subject that focuses on numerical methods. We plan to provide periodic updates to these notes.

## Key claim

Wavelet bases, particularly Daubechies orthogonal wavelets of compact support, provide accurate and efficient numerical approximations to the integral and differential equations of scattering theory because their matrix representations are well-approximated by sparse matrices and they efficiently represent functions with smooth structures on different scales; however, the paper presents these as pedagogical notes focusing on numerical methods rather than reporting new scattering results.

## Method

The notes develop wavelets as orthonormal basis functions generated from a single scaling function that satisfies a linear renormalization-group equation (the scaling equation), using combinations of translation and dilatation operators. The construction proceeds from Haar wavelets to general scaling functions, deriving the constraints on scaling coefficients from normalization, orthonormality, and moment conditions, and showing how these determine the Daubechies order-K wavelets. The scaling function is computed on dyadic points by solving eigenvalue equations derived from the scaling equation at integer arguments.

## Key numbers

- Haar scaling coefficients: \(h_0 = h_1 = 1/\sqrt{2}\) (unique solution for two non-zero coefficients)
- Daubechies K=2 scaling coefficients: \(h_0 = (1+\sqrt{3})/4\sqrt{2}\), \(h_1 = (3+\sqrt{3})/4\sqrt{2}\), \(h_2 = (3-\sqrt{3})/4\sqrt{2}\), \(h_3 = (1-\sqrt{3})/4\sqrt{2}\)
- Daubechies K=3 scaling coefficients: \(h_0 = (1+\sqrt{10}+\sqrt{5+2\sqrt{10}})/16\sqrt{2}\), \(h_1 = (5+\sqrt{10}+3\sqrt{5+2\sqrt{10}})/16\sqrt{2}\), \(h_2 = (10-2\sqrt{10}+2\sqrt{5+2\sqrt{10}})/16\sqrt{2}\), \(h_3 = (10-2\sqrt{10}-2\sqrt{5+2\sqrt{10}})/16\sqrt{2}\), \(h_4 = (5+\sqrt{10}-3\sqrt{5+2\sqrt{10}})/16\sqrt{2}\), \(h_5 = (1+\sqrt{10}-\sqrt{5+2\sqrt{10}})/16\sqrt{2}\)
- A scaling function with \(N\) non-zero scaling coefficients has support on \([0, N-1]\)
- The number of non-zero scaling coefficients must be even (2K for Daubechies order-K)

## Context

These notes provide a pedagogical introduction to wavelet methods for solving scattering equations, building on the foundational work of Daubechies (1988) on orthonormal bases of compactly supported wavelets and Strang (1989) on wavelets and dilation equations. The approach extends the authors' own work in Kessler, Payne, and Polyzou (2003) on scattering calculations with wavelets, and is intended to enable numerical solutions of integral and differential equations in scattering theory by exploiting the multiresolution analysis structure where approximation spaces \(V_m\) approach the full square-integrable function space as \(m \to -\infty\).
