# Microscopic folding model analysis of the radiative $(n,γ)$ reactions near the $Z=28$ shell-closure and the weak s-process

> The radiative thermal neutron capture cross sections over the range of thermal energies from 1 keV to 1 MeV are studied in statistical Hauser-Feshbach formalism. The optical model potential is constructed by folding the density dependent M3Y nucleon-nucleon interaction with radial matter densities of target nuclei obtained from relativistic-mean-field (RMF) theory. The standard nuclear reaction code TALYS1.8 is used for calculation of cross sections. The nuclei studied in the present work reside near the $Z=28$ proton shell closure and are of astrophysical interests taking part in p-, s-, and r-process of nucleosynthesis. The Maxwellian-averaged cross-section (MACS) values for energies important for astrophysical applications are presented.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a microscopic folding model using density-dependent M3Y interactions and relativistic-mean-field densities, implemented in TALYS1.8, can reproduce neutron capture cross sections near the Z=28 shell closure, though the model systematically underpredicts cross sections for some isotopes (e.g., 62,63Ni) and shows deviations from experimental Maxwellian-averaged cross sections (MACS) at 30 keV for several nuclei, indicating limitations of the statistical Hauser-Feshbach approach near closed shells.

## Method

The authors constructed a microscopic neutron optical potential by folding the density-dependent M3Y nucleon-nucleon interaction with radial matter densities from relativistic-mean-field (RMF) theory using the FSU Gold parameterization, with both real and imaginary parts renormalized by numerical factors. They then calculated radiative neutron capture cross sections from 1 keV to 1 MeV using the statistical Hauser-Feshbach formalism in TALYS1.8, incorporating gamma-ray strength functions from HFB+QRPA calculations and nuclear level densities from a combinatorial microscopic model. The theoretical cross sections were compared with available experimental data, and Maxwellian-averaged cross sections (MACS) were derived by folding with the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.

## Key numbers

- MACS at kT=30 keV for 56Fe: 19.0 mb (present), compared to experimental 11.7±0.5 mb and MOST2005 prediction 36.0 mb
- MACS at kT=30 keV for 58Ni: 42.9 mb (present), compared to experimental 38.7±1.5 mb and MOST2005 prediction 72.2 mb
- MACS at kT=30 keV for 63Ni: 32.6 mb (present), no experimental data available; MOST2005 prediction 42.1 mb
- MACS at kT=30 keV for 60Fe: 3.65 mb (present), compared to experimental 5.15±1.41 mb and MOST2005 prediction 6.8 mb
- MACS at kT=30 keV for 67Zn: 153 mb (present), compared to experimental 153±15 mb and MOST2005 prediction 174 mb
- MACS at kT=30 keV for 62Ni: 11.2 mb (present), compared to experimental 22.3±1.6 mb and MOST2005 prediction 21.2 mb
- MACS at kT=30 keV for 59Co: 33.3 mb (present), compared to experimental 39.6±2.7 mb and MOST2005 prediction 53.7 mb
- RMF charge radius for 56Fe: 3.6936 fm (present), compared to experimental 3.7377 fm
- RMF charge radius for 59Co: 3.7505 fm (present), compared to experimental 3.7875 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic folding model studies near the N=82 and N=50 shell closures (Dutta et al., 2015; Dutta et al., 2014) to the Z=28 proton shell-closure region, providing theoretical neutron capture cross sections for nuclei involved in the weak s-process in massive stars (M≥8 solar masses) in the mass region A=60-100. The results enable improved network calculations for s-process nucleosynthesis, particularly for branch-point nuclei like 63Ni and 60Fe, and allow discrimination between weak and main s-process contributions for elements such as copper, gallium, germanium, and arsenic. The paper also provides MACS values over a range of energies for nuclei without experimental data, supplementing measurements by Heil et al. (2008) and others.
