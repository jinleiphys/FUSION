# Soft QCD Dynamics of Elastic Scattering in Impact Parameter Representation

> The elastic hadronic amplitude is calculated using the nonperturbative light-cone dipole representation for gluon bremsstrahlung. The data for large mass diffraction demand a two-scale structure of light hadrons: the gluon clouds of the valence quarks with a size of 0.3fm and the hadronic size 1fm. The presence of the two scales unavoidably leads to a specific form for the total hadronic cross section which consists of a steeply rising \propto s^Δ (Δ=0.17 +/- 0.01) term related to gluon radiation, and a large constant term originating from soft interactions which does not induce any gluon emission. Our calculations reproduce well the total cross sections and elastic slopes \cite{k3p}. To further test the model, we analyze the elastic pp and ppbar differential cross sections and extract the partial amplitudes in the impact parameter representation. The Pomeron trajectory as a function of the impact parameter is only slightly above one for central collisions, but steeply grows towards the periphery. The model predicts correctly the shape and energy dependence of the partial amplitude at all impact parameters.

## Key claim

The total hadronic cross section is not a simple power law \(s^{\Delta}\) but instead consists of a steeply rising term \(\propto s^{\Delta}\) (with \(\Delta = 0.17 \pm 0.01\)) related to gluon radiation from small constituent quarks, plus a large energy-independent term from soft interactions of the valence-quark skeleton; the effective energy dependence observed in data is much weaker (\(\Delta_{eff} \approx \Delta/2\)) because the constant term dominates at current energies.

## Method

The authors use the nonperturbative light-cone dipole representation to calculate gluon bremsstrahlung in high-energy hadronic collisions, treating the proton as three valence quarks each surrounded by a small gluon cloud of size \(r_0 = 0.3\) fm. They sum the contributions from radiation of \(n\) gluons in the leading-log approximation, exponentiate the logarithms to obtain a power-law energy dependence, and unitarize the partial amplitudes using a quasi-eikonal model that incorporates diffractive corrections via the AGK cutting rules. The only free parameter, the energy-independent Born cross section \(\tilde\sigma_0\), is fixed by fitting the \(\bar pp\) total cross section at \(\sqrt{s} = 546\) GeV.

## Key numbers

* \(\Delta = 0.17 \pm 0.01\): the exponent for the energy-dependent term in the total cross section, derived from the mean QCD coupling \(\langle\alpha_s\rangle = 0.38\)–\(0.43\) at the constituent-quark scale.
* \(r_0 = 0.3\) fm: the mean transverse size of the gluon cloud surrounding each valence quark, fixed by data on large-mass soft diffractive dissociation.
* \(\tilde\sigma_0 = 39.7\) mb: the fitted value of the energy-independent Born cross section for \(pp\) and \(\bar pp\) scattering, obtained by normalizing to the \(\bar pp\) total cross section at \(\sqrt{s} = 546\) GeV.
* \(\Delta_{eff} \approx \Delta/2\): the effective slope of the energy dependence of the total cross section at current energies, arising because the constant term dominates.
* \(B_0 = 2\langle r_{ch}^2\rangle/3\): the slope of elastic scattering for the valence-quark skeleton, related to the mean charge radius squared of the proton.

## Context

This work extends the light-cone dipole approach of Kopeliovich, Schafer, and Tarasov (1998) and the nonperturbative QCD model of Kopeliovich, Potashnikova, and Povh (1999) to elastic scattering, providing a dynamical explanation for the two-scale structure of hadrons (small gluon clouds of 0.3 fm versus the hadronic size of 1 fm). It contradicts the simple soft-Pomeron picture of Donnachie and Landshoff (1992) which assumes a single power law \(s^{\Delta}\) for the entire cross section, and instead supports the two-component structure suggested earlier by Kharzeev and Levin (1993). The model enables future analyses of elastic differential cross sections in impact-parameter space, allowing extraction of the Pomeron trajectory as a function of impact parameter and testing unitarity saturation in central collisions.
