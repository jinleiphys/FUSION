# Overtones of Isoscalar Giant Resonances in medium-heavy and heavy nuclei

> A semi-microscopic approach based on both the continum-random-phase-approximation (CRPA) method and a phenomenological treatment of the spreading effect is extended and applied to describe the main properties (particle-hole strength distribution, energy-dependent transition density, partial direct-nucleon-decay branching ratios) of the isoscalar giant dipole, second monopole, and second quadrupole resonances. Abilities of the approach are checked by description of gross properties of the main-tone resonances. Calculation results obtained for the resonances in a few singly- and doubly-closed-shell nuclei are compared with available experimental data.

# Overtones of Isoscalar Giant Resonances in Medium-Heavy and Heavy Nuclei

## Key claim

The semi-microscopic approach based on the continuum-random-phase-approximation (CRPA) method with a phenomenological treatment of the spreading effect can describe the main properties (strength distribution, transition density, and partial direct-nucleon-decay branching ratios) of the isoscalar giant dipole resonance (ISGDR), second monopole resonance (ISGMR2), and second quadrupole resonance (ISGQR2) in medium-heavy and heavy nuclei, provided that the smearing procedure is modified with a cut-off radius equal to the nuclear radius to avoid nonphysical absorption of escaped nucleons.

## Method

The authors extended a CRPA-based semi-microscopic approach that uses a phenomenological nuclear mean field and the Landau-Migdal particle-hole interaction, bound by self-consistency conditions, to describe high-energy giant resonances. They employed second-order probing operators (e.g., \(V_{L=1}^{(2)}(r)=r^3-\eta_{L=1}r\) for the ISGDR) chosen to suppress excitation of the main-tone resonances, and implemented a smearing procedure with an energy- and radial-dependent imaginary potential to account for the spreading effect. The energy-averaged strength functions, transition densities, and direct-nucleon-escape amplitudes were calculated for five nuclei (\(^{58}\)Ni, \(^{90}\)Zr, \(^{116}\)Sn, \(^{144}\)Sm, \(^{208}\)Pb) and compared with available experimental data.

## Key numbers

* ISGMR peak energy in \(^{208}\)Pb: 13.6 MeV (calculated), 13.5\(\pm\)0.2 MeV (experimental from Itoh).
* ISGQR peak energy in \(^{208}\)Pb: 10.0 MeV (calculated), 11.0\(\pm\)0.2 MeV (experimental from Young1).
* HE-ISGDR peak energy in \(^{208}\)Pb: 22.9 MeV (calculated), 22.8\(\pm\)0.3 MeV (experimental from Clark).
* ISGMR2 peak energy in \(^{208}\)Pb: 32.1 MeV (calculated, no experimental comparison given).
* ISGQR2 peak energy in \(^{208}\)Pb: 30.5 MeV (calculated, no experimental comparison given).
* Total direct-proton-decay branching ratio for HE-ISGDR in \(^{58}\)Ni over 15-40 MeV: 35.0% (calculated with unit spectroscopic factors).
* Total direct-proton-decay branching ratio for ISGMR2 in \(^{58}\)Ni over 23-40 MeV: 39.7% (calculated with unit spectroscopic factors).
* Total direct-proton-decay branching ratio for ISGQR2 in \(^{58}\)Ni over 25-40 MeV: 40.6% (calculated with unit spectroscopic factors).
* Parameter \(\eta_{L=1}\) for the ISGDR probing operator in \(^{208}\)Pb: 51.07 fm\(^2\).
* Parameter \(\eta_{L=0}\) for the ISGMR2 probing operator in \(^{208}\)Pb: 75.25 fm\(^2\).
* Parameter \(\eta_{L=2}\) for the ISGQR2 probing operator in \(^{208}\)Pb: 52.54 fm\(^2\).

## Context

This work extends earlier CRPA-based studies by Gorelik, Safonov, and Urin (2001-2003) that described gross properties of the ISGDR but encountered difficulties in evaluating partial direct-nucleon-decay branching ratios due to the treatment of the spreading effect. It also builds on microscopic Hartree-Fock+RPA calculations using Skyrme interactions by Colo and Shlomo (various years) and on relativistic RPA approaches by Vretenar (various years). The results enable comparison with forthcoming experimental data from \((\alpha,\alpha'N)\) reactions planned by Fujiwara and with existing data from Hunyadi, Garg, and collaborators on direct nucleon decay of the ISGDR. The approach provides a framework for predicting partial decay branching ratios of high-energy overtones, which carry information on microscopic structure and coupling to the continuum.
