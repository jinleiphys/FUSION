# Strangeness in Stellar Matter

> A protoneutron star is formed immediately after the gravitational collapse of the core of a massive star. At birth, the hot and high density matter in such a star contains a large number of neutrinos trapped during collapse. Trapped neutrinos generally inhibit the presence of exotic matter -- hyperons, a kaon condensate, or quarks. However, as the neutrinos diffuse out in about 10-15 s, the threshold for the appearance of strangeness is reduced; hence, the composition and the structure of the star can change significantly. The effect of exotic, negatively-charged, strangeness-bearing components is always to soften the equation of state, and the possibility exists that the star collapses to a black hole at this time. This could explain why no neutron star has yet been seen in the remnant of supernova SN1987A, even though one certainly existed when neutrinos were detected on Feb. 23, 1987. With new generation neutrino detectors it is feasible to test different theoretical scenarios observationally.

# Strangeness in Stellar Matter

## Key claim

The presence of negatively-charged strangeness-bearing components (hyperons, kaon condensates, or quarks) in protoneutron stars reverses the usual effect of neutrino trapping on maximum mass: for nucleons-only matter, trapped neutrinos reduce the maximum mass, but when strangeness-bearing components are present, trapped neutrinos increase the maximum mass, so that a metastable protoneutron star with mass between the neutrino-trapped and neutrino-free maximum masses can collapse to a black hole on the deleptonization timescale of 10-15 seconds.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations for hydrostatic equilibrium using equations of state for dense matter that include nucleons only, nucleons plus hyperons, and nucleons plus a mixed phase with quarks, both with and without trapped neutrinos. They compute equilibrium compositions under beta equilibrium and charge neutrality constraints, using a mean field model for baryons and a bag model for quarks, and compare maximum masses for neutrino-free matter (Y_nu=0) and neutrino-trapped matter (Y_Le=0.4) across different bag pressures and compositions.

## Key numbers

- Observed neutron star masses have an upper limit of 1.44 M_sun from PSR1913+16 and a lower limit of 1.36 M_sun from PSR 2127+11C
- The deleptonization time scale is approximately 10-15 seconds, with a semi-analytical estimate tau_d ~ 44.3/f * (R/10 km)^2 s, where f=3-10 accounts for degeneracy and interaction effects
- For nucleons-only matter without hyperons and with bag pressure B=200 MeV fm^-3, the maximum mass is 1.493 M_sun without neutrinos and 1.632 M_sun with trapped neutrinos
- For matter with hyperons and B=200 MeV fm^-3, the maximum mass is 1.471 M_sun without neutrinos and 1.597 M_sun with trapped neutrinos
- The binding energy of neutron stars follows B.E. = (0.065 +/- 0.01)(M_B/M_sun)^2 M_sun, yielding a remnant gravitational mass range of (1.14-1.55) M_sun for SN1987A
- The maximum stable mass drops by as much as 0.2 M_sun when trapped neutrinos depart if negatively charged particles are present
- The Keplerian frequency for rapidly rotating stars is Omega_K ~ 7.7 x 10^3 (M_max/M_sun)^(1/2) (R_max/10 km)^(-3/2) s^(-1)
- The maximum moment of inertia is I_max = 0.6 x 10^45 (M_max/M_sun)(R_max/10 km)^2 / [1-0.295(M_max/M_sun)/(R_max/10 km)] g cm^2

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Thorsson, Prakash and Lattimer (1994) on kaon condensates in neutron stars and by Keil and Janka (1995) on hyperonic stars, showing that the reversal of maximum mass behavior with neutrino trapping is generic to all forms of negatively-charged strangeness-bearing matter. The paper provides a theoretical framework for interpreting the absence of a neutron star in the SN1987A remnant, suggesting that if the remnant mass lies between the neutrino-trapped and neutrino-free maximum masses, black hole formation on the deleptonization timescale would explain why no neutron star has been observed despite the detected neutrino signal. This scenario enables future observational tests with next-generation neutrino detectors and connects to cooling observations of pulsars such as Vela, Geminga, PSR 0656+14, and PSR 1055-52, whose surface temperatures may distinguish between standard and rapid cooling scenarios.
