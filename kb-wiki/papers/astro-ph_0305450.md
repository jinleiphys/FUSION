# Evolution and Nucleosynthesis of Massive Stars and Related Nuclear Uncertainties

> Properties of atomic nuclei important for the prediction of astrophysical reaction rates are reviewed. In the first part, a recent simulation of evolution and nucleosynthesis of stars between 15 and 25 solar masses is presented. This study is used to illustrate the required nuclear input as well as to give examples of the sensitivity to certain rates. The second part focusses on the prediction of nuclear rates in the statistical model (Hauser-Feshbach) and direct capture (DWBA). Some of the important ingredients are addressed. Discussed in more detail are approaches to predict level densities, parity distributions, and optical alpha+nucleus potentials.

# Evolution and Nucleosynthesis of Massive Stars and Related Nuclear Uncertainties

## Key claim

The paper reviews how nuclear reaction rate uncertainties, particularly in the $^{12}$C($\alpha$,$\gamma$)$^{16}$O reaction, the $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n)/($\alpha$,$\gamma$) branching, and neutron capture on $^{62}$Ni, limit the predictive accuracy of massive star evolution and nucleosynthesis models, despite the use of an unprecedented adaptive reaction network of 4,679 isotopes. The author notes that while the statistical model (Hauser-Feshbach) can describe the majority of astrophysical reactions, certain cases require accuracy of 10% or better, which current global predictions cannot always provide.

## Method

The author presents simulations of evolution and nucleosynthesis for stars between 15 and 25 solar masses using the KEPLER stellar evolution code with an adaptive reaction network of unprecedented size, containing rate information for 4,679 isotopes from hydrogen to astatine and more than 15,000 reactions. The theoretical reaction rate backbone uses the FRDM-based Hauser-Feshbach code NON-SMOKER, supplemented with experimental neutron capture rates along stability and specific experimental ($\alpha$,$\gamma$) rates. The second part of the paper details improvements to global nuclear input predictions, including a microscopic-macroscopic level density description based on Fermi-gas formalism with microscopic corrections from mass models, parity-dependent level densities derived from shell model Monte Carlo calculations, and simultaneous fitting of optical $\alpha$+nucleus potentials to multiple reactions in the A~140 mass region.

## Key numbers

- The adaptive network includes 4,679 isotopes from hydrogen to astatine and more than 15,000 reactions
- The $^{12}$C($\alpha$,$\gamma$)$^{16}$O S-factor at 300 keV is taken as 146 keV barn, which is 1.2 times the value from Buchmann (1996), with a required accuracy of 10% or better
- The $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n) rate uncertainty accommodates a factor of two uncertainty in many s-process products for $80 \leq A \leq 90$
- For the $^{62}$Ni(n,$\gamma$) reaction, the enhanced direct capture cross section at 30 keV includes a p-wave contribution that alters the energy dependence, with resonances contributing less than 15%
- The parity-dependent level density calculation for $^{64}$Fe shows 20% difference in the (n,$\gamma$) channel compared to the standard equal-parity assumption
- The simultaneous fit of $\alpha$+nucleus potentials for the A~140 mass region used parameters: $V_0 = 162$ MeV, $r_r = 1.27$ fm, $a_r = 0.48$ fm, $W_0 = 19$ MeV, $r_V = 1.57$ fm, $a_V = 0.6$ fm for the Brown-Rho shape, with $E_0 = 6.35$ MeV and $\Delta = 28.4$ MeV
- The energy-independent potential found to work across multiple reactions uses $V_0 = 162.3$ MeV, $r_r = 1.27$ fm, $a_r = 0.48$ fm, $W_0 = 25$ MeV, $r_V = 1.4$ fm, $a_V = 0.52$ fm

## Context

This work extends the massive star nucleosynthesis calculations of Weaver and Woosley (1993) and Rauscher et al. (1994) by using an adaptive network of unprecedented size and updated nuclear inputs. The paper addresses the leading nuclear uncertainties identified in prior studies, particularly the $^{12}$C($\alpha$,$\gamma$)$^{16}$O rate, which determines the C/O ratio after helium burning and affects both nucleosynthesis and stellar collapse, and the $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n) rate governing the weak s-process component up to A~90. The improved global level density description using microscopic corrections from mass models (FRDM) and the parity-dependent level density approach based on Alhassid et al. provide a framework for predicting rates far from stability where experimental data are lacking, while the optical $\alpha$+nucleus potential work in the A~140 region (fitting data for $^{143}$Nd, $^{147}$Sm, and $^{144}$Sm reactions) demonstrates that an energy-independent potential can describe multiple reactions but may require additional treatment for $\alpha$-unstable final nuclei.
