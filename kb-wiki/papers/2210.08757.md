# A Quantum Algorithm for the Linear Response of Nuclei

> We present a quantum algorithm to obtain the response of the atomic nucleus to a small external electromagnetic perturbation. The Hamiltonian of the system is presented by a harmonic oscillator, and the linear combination of unitaries (LCU) based method is utilized to simulate the Hamiltonian on the quantum computer. The output of the Hamiltonian simulation is utilized in calculating the dipole response with the SWAP test algorithm. The results of the response function computed using the quantum algorithm are compared with the experimental data and provide a good agreement. We show the results for $^{120}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb to corroborate with the experimental data in Sn and Pb region and also compare the results with those obtained using the conventional linear response theory.

## Key claim

The authors present a quantum algorithm that uses a harmonic oscillator Hamiltonian and a linear combination of unitaries (LCU) method to compute the giant dipole resonance (GDR) response of nuclei, but the method is currently limited to spherical nuclei because deformed systems require a larger basis size that would demand an unfeasible number of qubits on available hardware.

## Method

The authors first simulate the nuclear Hamiltonian on a quantum computer using the LCU method, representing the mean field with a harmonic oscillator potential in second-quantized form and mapping fermionic operators to qubit operators via the Jordan-Wigner transformation. They then prepare excited states using a time-dependent state preparation method based on the dipole excitation operator, and compute the overlap between ground and excited states using the SWAP test algorithm. The resulting probabilities are used to calculate the dipole response function, which is compared with classical linear response theory (LRT) based on random phase approximation (RPA) and with experimental data for 120Sn and 208Pb.

## Key numbers

- For 120Sn, the dipole-dipole strength parameter kappa is set to 0.5 for the quantum computation and 0.4 for the classical computation.
- For 208Pb, the dipole-dipole strength parameter kappa is set to 0.85 for the quantum computation and 0.4 for the classical computation.
- The harmonic oscillator energy parameter is given by hbar-omega = 41 A^(-1/3) MeV.
- The parameter Gamma (width) is chosen as 2.0 MeV to reproduce the experimental width.
- The QASM simulator results use 100 independent runs with 8000 shots per data point.
- The error in GDR peak energy (Delta E0) remains below 2 MeV for both nuclei.
- The basis size used for quantum computation is 4 harmonic oscillator states (N = 3 to 6), requiring 4 qubits to represent the quantum state.

## Context

This work extends prior quantum simulations of nuclear many-body problems (Dumitrescu 2018, Roggero 2019, Roggero 2020) to the specific case of giant dipole resonances, and compares results with classical linear response theory based on random phase approximation (RPA) as described in references such as Gallardo (1988) and Ring (1983). The approach enables future extension to deformed nuclei using Nilsson-like Hamiltonians, though this would require more qubits than currently available on NISQ-era quantum computers.
