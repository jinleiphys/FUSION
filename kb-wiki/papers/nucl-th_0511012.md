# Information theory of open fragmenting systems

> An information theory description of finite systems explicitly evolving in time is presented. We impose a MaxEnt variational principle on the Shannon entropy at a given time while the constraints are set at a former time. The resulting density matrix contains explicit time odd components in the form of collective flows. As a specific application we consider the dynamics of the expansion in connection with heavy ion experiments. Lattice gas and classical molecular dynamics simulations are shown.

## Key claim

The paper extends the Gibbs ensemble formalism to finite, unbound, transient systems by imposing a MaxEnt variational principle on the Shannon entropy at a given time while the constraints are set at a former time, which necessarily introduces time-odd components (collective flows) into the density matrix; the authors hedge that this description is exact only when the constraining observables form a closed Lie algebra, otherwise an infinite number of Lagrange multipliers would be needed.

## Method

The authors formulate a generalized statistical ensemble by maximizing the Shannon entropy at time \(t\) subject to constraints on observables measured at earlier times \(t_\ell\), using the Liouville equation for deterministic evolution. They apply this formalism to a non-interacting gas of particles or fragments with a known mean square radius at a freeze-out time \(t_0\), deriving a density matrix that includes a radial flow term proportional to \(\hat{\vec{r}}_n\) and an effective confining term. The predictions are tested against Lattice Gas Model calculations and classical molecular dynamics simulations of a Lennard-Jones system freely expanding in vacuum.

## Key numbers

- For the ideal gas expansion, the effective inverse temperature evolves as \(\beta_{eff}(t) = \beta + 2\lambda_0 (t - t_0)^2 / m\) and the Hubble-like flow factor is \(\alpha(t) = \nu_0(t) / \beta_{eff}(t) = 2\lambda_0 (t - t_0) / [\beta m + 2\lambda_0 (t - t_0)^2]\).
- The effective confining Lagrange multiplier becomes \(\lambda_{eff}(t) = \lambda_0 \beta m / [\beta m + 2\lambda_0 (t - t_0)^2]\).
- In the Lattice Gas Model, the critical point shifts to higher pressures for flow contributions up to \(\Delta p^2/p^2 \approx 67\%\) (corresponding to \(m\alpha \approx 0.6\)), but the effect on cluster size distributions is very small in this regime.
- When collective flow exceeds a threshold \(\Delta p^2/p^2 \approx 100\%\), the average volume shows an exponential increase and the system undergoes complete fragmentation.
- In molecular dynamics simulations of a 147-particle Lennard-Jones system, the self-similar expansion predicted by the ideal gas approximation is remarkably fulfilled after approximately 10 Lennard-Jones time units for all total energies tested.

## Context

This work extends the information-theory approach of Jaynes (1957) to time-dependent constraints, addressing the conceptual problem that standard Gibbs ensembles in a confining box (e.g., Bondorf et al., 1995) require an infinite amount of information to specify boundary conditions for finite unbound systems. The formalism provides a statistical foundation for the common practice of treating radial flow as a collective velocity superimposed on thermal motion in heavy-ion collisions, and it enables a consistent interpretation of freeze-out observables in terms of an earlier static equilibrium in a harmonic confining potential. The results contradict the assumption that flow does not affect fragment size distributions at fixed volume, showing instead that flow modifies the effective pressure and can drive complete fragmentation above a threshold.
