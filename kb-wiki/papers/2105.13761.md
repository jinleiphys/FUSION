# Detecting Chiral Magnetic Effect via Deep Learning

> The search of chiral magnetic effect (CME) in heavy-ion collisions has attracted long-term attentions. Multiple observables have been proposed but all suffer from obstacles due to large background contaminations. In this Letter, we construct an observable-independent CME-meter based on a deep convolutional neural network. After trained over data set generated by a multiphase transport model, the CME-meter shows high accuracy in recognizing the CME-featured charge separation from the final-state pion spectra. It also exhibits remarkable robustness to diverse conditions including different collision energies, centralities, and elliptic flow backgrounds. In a transfer learning manner, the CME-meter is validated in isobaric collision systems, showing good transferability among different colliding systems. Based on variational approaches, we utilize the DeepDream method to derive the most responsive CME-spectra that demonstrates the physical contents the machine learns.

# Detecting Chiral Magnetic Effect via Deep Learning

## Key claim
The authors construct a deep convolutional neural network ("CME-meter") that can recognize chiral magnetic effect (CME) induced charge separation from final-state charged pion spectra in heavy-ion collisions, with the caveat that the network is trained on simulated data from the AMPT model and its performance depends on the charge separation fraction used in training (5% or 10%).

## Method
The authors generate training data using the string melting AMPT model for Au+Au collisions at eight beam energies from 7.7 to 200 GeV, with centrality bins from 0-60%. They implement CME by switching the y-components of momenta for a fraction f of up/down quarks, creating "charge separation" (CS) events (f > 0%) and "no CS" events (f = 0%). A convolutional neural network with periodic boundary conditions in azimuthal angle is trained on 2D spectra of charged pions in transverse momentum (20 bins) and azimuthal angle (24 bins), with 100 events averaged per sample to reduce fluctuations. The network outputs a probability P₁ that an event belongs to the CS class.

## Key numbers
- Validation accuracy of 78.47% for the model trained with 0% + 5% CS fraction events
- Validation accuracy of 93.38% for the model trained with 0% + 10% CS fraction events
- Input spectra use 20 pT-bins and 24 φ-bins (480 pixels total per pion species)
- Training data includes Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 7.7, 11.5, 14.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV
- 50,000 events per collision condition, with 100 events averaged per sample
- The CME-meter achieves approximately 5σ significance for distinguishing CS from no-CS events, averaged over all v₂/N values
- The DeepDream method starts from a flat spectrum with P₁ = 0 and tunes to maximize P₁ toward 1

## Context
This work addresses the long-standing challenge of detecting the chiral magnetic effect in heavy-ion collisions, where conventional observables like the γ-correlator (Voloshin, 2004) suffer from large background contamination from elliptic flow (Bzdak, 2011; Schlichting, 2010). The authors compare their CME-meter against the γ-correlator and show it is more robust to v₂ backgrounds. The approach extends prior applications of deep learning in nuclear physics (Pang, 2018; Zhou, 2019) and is validated on isobaric collision systems (Ru+Ru and Zr+Zr at 200 GeV), which were proposed specifically for CME searches (Voloshin, 2010; Deng, 2016). The DeepDream visualization technique provides interpretability of what the network learns, revealing a dipole charge separation pattern in momentum space.
