# Is the unusual near-threshold potential behavior in elastic scattering of weakly bound nuclei a precision error?

> We present the first example of a rigorous uncertainty quantification on elastic Nucleus-Nucleus scattering at energies near the Coulomb barrier. Experimental data has been analyzed using an energy dependent effective optical model potential with physical constraints imposed. We confirm the compatibility of these uncertainties with the well known Coulomb threshold anomaly, explained in terms of a dispersive relation, and contrast our results with previous analyses that suggest otherwise.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, when rigorous uncertainty quantification and physically motivated constraints (specifically, requiring the imaginary potential to decrease at energies below the Coulomb barrier) are applied, the elastic scattering data for weakly-bound nuclei like \(^6\)Li and \(^6\)He are consistent with the standard Coulomb Threshold Anomaly (TA), contradicting earlier claims of a "Breakup Threshold Anomaly" (BTA) that had been reported for such systems.

## Method

The authors performed a global fit of an energy-dependent Woods-Saxon optical model potential (OMP) to elastic scattering data for \(^6\)Li + \(^{209}\)Bi and \(^6\)He + \(^{208}\)Pb at multiple energies near the Coulomb barrier. To avoid underestimating uncertainties from linear correlation assumptions, they used a Monte-Carlo resampling technique (parametric bootstrap) to propagate experimental errors, and imposed physical constraints (attractive potential, small imaginary strength below the barrier) via penalty functions in the \(\chi^2\) minimization.

## Key numbers

* For \(^6\)Li + \(^{209}\)Bi (227 data points), the \(\chi^2\) per degree of freedom was 1.38 for the physically constrained fit (small strengths), compared to 1.32 for the unconstrained fit.
* For \(^6\)He + \(^{208}\)Pb (246 data points), the \(\chi^2\) per degree of freedom was 0.80 for the constrained fit, compared to 0.79 for the unconstrained fit.
* The dispersion relation parameters for \(^6\)Li + \(^{209}\)Bi: \(V_0 = 6.5\) MeV, \(W_0 = 5.6\) MeV, \(E_1 = 24.0\) MeV, \(E_2 = 26.5\) MeV.
* The dispersion relation parameters for \(^6\)He + \(^{208}\)Pb: \(V_0 = 2.00\) MeV, \(W_0 = 1.05\) MeV, \(E_1 = 13.5\) MeV, \(E_2 = 19.0\) MeV.
* The sensitivity radius used for the \(^6\)Li + \(^{209}\)Bi analysis was \(r_s = 10.15\) fm; for \(^6\)He + \(^{208}\)Pb it was \(r_s = 10.6\) fm.

## Context

This work directly challenges the "Breakup Threshold Anomaly" (BTA) hypothesis proposed by Hussein (2006) and Canto (2015), which claimed that the imaginary potential for weakly-bound nuclei increases as energy decreases below the barrier due to breakup coupling. The authors contrast their rigorous uncertainty quantification with previous analyses (e.g., Yang (2017) for \(^6\)He + \(^{209}\)Bi) that reported \(\chi^2/{\rm d.o.f.} \ll 1\) and concluded the dispersion relation was inapplicable. By showing that physically constrained fits yield \(\chi^2/{\rm d.o.f.} \simeq 1\) and satisfy the dispersive relation, the paper enables future studies to apply similar Bayesian or penalty-based methods to other weakly-bound systems like \(^7\)Li or \(^9\)Be, and suggests that the universal threshold anomaly applies to all nuclei.
