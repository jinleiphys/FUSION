# Hans Bethe and the Global Energy Problems

## Key claim

Bethe argued that nuclear power is a *necessity* for the future global energy supply, not merely an option, but only if the nuclear industry is built around breeder reactors using a two-component system of thermal heavy-water reactors (fueled by uranium-233, uranium-238, and thorium) and a smaller number of fast breeder reactors, with the explicit condition that international agreements and restrictions on national sovereignty are accepted to prevent nuclear weapons proliferation.

## Method

The paper is a historical and policy review of Hans Bethe's published positions on global energy problems, drawn from Bethe's papers from 1976–1977 and later reports. It recounts Bethe's analysis of energy consumption projections, his evaluation of alternative energy sources (coal, fusion, solar), and his detailed proposal for a two-component nuclear fuel cycle involving thermal heavy-water reactors (specifically CANDU-type) and fast breeder reactors with thorium blankets, including his assessment of reactor safety based on the Rasmussen report, the APS Study Group on Light Water Reactor Safety, and the post-Chernobyl U.S. panel findings.

## Key numbers

- Bethe predicted oil and gas production would drop around the year 2000 and prices would jump up (qualitative prediction, no numerical price given in text).
- The Rasmussen report estimated the probability of a core meltdown accident with radioactivity release as 1/200,000 per 1000 megawatts electrical reactor year.
- The Rasmussen report estimated 300 fatalities from such an accident; the APS Study Group estimated about 10,000 fatalities from delayed cancers.
- Bethe cited that in the Three Mile Island accident, about one-millionth of the amount of iodine released at Chernobyl was released to the environment.
- Bethe expected fusion might contribute only a few percent of USA's power supply no earlier than 2020.
- Bethe stated that after Three Mile Island and Chernobyl, safety requirements resulted in three times increasing of nuclear power plant costs in the USA.

## Context

This paper extends Bethe's earlier work on nuclear reactor physics and energy policy from the 1970s (Bethe, 1976, 1977) and presents his specific proposal based on the Taylor and Feiverson (reference in text) two-component fuel cycle scheme. It contrasts Bethe's views with the Teller "Classical Super" hydrogen bomb design, where Bethe correctly predicted negative energy balance due to inverse Compton scattering. The paper enables future discussion of the viability of thorium-based nuclear fuel cycles and the political challenges of non-proliferation, while noting that Bethe's economic conclusions predate the Three Mile Island and Chernobyl accidents and require updated analysis.
