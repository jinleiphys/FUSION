# The impact of individual nuclear masses on $r$-process abundances

> We have performed for the first time a comprehensive study of the sensitivity of $r$-process nucleosynthesis to individual nuclear masses across the chart of nuclides. Using the latest version (2012) of the Finite-Range Droplet Model, we consider mass variations of $\pm0.5$ MeV and propagate each mass change to all affected quantities, including $Q$-values, reaction rates, and branching ratios. We find such mass variations can result in up to an order of magnitude local change in the final abundance pattern produced in an $r$-process simulation. We identify key nuclei whose masses have a substantial impact on abundance predictions for hot, cold, and neutron star merger $r$-process scenarios and could be measured at future radioactive beam facilities.

# The impact of individual nuclear masses on $r$-process abundances

## Key claim
Individual nuclear mass uncertainties of ±0.5 MeV can cause up to an order of magnitude local change in final $r$-process abundance patterns, but only when mass variations are consistently propagated to all affected nuclear properties (Q-values, reaction rates, branching ratios) rather than to photodissociation rates alone. The most impactful nuclei lie along the equilibrium $r$-process path and decay pathways back to stability, with the largest global effects occurring around closed neutron shells.

## Method
The authors performed sensitivity studies using four baseline astrophysical trajectories: a low entropy hot wind (entropy 30 kB, electron fraction Ye=0.20, timescale 70 ms), a high entropy hot wind (entropy 100 kB, Ye=0.25, timescale 80 ms), a cold neutrino-driven wind (Ye=0.31), and a neutron star merger trajectory. For each nuclear species from Z=30 to Z=80 and N=60 to N=130, they varied the mass by ±0.5 MeV using the Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM2012) and recalculated all affected neutron capture rates (via TALYS), photodissociation rates (via detailed balance), β-decay rates (phase space factors recalculated with QRPA strength functions unchanged), and β-delayed neutron emission probabilities (via QRPA-Hauser-Feshbach approach). The impact was quantified using the F metric, which sums the absolute differences in final isobaric mass fractions across all mass numbers.

## Key numbers
- Mass variations of ±0.5 MeV were applied, approximately equal to the root-mean-square error of FRDM2012 (0.57 MeV compared to AME2012)
- The highest impact was found for Cadmium-132 (Z=48, N=84) with Fmax = 101.39 in the high entropy hot wind trajectory
- Tin-136 (Z=50, N=86) showed Fmax = 83.68 in the high entropy hot wind
- Indium isotopes (Z=49) with N=84-89 showed Fmax values ranging from 66.43 to 74.59 in the high entropy hot wind
- Palladium-132 (Z=46, N=86) showed Fmax = 55.56 in the neutron star merger trajectory and Fmax = 48.67 in the cold wind
- A ±0.5 MeV mass variation resulted in neutron capture rate changes of approximately a factor of two to five
- The same mass variation led to half-life changes of roughly a factor of two to four
- Roughly 1100 nuclei were included in each study (30 ≤ Z ≤ 80, 60 ≤ N ≤ 130)
- Any nucleus with F ∼ 10 or above implies a significant global change to abundances
- Only Palladium, Cadmium, Indium, and Tin isotopes showed sensitivities above F = 20

## Context
This work extends earlier sensitivity studies that considered mass variations propagated only to photodissociation rates (Brett 2012) or were limited to closed-shell nuclei (Mumpower 2015a). The authors note that their new full-chart studies are the first reliable estimates of nuclear mass impact in cold wind and merger scenarios, where photodissociation is suppressed and masses influence the r-process via decay properties and neutron capture rates. The results show that current mass model uncertainties (∼0.5 MeV) are too large for precision abundance predictions capable of differentiating between r-process sites, and that reducing global model uncertainties to less than 0.1 MeV would be required to resolve finer abundance features such as the rare earth peak at A ∼ 160. The identified key nuclei are within predicted experimental reach of the upcoming Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB).
