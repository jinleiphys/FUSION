# Efimov Physics and Connections to Nuclear Physics

> Physical systems characterized by a shallow two-body bound or virtual state are governed at large distances by a continuous-scale invariance, which is broken to a discrete one when three or more particles come into play. This symmetry induces a universal behavior for different systems, independent of the details of the underlying interaction, rooted in the smallness of the ratio $\ell/a_B \ll 1$, where the length $a_B$ is associated to the binding energy of the two-body system $E_2=\hbar^2/m a_B^2$ and $\ell$ is the natural length given by the interaction range. Efimov physics refers to this universal behavior, which is often hidden by the on-set of system-specific non-universal effects. In this work we identify universal properties by providing an explicit link of physical systems to their unitary limit, in which $a_B\rightarrow\infty$, and show that nuclear systems belong to this class of universality.

# Efimov Physics and Connections to Nuclear Physics

## Key claim

The authors argue that nuclear systems (such as the deuteron and nucleon-nucleon scattering states) belong to the same class of universality as atomic systems like the helium dimer, governed by Efimov physics, provided one accounts for finite-range corrections through a Gaussian characterization that maps real systems onto a universal scaling curve.

## Method

The authors solve the Schrodinger equation for two- and three-body systems using the Hyperspherical Harmonic method and the stochastic variational method with correlated-Gaussian basis sets. They employ a two-parameter Gaussian potential as a minimal low-energy representation of the two-particle interaction, fixed by two low-energy data (the energy length a_B and the scattering length a), and vary the potential strength to explore trajectories through the unitary window. Real systems are placed on universal curves using their experimentally determined low-energy parameters.

- Singlet np scattering length: a_s = -23.74 fm, with effective range r_e = 2.77 fm
- Singlet nn scattering length: a_nn = -18.90 fm, with effective range r_e = 2.75 fm

- Helium trimer ground state energy: E_3^(0) = 126.4 mK; first excited state: E_3^(1) = 2.27 mK
- Universal energy ratio for three equal bosons: E_3^(n+1)/E_3^(n) = e^{-2pi/s_0} with s_0 = 1.006237, giving e^{-2pi/s_0} approximately (1/22.7)^2

- Critical pion mass for diverging scattering lengths: m_pi approximately 200 MeV

## Context

This work extends the universal description of Efimov physics, originally predicted by Efimov (1970, 1971) and observed by Kraemer et al. (2006) in cold atoms, to nuclear systems. It builds on the pionless-EFT approach developed by van Kolck (1999), Kaplan et al. (1998), and others, which exploits the separation of scales between the scattering length and the interaction range. The Gaussian characterization provides a bridge between zero-range models and realistic finite-range interactions, enabling predictions for systems such as the tetraneutron observed by Marques et al. (2002) and Kisamori et al. (2016), and for halo nuclei studied by Jensen et al. (2004) and Hammer et al. (2017). The approach enables systematic inclusion of range corrections beyond leading order in the EFT expansion.
