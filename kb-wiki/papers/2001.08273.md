# Gradient Tomography of Jet Quenching in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Transverse momentum broadening and energy loss of a propagating parton are dictated by the space-time profile of the jet transport coefficient $\hat q$ in a dense QCD medium. The spatial gradient of $\hat q$ perpendicular to the propagation direction can lead to a drift and asymmetry in parton transverse momentum distribution. Such an asymmetry depends on both the spatial position along the transverse gradient and path length of a propagating parton as shown by numerical solutions of the Boltzmann transport in the simplified form of a drift-diffusion equation. In high-energy heavy-ion collisions, this asymmetry with respect to a plane defined by the beam and trigger particle (photon, hadron or jet) with a given orientation relative to the event plane is shown to be closely related to the transverse position of the initial jet production in full event-by-event simulations within the linear Boltzmann transport model. Such a gradient tomography can be used to localize the initial jet production position for more detailed study of jet quenching and properties of the quark-gluon plasma along a given propagation path in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that the spatial gradient of the jet transport coefficient \(\hat q\) perpendicular to a parton's propagation direction creates a measurable transverse momentum asymmetry, and that this asymmetry can be used to localize the initial jet production position in the transverse plane of heavy-ion collisions, enabling a "gradient tomography" of the quark-gluon plasma.

## Method
The authors first derive a drift-diffusion equation from the Boltzmann transport equation under the assumption of small-angle scattering, and solve it analytically for a static, uniform medium. They then perform full event-by-event simulations using the linear Boltzmann transport (LBT) model coupled with the CLVisc (3+1)D viscous hydrodynamic model for \(\gamma\)-jet events in 0-10% central Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=2.76\) TeV, using PYTHIA 8 for initial parton showers and AMPT model initial conditions for the hydrodynamic evolution.

## Key numbers
- The jet transport coefficient at the center of the medium is set to \(\hat q_0 = 5\) GeV\(^2\)/fm at initial time \(t_0 = 0.5\) fm/\(c\) for the simplified drift-diffusion study.
- The analytic solution gives diffusion widths: \(\sqrt{\langle k_\perp^2\rangle} = \sqrt{\hat q_a t}\) and \(\sqrt{\langle r_\perp^2\rangle} = t\sqrt{(\hat q_a t/3)}/\omega\) for a parton of energy \(\omega\).
- The LBT simulation uses \(\gamma\)-jet events with trigger photon transverse momentum \(p_T^\gamma = 60-80\) GeV/\(c\) and jet transverse momentum \(p_T^{\rm jet} = 80-90\) GeV/\(c\).
- The initial time for hydrodynamic evolution is \(\tau_0 = 0.2\) fm/\(c\) and the freeze-out temperature is \(T_{\rm f} = 137\) MeV.
- A cut of \(p_T > 3\) GeV/\(c\) is imposed on final partons from LBT to focus on jet shower partons.
- The simulation averages over 200 hydro events and \(10^5\) \(\gamma\)-jet events per hydro event.

## Context
This work extends the extraction of the jet transport coefficient \(\hat q\) from single inclusive hadron suppression data by the JET Collaboration (Burke et al., 2014) and similar efforts (Andres et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2018; Feal et al., 2019), which assumed a Glauber model spatial distribution for initial jet production. It also builds on studies of azimuthal anisotropy of jet quenching (Wang, 2000; Gyulassy et al., 2000) and earlier attempts to select surface, tangential, or volume emission using single hadron, dihadron, and \(\gamma\)-hadron measurements (Renk, 2006; Zhang et al., 2007, 2009). The proposed gradient tomography enables localization of the initial jet production position, allowing more detailed study of jet quenching along a given propagation path and potentially enabling measurements of \(\hat q\) for specific paths. The method could also be applied to jet production at a future electron-ion collider for studying gluon saturation and jet transport in cold nuclei.
