# The interplay of the sign problem and the infinite volume limit: gauge theories with a theta term

> QCD and related gauge theories have a sign problem when a $θ$-term is included; this complicates the extraction of physical information from Euclidean space calculations as one would do in lattice studies. The sign problem arises in this system because the partition function for configurations with fixed topological charge $Q$, $\mathcal{Z}_Q$, are summed weighted by $\exp(i Q θ)$ to obtain the partition function for fixed $θ$, $ \mathcal{Z}(θ)$. The sign problem gets exponentially worse numerically as the space-time volume is increased. Here it is shown that apart from the practical numerical issues associated with large volumes, there are some interesting issues of principle. A key quantity is the energy density as a function of $θ$, $\varepsilon(θ) = -\log \left( \mathcal{Z}(θ) \right )/V$. This is expected to be well defined in the large 4-volume limit. Similarly, one expects the energy density for a fixed topological density $\tilde{\varepsilon}(Q/V) = -\log \left(\mathcal{Z}_Q \right )/V$ to be well defined in the limit of large 4-volumes. Intuitively, one might expect that if one had the infinite volume expression for $\tilde{\varepsilon}(Q/V)$ to arbitrary accuracy, that one could reconstruct $\varepsilon(θ)$ by directly summing over the topological sectors of the partition function. We show here that there are circumstances where this is not the case. In particular, this occurs in regions where the curvature of $\varepsilon(θ)$ is negative.

## Key claim

The paper shows that even with perfect knowledge of the infinite-volume energy density at fixed topological charge, \(\tilde{\varepsilon}(q)\), one cannot always reconstruct the \(\theta\)-dependent energy density \(\varepsilon(\theta)\) by directly summing over topological sectors; this failure occurs specifically in regions where the curvature \(\varepsilon''(\theta)\) is negative, revealing a theoretical limitation beyond the practical numerical sign problem.

## Method

The authors compare two definitions of the \(\theta\)-dependent energy density: \(\varepsilon(\theta)\), where the infinite-volume limit is taken simultaneously in the sum over topological charges, and \(\underline{\varepsilon}(\theta)\), where the infinite-volume limit of \(\tilde{\varepsilon}(q,V)\) is taken first and then the sum over charges is performed. They analyze a dilute instanton gas toy model analytically using Bessel function asymptotics and numerically evaluate the sum over topological sectors at large but finite volume. They then generalize the argument using a saddle-point approximation and an identity relating sums to integrals to show that the reconstruction fails when \(\varepsilon''(\theta) \le 0\).

## Key numbers

* For the dilute instanton gas model, \(\varepsilon(\theta) = \varepsilon_0 + \chi_0 (1 - \cos\theta)\) with \(\chi_0 \equiv c e^{-S_0}\); the infinite-volume fixed-charge energy density is \(\tilde{\varepsilon}(q) = \varepsilon_0 + \chi_0 + q \sinh^{-1}(q/\chi_0) - \sqrt{\chi_0^2 + q^2}\).
* The numerical evaluation of \(\underline{\varepsilon}(\theta)\) used a volume such that \(V \chi_0 = 350\); results were stable except near \(\theta = \pi/2\) by \(V \chi_0 = 30\).
* The transition between agreement and disagreement of \(\varepsilon(\theta)\) and \(\underline{\varepsilon}(\theta)\) occurs at \(\theta = \pi/2\), which is the inflection point of \(\varepsilon(\theta)\) in the toy model.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of the sign problem in gauge theories with a \(\theta\)-term, which is known to make lattice calculations exponentially costly as volume increases (Witten 1980, Brower et al.). The paper shows that even in principle, the sign problem can prevent reconstruction of \(\varepsilon(\theta)\) from \(\tilde{\varepsilon}(q)\) when the curvature is negative, a condition relevant to determining whether CP is spontaneously broken at \(\theta = \pi\) (Dashen's phenomenon). This theoretical limitation complements practical approaches such as analytic continuation from imaginary \(\theta\) (Elia et al., Alles et al.) and suggests that finite-volume effects in \(\tilde{\varepsilon}(q,V)\) must be understood to resolve the issue.
