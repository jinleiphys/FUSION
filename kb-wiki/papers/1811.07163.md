# Nuclear electromagnetic dipole response with the Self-Consistent Green's Function formalism

> Microscopic calculations of the electromagnetic response of medium-mass nuclei are now feasible thanks to the availability of realistic nuclear interactions with accurate saturation and spectroscopic properties, and the development of large-scale computing methods for many-body physics. The purpose is to compute isovector dipole electromagnetic (E1) response and related quantities, i.e. integrated dipole cross section and polarizability, and compare with data from photoabsorption and Coulomb excitation experiments. The single-particle propagator is obtained by solving the Dyson equation, where the self-energy includes correlations non-perturbatively through the Algebraic Diagrammatic Construction (ADC) method. The particle-hole ($ph$) polarization propagator is treated in the Dressed Random Phase Approximation (DRPA), based on an effective correlated propagator that includes some $2p2h$ effects but keeps the same computation scaling as the standard Hartree-Fock propagator. The E1 responses for $^{14,16,22,24}$O, $^{36,40,48,52,54,70}$Ca and $^{68}$Ni have been computed: the presence of a soft dipole mode of excitation for neutron-rich nuclei is found, and there is a fair reproduction of the low-energy part of the experimental excitation spectrum. This is reflected in a good agreement with the empirical dipole polarizability values. For a realistic interaction with an accurate reproduction of masses and radii up to medium-mass nuclei, the Self-Consistent Green's Function method provides a good description of the E1 response, especially in the part of the excitation spectrum below the Giant Dipole Resonance. The dipole polarizability is largely independent from the strategy of mapping the dressed propagator to a simplified one that is computationally manageable

## Key claim

The Self-Consistent Green's Function method, using the Dressed Random Phase Approximation with an effective correlated propagator, provides a good description of the isovector dipole response for medium-mass nuclei, particularly below the Giant Dipole Resonance, but the fragmentation introduced by the dressed propagator without full restoration of dynamical self-consistency spoils predictions of the energy centroid of the Giant Dipole Resonance at high energies.

## Method

The single-particle propagator was obtained by solving the Dyson equation with the self-energy expanded non-perturbatively through the Algebraic Diagrammatic Construction method at third order (ADC(3)), including both two-nucleon and three-nucleon chiral interactions (NNLOsat). The particle-hole polarization propagator was treated in the Dressed Random Phase Approximation (DRPA), where the free polarization propagator was built from an effective correlated propagator (Optimized Reference State propagator) that includes some 2p2h effects while keeping the same computational scaling as the standard Hartree-Fock propagator. The E1 response was computed for closed-subshell nuclei from Oxygen-14 to Nickel-68 by diagonalizing the RPA matrices constructed from the quasihole and quasiparticle states of the reduced propagator.

## Key numbers

* Dipole polarizability for Oxygen-16: 0.4996 fm^3 (for Nmax=13, hbar-omega=20 MeV), with relative differences of approximately 1.5% between different oscillator frequencies and approximately 1% between Nmax=11 and Nmax=13
* The Giant Dipole Resonance peak for Oxygen isotopes lies within the range approximately 19-24 MeV
* The Lorentzian width used to fold the discrete response was consistently set to Gamma = 3 MeV, which reproduces the experimental width of the GDR in Oxygen-16
* The model space for the NNLOsat interaction included all matrix elements with N1+N2+N3 less than or equal to 16, where Ni = 2ni + li is the major oscillator quantum number

## Context

This work extends previous Self-Consistent Green's Function calculations of the E1 response and dipole polarizability (Raimondi and Barbieri, 2017) to a wider range of medium-mass nuclei using the NNLOsat chiral interaction (Ekstrom, 2015), which accurately reproduces radii up to the Calcium isotopes. The approach builds on the Coupled Cluster with Lorentz Integral Transform method (PhysRevC, 2017, 2018) that computed E1 responses from Helium-4 to Calcium-48, and relates to phenomenological models such as the Quasiparticle-Phonon Model (Bertulani, 1999) and the Extended Theory of Finite Fermi Systems (Kamerdzhiev, 2004). The results enable future work to improve the interaction kernel beyond first order to include bubble exchange diagrams and restore dynamical self-consistency according to the Baym-Kadanoff approach.
