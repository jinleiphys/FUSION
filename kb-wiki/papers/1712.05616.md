# Analysis of the total kinetic energy of fission fragments with the Langevin equation

> We analyzed the total kinetic energy (TKE) of fission fragments with three-dimensional Langevin calculations for a series of actinides and Fm isotopes at various excitation energies. This allowed us to establish systematic trends of TKE with $Z^2/A^{1/3}$ of the fissioning system and as a function of excitation energy. In the mass-energy distributions of fission fragments we see the contributions from the standard, super-long, and super-short (in the case of $^{258}$Fm) fission modes. For the fission fragments mass distribution of $^{258}$Fm we obtained a single peak mass distribution. The decomposition of TKE into the prescission kinetic energy and Coulomb repulsion showed that decrease of TKE with growing excitation energy is accompanied by a decrease of prescission kinetic energy. It was also found that transport coefficients (friction and inertia tensors) calculated by a microscopic model and by macroscopic models give drastically different behavior of TKE as a function of excitation energy. The results obtained with microscopic transport coefficients are much closer to experimental data than those calculated with macroscopic ones.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that three-dimensional Langevin calculations using microscopic transport coefficients (derived from linear response theory) reproduce experimental total kinetic energy (TKE) trends for fission fragments significantly better than calculations using macroscopic transport coefficients (Werner-Wheeler inertia and wall-and-window friction), especially regarding the dependence of TKE on excitation energy.

## Method

The authors performed three-dimensional Langevin calculations for a series of actinides and Fm isotopes using three collective coordinates: elongation (z₀/R₀), fragment deformation (δ), and mass asymmetry (α). The potential energy surface was calculated using the macroscopic-microscopic method with the two-center shell model, while the mass and friction tensors were computed either microscopically via linear response theory with pairing or macroscopically using the Werner-Wheeler method and wall-and-window formula. The TKE was decomposed into prescission kinetic energy and Coulomb repulsion contributions to understand its variation with excitation energy.

## Key numbers

- The zero-point energy for the effective temperature T* was set to ℏω = 2 MeV, giving T* = 1 MeV at T = 0
- The neck parameter ε was fixed at 0.35, as recommended by Yamaji (1988)
- The nuclear radius constant r₀ = 1.2 fm was used throughout
- The shape-independent reduction factor k_s = 0.27 was applied to the macroscopic wall-and-window friction
- The level density parameter was defined as a = A [1.0 + 3.114 A⁻¹/³ + 5.626 A⁻²/³] / 14.61

## Context

This work extends the three-dimensional Langevin approach of Usang (2016) by applying microscopic transport coefficients from Hofmann (1997) and Ivanyuk (2000) to a wider range of fissioning systems including ²³⁸U, ²⁴⁰Pu, ²³²Pa, and ²⁵⁸Fm. The results contradict the common use of macroscopic transport coefficients (Werner-Wheeler and wall-and-window formulas from Nix and Sierk) for low-energy fission, showing that microscopic coefficients are essential for correct TKE behavior. This enables more reliable predictions of TKE for gaps in experimental data, which is important for calculating prompt fission neutron multiplicities as discussed by Capote (2016).
