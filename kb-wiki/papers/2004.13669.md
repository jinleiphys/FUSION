# Program to calculate coefficients of transformations between three-particle hyperspherical harmonics

> A program to calculate the three-particle hyperspherical brackets is presented. Test results are listed and it is seen that the program is well applicable up to very high values of the hypermomentum and orbital momenta. The listed runs show that it is also very fast. Applications of the brackets to calculating interaction matrix elements and constructing hyperspherical bases for identical particles are described. Comparisons are done with the programs published previously.

## Key claim

The program calculates hyperspherical brackets (Raynal-Revai coefficients) for three-particle systems and is applicable up to very high hypermomentum values (tested up to K=200) and orbital momenta (tested up to L=31), with the caveat that accuracy is mostly determined by the precision of the 3j-symbol calculation.

## Method

The program calculates hyperspherical brackets using an explicit expression with few summations for the minimum possible hypermomentum at given Jacobi orbital momenta, then employs a K→K+2 recursion relation to compute brackets at larger hypermomenta. The implementation uses double precision Fortran-90, with factorials and double factorials computed as exp(ln A) to avoid overflow from large numbers, and 3j symbols calculated via a sum of exponentials of logarithms of factorials.

## Key numbers

- At K=200, L=30, the largest deviation from unity in orthogonality relations was 3×10⁻⁹
- At K=30, L=5, the largest deviation from unity in orthogonality relations was 9×10⁻¹⁴
- At K=200, L=0, the ⟨00|00⟩ bracket coincided with the analytic expression sin(K+2)φ/sin 2φ multiplied by 2/(K+2) to 14 digits
- Total computation time for all brackets at K=200, L=30 (7,107,556 brackets) was 48.7 seconds on a 2.67 GHz Intel Core i5 (2010)
- Average time per bracket at K=200, L=30 was 6.9×10⁻⁶ seconds
- Average time per bracket at K=6, L=2 was 1.8×10⁻⁷ seconds
- At K=6, the present program is more than 400 times faster than the programs of Kitipova (1992) and Kitipova and Zubarev (1991)

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier unpublished program RRCOE from 1987, which was used in studies of halo nuclei by Danilin et al. and by the author and collaborators. The program enables calculation of interaction matrix elements and construction of hyperspherical bases for identical particles, including the three-nucleon case relevant to nuclear interactions derived from Effective Field Theory. The program is compared with earlier programs by Kitipova (1992) and Kitipova and Zubarev (1991), which used different algorithms and were substantially slower.
