# Unconventional decay law for excited states in closed many-body systems

> We study the time evolution of an initially excited many-body state in a finite system of interacting Fermi-particles in the situation when the interaction gives rise to the ``chaotic'' structure of compound states. This situation is generic for highly excited many-particle states in quantum systems, such as heavy nuclei, complex atoms, quantum dots, spin systems, and quantum computers. For a strong interaction the leading term for the return probability $W(t)$ has the form $W(t)\simeq \exp (-Δ_E^2t^2)$ with $Δ_E^2$ as the variance of the strength function. The conventional exponential linear dependence $W(t)=C\exp (-Γt)$ formally arises for a very large time. However, the prefactor $C$ turns out to be exponentially large, thus resulting in a strong difference from the conventional estimate for $W(t)$.

## Key claim

For strongly interacting closed many-body systems, the return probability initially follows a Gaussian decay \(W(t) \simeq \exp(-\Delta_E^2 t^2)\) rather than the conventional exponential law; the exponential form \(W(t) = C \exp(-\Gamma t)\) only emerges at very large times, but with an exponentially large prefactor \(C\) that makes the standard estimate strongly inaccurate.

## Method

The authors analytically study the time evolution of an initially excited basis state in a finite system of interacting Fermi particles, using the Two-Body Random Interaction (TBRI) model where two-body matrix elements are treated as completely random. They derive the return probability \(W(t)\) from the strength function (local spectral density of states) via Fourier transform, analyzing both the weak-interaction (Breit-Wigner) and strong-interaction (Gaussian) limits. The analysis includes matching the short-time Gaussian regime to the long-time exponential regime, and accounts for finite-size effects such as saturation and oscillations due to reflection from the edges of the energy shell.

## Key numbers

- The short-time return probability is \(W_i(t) = 1 - \Delta_E^2 t^2\), where \(\Delta_E^2 = \frac14 V_0^2 n(n-1)(m-n)(m-n+3)\) for the TBRI model with \(n\) particles and \(m\) orbitals, and \(V_0^2\) is the variance of off-diagonal matrix elements.
- For strong interaction (\(\Gamma_0 \ge \Delta_E\)), the leading term is \(W(t) \simeq \exp(-\Delta_E^2 t^2)\).
- The transition time between Gaussian and exponential decay is \(t_c \sim \Gamma / \Delta_E^2\); for the example shown in Fig. 1, \(\tilde\Gamma = 0.5\), \(\Delta_E = 1.2\), giving \(t_c \approx 0.17\).
- For strong interaction (\(\Gamma \gg \Delta_E\)), the long-time exponential form is \(W(t) \approx \frac{\pi^2 \Gamma^2}{8\Delta_E^2} \exp\left( \frac14 \frac{\Gamma^2}{\Delta_E^2} - \Gamma t \right)\).
- The equilibrium value of the return probability is \(W_\infty \approx 3 N_{pc}^{-1}\), where \(N_{pc} \sim \Delta / D\) is the number of principal components in an eigenstate.

## Context

This work extends the quantum chaos approach to time-dependent problems in finite many-body systems, building on earlier studies of chaotic eigenstates in complex atoms (Ce), nuclei (nuclei), and spin systems (Nobel, spins). The authors derive analytical results for the return probability using the strength function formalism previously developed for the TBRI model (FI00, FI97, BM69). The findings are directly relevant to understanding decoherence and relaxation in heavy nuclei, complex atoms, quantum dots, spin systems, and quantum computers, where the density of states is so high that individual energy levels cannot be resolved. The paper enables quantitative predictions for the time evolution of wave packets in these systems, including the identification of a Gaussian decay regime that differs fundamentally from the exponential decay assumed in standard treatments.
