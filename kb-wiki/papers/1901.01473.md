# What is wrong with our current nuclear forces?

> I discuss ab initio predictions for light and intermediate-mass nuclei as well as nuclear matter. Problems and open issues are outlined and an attempt is made to relate them to specific deficiencies of the chiral two- and many-nucleon forces currently in use. In particular, I identify the softness of the NN potential (due to non-locality) as one important factor for the improvement of microscopic predictions. This finding is very much in tune with the recent investigation by Lu et al. (arXiv:1812.10928) where---within a simple, but realistic model---it is shown that proper nuclear matter saturation requires a considerable amout of non-locality in the NN interaction.

# What is wrong with our current nuclear forces?

## Key claim
The persistent problems in *ab initio* nuclear structure predictions, particularly overbinding and too-small radii in medium-mass nuclei, can be traced to insufficient softness (non-locality) in chiral two-nucleon forces. The author argues that a 2NF must be extremely soft (producing overbinding when used alone) so that a repulsive density-dependent three-nucleon force can then restore correct nuclear matter saturation, but cautions that existing demonstrations of this principle (e.g., using RG-evolved potentials) are not fully consistent because they omit the induced three-nucleon force.

## Method
The paper reviews *ab initio* predictions from chiral effective field theory (chiral EFT) for light and intermediate-mass nuclei and for nuclear matter, comparing results from various two-nucleon force (2NF) and three-nucleon force (3NF) combinations. It examines the role of potential softness, quantified by deuteron D-state probability and triton binding energy from the 2NF alone, and contrasts the successful soft-potential approach (exemplified by the 1.8/2.0(EM) potential) with the failure of repulsive 2NF plus attractive 3NF combinations (e.g., Argonne V18 plus Urbana IX or Illinois-7). The author then points to newly constructed chiral potentials that are soft from the outset (LO to N4LO) as the path forward for fully consistent calculations.

## Key numbers
- The 1.8/2.0(EM) potential fits NN data up to 290 MeV laboratory energy and, with appropriate 3NFs, reproduces ground-state energies of closed-shell nuclei from 4He to 78Ni very well, though radii come out slightly too small.
- The NNLOsat potential reproduces NN data only up to 35 MeV, sacrificing satisfactory description of NN scattering above that energy.
- For the necessary softness of a 2NF, the author states that a deuteron D-state probability P_D < 4.5% and a triton binding energy B_t > 8.0 MeV (from the 2NF alone) are desirable.
- The combination of Argonne V18 2NF plus Urbana IX 3NF could not reproduce both nuclear matter saturation density and energy; medium-mass nuclei are severely underbound with this combination and with AV18 plus Illinois-7 3NF.

## Context
This work extends the line of investigation by Hebeler et al. (2011) and Simonis et al. (2017), who showed that an extremely soft RG-evolved 2NF combined with a repulsive 3NF can reproduce nuclear matter saturation and finite-nuclei properties, though those calculations were not fully consistent (the induced 3NF from RG evolution was omitted). The paper contrasts this with the failure of the Argonne V18 plus Urbana IX approach (Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall, 1998) and similar combinations. It points to the newly constructed chiral potentials of Entem, Machleidt, and Nosyk (2017) as the next step for systematic studies of intermediate-mass nuclei, and notes that the required softness is attributable to non-locality, consistent with the model study by Lu et al. (2018).
