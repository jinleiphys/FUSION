# Universality and Non-Universality in Distributed Nuclear Burning in Homogeneous Isotropic Turbulence

> Nuclear burning plays a key role in a wide range of astrophysical stellar transients, including thermonuclear, pair instability, and core collapse supernovae, as well as kilonovae and collapsars. Turbulence is now understood to also play a key role in these astrophysical transients. Here we demonstrate that turbulent nuclear burning may lead to large enhancements above the uniform background burning rate, since turbulent dissipation gives rise to temperature fluctuations, and in general the nuclear burning rates are highly sensitive to temperature. We derive results for the turbulent enhancement of the nuclear burning rate under the influence of strong turbulence in the distributed burning regime in homogeneous isotropic turbulence, using probability distribution function (PDF) methods. We demonstrate that the turbulent enhancement obeys a universal scaling law in the limit of weak turbulence. We further demonstrate that, for a wide range of key nuclear reactions, such as C$^{12}$(O$^{16}$, $α$)Mg$^{24}$ and triple-$α$, even relatively modest temperature fluctuations, of the order ten percent, can lead to enhancements of 1 - 3 orders of magnitude in the turbulent nuclear burning rate. We verify the predicted turbulent enhancement directly against numerical simulations, and find very good agreement. We also present an estimation for the onset of turbulent detonation initiation, and discuss implications of our results for the modeling of stellar transients.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that turbulent nuclear burning in homogeneous isotropic turbulence exhibits a universal scaling law (enhancement proportional to \((r/L)^{2/3}\)) in the weak turbulence limit, but that this universality breaks down for strong turbulence where the enhancement depends sensitively on the specific nuclear reaction. The authors claim that even modest temperature fluctuations of order ten percent can produce enhancements of 1-3 orders of magnitude in the turbulent nuclear burning rate, but this result is conditional on the distributed burning regime where the Karlovitz number Ka \(\gg\) 1 and the burning backreaction on the turbulent fields can be neglected.

## Method

The authors developed an analytic probability distribution function (PDF) formalism assuming Gaussian distributions for temperature, density, and composition fluctuations with Obukhov-Corrsin scaling for passive scalars in homogeneous isotropic turbulence, including cross-correlations between these quantities (with a density-temperature correlation \(r_{\rm corr}(\rho, T) \simeq 0.5\) measured from simulations). They then verified their analytic predictions against three-dimensional hydrodynamical simulations using the FLASH4 code with a 19-isotope network (78 rates) at \(256^3\) resolution, driven by large-scale stochastic forcing, where nuclear burning was activated only after achieving steady-state turbulence.

## Key numbers

- Temperature fluctuations of order ten percent can lead to enhancements of 1-3 orders of magnitude in the turbulent nuclear burning rate for reactions such as C\(^{12}\)(O\(^{16}\), \(\alpha\))Mg\(^{24}\) and triple-\(\alpha\).
- The density-temperature correlation measured from 3D simulations is \(r_{\rm corr}(\rho, T) \simeq 0.5\).
- For the weak enhancement regime, the fractional turbulent enhancement scales as \((r/L)^{2/3}\), independent of the reaction.
- For the C\(^{12}\)(O\(^{16}\), \(\alpha\))Mg\(^{24}\) reaction, temperature fluctuations of order 10% are sufficient to produce detonation initiation on a background temperature of \(T_0 = 2 \times 10^9\) K, with background density \(\rho_0 = 1 \times 10^7\) g cm\(^{-3}\) and integral scale \(L = 100\) km.
- The simulations show agreement with predicted enhancement rates to within fractional errors much less than 1% in the weak regime (\(\delta T/T_0 \ll 0.1\)), and to within 10% in the moderately strong regime (\(\delta T/T_0 \simeq 0.1\)).
- The power-law temperature index \(n\) used for representative reactions: \(n = 8\) for neutrino cooling via URCA process, \(n = 23\) for \(^{12}\)C-\(^{12}\)C burning, and \(n = 41\) for triple-\(\alpha\) reaction.

## Context

This work extends prior theoretical studies of turbulent nuclear burning by Lisewski et al. (2000a, 2000b) and Pan et al. (2008) that examined how small-scale turbulent intermittent velocity fluctuations influence burning rates, but focuses specifically on the role of stochastic thermodynamic fluctuations. The authors build upon the distributed burning regime framework of Aspden et al. (2008) and the Obukhov-Corrsin scaling for passive scalars in turbulence (Obukhov 1949, Corrsin 1951). The results enable improved modeling of nuclear burning in stellar transients including thermonuclear supernovae, white dwarf mergers (Fisher et al. 2019), and X-ray bursts on neutron stars (Timmes and Niemeyer 2000), particularly by providing a criterion for turbulent detonation initiation that the authors show is in rough agreement with detailed numerical simulations from Fisher et al. (2019).
