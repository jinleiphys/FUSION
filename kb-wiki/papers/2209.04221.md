# The $\mathbf{X(3872)}$'s excitation and its connection with production at hadron colliders

> The LHCb collaboration has found that the production rate of $X(3872)$ in proton-proton collisions decreases as final state particle multiplicity increases. Moreover, the ALICE experiment at CERN has observed that the number of deuterons produced increases with multiplicity, a behavior that is qualitatively different from that of the $X(3872)$. These experimental findings may point to a compact structure for the $X(3872)$ or, at least, that its hadronization could proceed through a charm-anticharm core. We have recently used a diffusion Monte Carlo method to solve the many-body Schrödinger equation that describes the $X(3872)$ as a $c \bar c q \bar q$ tetraquark system with quantum numbers $I^G(J^{PC})=0^+(1^{++})$ and $1^-(1^{++})$. According to our structural analysis, the quark--(anti-)quark correlations resemble light-meson--heavy-meson molecules of type $ωJ/ψ$ and $ρJ/ψ$, rather than the most extended $D\bar D^{\ast}$ interpretation. It was argued that this fact may be the key to make compatible the molecular features of the $X(3872)$ with its production observables. The same formalism allows us to compute the first color excited $c \bar c q \bar q$ tetraquark state with either $I^G(J^{PC})=0^+(1^{++})$ or $1^-(1^{++})$. A bound-state is found in each channel, their masses are around 4.0 GeV which is an energy region where many new exotic candidates have been collected by the Particle Data Group. Concerning their structural properties, these states cluster in a compact diquark-antidiquark arrangement which matches perfectly with a so-called Born-Oppenheimer tetraquark configuration. The promptly production rates of these states in proton-proton, proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions should fall off equal to or even faster than those of the $X(3872)$.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the X(3872) can be interpreted as a hadro-charmonium state (a compact color-singlet charm-anticharm pair surrounded by a color-singlet light quark-antiquark pair) rather than a D D* molecule, and that this structural interpretation can reconcile the molecular features of the X(3872) with its observed production properties at hadron colliders.

## Method

The authors used a diffusion Monte Carlo method to solve the non-relativistic many-body Schrodinger equation for a c cbar q qbar tetraquark system with quantum numbers I^G(J^PC)=0^+(1++) and 1^-(1++). The dynamics was driven by a two-body potential consisting of Coulomb, linear-confining, and hyperfine spin-spin terms, with Goldstone-boson exchange interactions between light quarks also included. The same formalism was applied to compute the first color-excited tetraquark state by imposing an orthogonal color wave function corresponding to a color-octet charm-anticharm pair bound to a color-octet light quark-antiquark pair.

## Key numbers

- The ground state masses for the X(3872) candidates were 3834 MeV (isoscalar) and 3842 MeV (isovector), using quark masses m_q=315 MeV and m_c=1836 MeV
- The first color-excited tetraquark state has binding energy -302 MeV in the isoscalar sector and -294 MeV in the isovector sector, corresponding to absolute masses of 4000 MeV and 4008 MeV respectively
- The chiral contribution to the mass of the X(3872) represented only 10% of the total mass
- The color wave function of the X(3872) ground state is |X(3872)>_color = 0.57 |3bar_qc 3_qbarc>_color + 0.82 |6_qc 6bar_qbarc>_color, which translates to approximately |1_c cbar 1_q qbar>_color

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous diffusion Monte Carlo study of the X(3872) as a tetraquark system (Gordillo, 2021) to include the first color-excited state. The results contradict the most common D D* molecular interpretation of the X(3872) and instead support a hadro-charmonium picture similar to the Born-Oppenheimer tetraquark configuration proposed by Braaten (2013). The predicted masses around 4.0 GeV fall in an energy region where the Particle Data Group reports nine observed states between 3.9 and 4.1 GeV, suggesting these excited tetraquarks could correspond to some of these experimentally known exotic candidates. The authors propose that LHCb and ALICE experiments could test this interpretation by measuring the production rates of these states as a function of final state particle multiplicity in proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions.
