# Neutrino and anti-neutrino energy loss rates due to iron isotopes suitable for core-collapse simulations

> Accurate estimate of neutrino energy loss rates are needed for the study of the late stages of the stellar evolution, in particular for cooling of neutron stars and white dwarfs. The energy spectra of neutrinos and antineutrinos arriving at the Earth can also provide useful information on the primary neutrino fluxes as well as neutrino mixing scenario (it is to be noted that these supernova neutrinos are emitted after the supernova explosion which is a much later stage of stellar evolution than that considered in this paper). Recently an improved microscopic calculation of weak-interaction mediated rates for iron isotopes was introduced using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory. Here I present for the first time the fine-grid calculation of the neutrino and anti-neutrino energy loss rates due to $^{54,55,56}$Fe in stellar matter. In the core of massive stars isotopes of iron, $^{54,55,56}$Fe, are considered to be key players in decreasing the electron-to-baryon ratio ($Y_{e}$) mainly via electron capture on these nuclide. Core-collapse simulators may find this calculation suitable for interpolation purposes and for necessary incorporation in the stellar evolution codes. The calculated cooling rates are also compared with previous calculations.

## Key claim

This paper presents the first fine-grid calculation of neutrino and anti-neutrino energy loss rates due to the iron isotopes ⁵⁴Fe, ⁵⁵Fe, and ⁵⁶Fe in stellar matter, using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory. The author notes that these rates are intended for interpolation purposes in core-collapse simulation codes, and that the calculated cooling rates are compared with previous calculations from Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (FFN) and the large-scale shell model (LSSM).

## Method

The author used the pn-QRPA theory to perform a microscopic state-by-state calculation of Gamow-Teller (GT) strength distributions for all excited states of the parent nuclei, avoiding the Brink hypothesis. Experimentally adopted deformation parameters were used for ⁵⁴Fe and ⁵⁶Fe, while the deformation for ⁵⁵Fe was calculated from its electric quadrupole moment. A total of 246 parent excited states for ⁵⁴Fe, 297 for ⁵⁵Fe, and 266 for ⁵⁶Fe were considered, with transitions calculated to 150 daughter excited states for each parent state, covering excitation energies of 15-20 MeV in parent and daughter nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For ⁵⁴Fe at low temperatures and densities, the pn-QRPA neutrino energy loss rates are around five times bigger than those calculated by LSSM.
- During oxygen shell burning and silicon core burning phases, the pn-QRPA energy loss rates for ⁵⁴Fe are around 3-4 times bigger than LSSM rates.
- At T₉ = 1 K and ρYₑ = 10⁶ g cm⁻³, the pn-QRPA anti-neutrino energy loss rates for ⁵⁴Fe are up to four orders of magnitude smaller than the corresponding LSSM rates.
- For ⁵⁵Fe at T₉ = 1 K and ρYₑ = 10⁸ g cm⁻³, the anti-neutrino energy loss rates are around four orders of magnitude smaller than LSSM rates.
- For ⁵⁶Fe, the FFN neutrino energy loss rates are generally bigger than pn-QRPA rates, with FFN anti-neutrino rates around 4 orders of magnitude bigger at low temperatures.
- The calculated rates are tabulated in logarithmic (base 10) scale for stellar densities log(ρYₑ) from 0.5 to 11.0 (g cm⁻³) and temperatures T₉ from 0.5 to 30 (10⁹ K), with selected values shown for log(ρYₑ) = 6.5 and 7.0.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous pn-QRPA calculations of weak-interaction rates on iron isotopes (Nabi, 2009) and neutrino cooling rates (Nabi, 2011) by providing a finer temperature-density grid more suitable for core-collapse simulation codes. The results are compared with the pioneering FFN calculations (Fuller, Fowler, and Newman, 1980) and the large-scale shell model calculations (Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo, 2000). The fine-grid tabulation enables more accurate interpolation for simulators studying presupernova evolution of massive stars, particularly for the oxygen shell burning through convective core silicon burning phases.
