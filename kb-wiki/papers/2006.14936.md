# Parameter estimation for strong phase transitions in supranuclear matter using gravitational-wave astronomy

> At supranuclear densities, explored in the core of neutron stars, a strong phase transition from hadronic matter to more exotic forms of matter might be present. To test this hypothesis, binary neutron-star mergers offer a unique possibility to probe matter at densities that we can not create in any existing terrestrial experiment. In this work, we show that, if present, strong phase transitions can have a measurable imprint on the binary neutron-star coalescence and the emitted gravitational-wave signal. We construct a new parameterization of the supranuclear equation of state that allows us to test for the existence of a strong phase transition and extract its characteristic properties purely from the gravitational-wave signal of the inspiraling neutron stars. We test our approach using a Bayesian inference study simulating 600 signals with three different equations of state and find that for current gravitational-wave detector networks already twelve events might be sufficient to verify the presence of a strong phase transition. Finally, we use our methodology to analyze GW170817 and GW190425, but do not find any indication that a strong phase transition is present at densities probed during the inspiral.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that strong first-order phase transitions in neutron-star matter can leave a measurable imprint on gravitational-wave signals from binary inspiral, and that with current detector networks approximately twelve detections might suffice to confidently identify such a phase transition; however, when applied to the actual events GW170817 and GW190425, the method finds no indication of a strong phase transition at the densities probed during inspiral.

## Method

The authors constructed a five-parameter piecewise-polytrope equation-of-state parameterization (the "Maxwell parameterization") that includes a zero-speed-of-sound segment to represent a first-order phase transition via a Maxwell construction, characterized by the transition onset pressure, the density jump, and three adiabatic indices. They performed a Bayesian inference study simulating 600 gravitational-wave signals from binary neutron-star mergers using three different equations of state (one with a twin-star solution, one with a kink, and one with a mixed-phase transition), and analyzed the simulated signals with the TaylorF2 post-Newtonian waveform model. The method was then applied to the real gravitational-wave events GW170817 and GW190425.

## Key numbers

- Twelve events might be sufficient to verify the presence of a strong phase transition with current gravitational-wave detector networks (based on 600 simulated signals across three equations of state).
- The low-density equation of state is constrained by chiral effective field theory up to nuclear saturation density, \(n_{\rm sat}=0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\), corresponding to a mass density of \(2.7 \times 10^{14}\) g cm\(^{-3}\).
- The three chosen equations of state all support a maximum mass greater than \(1.93\,M_{\odot}\), consistent with observed heavy neutron stars.
- The analysis of GW170817 and GW190425 found no indication of a strong phase transition at densities probed during the inspiral.

## Context

This work extends previous searches for phase transitions in neutron-star matter, such as those by Chatziioannou (2019) which used quasi-universal relations and found that the transition mass could be measured with 50-100 detections, and by Landry (2020) which found a Bayes factor of \(1.8\pm0.2\) in favor of multiple stable branches from combined pulsar, GW170817, and NICER observations. The key advance is that the new method can detect phase transitions even when only a single stable branch exists in the mass-radius relation (not just twin-star solutions), and it simultaneously performs hypothesis testing and parameter estimation for the microscopic properties of the phase transition. The approach enables future gravitational-wave catalogs to statistically determine whether strong phase transitions occur in supranuclear matter.
