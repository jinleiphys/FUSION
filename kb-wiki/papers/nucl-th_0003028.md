# LOCV calculation for Beta-stable matter at finite temperature

> The method of lowest-order constrained variational, which predicts reasonably the nuclear matter semi-empirical data is used to calculate the equation of state of beta-stable matter at finite temperature. The Reid soft-core with and without the N-$Δ$ interactions which fits the N-N scattering data as well as the $UV_{14}$ potential plus the three-nucleon interaction are considered in the nuclear many-body Hamiltonian. The electron and muon are treated relativistically in the total Hamiltonian at given temperature, to make the fluid electrically neutral and stable against beta decay. The calculation is performed for a wide range of baryon density and temperature which are of interest in the astrophysics. The free energy, entropy, proton abundance, etc. of nuclear beta-stable matter are calculated. It is shown that by increasing the temperature, the maximum proton abundance is pushed to the lower density while the maximum itself increases as we increase the temperature. The proton fraction is not enough to see any gas-liquid phase transition. Finally we get an overall agreement with other many-body techniques, which are available only at zero temperature.

# LOCV Calculation for Beta-Stable Matter at Finite Temperature

## Key claim
The lowest-order constrained variational (LOCV) method predicts that increasing temperature pushes the maximum proton abundance to lower baryon density while the maximum itself increases, but the proton fraction never reaches the critical value of about 15 percent needed for direct Urca processes or a gas-liquid phase transition, even at finite temperature.

## Method
The authors apply the LOCV method to beta-stable nuclear matter at finite temperature, using the Reid soft-core potential with and without N-Δ interactions, as well as the UV₁₄ potential plus a three-nucleon interaction. They treat electrons and muons relativistically in the total Hamiltonian and enforce charge neutrality and beta-decay equilibrium by minimizing the total free energy with respect to proton abundance, effective masses, and two-body correlation functions. The calculation covers a wide range of baryon density and temperature relevant to astrophysics.

## Key numbers
- The three-body cluster energy is less than 1 MeV for Fermi momentum k_f ≤ 1.6 fm⁻¹, and the normalization integral ⟨ψ|ψ⟩ is normalized within one percent.
- At zero temperature, the LOCV results for proton abundance y_p agree well with Wiringa et al. (WFF) and Baldo et al. (BHF) calculations, especially at low baryonic densities; for n_B ≥ 0.5 fm⁻³ the BHF calculation increases while LOCV and WFF results decrease.
- The proton fraction does not reach the critical value of about 15 percent needed for direct Urca processes.
- The muon entropy shows a peak at a given temperature, and the baryon density at which this peak occurs shifts to lower densities as temperature increases.

## Context
This work extends the LOCV method, previously applied to pure neutron matter and symmetric nuclear matter by Modarres and Irvine (earlier works), to beta-stable matter at finite temperature. It provides a finite-temperature comparison to zero-temperature calculations by Wiringa et al. (WFF) using variational FHNC and by Baldo et al. (BHF) using Brueckner theory. The results enable improved modeling of hot dense matter in supernova explosions and neutron star cooling, and suggest that temperature may help reach higher proton fractions needed for direct Urca processes.
