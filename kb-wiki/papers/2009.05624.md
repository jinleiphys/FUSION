# Nuclear resonance fluorescence of $^{208}$Pb in heavy-ion colliders

> In ultraperipheral collisions (UPC) of nuclei the impact of Lorentz-contracted electromagnetic fields of collision partners leads to their excitations. In case of heavy nuclei the emission of neutrons is a main deexcitation channel and forward neutrons emitted in UPC were detected at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC) and at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) by means of Zero Degree Calorimeters. However, the excitation of low-lying discrete nuclear states is also possible in UPC below the neutron separation energy. In this work by means of the Weizsacker-Williams method the data on nuclear resonance fluorescence (NRF) induced by real photons in 208 Pb are used to model the excitations of discrete levels in colliding nuclei. Due to Lorentz boosts one can expect that deexcitation photons with energies up to 40 GeV and 300 GeV are emitted in very forward direction, respectively, at the LHC and at the Future Circular Collider (FCC-hh). Energy, rapidity and angular distributions of such photons are calculated in the laboratory system, which can be used for monitoring of collider luminosity or triggering particle production in UPC.

## Key claim

The authors model the excitation of discrete nuclear levels in $^{208}$Pb below the neutron separation energy during ultraperipheral collisions (UPC) at the LHC and Future Circular Collider (FCC-hh), showing that nuclear resonance fluorescence (NRF) photons with energies up to 40 GeV and 300 GeV, respectively, are emitted in the very forward direction; the claim is conditional on using the Weizsacker-Williams method with the approximation that the multipole expansion can be replaced by a single equivalent photon spectrum at ultrarelativistic energies where $\gamma \gg 10$.

## Method

The authors apply the Weizsacker-Williams method to calculate the equivalent photon flux in ultraperipheral $^{208}$Pb--$^{208}$Pb collisions, then fold this flux with Breit-Wigner photoabsorption cross sections for 14 discrete excited states of $^{208}$Pb below the neutron separation energy (4 to 8 MeV), using level parameters (energy, width, spin, parity) from prior NRF experiments with real photons. The NRF cross section for each level is computed as $\sigma_{\rm NRF}(E_r) = n_{\rm WW}(E_r) I(E_r)$, where $n_{\rm WW}$ is the equivalent photon spectrum and $I(E_r)$ is the integral photoabsorption cross section; the resulting photon energy, angular, and pseudorapidity distributions in the laboratory frame are then obtained via Lorentz boosts assuming isotropic emission in the nucleus rest frame.

## Key numbers

- Total NRF cross section for all 14 discrete levels in $^{208}$Pb: 4.15 b at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=5.02$ TeV (LHC) and 5.16 b at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=39.4$ TeV (FCC-hh)
- Maximum photon energy in laboratory frame: up to 40 GeV at LHC and up to 300 GeV at FCC-hh, corresponding to Lorentz factors $\gamma = 2.7 \times 10^3$ and $2.1 \times 10^4$, respectively
- Largest individual NRF cross section: 1.194 b at LHC and 1.485 b at FCC-hh for the level at $E_r = 5.5122$ MeV with width $\Gamma_r = 32.91$ eV
- First four levels contribute approximately 58% of the total NRF cross section at both collision energies
- Lifetime of the level with largest NRF cross section ($E_r=5.5122$ MeV): $\tau = 2 \times 10^{-17}$ s in nucleus rest frame, corresponding to $l \approx 16$ $\mu$m travel distance at LHC
- For the narrowest level ($E_r=4.085$ MeV, $\Gamma_r=0.783$ eV): $l \approx 0.67$ mm at LHC and $l \approx 5.2$ mm at FCC-hh
- Beam rapidities: $y_{\rm beam}=8.6$ at LHC and $y_{\rm beam}=10.7$ at FCC-hh

## Context

This work extends the study of ultraperipheral collisions at RHIC and LHC, where previous measurements focused on electromagnetic dissociation with neutron emission (Abelev 2012) and giant dipole resonance excitations (Bertulani 1988, Aumann 1998), to the regime of low-lying discrete nuclear states below the neutron separation energy. The approach builds on the Weizsacker-Williams method used in prior theoretical studies of discrete level excitation in UPC (Korotkikh 2002, Kharlov 2004), but considers direct excitation by equivalent photons rather than through secondary processes like electron-positron pair production. The results enable the use of NRF photons for collider luminosity monitoring, triggering particle production in UPC, and potentially improving vertex position determination in detectors like ALICE, while also providing a lower-cost alternative to proposed photon-nucleus colliders (Yavas 2005).
