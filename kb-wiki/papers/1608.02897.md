# The neutron within the deuteron as a surrogate for neutron-induced reactions

> We propose the use of neutron poisons in reactions induced by radioactive beams as a test of theoretical models aiming to relate neutron capture in nuclei with neutron surrogate reactions such as (d,p) reactions. We exploit the approximations necessary to obtain a direct relation between the two reactions; surrogate vs. neutron capture. We also show how this is intimately related to the momentum distribution of the neutron within the deuteron. The models we use are based on the theory of inclusive breakup reactions commonly employed in the treatment of incomplete fusion and surrogate method. Such theories were developed in the 80's by Ichimura, Autern and Vincent [Phys. Rev. C 32, 431 (1985)], Udagawa and Tamura [Phys. Rev. C 24, 1348 (1981)] and Hussein and McVoy [Nucl. Phys. A 445, 124 (1985)]. We use these theories to derive an expression for the proton yield in the reaction A(d,p)B. The capture reaction $n + A \rightarrow B$ is then extracted using reasonable approximations. By recalling an old method proposed by Serber [Phys. Rev. 80, 1098 (1950); Nature 166, 709 (1950)] we explain how the momentum distribution of neutrons within the deuteron will depend on the short-range dependence of the nucleon-nucleon force. The relevance of our work to nucleosynthesis in the rapid neutron capture process is emphasized.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that neutron poisons (nuclei with extremely large neutron capture cross sections) can be used as benchmarks to test theoretical models that relate neutron capture reactions to surrogate (d,p) reactions, but only under specific approximations that reveal how the momentum distribution of the neutron within the deuteron and geometric distortion effects modify the simple proportionality between the two reaction types.

## Method

The authors derive an expression for the inclusive proton yield in A(d,p)B reactions using the Hussein-McVoy theory of inclusive breakup, which avoids solving an inhomogeneous equation by using a source function based on the non-orthogonality of distorted waves. They then introduce a neutron surrogate factor and a momentum matching function to relate the (d,p) cross section to the neutron-induced reaction cross section, using eikonal approximations for the S-matrices of the proton and neutron. The formalism is applied to the specific case of neutron poisons such as $^{135}$Xe, where the large cross sections would provide high-statistics tests of the surrogate method.

## Key numbers

- Thermal neutron capture cross section on $^{135}$Xe: $3 \times 10^{6}$ barns
- Thermal neutron capture cross section on $^{157}$Gd: $2.54 \times 10^{5}$ barns
- Thermal neutron capture cross section on $^{10}$B: $3.80 \times 10^{3}$ barns
- Thermal neutron capture cross section on $^{153}$Cd: $2 \times 10^{4}$ barns
- For a deuteron projectile with 50 MeV, the relative number of neutrons available for capture at thermal energies decreases by a factor $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-3}$ depending on the deuteron wavefunction used
- The half-life of $^{11}$Be is $13.76 \pm 0.07$ s; the half-life of $^{22}$C is $9 \pm 3$ ms
- The probability for doorway enhancement of neutron capture cross sections is given by $P(\eta_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{1}{1 + \eta_{0}}$, where $\eta_0$ is the ratio of doorway neutron width to compound neutron width

## Context

This work extends the inclusive breakup theories developed by Ichimura, Austern and Vincent (1985), Udagawa and Tamura (1981), and Hussein and McVoy (1985) to the problem of relating (d,p) surrogate reactions to neutron capture. The authors recall the Serber method (1950) for understanding the momentum distribution of neutrons within the deuteron and its dependence on short-range nucleon-nucleon forces. The paper enables future experimental tests using radioactive beams of neutron poisons (such as $^{135}$Xe) at facilities producing secondary beams, which would provide benchmarks for determining whether surrogate reactions can reliably extract neutron capture cross sections relevant for the r-process in nuclear astrophysics.
