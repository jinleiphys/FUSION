# On kaonic hydrogen. Quantum field theoretic and relativistic covariant approach

> We study kaonic hydrogen, the bound K^-p state A_(Kp). Within a quantum field theoretic and relativistic covariant approach we derive the energy level displacement of the ground state of kaonic hydrogen in terms of the amplitude of K^-p scattering for arbitrary relative momenta. The amplitude of low-energy K^-p scattering near threshold is defined by the contributions of three resonances Lambda(1405), Lambda(1800) and Sigma^0(1750) and a smooth elastic background. The amplitudes of inelastic channels of low-energy K^-p scattering fit experimental data on near threshold behaviour of the cross sections and the experimental data by the DEAR Collaboration. We use the soft-pion technique (leading order in Chiral Perturbation Theory) for the calculation of the partial width of the radiative decay of pionic hydrogen A_(pi p) -> n + gamma and the Panofsky ratio. The theoretical prediction for the Panofsky ratio agrees well with experimental data. We apply the soft-kaon technique (leading order in Chiral Perturbation Theory) to the calculation of the partial widths of radiative decays of kaonic hydrogen A_(Kp) -> Lambda^0 + gamma and A_(Kp) -> Sigma^0 + gamma. We show that the contribution of these decays to the width of the energy level of the ground state of kaonic hydrogen is less than 1%.

## Key claim

The paper derives a quantum field theoretic and relativistic covariant generalization of the Deser-Goldberger-Baumann-Thirring (DGBT) formula for the energy level displacement of kaonic hydrogen, showing that the standard DGBT formula is an approximation valid only when the amplitude of K⁻p scattering is a smooth function near threshold; the authors model the low-energy K⁻p scattering amplitude using three resonances (Λ(1405), Λ(1800), Σ⁰(1750)) plus a smooth elastic background, and find that radiative decay channels contribute less than 1% to the ground-state width.

## Method

The authors used a quantum field theoretic and relativistic covariant approach to derive the energy level displacement of the ground state of kaonic hydrogen from the K⁻p scattering amplitude for arbitrary relative momenta. They modeled the low-energy K⁻p scattering amplitude near threshold using phenomenological Lagrangians for the Λ(1405), Λ(1800), and Σ⁰(1750) resonances as elementary fields, combined with a smooth elastic background calculated within an Effective quark model with chiral U(3)×U(3) symmetry. They applied the soft-pion technique (leading order in Chiral Perturbation Theory) to compute the Panofsky ratio for pionic hydrogen and the soft-kaon technique to compute radiative decay widths of kaonic hydrogen.

- DEAR Collaboration experimental energy level displacement: -ε_exp_1s + i(Γ_exp_1s/2) = (-183 ± 62) + i(106 ± 69) eV
- Theoretical prediction for total width: Γ^th_1s = (227 ± 27) eV, compared to DEAR experimental value Γ_exp_1s = (213 ± 138) eV
- Real part of resonance contribution to scattering amplitude: Re f⁰(0)_R = (-0.154 ± 0.009) fm
- Imaginary part of resonance contribution: Im f⁰(0)_R = (0.269 ± 0.032) fm
- Coupling constant g₁ = 0.907 (from Λ(1405) total width Γ = 50 MeV at mass 1406 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the Deser-Goldberger-Baumann-Thirring (DGBT) formula (1954) for kaonic hydrogen energy level shifts by providing a quantum field theoretic and relativistic covariant derivation that accounts for finite relative momenta of the K⁻p pair, rather than only zero-momentum scattering. The approach is consistent with experimental data from the DEAR Collaboration (2003) and with earlier measurements of inelastic K⁻p cross sections by Tovee et al. (1971) and Nowak et al. (1978). The soft-pion technique used for the Panofsky ratio follows leading-order Chiral Perturbation Theory as developed by Gasser and Leutwyler (1983, 1999), and the resonance treatment builds on work by Meissner and Raha (1996). The results enable more precise extraction of K⁻p scattering lengths from kaonic hydrogen measurements and provide a framework for including radiative corrections and isospin-breaking effects.
