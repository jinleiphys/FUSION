# Importance of Q-values in astrophysical rp-proces

> The importance of measuring Q-values in rapid proton capture process has been investigated. The microscopic optical model, derived using a nucleon nucleon interaction and densities from relativistic mean field calculations, has been utilized to calculate the reaction rates. It has been observed that the Q-values involved in the reactions at waiting points at A = 60 and 64 are very important in determining the final abundance of the process. Some other Q-values also play a crucial role in the final abundance of nuclei near the end point of the process.

## Key claim

The Q-values (proton separation energies) of specific proton-capture reactions at the waiting points A=60 and A=64 are very important in determining the final abundance of the rp-process, but the effect is conditional on the temperature-density profile: under a constant high temperature (1.5 GK) the waiting point at A=64 is effectively bridged by two-proton capture, while under a more realistic time-varying profile the sensitivity is reduced.

## Method

The authors calculated reaction rates using a microscopic optical model built from the density-dependent M3Y (DDM3Y) nucleon-nucleon interaction folded with nuclear densities from relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations. They solved a set of coupled differential equations for nuclear abundances as a function of time, using experimental beta-decay half-lives from Audi et al. (2003) and theoretical half-lives from Moller et al. (1997) where experimental data were absent. They varied the Q-values of proton-capture reactions over a range of Q0 ± σ, where σ was taken as the experimental error or, for unmeasured masses, the rms error of a phenomenological mass formula (376 keV), and studied the resulting changes in final abundances for two scenarios: a constant-temperature model (Model I, 1.5 GK, density 10^6 g/cm^3, proton fraction 0.7) and a more realistic time-varying temperature-density-proton fraction profile (Model IV).

## Key numbers

- The rms error of the phenomenological mass formula used for unmeasured masses is 376 keV.
- The experimental Q-value for the proton-capture reaction on 64Ge was measured as -255 (104) keV by Schury et al. and as -90 (85) keV in a more recent measurement; another reference found it to be 0.401(530) keV.
- The experimental error on the Q-value for the 65As(p,γ) reaction is 200 keV.
- In the constant-temperature model (1.5 GK), the population at the waiting point A=76 changes by a factor of less than 1.5 over the range of Q-values chosen for capture reactions at 61Ga and 65As.
- In the realistic time-varying model, the system starts at T = 0.4 GK and ρ = 10^6 g/cm^3, reaches a maximum temperature of 1.36 GK and minimum density of 5 × 10^5 g/cm^3 after 4 seconds, and after 100 seconds the temperature drops to 0.7 GK, density increases to 1.4 × 10^6 g/cm^3, and proton fraction decreases to 0.16.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the rp-process path and waiting-point bridging, particularly the calculations by Lahiri et al. (2013) and the reviews by Illiadis (2007) and Schatz (2006). It uses the same microscopic optical model and DDM3Y interaction previously applied to low-energy proton reactions in the mass 60-100 region by the same group. The results identify specific nuclei whose masses need improved experimental measurement: 62Ge, 65As, 66Se, 86Tc, 87Ru, 89Ru, 90Rh, 91Pd, 93Pd, and 94Ag. The work enables future experimental campaigns to prioritize these mass measurements to reduce uncertainties in rp-process abundance predictions.
