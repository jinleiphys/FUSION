# Influence of light nuclei on neutrino-driven supernova outflows

> We study the composition of the outer layers of a protoneutron star and show that light nuclei are present in substantial amounts. The composition is dominated by nucleons, deuterons, tritons and alpha particles; 3He is present in smaller amounts. This composition can be studied in laboratory experiments with new neutron-rich radioactive beams that can reproduce similar densities and temperatures. After including the corresponding neutrino interactions, we demonstrate that light nuclei have a small impact on the average energy of the emitted electron neutrinos, but are significant for the average energy of antineutrinos. During the early post-explosion phase, the average energy of electron antineutrinos is slightly increased, while at later times during the protoneutron star cooling it is reduced by about 1 MeV. The consequences of these changes for nucleosynthesis in neutrino-driven supernova outflows are discussed.

## Key claim

Light nuclei (deuterons, tritons, and alpha particles) are present in substantial amounts in the outer layers of a protoneutron star, and while they have a small impact on the average energy of emitted electron neutrinos, they are significant for the average energy of electron antineutrinos; during the early post-explosion phase the average energy of electron antineutrinos is slightly increased, while at later times during protoneutron star cooling it is reduced by about 1 MeV, with the net effect depending on the competition between composition changes and adjustments to beta equilibrium.

## Method

The authors used the protoneutron star model M15-l1-r1 of Arcones et al. (2007) and compared three equations of state: a reference NSE EOS with neutrons, protons, and alpha particles; an NSE EOS including several thousand nuclei; and a virial EOS based on nuclei with A <= 4 and their interactions through second virial coefficients. They computed neutrino opacities including charged-current and neutral-current interactions with deuterons and tritons (using RPA-calculated cross sections for tritons), determined neutrinosphere radii from the condition that the effective optical depth equals 2/3, and iteratively solved for neutrino spectral temperatures and degeneracy parameters under the assumption of zero net electron neutrino minus antineutrino number flux.

## Key numbers

- At t = 2 s post bounce, the reference case (case A) gives an electron antineutrino average energy of 25.64 MeV and an electron neutrino average energy of 20.71 MeV; with the virial EOS (case D) these become 26.12 MeV and 20.87 MeV respectively
- At t = 10 s post bounce, the reference case gives an electron antineutrino average energy of 21.86 MeV and an electron neutrino average energy of 15.05 MeV; with the virial EOS these become 20.32 MeV and 14.80 MeV respectively
- The electron fraction of the wind ejecta (Y_e^w) at t = 2 s is 0.514 for the reference case and 0.509 for the virial EOS; at t = 10 s it is 0.431 for the reference case and 0.458 for the virial EOS
- The antineutrinosphere radius at t = 2 s is 10.01 km for the reference case and 9.979 km for the virial EOS; at t = 10 s it is 9.041 km for the reference case and 9.065 km for the virial EOS
- The virial and NSE EOS predict deuteron and triton mass fractions significantly larger than that of free protons in the vicinity of the neutrinospheres, at densities up to about 10^13 g/cm^3

## Context

This work extends the protoneutron star atmosphere models of Arcones et al. (2007) and the virial EOS developed by Horowitz and Schwenk (2006) by including the specific neutrino interactions with light nuclei (deuterons and tritons) and quantifying their effect on neutrino spectra and the electron fraction of the neutrino-driven wind. The results show that the competing effects of composition changes (which reduce free protons) and adjustments to beta equilibrium (which increase Y_e) make the net impact on antineutrino energies sensitive to the post-bounce time, with implications for nucleosynthesis in neutrino-driven supernova outflows and for interpreting supernova neutrino detections. The warm nuclear system near the neutrinosphere can be studied experimentally with heavy-ion collisions, as demonstrated by Kowalski et al. (2006) using 64Zn on 92Mo and 197Au targets, and future radioactive beam experiments could probe more neutron-rich conditions.
