# Revealing Type Ia supernova physics with cosmic rates and nuclear gamma rays

> Type Ia supernovae (SNIa) remain mysterious despite their central importance in cosmology and their rapidly increasing discovery rate. The progenitors of SNIa can be probed by the delay time between progenitor birth and explosion as SNIa. The explosions and progenitors of SNIa can be probed by MeV nuclear gamma rays emitted in the decays of radioactive nickel and cobalt into iron. We compare the cosmic star formation and SNIa rates, finding that their different redshift evolution requires a large fraction of SNIa to have large delay times. A delay time distribution of the form t^{-1.0 +/- 0.3} provides a good fit, implying 50% of SNIa explode more than ~ 1 Gyr after progenitor birth. The extrapolation of the cosmic SNIa rate to z = 0 agrees with the rate we deduce from catalogs of local SNIa. We investigate prospects for gamma-ray telescopes to exploit the facts that escaping gamma rays directly reveal the power source of SNIa and uniquely provide tomography of the expanding ejecta. We find large improvements relative to earlier studies by Gehrels et al. in 1987 and Timmes & Woosley in 1997 due to larger and more certain SNIa rates and advances in gamma-ray detectors. The proposed Advanced Compton Telescope, with a narrow-line sensitivity ~ 60 times better than that of current satellites, would, on an annual basis, detect up to ~ 100 SNIa (3 sigma) and provide revolutionary model discrimination for SNIa within 20 Mpc, with gamma-ray light curves measured with ~ 10 sigma significance daily for ~ 100 days. Even more modest improvements in detector sensitivity would open a new and invaluable astronomy with frequent SNIa gamma-ray detections.

## Key claim

The paper finds that the redshift evolution of Type Ia supernova (SNIa) rates, when compared to cosmic star formation rates, requires a large fraction of SNIa to have cosmologically large delay times, with a delay-time distribution of the form \(t^{-1.0 \pm 0.3}\) providing a good fit, implying that 50% of SNIa explode more than approximately 1 Gyr after progenitor birth.

## Method

The authors compared the cosmic star formation rate density, compiled from multiple indicators including mid-infrared galaxies, Lyman break galaxies, and gamma-ray burst host galaxies, with a comprehensive compilation of SNIa rate data over a substantial redshift range. They convolved the star formation rate with a power-law delay-time distribution \(\Phi(t) \propto t^{-\alpha}\) and fitted for the normalization at redshift zero and the exponent \(\alpha\) using a selection of the most reliable SNIa rate measurements. They then used the resulting cosmic SNIa rate to calculate the SNIa contribution to the cosmic gamma-ray background and to assess detection prospects for future gamma-ray telescopes.

## Key numbers

* Best-fit delay-time distribution exponent: \(\alpha = 1.0 \pm 0.3\), from fitting the cosmic SNIa rate to reliable data (filled points) with \(\chi^2 = 5.4\) for 17 degrees of freedom.
* Best-fit SNIa rate at redshift zero: \(R_{\rm Ia}(0) = 0.24 \pm 0.04\) SNIa per year per cubic Mpc.
* Best-fit efficiency of making SNIa: \((5 \pm 1) \times 10^{-4}\) SNIa per solar mass of stars formed, corresponding to a SNIa fraction of \(2.4 \pm 0.5\%\) assuming a progenitor mass range of 3–8 solar masses.
* The proposed Advanced Compton Telescope, with a narrow-line sensitivity approximately 60 times better than current satellites, would detect up to approximately 100 SNIa per year at \(3\sigma\) significance and provide gamma-ray light curves measured with approximately \(10\sigma\) significance daily for approximately 100 days for SNIa within 20 Mpc.
* The SNIa contribution to the cosmic gamma-ray background is at least a factor of approximately 5 smaller than current measurements, and up to approximately 20 times smaller depending on the SNIa rate and model.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Gehrels et al. (1987) and Timmes and Woosley (1997) by using larger and more certain SNIa rates and advances in gamma-ray detectors. The derived delay-time distribution with \(\alpha \approx 1\) is consistent with both the single-degenerate and double-degenerate progenitor scenarios, as both can produce power-law distributions with this index. The results enable improved predictions for gamma-ray detection prospects and provide a framework for discriminating between SNIa progenitor models as rate data and model predictions improve.
