# Neutron rich nuclei and neutron stars

> The PREX experiment at Jefferson Laboratory measures the neutron radius of 208Pb with parity violating electron scattering in a way that is free from most strong interaction uncertainties. The 208Pb radius has important implications for neutron rich matter and the structure of neutron stars. We present first PREX results, describe future plans, and discuss a follow on measurement of the neutron radius of 48Ca. We review radio and X-ray observations of neutron star masses and radii. These constrain the equation of state (pressure versus density) of neutron rich matter. We present a new energy functional that is simultaneously fit to both nuclear and neutron star properties. In this approach, neutron star masses and radii constrain the energy of neutron matter. This avoids having to rely on model dependent microscopic calculations of neutron matter. The functional is then used to predict the location of the drip lines and the properties of very neutron rich heavy nuclei.

# Neutron rich nuclei and neutron stars

## Key claim
The neutron radius of 208Pb measured by PREX constrains the equation of state of neutron matter and correlates with neutron star radii, but the first measurement yields a result with large uncertainty (0.33^{+0.16}_{-0.18} fm for Rn-Rp) that does not yet reach the 1% precision goal; a second run is needed to achieve that precision.

## Method
The PREX experiment at Jefferson Laboratory measured the parity violating asymmetry Apv for 1.05 GeV electrons elastically scattered from 208Pb at laboratory angles near five degrees. The asymmetry is proportional to the weak form factor, which is the Fourier transform of the weak charge density and closely related to the neutron density, allowing extraction of the neutron radius with minimal strong interaction uncertainties. Separately, a new energy density functional (TOV-min) was fit simultaneously to properties of finite nuclei and to the maximum mass and radius of neutron stars, using neutron star observations to constrain the energy of neutron matter rather than relying solely on model-dependent microscopic calculations.

## Key numbers
- PREX first measurement: Apv = 0.656 ± 0.060 (statistical) ± 0.014 (systematic) ppm for 1.05 GeV electrons on 208Pb at angles near five degrees
- Rn - Rp for 208Pb = 0.33^{+0.16}_{-0.18} fm from the first PREX measurement
- The second PREX run aims to determine Rn to 1% precision (±0.05 fm)
- The correlation between Rn and neutron matter pressure involves densities near 0.1 fm^{-3} (about 2/3 of nuclear density)
- Hebeler et al. predicted Rn - Rp = 0.17 ± 0.03 fm using chiral perturbation theory
- The TOV-min functional was fit to the maximum mass of a neutron star and the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star

## Context
This work extends the connection between laboratory measurements of neutron-rich nuclei and neutron star astrophysics, building on prior calculations by Hebeler et al. (2013) using chiral perturbation theory and by Carlson et al. using Monte Carlo methods, both of which found sensitivity to three-neutron forces. The TOV-min functional improves upon earlier functionals such as SV-min, which had large uncertainties in neutron star mass-radius predictions, and follows the approach of Chamel et al. (2011) who selected the BSk21 functional by testing against both finite nuclei and microscopic neutron matter calculations. The results enable future measurements of the neutron radius of 48Ca, where microscopic coupled cluster calculations are feasible, and allow predictions for the location of drip lines and properties of very neutron rich heavy nuclei.
