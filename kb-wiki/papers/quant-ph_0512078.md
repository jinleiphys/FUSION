> The concept of decoherence is defined, and discussed in a historical context. This is illustrated by some of its essential consequences which may be relevant for the interpretation of quantum theory. Various aspects of the formalism are also reviewed for this purpose. # Roots and Fruits of Decoherence

## Key claim

Decoherence is defined as the dynamical dislocalization of quantum mechanical superpositions through entanglement with an environment, and while it explains the appearance of classicality and superselection rules, it does not by itself solve the measurement problem by explaining the stochastic nature of measurements from a universal Schrödinger equation without additional assumptions.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of the decoherence concept, tracing its roots from nuclear physics examples such as time-dependent Hartree-Fock approximations and deformed nuclei described by superpositions of rotated determinants. It then analyzes the quantum-to-classical transition through environment-induced superselection rules, and presents a formalism in which observables are derived from state vectors and their inner products rather than introduced as fundamental concepts, showing how decoherence explains the emergence of a classical center in the algebraic structure of quantum theory.

## Context

This paper extends Zeh's earlier work from the 1960s and 1970s on decoherence and superselection rules (Zeh 1968, Zeh 1986-88), which itself grew out of nuclear physics methods for describing deformed nuclei. The work contrasts with the Copenhagen interpretation as represented by Ulfbeck and Bohr (2005), who deny the wave function's meaning after measurement, and with collapse models suggested by Omnès (2005). The paper enables a consistent quantum cosmology without fundamental classical concepts, building on the Wheeler-DeWitt equation and the Everett interpretation, and is complementary to the experimental confirmations of decoherence by Haroche (2005) and Zeilinger (2005) and the theoretical treatments by Zurek (2003) and Joos (2003).
