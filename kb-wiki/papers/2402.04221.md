# Classical and Quantum Computing of Shear Viscosity for $2+1D$ SU(2) Gauge Theory

> We perform a nonperturbative calculation of the shear viscosity for $(2+1)$-dimensional SU(2) gauge theory by using the lattice Hamiltonian formulation. The retarded Green's function of the stress-energy tensor is calculated from real time evolution via exact diagonalization of the lattice Hamiltonian with a local Hilbert space truncation, and the shear viscosity is obtained via the Kubo formula. When taking the continuum limit, we account for the renormalization group flow of the coupling but no additional operator renormalization. We find the ratio of the shear viscosity and the entropy density $\fracη{s}$ is consistent with a well-known holographic result $\frac{1}{4π}$ at several temperatures on a $4\times4$ honeycomb lattice with the local electric representation truncated at $j_{\rm max}=\frac{1}{2}$. We also find the ratio of the spectral function and frequency $\frac{ρ^{xy}(ω)}ω$ exhibits a peak structure when the frequency is small. Both the exact diagonalization method and simple matrix product state classical simulation method beyond $j_{\rm max}=\frac{1}{2}$ on bigger lattices require exponentially growing resources. So we develop a quantum computing method to calculate the retarded Green's function and analyze various systematics of the calculation including $j_{\rm max}$ truncation and finite size effects, Trotter errors and the thermal state preparation efficiency. Our thermal state preparation method still requires resources that grow exponentially with the lattice size, but with a very small prefactor at high temperature. We test our quantum circuit on both the Quantinuum emulator and the IBM simulator for a small lattice and obtain results consistent with the classical computing ones.

## Key claim

The ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density \(\eta/s\) for \(2+1D\) SU(2) gauge theory, calculated nonperturbatively via the Kubo formula from real-time evolution on a \(4\times4\) honeycomb lattice with local Hilbert space truncation at \(j_{\rm max}=1/2\), is consistent with the holographic bound \(1/(4\pi)\) at several temperatures, but only at tree-level matching (neglecting long-time tail corrections that make \(\eta\) scale-dependent in two spatial dimensions).

## Method

The shear viscosity was computed from the retarded Green's function of the stress-energy tensor \(T^{xy}\) using the Kubo formula, with real-time evolution obtained via exact diagonalization of the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian on a honeycomb lattice. The lattice Hamiltonian was truncated to the \(j_{\rm max}=1/2\) electric representation, mapping the theory to a 2D Ising-like spin model, and the continuum limit was taken by following the renormalization group flow of the coupling without additional operator renormalization. A quantum algorithm was also developed, using imaginary-time propagation for thermal state preparation, Trotter-decomposed real-time evolution, and a commutator evaluation circuit based on Pauli string rotations, tested on Quantinuum and IBM simulators for a small lattice.

## Key numbers

* \(\eta/s\) is consistent with \(1/(4\pi)\) at several temperatures on a \(4\times4\) honeycomb lattice with \(j_{\rm max}=1/2\).
* The spectral function ratio \(\rho^{xy}(\omega)/\omega\) exhibits a peak structure at small frequency.
* The thermal state preparation success probability at \(T=0\) has a lower bound of \(1/2^{n_s}\), where \(n_s\) is the number of system qubits.
* The rescaled coupling obeys \({\rm d}\ln(ag^2)/{\rm d}\ln a = 1\), meaning \(ag^2 \propto a\) in the continuum limit.

## Context

This work extends nonperturbative transport calculations for gauge theories beyond Euclidean lattice methods (Meyer, 2007; Mages, 2015; Altenkort, 2022), which suffer from an ill-defined inverse problem when extracting real-time quantities from imaginary-time correlation functions. It builds on the Hamiltonian formulation of lattice gauge theory and prior quantum algorithm proposals for stress-energy tensor operators and thermal state preparation (Cohen, 2021). The results enable future quantum computations of shear viscosity in larger lattices and with higher truncations, though the thermal state preparation method still requires resources that grow exponentially with lattice size, with a small prefactor only at high temperature.
