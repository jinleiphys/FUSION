# Axion-Polaritons in the Magnetic Dual Chiral Density Wave Phase of Dense QCD

> We investigate the propagation of electromagnetic radiation in the magnetic dual chiral density wave (MDCDW) phase of dense quark matter. Considering the theory of low-energy fluctuations in this phase, we show how linearly polarized photons reaching the MDCDW medium couple to the fluctuation field to produce two hybridized modes of propagation that we call in analogy with similar phenomenon in condensed matter physics axion polaritons, one of them being gapless and the other gapped. The gapped mode's gap is proportional to the background magnetic field and inversely proportional to the amplitude of the inhomogeneous condensate. The generation of axion polaritons can be traced back to the presence of the chiral anomaly in the low-energy theory of the fluctuations. Considering the Primakoff effect in the MDCDW medium, we argued that axion polaritons can be generated inside quark stars bombarded by energetic photons coming from gamma-ray bursts and point out that this mechanism could serve to explain the missing pulsar paradox in the galaxy center.

# Axion-Polaritons in the Magnetic Dual Chiral Density Wave Phase of Dense QCD

## Key claim

Linearly polarized photons entering the magnetic dual chiral density wave (MDCDW) medium couple to the phonon fluctuation field via the chiral anomaly, producing two hybridized propagation modes called axion polaritons, one gapless and one gapped, with the gap proportional to the background magnetic field and inversely proportional to the amplitude of the inhomogeneous condensate; this mechanism could serve to explain the missing pulsar paradox in the galaxy center, but only if the interior of magnetars hosts quarks in the MDCDW phase and sufficient gamma-ray photons from bursts reach the star.

## Method

The authors start from the low-energy Lagrangian density of phonon fluctuations in the MDCDW phase, expressed in terms of an axion field proportional to the phonon fluctuation, and add the electromagnetic action including the chiral anomaly term (kappa/8) theta F_{mu nu} tilde{F}^{mu nu} that couples the axion field to the electromagnetic field. They linearize the coupled field equations for a static uniform background magnetic field and an electromagnetic wave with electric field parallel to that background field, then solve the resulting dispersion relation to find the hybridized eigenmodes. The quadratic Lagrangian density is diagonalized via a unitary transformation with a momentum-dependent mixing angle to obtain the axion-polariton eigenfields and their conserved Noether current.

## Key numbers

- The gap of the gapped axion-polariton mode is delta = alpha B_0 / (pi m), where alpha is the fine-structure constant, B_0 is the background magnetic field, and m is the amplitude of the inhomogeneous chiral condensate.
- For a magnetic field value of B ~ 10^17 G, the gap delta is in the range [0.06, 0.5] MeV, with m in the range [23.5, 2.8] MeV for intermediate baryonic densities rho ~ 3 rho_s (where rho_s is nuclear saturation density).
- Gamma-ray burst events have energy output ~ 10^56 - 10^59 MeV, with photon energies of order 0.1 - 1 MeV.
- Theoretical predictions indicate there should be more than 10^3 active radio pulsars within 10 pc of the galactic center.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Ferrer and de la Incera (2019) on the low-energy fluctuation theory of the MDCDW phase, which showed that the magnetic field prevents the Landau-Peierls instability that affects single-modulated condensates in the DCDW phase studied by Tatsumi and others. The paper builds on the topological transport properties of the MDCDW phase derived using Fujikawa's method (Topological-Transport-1, Topological-Transport-2), and draws an analogy with axion polaritons found in topological magnetic insulators in condensed matter physics. The results enable future investigation of how gamma-ray bombardment of quark stars with MDCDW cores could produce enough gapped axion polaritons to reach the Chandrasekhar limit and destroy pulsars, offering an alternative explanation for the missing pulsar problem in the galactic center.
