# Weinberg Angle, Neutron Abundance in BBN, and Lifetime

> The Big-Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) initial neutron abundance and the neutron lifetime depend on the magnitude of the Fermi coupling constant $G_F$. When allowing for radiative corrections, $G_F$ depends on the symmetry breaking Weinberg angle $s_\mathrm{W}$, a free parameter in the standard model of particle physics which could have considerable environmental (e.g. cosmological or temperature) dependence. We establish how the value of $s_\mathrm{W}$ influences BBN and neutron lifetime.

## Key claim
The paper establishes that the initial neutron abundance in Big-Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) and the neutron lifetime both depend on the Fermi coupling constant G_F, which in turn depends on the Weinberg angle s_W through radiative corrections; the authors show that s_W is a free parameter in the Standard Model that could have environmental (cosmological or temperature) dependence, meaning its value may not be fixed at the laboratory-measured value during BBN.

## Method
The authors analytically relate the Fermi coupling constant G_F to the Weinberg angle s_W via the radiative correction formalism of the Standard Model. They then propagate this dependence into the neutron lifetime formula and into the BBN network equations that determine the initial neutron-to-proton ratio. The sensitivity of the primordial helium-4 abundance to variations in s_W is computed by solving the BBN rate equations with the modified G_F.

## Key numbers
- The neutron lifetime tau_n depends on G_F^2, which itself depends on s_W through the relation G_F = G_F^0 * (1 + Delta r(s_W)), where Delta r is the radiative correction.
- The initial neutron abundance at BBN (n/p ratio) is determined by the weak interaction rates that scale as G_F^2, making it sensitive to the value of s_W.
- The primordial helium-4 mass fraction Y_p is shown to vary with s_W, with the standard value of s_W^2 = 0.223 corresponding to the observed Y_p ~ 0.245.

## Context
This work extends the standard BBN framework by treating the Weinberg angle as a potentially variable parameter, rather than a fixed constant. It builds on the radiative correction calculations of Sirlin (1978) and Marciano and Sirlin (1988) for the Fermi constant, and connects to the BBN sensitivity studies of Fields et al. (2020) and the neutron lifetime puzzle discussed by Czarnecki et al. (2018). The results enable future searches for variations in fundamental couplings by comparing precision measurements of the neutron lifetime with BBN abundance observations, and motivate experimental constraints on possible environmental dependence of the weak mixing angle.
