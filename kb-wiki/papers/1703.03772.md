# How Well Do We Know The Supernova Equation of State?

> We give an overview about equations of state (EOS) which are currently available for simulations of core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers. A few selected important aspects of the EOS, such as the symmetry energy, the maximum mass of neutron stars, and cluster formation, are confronted with constraints from experiments and astrophysical observations. There are just very few models which are compatible even with this very restricted set of constraints. These remaining models illustrate the uncertainty of the uniform nuclear matter EOS at high densities. In addition, at finite temperatures the medium modifications of nuclear clusters represent a conceptual challenge. In conclusion, there has been significant development in the recent years, but there is still need for further improved general purpose EOS tables.

# How Well Do We Know The Supernova Equation of State?

## Key claim
Only three of seventeen nucleonic general-purpose equations of state (EOS) pass both the symmetry-energy constraint from Lattimer and Lim (2013) and the neutron-star maximum-mass constraint from pulsar observations, and only one EOS that includes additional degrees of freedom (BHBΛφ) satisfies both constraints simultaneously, illustrating that current supernova EOS tables remain highly uncertain and incomplete.

## Method
The authors compiled the 17 available nucleonic general-purpose EOS tables and 23 EOS tables that include additional degrees of freedom (hyperons, pions, quarks), all based on mean-field interactions and phenomenological descriptions of nuclei. They compared each EOS against experimental constraints on the symmetry energy at saturation density (J and L parameters) from Lattimer and Lim (2013), the observed neutron-star maximum mass lower limit of 1.97 solar masses from Antoniadis (2013), and experimental cluster yields from heavy-ion collisions measured by Qin et al. (2012).

## Key numbers
- Symmetry energy constraint from Lattimer and Lim (2013): J = 29.0–32.7 MeV and L = 40.5–61.9 MeV at saturation density
- Maximum mass lower limit from Antoniadis (2013): 1.97 solar masses (1-sigma)
- Only 3 of 17 nucleonic general-purpose EOSs pass both constraints: HS(DD2), SFHo, and SHO(FSU2.1)
- Maximum masses of excluded nucleonic EOSs: LS180 at 1.84 solar masses, HS(IUFSU) at 1.95 solar masses, HS(FSU) at 1.74 solar masses, SHO(FSU) at 1.75 solar masses
- Only 4 EOSs with additional degrees of freedom have maximum masses above 1.97 solar masses: STOSQ139s, STOSQ145s, STOSπ, and BHBΛφ
- BHBΛφ is the only EOS with additional degrees of freedom that passes both constraints simultaneously
- General-purpose EOS tables must cover temperatures 0 to 100 MeV, densities 10^4 to 10^15 g/cm^3, and electron fractions 0 to 0.6

## Context
This review extends the analysis of available supernova EOSs by applying multiple constraints simultaneously, building on the compilation of EOS tables from Hempel (2010), Shen (2011), and Steiner (2013). The work identifies that most existing EOSs fail when confronted with the symmetry-energy constraints from Lattimer and Lim (2013) and the maximum-mass observations from Antoniadis (2013) and Demorest (2010). The remaining viable models (HS(DD2), SFHo, SHO(FSU2.1)) illustrate the current uncertainty in the high-density nucleon interaction, and the absence of any EOS that simultaneously satisfies constraints while including pions or quarks points to a clear need for new, improved general-purpose EOS tables.
