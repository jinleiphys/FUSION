# Electromagnetic Properties of Indium Isotopes Elucidate the Doubly Magic Character of $^{100}$Sn

> Our understanding of nuclear properties in the vicinity of $^{100}$Sn, suggested to be the heaviest doubly magic nucleus with equal numbers of protons (Z=50) and neutrons (N=50), has been a long-standing challenge for experimental and theoretical nuclear physics. Contradictory experimental evidence exists on the role of nuclear collectivity in this region of the nuclear chart. Using precision laser spectroscopy, we measured the ground-state electromagnetic moments of indium (Z=49) isotopes approaching the N=50 neutron number down to 101In, and nuclear charge radii of 101-131In spanning almost the complete range between the two major neutron closed-shells at N=50 and N=82. Our results for both nuclear charge radii and quadrupole moments reveal striking parabolic trends as a function of the neutron number, with a clear reduction toward these two neutron closed-shells, thus supporting a doubly magic character of $^{100}$Sn. Two complementary nuclear many-body frameworks, density functional theory and ab initio methods, elucidate our findings. A detailed comparison with our experimental results exposes deficiencies of nuclear models, establishing a benchmark for future theoretical developments.

## Key claim
The paper provides strong experimental evidence for the doubly magic character of $^{100}$Sn by measuring electromagnetic properties of indium isotopes, but the claim is hedged because $^{100}$Sn itself was not directly measured, the authors infer its doubly magic nature from the clear reduction in quadrupole moments and charge radii as neutron number approaches N=50 from above, down to $^{101}$In (N=52).

## Method
Precision collinear resonance ionization spectroscopy (CRIS) at CERN-ISOLDE was used to measure hyperfine structures and isotope shifts of $^{101-131}$In isotopes produced via 1.4 GeV proton-induced reactions in lanthanum carbide (for $^{101-115}$In) or uranium carbide (for $^{113-131}$In) targets. From these spectra, the authors extracted nuclear magnetic dipole moments, spectroscopic quadrupole moments $Q_s$, and differential mean-square charge radii $\delta\langle r^2\rangle$, then compared these experimental results with predictions from two complementary many-body frameworks: valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) ab initio calculations and nuclear density functional theory (DFT) using Skyrme and Fayans functionals.

## Key numbers
- Spectroscopic quadrupole moments $Q_s$ were measured for $^{101-115}$In (this work) and combined with $^{117-131}$In data from Ver22; the $Q_s$ values at N=50+2 and N=82-2 agree within one combined standard deviation, indicating similar proton core polarization at both closed shells.
- Differential mean-square nuclear charge radii $\delta\langle r^2\rangle$ were measured for $^{101-131}$In relative to stable $^{115}$In, spanning almost the complete range between N=50 and N=82.
- The residual differential charge radii $\delta\langle r^2_{\rm{res}}\rangle$ show a clear reduction toward N=50, with the trend established down to $^{101}$In (N=52).
- The electric-field gradient used for $Q_s$ extraction was calculated as 57,600(400) $e\cdot$fm$^2$/MHz from Gar18.

## Context
This work extends previous laser spectroscopy studies of indium isotopes by Eberz et al. (1987) which were limited to N$\geq$56, and complements $B(E2)$ measurements in tin isotopes that had large uncertainties for neutron-deficient cases (Rad04, Rad05; Gorska et al. 2019). The results resolve contradictory experimental evidence on nuclear collectivity near $^{100}$Sn by establishing a clear reduction in both quadrupole moments and charge radii toward N=50. The detailed comparison with VS-IMSRG and DFT calculations exposes deficiencies in current nuclear models, particularly the underestimation of quadrupole collectivity by ab initio methods and the need for improved treatment of pairing and deformation effects in DFT, establishing a benchmark for future theoretical developments and motivating direct measurements of $^{100}$Sn at next-generation rare-isotope facilities such as FRIB, RIKEN, and SPIRAL2.
