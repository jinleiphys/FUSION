# Probing neutron star interiors and the properties of cold ultra-dense matter with the SKAO

> Matter inside neutron stars is compressed to densities several times greater than nuclear saturation density, while maintaining low temperatures and large asymmetries between neutrons and protons. Neutron stars, therefore, provide a unique laboratory for testing physics in environments that cannot be recreated on Earth. To uncover the highly uncertain nature of cold, ultra-dense matter, discovering and monitoring pulsars is essential, and the SKA will play a crucial role in this endeavour. In this paper, we will present the current state-of-the-art in dense matter physics and dense matter superfluidity, and discuss recent advances in measuring global neutron star properties (masses, moments of inertia, and maximum rotation frequencies) as well as non-global observables (pulsar glitches and free precession). We will specifically highlight how radio observations of isolated neutron stars and those in binaries -- such as those performed with the SKA in the near future -- inform our understanding of ultra-dense physics and address in detail how SKAO's telescopes unprecedented sensitivity, large-scale survey and sub-arraying capabilities will enable novel dense matter constraints. We will also address the potential impact of dark matter and modified gravity models on these constraints and emphasise the role of synergies between the SKA and other facilities, specifically X-ray telescopes and next-generation gravitational wave observatories.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) will provide unprecedented constraints on the properties of cold ultra-dense matter inside neutron stars, but it acknowledges that even precise measurements of the equation of state may not be sufficient to unambiguously determine the composition of matter at the centre of the most massive neutron stars without additional information from transport properties, glitches, or oscillation modes.

## Method

The paper reviews the current state-of-the-art in dense matter physics and neutron star superfluidity, then examines five classes of neutron star observables accessible through radio pulsar timing: masses, moments of inertia, maximum rotation frequencies, pulsar glitches, and free precession. It evaluates how SKA observations of isolated neutron stars and those in binaries will constrain the equation of state and superfluid properties, and discusses synergies with X-ray telescopes and next-generation gravitational wave observatories.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density is approximately $2 \times 10^{14} \, \text{g/cm}^3$, with neutron star core densities reaching up to ten times this value
- Neutron drip density in the inner crust begins at $4 \times 10^{11} \, \text{g/cm}^3$
- Neutron stars cool to temperatures around $10^9 \, \text{K}$ within months to years after formation
- The most massive neutron star determined via pulsar timing is PSR J0740+6620 with a mass of $2.08 \pm 0.07 \, M_\odot$
- PSR J1614-2230 has a mass of $1.97 \pm 0.04 \, M_\odot$
- Chiral effective field theory calculations are reliable for low-density neutron matter up to around $1.5$ times nuclear saturation density
- Perturbative QCD calculations apply at very high densities above approximately $40$ times nuclear saturation density
- 61 neutron star mass measurements exist with relative 1-sigma uncertainty of less than 15 percent
- Black widow spider systems have companion masses below $0.05 \, M_\odot$, while redbacks have companion masses above $0.1 \, M_\odot$

## Context

This white paper extends the work of Lattimer and Prakash (2001) on neutron star structure and equation of state modelling, and builds upon recent Bayesian inference approaches such as those by Char (2023) and Montefusco (2025) for constraining the equation of state from observational data. The paper specifically addresses how the SKA will improve upon current constraints from NICER X-ray pulse profile modelling of PSR J0740+6620 (Salmi 2024) and from gravitational wave events detected by LIGO and Virgo, while also discussing how to disentangle dense matter effects from potential dark matter and modified gravity contributions.
