# Speed of sound constraints from tidal deformability of neutron stars

> The upper bound of the speed of sound in dense nuclear matter is one of the most interesting but still unsolved problems in Nuclear Physics. Theoretical studies in connection with recent observational data of isolated neutron stars as well as binary neutron stars systems offer an excellent opportunity to shed light on this problem. In the present work, we suggest a method to directly relate the measured tidal deformability (polarizability) of binary neutron stars system (before merger) to the maximum neutron star mass scenario and possible upper bound on the speed of sound. This method is based on the simple but efficient idea that while the upper limit of the effective tidal deformability favors soft equations of state, the recent high measured values of neutron star mass favor stiff ones. In the present work, firstly, using a simple well established model we parametrize the stiffness of the equation of state with the help of the speed of sound. Secondly, in comparison with the recent observations by LIGO/VIRGO collaboration of two events, GW170817 and GW190425, we suggest possible robust constraints. Moreover, we evaluate and postulate, in the framework of the present method, what kind of future measurements could help us to improve the stringent of the constraints on the neutron star equation of state.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that combining constraints from the upper limit of effective tidal deformability (which favors soft equations of state) with measurements of high neutron star masses (which favor stiff equations of state) can yield robust constraints on the speed of sound in dense nuclear matter, but the method depends on the specific transition density at which the speed of sound bound is applied and on which gravitational-wave event is used.

## Method

The authors parametrize the neutron star equation of state using a piecewise model where the high-density region above a transition density \(n_{\rm tr}\) is constructed with a fixed speed of sound, either \(v_s = c/\sqrt{3}\) or \(v_s = c\), while the low-density region uses a known hadronic EoS (MDI model based on Akmal et al. (1998)). They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations to obtain mass-radius relations and compute the tidal deformability \(\Lambda\) and effective tidal deformability \(\tilde{\Lambda}\) for binary systems, then compare these predictions with observational constraints from the gravitational-wave events GW170817 and GW190425.

## Key numbers

- The upper limit on effective tidal deformability for GW170817 is \(\tilde{\Lambda} \leq 720\) (after reanalysis, with chirp mass \(\mathcal{M}_c = 1.186 M_\odot\)) at the 90% confidence level.
- The upper limit on effective tidal deformability for GW190425 is \(\tilde{\Lambda} \leq 600\) (with chirp mass \(\mathcal{M}_c = 1.44 M_\odot\)).
- The transition densities studied are \(n_{\rm tr} = 1, 1.5, 2, 3\) times the saturation density \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- Observed maximum neutron star masses used include \(2.01 \pm 0.04 M_\odot\) (Antoniadis 2013), \(2.14^{+0.10}_{-0.09} M_\odot\) (Cromartie 2019), and \(2.27^{+0.17}_{-0.15} M_\odot\) (Linares 2018).
- For GW170817, the \(\tilde{\Lambda} \leq 720\) constraint excludes both speed of sound cases for transition densities \(n_{\rm tr} = 1 n_0\) and \(n_{\rm tr} = 1.5 n_0\).
- For GW190425, the \(\tilde{\Lambda} \leq 600\) constraint excludes only the EoS with transition density \(n_{\rm tr} = 1 n_0\) for both speed of sound cases.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Bedaque and Steiner (2015), who argued that the existence of two-solar-mass neutron stars is inconsistent with the \(c/\sqrt{3}\) bound, and builds on the parametrization method of Tews (2018) for smooth speed-of-sound matching. The method enables future gravitational-wave observations to further constrain the speed of sound and transition density, with the authors noting that lower-mass binary neutron star mergers (like GW170817) provide more stringent constraints than higher-mass mergers (like GW190425).
