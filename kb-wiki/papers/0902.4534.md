# Nuclear Physics with Electroweak Probes

> In recent years, the italian theoretical Nuclear Physics community has played a leading role in the development of a unified approach, allowing for a consistent and fully quantitative description of the nuclear response to electromagnetic and weak probes. In this paper I review the main achievements in both fields, point out some of the open problems, and outline the most promising prospects.

## Key claim

The Italian theoretical nuclear physics community has developed a unified approach for a consistent and fully quantitative description of nuclear responses to both electromagnetic and weak probes, but the paper acknowledges that a quantitative understanding of final state interactions (FSI) is required to unambiguously identify correlation effects in inclusive electron scattering, and that the assumption of FSI cancellation underlying the analysis of cross section ratios remains controversial.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches to nuclear response functions, including the spectral function formalism for describing nucleon momentum distributions, the convolution model incorporating FSI effects through a folding function related to nuclear transparency, and the superscaling analysis that relates scaling functions across different nuclei. For neutrino-nucleus scattering, the author compares the Relativistic Fermi Gas Model (RFGM) with approaches based on realistic spectral functions, and validates nuclear models by comparing theoretical results to electron scattering data from SLAC, JLab, Frascati, NIKHEF-K, and MAMI.

- Fermi energy: approximately 30 MeV

## Context

This review extends the long-standing work of the Italian theoretical nuclear physics community on electron-nucleus scattering, particularly the spectral function approach developed by Benhar (1989, 1994) and the convolution formalism for FSI by Benhar (1991). It directly addresses the systematic uncertainties in neutrino oscillation experiments (NUINT workshops starting 2001) and the need to improve upon the RFGM currently used by experiments like K2K and MiniBooNE. The superscaling analysis builds on the work of Donnelly (1999) and Sick (1980) on y-scaling, and enables more accurate predictions of neutrino-nucleus cross sections for ongoing long-baseline oscillation experiments, while also connecting to astrophysical applications such as supernova simulations and neutron star cooling.
