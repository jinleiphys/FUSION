# Neutrino Emission from Goldstone Modes in Dense Quark Matter

> We calculate neutrino emissivities from the decay and scattering of Goldstone bosons in the color-flavor-locked (CFL) phase of quarks at high baryon density. Interactions in the CFL phase are described by an effective low-energy theory. For temperatures in the tens of keV range, relevant to the long-term cooling of neutron stars, the emissivities involving Goldstone bosons dominate over those involving quarks, because gaps in the CFL phase are $\sim 100$ MeV while the masses of Goldstone modes are on the order of 10 MeV. For the same reason, the specific heat of the CFL phase is also dominated by the Goldstone modes. Notwithstanding this, both the emissivity and the specific heat from the massive modes remain rather small, because of their extremely small number densities. The values of the emissivity and the specific heat imply that the timescale for the cooling of the CFL core in isolation is $\sim 10^{26}$ y, which makes the CFL phase invisible as the exterior layers of normal matter surrounding the core will continue to cool through significantly more rapid processes. If the CFL phase appears during the evolution of a proto-neutron star, neutrino interactions with Goldstone bosons are expected to be significantly more important since temperatures are high enough ($\sim 20-40$ MeV) to admit large number densities of Goldstone modes.

## Key claim

Neutrino emissivities from Goldstone boson decay and scattering in the color-flavor-locked (CFL) phase dominate over those from quarks at temperatures in the tens of keV range relevant to long-term neutron star cooling, but both the emissivity and specific heat remain very small due to extremely low number densities of the Goldstone modes, implying a cooling timescale of approximately 10^26 years for an isolated CFL core.

## Method

The authors constructed an effective low-energy chiral Lagrangian for the CFL phase, coupled it to electroweak gauge fields via minimal coupling, and computed neutrino emissivities from charged pion decay (π± → l± + ν̄l), neutral pion decay (π0 → ν + ν̄), kaon decay (K± → l± + ν̄l), and Goldstone boson scattering (φ+φ → φ+ν+ν̄). The calculations were performed in the low-temperature limit (T ≪ mπ) using Boltzmann statistics for the pions and neglecting Pauli blocking for outgoing neutrinos, with the pion decay constant fπ computed by matching the chiral effective theory to a microscopic theory of quasiparticles near the Fermi surface.

## Key numbers

- Emissivity from charged pion decay: ε_π = 1.18 × 10^28 μ_100^2 m_10^2 (m_10 T_9)^(3/2) exp(-116 m_10 / T_9) [1 + 1.14 (m_π/m_e)(T/m_e)] erg cm^(-3) s^(-1), where μ_100 is quark chemical potential in units of 100 MeV, m_10 is pion mass in units of 10 MeV, and T_9 = T/10^9 K
- Emissivity from neutral pion decay (one neutrino flavor): ε_νν̄ = 4.52 × 10^28 μ_100^2 m_10^2 (m_10 T_9)^(5/2) exp(-116 m_10 / T_9) erg cm^(-3) s^(-1)
- Emissivity from kaon decay: ε_K = 3.38 × 10^26 μ_100^2 E_10^2 (m_10 T_9)^(3/2) exp(-116 E_10 / T_9) [1 + 2(δf_π/f_π ± μ_eff/E_K±) + (2 m_K± T / v_K^2 m_e^2)(δf_π/f_π ± μ_eff/E_K±)^2] erg cm^(-3) s^(-1), where E_10 is kaon energy in units of 10 MeV
- Emissivity from massless Goldstone boson scattering: ε_GB ≈ 10^(-11) × T_9^15 μ_100^(-6) erg cm^(-3) s^(-1)
- Cooling timescale for isolated CFL core: ∼ 10^26 years
- Quark gaps in CFL phase: ∼ 100 MeV
- Goldstone boson masses: ∼ 10 MeV
- Pion decay constant: f_π^2 = [(21 - 8 ln 2)/18] (μ^2 / 2π^2)
- Goldstone boson velocity in weak coupling limit: v_π^2 = 1/3
- Proto-neutron star temperatures where Goldstone modes become important: ∼ 20-40 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutrino emission from color superconducting quark matter by Blaschke (2000) and Prakash (2001), which considered processes involving gapped quarks and found that large quark gaps render quark matter invisible in cooling. The authors show that at low temperatures (tens of keV), Goldstone boson decays dominate over the quark direct Urca process (Iwamoto, 1980) and quark pair-breaking and formation processes (Jaikumar, 2002), but the overall emissivity remains too small to affect observable neutron star cooling because the exterior normal matter layers cool much faster. The results enable future work on neutrino interactions with Goldstone bosons in proto-neutron star evolution, where temperatures of 20-40 MeV allow large number densities of Goldstone modes.
