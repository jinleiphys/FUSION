# Power laws in elementary and heavy-ion collisions - A story of fluctuations and nonextensivity?

> We review from the point of view of nonextensive statistics the ubiquitous presence in elementary and heavy-ion collisions of power-law distributions. Special emphasis is placed on the conjecture that this is just a reflection of some intrinsic fluctuations existing in the hadronic systems considered. These systems summarily described by a single parameter q playing the role of a nonextensivity measure in the nonextensive statistical models based on Tsallis entropy.

# Power laws in elementary and heavy-ion collisions - A story of fluctuations and nonextensivity?

## Key claim

The ubiquitous power-law distributions observed in elementary and heavy-ion collisions can be understood as a consequence of intrinsic fluctuations in the hadronizing system, described by the nonextensivity parameter q in Tsallis statistics, rather than requiring separate dynamical explanations for each departure from Boltzmann-Gibbs behavior. The authors propose that the parameter q is directly related to the normalized variance of temperature fluctuations (q = 1 + ω) or, in the longitudinal phase space, to fluctuations of the mean multiplicity.

## Method

The authors review fits of the Tsallis q-exponential distribution, exp_q(-X/λ) = [1 - (1-q)X/λ]^(1/(1-q)), to single-particle distributions in both transverse momentum (p_T) and rapidity (y) from elementary (pp, p̄p, e⁺e⁻) and heavy-ion (Pb+Pb, Au+Au, S+S) collisions. They derive the connection between q and temperature fluctuations using a Langevin equation with multiplicative noise, showing that a gamma-distributed fluctuating temperature leads to the Tsallis form. For longitudinal distributions, they compare the extracted q values with the parameter 1/k from Negative Binomial multiplicity distributions.

## Key numbers

- For p_T spectra in p̄p collisions (UA1), the fitted parameters (T_T [GeV], q_T) are: (0.134, 1.095) at 200 GeV, (0.135, 1.105) at 500 GeV, and (0.14, 1.11) at 900 GeV.
- For rapidity distributions in the same p̄p collisions, the corresponding (T_L [GeV], q_L) values are: (11.74, 1.2) at 200 GeV, (20.39, 1.26) at 500 GeV, and (30.79, 1.29) at 900 GeV.
- For e⁺e⁻ annihilations at 91.2 GeV, the rapidity distribution is fitted with q = 0.6 and K_q = 1.
- For Pb+Pb collisions (NA49, π⁻ production), the fitted (q; K_q) values are: (1.2; 0.33) at 17.3 GeV, (1.164; 0.3) at 12.3 GeV, and (1.04; 0.22) at 8.6 GeV per nucleon.
- For Au+Au collisions (PHOBOS, most central events), the fitted q values are 1.29 at 19.6 GeV, 1.26 at 130 GeV, and 1.27 at 200 GeV.
- For S+S collisions, the deviation from exponential is very small with |q-1| ~ 0.015, corresponding to relative temperature fluctuations ΔT/T ~ 0.12.
- The relation between q and the Negative Binomial parameter k is given by q = 1 + 1/k, where 1/k = -0.104 + 0.058·ln √s.

## Context

This review extends the authors' earlier work (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2000) connecting the Tsallis parameter q to fluctuations, and the interpretation of q in terms of superstatistics (Beck, 2001). It contrasts with purely dynamical explanations for power-law tails such as resonance production and flow (cited as "dynamics"). The work enables using single-particle distributions to extract not only the temperature T of the hadronizing system but also its total heat capacity C via the relation q - 1 = 1/C, which could provide information on phase transitions such as the Quark Gluon Plasma formation.
