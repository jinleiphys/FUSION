# Evolution to the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> Theoretical studies on the early-time dynamics in the ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions are reviewed including pedagogical introductions on the initial condition with small-x gluons treated as a color glass condensate, the bottom-up thermalization scenario, plasma/glasma instabilities, basics of some formulations such as the kinetic equations and the classical statistical simulation. More detailed discussions follow to make an overview of recent developments on the fast isotropization, the onset of hydrodynamics, and the transient behavior of momentum spectral cascades.

## Key claim

This review argues that the missing link in understanding Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) formation is the bridge between the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) initial condition and the hydrodynamic description, but it hedges that the thermalization problem remains unsolved because the system's evolution involves three distinct but related processes (isotropization, hydrodynamization, and spectral cascades) whose interplay is not fully understood, and that almost all simulations require unphysically long times, hinting that something important may be still missing.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches to early-time dynamics in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, starting with the CGC effective theory where soft gluons are described by classical Yang-Mills fields sourced by a color charge distribution. It then examines the bottom-up thermalization scenario, plasma/glasma instabilities, and various theoretical formulations including kinetic equations (Boltzmann equation in relaxation time approximation) and classical statistical simulations (solving classical equations of motion with Wigner functions). The review also covers recent developments in fast isotropization, the onset of hydrodynamics (including the aHydro reformulation), and transient behavior of momentum spectral cascades, with discussions of the Kadanoff-Baym equations, stochastic quantization, and gauge/gravity correspondence.

## Key numbers

- The QCD scale Lambda_QCD is approximately 0.2 GeV (approximately 2 x 10^12 K)
- The saturation scale Q_s(x) is parametrized as Q_s^2(x) = Q_0^2 (x/x_0)^{-lambda} with Q_0 = 1 GeV, x_0 = 3.04 x 10^{-4}, and lambda = 0.288, determined from data at x < 10^{-2}
- For RHIC energy of 200 GeV/nucleon, the relevant x is roughly 10^{-2}; for LHC energy of 5.5 TeV/nucleon, the relevant x is roughly 10^{-3}
- The nuclear thickness scales with A^{1/3}, giving a factor of approximately 6 for gold and lead ions compared to protons
- The collision energy is approximately 27 times increased from RHIC to LHC, making relevant x approximately 1/27 times smaller, increasing Q_s(x) by a factor of approximately 2.6
- The proper time unit is Q_s^{-1} approximately 0.2 fm/c for RHIC and approximately 0.1 fm/c for LHC
- The gluon distribution function is about twenty times larger than the quark distribution function already around x approximately 10^{-2}

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on the bottom-up thermalization scenario by Baier (2000) and the early discussion of thermalization difficulty by Baym (1984) and Kovchegov (2005), while building on the CGC framework developed by McLerran and Venugopalan (1993) and the JIMWLK equation by Iancu (2000) and others. It enables future work on classifying scaling solutions, testing the aHydro formulation by Nopoush (2014), and investigating the speculative scenario of gluonic Bose-Einstein condensate formation, while noting that the most serious problem is the technical difficulty in estimating relevant time scales.
