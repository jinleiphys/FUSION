# Model independent analysis of coupled-channel scattering: a deep learning approach

> We develop a robust method to extract the pole configuration of a given partial-wave amplitude. In our approach, a deep neural network is constructed where the statistical errors of the experimental data are taken into account. The teaching dataset is constructed using a generic S-matrix parametrization, ensuring that all the poles produced are independent of each other. The inclusion of statistical error results into a noisy classification dataset which we should solve using the curriculum method. As an application, we use the elastic $πN$ amplitude in the $I(J^P)=1/2(1/2^{-})$ sector where $10^6$ amplitudes are produced by combining points in each error bar of the experimental data. We fed the amplitudes to the trained deep neural network and find that the enhancements in the $πN$ amplitude are caused by one pole in each nearby unphysical sheet and at most two poles in the distant sheet. Finally, we show that the extracted pole configurations are independent of the way points in each error bar are drawn and combined, demonstrating the statistical robustness of our method.

# Model independent analysis of coupled-channel scattering: a deep learning approach

## Key claim

The enhancements in the elastic πN amplitude in the I(J^P)=1/2(1/2^-) sector are caused by one pole in each nearby unphysical sheet ([bt] and [bb]) and at most two poles in the distant sheet ([tb]), with the extracted pole configuration being independent of how points within each experimental error bar are drawn and combined, provided the confidence level is sufficiently high.

## Method

The authors constructed a deep neural network (DNN) trained on a generic S-matrix parametrization that satisfies analyticity, unitarity, and hermiticity for two-channel scattering (πN and ηN). The training dataset of 1.8×10^6 labeled amplitudes was generated with controlled, independent poles on specified Riemann sheets, using a curriculum learning approach that progressively introduced more complex pole configurations (from at-most-one-pole to at-most-four-pole configurations). For inference, 10^6 amplitudes were produced directly from the GW-SAID experimental data by drawing points within each error bar using Gaussian distributions at various confidence levels, then fed to the trained DNN to identify the most frequent pole configuration.

## Key numbers

* Training dataset: 1.8×10^6 labeled amplitudes divided uniformly into 35 possible pole configurations, with an independent testing set of 3.5×10^4 labeled amplitudes
* Energy range divided into 37 bins (corresponding to 37 data points in the GW-SAID πN data), spanning from the πN threshold to the KΣ threshold
* Counting region for poles: Re(E_pole) from T2-50 MeV to T2+200 MeV for all Riemann sheets; Im(E_pole) from -200 to 0 MeV for [bt] and [bb] sheets, and from 0 to 200 MeV for [tb] sheet
* Final DNN training accuracy: 76.5%; testing accuracy: 80.4%
* For inference: 10^6 amplitudes generated from experimental data by combining points in each error bar using Gaussian distributions

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous deep learning approach to hadron spectroscopy (Sombillo 2020, Sombillo 2021) by incorporating statistical errors directly into the analysis through a curriculum learning method. It contrasts with conventional parameter-fitting approaches such as Breit-Wigner or Flatté parametrizations and K-matrix formalism (Burkert and Lee 2004), which are limited by model assumptions. The method enables model-independent determination of pole configurations from experimental data, providing constraints that can guide subsequent dynamical model calculations (Morgan 1992, Morgan and Pennington 1993, Hyodo 2002, Magas 2005).
