# Patchy nuclear chain reactions

> Stochastic fluctuations of the neutron population within a nuclear reactor are typically prevented by operating the core at a sufficient power, since a deterministic behavior of the neutron population is required by automatic safety systems to detect unwanted power excursions. Recent works however pointed out that, under specific circumstances, non-Poissonian patterns could affect neutron spatial distributions. This motivated an international program to experimentally detect and characterize such fluctuations and correlations, which took place in 2017 at the Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute Reactor Critical Facility. The main findings of this program will indeed unveil patchiness in snapshots of neutron spatial distributions -- obtained with a dedicated numerical twin of the reactor -- that support this first experimental characterization of the 'neutron clustering' phenomenon, while a stochastic model based on reaction-diffusion processes and branching random walks will reveal the key role played by the reactor intrinsic sources in understanding neutron spatial correlations.

## Key claim

The paper claims that neutron clustering and non-Poissonian spatial correlations can be experimentally observed in a low-power nuclear reactor, but this phenomenon is fundamentally shaped by intrinsic neutron sources (spontaneous fission) which prevent the theoretically predicted "critical catastrophe" of unbounded power fluctuations. The transition from stochastic to deterministic behavior cannot be identified by power level alone.

## Method

The authors conducted experiments at the Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute Reactor Critical Facility (RCF) in August 2017, using in-core ³He detectors and ex-core NoMAD detector arrays to measure neutron fluctuations and spatial correlations at powers from 0.6 mW to 100 mW. They built a numerical twin of the reactor using the MORET6 Monte Carlo code with analog neutron transport and the LLNL Fission Library, simulating every individual neutron in the sub-Watt core. A stochastic model based on reaction-diffusion processes and branching random walks was developed to interpret the experimental and numerical results, incorporating intrinsic spontaneous fission sources and delayed neutron effects.

## Key numbers

- The RCF reactor operates with 4.81% enriched UO₂ ceramic fuel, active fuel length of 36 inches, and fuel pin pitch of 0.64 inches, licensed up to 100 Watts.
- For a 10 minute run at P = 0.66 mW, counting rates were compared to a MORET6 simulation at P = 0.79 mW, showing 2-σ agreement between experimental and simulated neutron densities.
- Above P = 1 W, the reactor exhibits a cosine-shaped neutron density; below P = 10 mW, the spatial shape is driven by spontaneous fissions and fitted by an exponentially decaying function.
- The variance-to-mean ratio of detected neutrons follows Var_z/⟨z_∞⟩ ∝ P², leading to σ_z/⟨z_Δt⟩ ∝ √(P/Δt) for time gates larger than the delayed neutron time constant.
- A 1/P^α fit of spatial correlation data gave α = 0.96 ± 0.046 for simulated data and α = 1.11 ± 0.044 for experimental data, consistent with g ∝ 1/P.
- Fluctuations at 0.8 mW (MORET6) were σ_n/⟨n_∞⟩ ≈ 5%, while at 9 mW (RCF) they were ≈ 14%.
- The delayed neutron fraction β is roughly 0.7% for thermal fissions on ²³⁵U.
- ²³⁸U emits 10⁻² n/s/g from spontaneous fissions, 20 times higher than ²³⁵U.

## Context

This work extends theoretical predictions of neutron clustering in low-power reactors (Dumonteil 2014, Zoia 2015) and the "critical catastrophe" concept from Williams (1974) by providing the first experimental characterization of these phenomena. The results contradict the assumption that a clear transition from stochastic to deterministic regimes exists based on power level alone. The findings enable improved modeling of neutron noise in reactor startup and shutdown conditions, and suggest that for power reactors during post-refueling startup, protection systems must be carefully dimensioned to avoid unwanted scramming from power fluctuations that scale as √P.
