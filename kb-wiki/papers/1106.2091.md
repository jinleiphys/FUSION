# Leading twist nuclear shadowing phenomena in hard processes with nuclei

> We present and discuss the theory and phenomenology of the leading twist theory of nuclear shadowing which is based on the combination of the generalization of the Gribov-Glauber theory, QCD factorization theorems, and the HERA QCD analysis of diffraction in lepton-proton deep inelastic scattering (DIS). We apply this technique for the analysis of a wide range of hard processes with nuclei---inclusive DIS on deuterons, medium-range and heavy nuclei, coherent and incoherent diffractive DIS with nuclei, and hard diffraction in proton-nucleus scattering---and make predictions for the effect of nuclear shadowing in the corresponding sea quark and gluon parton distributions. We also analyze the role of the leading twist nuclear shadowing in generalized parton distributions in nuclei and in certain characteristics of final states in nuclear DIS. We discuss the limits of applicability of the leading twist approximation for small x scattering off nuclei and the onset of the black disk regime and methods of detecting it. It will be possible to check many of our predictions in the near future in the studies of the ultraperipheral collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). Further checks will be possible in pA collisions at the LHC and forward hadron production at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC). Detailed tests will be possible at an Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) in the USA and at the Large Hadron-Electron Collider (LHeC) at CERN.

## Key claim

The leading twist approximation for nuclear shadowing in deep inelastic scattering (DIS) breaks down at sufficiently small x due to probability conservation, leading to the onset of a black disk regime (BDR) where the interaction of small-color-singlet dipoles with nucleons and nuclei reaches its geometric limit; however, in the HERA kinematics and at the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), only an onset (approach) to the full BDR may take place, not the full regime.

## Method

The authors combine the generalization of the Gribov-Glauber theory with QCD factorization theorems and the HERA QCD analysis of diffraction in lepton-proton DIS to construct the leading twist theory of nuclear shadowing. They apply this framework to calculate nuclear impact factors for dipole-nucleus scattering using a dipole model that interpolates between perturbative QCD for small dipoles and soft QCD for large dipoles, incorporating the impact parameter dependence through the two-gluon form factor of the nucleon and the nuclear optical density.

## Key numbers

- The effective parameter characterizing the convergence of the pQCD series is η = α_s(Q²_0)(N_c/2π) ln(Q²/Q²_0) ln(x_0/x) ≥ 1, where the series loses its advantage as an asymptotic series
- For a transversally polarized virtual photon in the BDR, F_T(x,Q²,b) ≤ c Q² ln(x_0/x), i.e., only logarithmic growth with energy is allowed
- The integrated structure function bound is F_T(x,Q²) ≤ c Q² ln³(x_0/x)
- The two-gluon form factor of the nucleon is parameterized as F_{2g}(t) = 1/(1-t/m²_g)² with m_{2g} ≈ 1 GeV
- The gluon distribution at zero impact parameter corresponds to a probability of interaction of the color singlet gluon dipole of ≈ 1/2
- The ratio σ_el/σ_tot ≈ 1/2 for the interaction of the color singlet gluon dipole at central impact parameters
- The nuclear impact factor for ²⁰⁸Pb at Q² = 4 GeV² is presented for x = 10⁻⁵, 10⁻⁴, and 10⁻³ and dipole sizes d_⊥ = 0.2, 0.4, and 0.6 fm
- The Casimir operator ratio gives F²(8)/F²(3) = 9/4, meaning two-gluon dipoles reach the BDR at significantly lower energies than q\bar{q} dipoles

## Context

This work extends the leading twist theory of nuclear shadowing developed by the same authors and collaborators, building on the Gribov-Glauber theory and QCD factorization theorems established in earlier work. The analysis of the black disk regime contrasts with the popular hypothesis of saturation (energy independence of parton densities at extremely small x) as discussed by Gelis (2010) and others. The predictions can be tested in ultraperipheral collisions at the LHC, pA collisions at the LHC, forward hadron production at RHIC, and will be further testable at a future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) in the USA and the Large Hadron-Electron Collider (LHeC) at CERN.
