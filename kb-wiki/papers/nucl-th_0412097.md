# Kaon Photoproduction in the Feynman and Regge Theories

> Kaon photoproduction on the nucleon has been investigated in the isobar (Feynman) and Regge frameworks. Two possible combinations of the theories are discussed. Results are compared with present experimental data.

# Kaon Photoproduction in the Feynman and Regge Theories

## Key claim
Combining isobar (Feynman) and Regge models can describe kaon photoproduction data from threshold up to 16 GeV laboratory photon energy, but neither of the two mixing methods tested fully reproduces all observables; the "mixed 2" method (exponential transition) gives a better overall fit (χ²/N = 2.61) but still fails to reproduce recoil polarization structure near W ≈ 1.75 GeV and low-energy backward-angle cross section peaks.

## Method
The authors construct invariant amplitudes A_i for γ + p → K⁺ + Λ using both an isobar (Feynman diagram) model with hadronic form factors and a Regge model where Feynman propagators are replaced by Regge propagators for K and K* trajectories. Two mixing schemes are tested: "mixed 1" uses a linear interpolation between isobar and Regge amplitudes over a transition region from threshold to √s₂ = 2.5 GeV, while "mixed 2" uses an exponential damping with a fitted constant a = 1.73 GeV² to smoothly transition from isobar to Regge dominance.

## Key numbers
- Laboratory photon energy range covered: from threshold up to E_γ^lab = 16 GeV (corresponding to total c.m. energy W ≈ 5 GeV)
- Transition region for "mixed 1": √s₁ = W_thr to √s₂ = 2.5 GeV
- Best-fit mixing parameter for "mixed 2": a = 1.73 GeV², yielding χ²/N = 2.61
- Equivalent Regge contribution in "mixed 2" would require a = 2.74 GeV², giving χ²/N = 2.96
- Recoil polarization shows unresolved structure at W ≈ 1.75 GeV

## Context
This work extends earlier isobar model studies by Mart (2000) and Regge model approaches by Guidal (1997) to cover kaon photoproduction from threshold to high energies, motivated by Jefferson Lab experiments up to W = 3 GeV and proposed 12 GeV upgrades. The mixing procedure follows Barbour (1978) who successfully applied a similar method to single-pion photoproduction. The results enable future investigations of the GDH sum rule and searches for missing nucleon resonances, though the authors note that additional resonances or different mixing recipes are needed to improve agreement with polarization data.
