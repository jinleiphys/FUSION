# Quantum state preparation by adiabatic evolution with customized gates

> Quantum state preparation by adiabatic evolution is currently rendered ineffective by the long implementation times of the underlying quantum circuits, comparable to the decoherence time of present and near-term quantum devices. These implementation times can be significantly reduced by realizing the evolution with a minimal number of customized gates. Employing a realistic model of a two-qubit processor, we carried out classical device-level simulations of the adiabatic evolution of a two-spin system implemented with customized two-qubit gates. These device-level simulations were compared with (experimental) ones solving the same problem on IBMQ systems. When used to emulate the IBMQ quantum circuit, our device-level simulations reached state fidelities ranging from 65% to 85%, similar to the actual performance of a diverse set of IBMQ devices.When we reduced the implementation time by using a minimal number of customized gates, however, the loss of fidelity was reduced by at least a factor of four, allowing us to accurately extract the energy of the target state. This improvement is enough to render adiabatic evolution useful for quantum state preparation for small systems or as a preconditioner for other state preparation methods.

# Quantum state preparation by adiabatic evolution with customized gates

## Key claim
By replacing the standard decomposition of short-time propagators into elementary gates with a minimal number of customized two-qubit gates realized via optimized microwave pulses, the implementation time of adiabatic evolution can be reduced sufficiently that the loss of fidelity is reduced by at least a factor of four, making adiabatic evolution useful for quantum state preparation on near-term devices. However, the shortest pulses (120 ns) that achieve gate infidelities below 10^{-4} produce amplitudes that may exceed practical hardware limits.

## Method
The authors modeled a two-qubit processor as two capacitively coupled superconducting transmons controlled by microwave pulses, solving the Lindblad master equation for the density matrix with decoherence parameters taken from IBMQ calibration data. They used the GRAPE algorithm to find control pulses that realize each short-time propagator of the adiabatic evolution as a single customized two-qubit gate, with pulse lengths ranging from 2500 ns down to 120 ns. For comparison, they also implemented the same adiabatic evolution on four IBMQ systems (ibmq_belem, casablanca, lima, manila) using the standard decomposition of each propagator into three CNOT gates and eight U3 gates.

## Key numbers
- Target ground state energy of the two-spin system: E_T = -2.328 (dimensionless units)
- Adiabatic evolution time: T = 20 ns, divided into n = 20 time steps
- State fidelities on IBMQ systems: 60% (ibmq_belem), 75% (casablanca), 80% (lima), 72% (manila)
- Device-level simulations with customized gates of length τ = 120 ns reached target state with fidelities around 95%
- Device-level simulations with customized gates of length τ = 400 ns reached fidelities around 90%
- Device-level simulations emulating IBMQ circuit (τ ≈ τ_U = 900-2500 ns) reached fidelities ranging from 65% to 85%
- Implementation times on IBMQ systems: 52.5 μs (belem), 50.4 μs (casablanca), 21 μs (lima), 18.9 μs (manila)
- Relaxation times T1 on IBMQ systems: 102.6 μs (belem qubit 0), 70.4 μs (belem qubit 1), 111.7 μs (casablanca qubit 0), 130.1 μs (casablanca qubit 1), 101.6 μs (lima qubit 0), 113.0 μs (lima qubit 1), 136.0 μs (manila qubit 0), 244.2 μs (manila qubit 1)
- Dephasing times T2 on IBMQ systems: 127.3 μs (belem qubit 0), 104.5 μs (belem qubit 1), 40.7 μs (casablanca qubit 0), 102.2 μs (casablanca qubit 1), 180.0 μs (lima qubit 0), 106.9 μs (lima qubit 1), 112.8 μs (manila qubit 0), 46.7 μs (manila qubit 1)
- CNOT gate implementation times: 810.7 ns (belem), 760.9 ns (casablanca), 305.8 ns (lima), 277.3 ns (manila)
- Short-time propagator implementation times τ_U: approximately 2500 ns (belem), 2400 ns (casablanca), 1000 ns (lima), 900 ns (manila)
- Energies extracted from IBMQ simulations ranged from -1.25 to -0.25, versus ideal value E_T = -2.328
- Transmon anharmonicity α = 200 MHz, capacitive coupling strength g = 3 MHz
- Customized gate pulse lengths: 2500 ns, 400 ns, and 120 ns (shortest with gate infidelity below 10^{-4})
- Control pulse optimization parameters: ε_cut = 30 MHz, n = 3, χ = 10^{-3}

## Context
This work extends the hybrid digital-analog quantum computing approach previously applied to real-time evolution of neutron spins by Wu (2021) and Holland (2020), applying it to adiabatic state preparation. The authors demonstrate that customized gates can mitigate the decoherence problem that makes standard adiabatic evolution ineffective on NISQ devices, where implementation times are comparable to T1 and T2 decoherence times. This improvement enables adiabatic evolution to serve as a preconditioner for other eigenstate preparation methods such as phase estimation (Kitaev, 1995) or the rodeo algorithm (Choi, 2020; Qian, 2021), where even noisy adiabatic evolution can enhance initial state overlap probability from 0.1% to 5%, providing a fifty-fold improvement in algorithmic efficiency.
