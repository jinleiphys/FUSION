# Study of fusion reactions of light nuclei at low energies using complex nucleon-nucleus potential function

> Nuclear fusion reactions, at energies, far below the Coulomb barrier play a significant role in the synthesis of light elements in the primordial nucleosynthesis as well as in the interior of compact stellar objects. Many different kinds of nuclear reactions are occurring simultaneously inside the stellar core depending upon the density and temperature conditions of the nuclear plasma along with other relevant parameters of these stars. Nuclear fusion reactions in the energy range ($E\sim$ 1 eV to few keV) can be explained successfully by quantum mechanical tunneling through the mutual Coulomb barrier of interacting nuclei. The measurement of the cross-sections at extremely low energy is quite difficult because of the larger width of the Coulomb barrier, which results in a very small value of the reaction cross-section. Hence, any improvement in the data on astrophysical S-factors for the light nuclei fusion may give a better picture of the elemental abundance in nucleosynthesis. In this work, we have theoretically investigated the energy dependence of fusion cross-sections and astrophysical S-factors for fusion reaction of light nuclei like D-D and p-$^{11}$B using complex Gaussian nuclear potential with adjustable depth and range parameters plus the mutual Coulomb interaction of the interacting nuclei. Numerical computation of the observables is done in the framework of the selective resonant tunneling model approach. The results of our calculation are compared with those found in the literature.

## Key claim

The selective resonant tunneling model (SRTM) using a complex Gaussian nuclear potential can reproduce experimental fusion cross-sections and astrophysical S-factors for D-D and p-¹¹B reactions at deep sub-barrier energies, but only when the four adjustable potential parameters (depths and ranges of the real and imaginary components) are fine-tuned to match either a three-parameter or five-parameter fitting formula from the NRL Plasma Formulary.

## Method

The authors solved the one-dimensional Schrödinger equation for a complex nuclear potential consisting of a real Gaussian term and an imaginary Gaussian term plus the mutual Coulomb interaction, using the selective resonant tunneling model (SRTM) approach of Li et al. (2000). They adjusted four prime tuning parameters, the depths Vr and Vi and ranges βr and βi of the real and imaginary nuclear potential components, along with the nuclear radius parameter R0, to search for resonance energies and match computed cross-sections to the three-parameter and five-parameter fitting formulae from the NRL Plasma Formulary (Huba 2013). The fusion cross-section and astrophysical S-factor were computed from the complex phase shift using equations derived from the SRTM formalism.

## Key numbers

* D-D fusion: resonance peak near 120 keV with a fusion cross-section of approximately 0.174 barn (data-set-0, Figure 1).
* p-¹¹B fusion: resonance peak near 650 keV (specifically 647.22 keV) with a fusion cross-section of about 1.09 barn (data-set-0, Figure 4), in good agreement with Nevins and Swain (2000) and Becker et al. (1987) who reported a peak cross-section of about 1.2 barn near 654 keV.
* p-¹¹B fusion: resonance peak near 760 keV (specifically 760.024 keV) with a fusion cross-section of about 0.635 barn (data-set-1, Figure 5), in excellent agreement with Beckman et al. (1953) who reported a peak cross-section of about 0.623 barn near 757 keV.

## Context

This work extends the selective resonant tunneling model previously applied by Li et al. (2000, 2002, 2004) and Singh et al. (2019) using complex square-well potentials, by instead employing a complex Gaussian nuclear potential for D-D and p-¹¹B fusion reactions. The computed results are compared with the three-parameter and five-parameter fitting formulae from the NRL Plasma Formulary (Huba 2013) and with experimental data from Nevins and Swain (2000), Becker et al. (1987), Beckman et al. (1953), and Kamke and Krug (1967). The approach enables improved theoretical estimates of astrophysical S-factors for light nuclei fusion, which may give a better picture of elemental abundance in nucleosynthesis, particularly for reactions where direct laboratory measurement is impossible due to extremely small cross-sections at stellar energies.
