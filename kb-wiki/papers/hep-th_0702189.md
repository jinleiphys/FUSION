# Fluctuations, correlations and finite volume effects in heavy ion collision

> Finite volume corrections to higher moments are important observable quantities. They make possible to differentiate between different statistical ensembles even in the thermodynamic limit. It is shown that this property is a universal one. The classical grand canonical distribution is compared to the canonical distribution in the rigorous procedure of approaching the thermodynamic limit.

## Key claim

Finite volume corrections of order 1/V to higher moments (fluctuations and correlations) are universal and persist even in the thermodynamic limit, making them observable quantities that can differentiate between the grand canonical and canonical ensembles.

## Method

The paper analytically computes the asymptotic expansion of probability distributions for particle number and energy in the thermodynamic limit for a noninteracting gas, using the Watson-Laplace theorem to obtain terms up to order 1/V. The grand canonical ensemble (Poisson distribution) is compared to the canonical ensemble by evaluating how semi-intensive variables, ratios of extensive moments that remain finite in the thermodynamic limit, retain memory of finite-volume corrections.

## Key numbers

- For the grand canonical Poisson distribution, the density variance \(\Delta n^2 = \langle n \rangle / V \to 0\) in the thermodynamic limit, showing equivalence with the canonical ensemble at leading order.
- The scaled particle variance for the grand canonical ensemble is \(\Delta N^2 / \langle N \rangle = 1\), compared to zero for the canonical distribution.
- The density probability distribution in the large volume limit is \(\mathcal{P}_{\langle n \rangle}(n;V) \sim \delta(n - \langle n \rangle) + (\langle n \rangle / 2V) \delta''(n - \langle n \rangle) + \mathcal{O}(V^{-2})\).
- Particle number moments are \(\langle N^k \rangle = V^k \langle n \rangle^k + V^{k-1} [k(k-1)/2] \langle n \rangle^{k-1} + \mathcal{O}(V^{k-2})\).

## Context

This work extends earlier analyses by Turko and collaborators (2005) on 1/V corrections for semi-intensive quantities, which resolved ambiguities noted by Begun et al. (2004) regarding "spurious non-equivalence" of statistical ensembles in heavy ion collisions. The paper shows that finite-volume effects are not specific to systems with internal symmetries but appear even in the simplest classical Poisson problem, and that semi-intensive variables enable distinguishing ensembles experimentally. This enables using higher moments of particle distributions as practical observables to test thermal models of heavy ion collisions, which reproduce particle yields well via the hadronic resonance gas model (Braun-Munzinger et al., 2003).
