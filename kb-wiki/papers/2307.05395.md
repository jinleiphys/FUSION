# Graphene is neither Relativistic nor Non-Relativistic case: Thermodynamics Aspects

> Discovery of electron hydrodynamics in graphene system has opened a new scope of analytic calculations in condensed matter physics, which was traditionally well cultivated in science and engineering as a non-relativistic hydrodynamics and in high energy nuclear and astro physics as relativistic hydrodynamics. Electrons in graphene follow neither non-relativistic nor relativistic hydrodynamics and thermodynamics. Present article has gone through systematic microscopic calculations of thermodynamical quantities like pressure, energy density, etc. of electron-fluid in graphene and compared with corresponding estimations for non-relativistic and ultra-relativistic cases. Identifying the Dirac fluid and Fermi liquid domains, we have sketched the transition of temperature and Fermi energy dependency of electron thermodynamics for graphene and other cases. An equivalent transition for quark matter is also discussed. The most exciting part is the general expression of specific heat, whose Fermi to Dirac fluid domain transition can be realized as a transition from a solid-based to a fluid-based picture. This understanding may be connected to the experimentally observed Wiedemann-Franz Law violation in the Dirac fluid domain of graphene system.

## Key claim

Graphene's electron fluid follows neither non-relativistic nor ultra-relativistic thermodynamics, despite its linear dispersion relation \(E = p v_g\) that superficially resembles the relativistic case; the paper systematically demonstrates that graphene thermodynamics is distinct from both limits, though some particular relations (equation of state and equipartition law) can mislead one into thinking it follows a relativistic description.

## Method

The authors performed microscopic statistical mechanical calculations of thermodynamic quantities (energy density, pressure, number density, specific heat) for electrons in graphene using the dispersion relation \(E = p v_g\) and the Fermi-Dirac distribution function, expressing results in terms of Fermi integral functions. They derived general expressions for both 3D and 2D systems, then evaluated the two limiting cases, the Dirac fluid (DF) domain (\(\mu/T \ll 1\), using Riemann zeta functions) and the Fermi liquid (FL) domain (\(T \rightarrow 0\), using Sommerfeld expansion), and compared these with corresponding calculations for non-relativistic (\(E = p^2/2m\)) and ultra-relativistic (\(E = pc\)) cases.

## Key numbers

* Energy density for 3D graphene in the \(\mu \rightarrow 0\) limit: \(\epsilon_G^{3D} = (0.2665 \times 10^7) T^4\) (in units where \(v_g\) is included in the constant)
* Energy density for 3D graphene in the \(T \rightarrow 0\) limit: \(\epsilon_G^{3D} = (0.0117 \times 10^7) \mu^4\)
* Number density for 3D graphene in the \(\mu \rightarrow 0\) limit: \(n_G^{3D} = (0.0846 \times 10^7) T^3\)
* Number density for 3D graphene in the \(T \rightarrow 0\) limit: \(n_G^{3D} = (0.0156 \times 10^7) \mu^3\)
* Energy density for 2D graphene in the \(\mu \rightarrow 0\) limit: \(\epsilon_G^{2D} = (0.1594 \times 10^5) T^3\)
* Energy density for 2D graphene in the \(T \rightarrow 0\) limit: \(\epsilon_G^{2D} = (2.9473 \times 10^3) \mu^3\)
* Specific heat per particle for 3D graphene: \(\prescript{3D}{G}{[C_v]_{e}} = 3 k_B \left[4 \frac{f_4(A)}{f_3(A)} - \frac{\mu}{k_B T}\right]\), where \(A = e^{\mu/k_B T}\)
* Specific heat per particle for 2D graphene: \(\prescript{2D}{G}{[C_v]_{e}} = 2k_B \left[3 \frac{f_3(A)}{f_2(A)} - \frac{\mu}{k_B T}\right]\)

## Context

This work extends the study of unconventional hydrodynamics in graphene, following the dissipative component analysis of the energy-momentum tensor by the same group (Phys. Rev. B 108, 235172, 2023), by now systematically addressing the ideal component connected to thermodynamics. It contrasts with prior thermodynamic studies of graphene in magnetic fields (G_thermo_1, G_thermo_2, G_thermo_3) and interacting corrections (G_thermo_4, G_thermo_5) by focusing on the non-interacting case without external fields, and it provides the thermodynamic foundation for understanding the experimentally observed violation of the Wiedemann-Franz law in the Dirac fluid domain of graphene.
