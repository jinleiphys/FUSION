# Coupled-channel evaluations of cross sections for scattering involving particle-unstable resonances

> How does the scattering cross section change when the colliding bound-state fragments are allowed particle-emitting resonances? This question is explored in the framework of a multi-channel algebraic scattering method of determining nucleon-nucleus cross sections at low energies. Two cases are examined, the first being a gedanken investigation in which n + carbon-12 scattering is studied with the target states assigned artificial widths. The second is a study of neutron scattering from beryllium-8; a nucleus that is particle unstable. Resonance character of the target states markedly varies evaluated cross sections from those obtained assuming stability in the target spectrum.

# Coupled-channel evaluations of cross sections for scattering involving particle-unstable resonances

## Key claim
When target nuclei in low-energy neutron scattering are allowed to have finite particle-emission widths rather than being treated as stable states, the resulting compound-nucleus resonance centroids shift by at most a few tens of keV, but the resonance widths increase substantially, often making them indistinguishable from the scattering background, yet closer to experimentally determined widths.

## Method
The authors extended the multi-channel algebraic scattering (MCAS) method, which solves coupled Lippmann-Schwinger equations using finite-rank separable expansions in sturmian functions, to incorporate complex channel energies reflecting finite target-state widths. They applied this to two systems: a gedanken study of n+¹²C scattering with artificial widths assigned to the 2⁺₁ (4.43 MeV) and 0⁺₂ (7.64 MeV) states of ¹²C, and a realistic study of n+⁸Be scattering using the known α-decay widths of the 2⁺ (3.03 MeV, Γ=1.5 MeV) and 4⁺ (11.35 MeV, Γ≈3.5 MeV) states of ⁸Be.

## Key numbers
- For n+¹²C with artificial widths: the 2⁺₁ state was assigned widths of 0.20, 0.40, 0.60, and 0.80 MeV; the 0⁺₂ state was assigned widths of 0.60, 1.20, 1.80, and 2.40 MeV (Table I).
- For the ⁵/₂⁺ resonance in ¹³C at centroid 1.95 MeV: width increased from 0.01 MeV (zero-width target states) to 0.38 MeV (broadest artificial widths, set (e)).
- For the ¹/₂⁻ resonance in ¹³C at centroid 2.75 MeV: width increased from 8×10⁻⁴ MeV (zero-width) to 1.11 MeV (broadest artificial widths).
- For n+⁸Be: the ⁵/₂⁺ resonance at centroid 1.37-1.39 MeV had width 0.118 MeV (zero-width ⁸Be states) and 0.244 MeV (with empirical widths), compared to experimental width 0.282±0.011 MeV.
- For n+⁸Be: the ³/₂⁻ resonance at centroid 1.67-1.74 MeV had width 0.022 MeV (zero-width) and 0.682 MeV (with empirical widths), compared to experimental width 1.330±0.360 MeV.
- The ⁸Be ground state has width 6×10⁻⁶ MeV for α-decay; the 2⁺ state has centroid 3.03 MeV with width 1.5 MeV; the 4⁺ state has centroid 11.35 MeV with width ≈3.5 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the MCAS method developed by Amos et al. (2003) and Canton et al. (2005), which previously treated all target states as having zero width, to handle particle-unstable target nuclei relevant to radioactive-ion-beam experiments. The approach enables more realistic coupled-channel calculations for scattering from exotic nuclei such as ⁸Be, and demonstrates that including target-state widths brings calculated compound-nucleus resonance widths significantly closer to experimental values from Tilley et al. (2004), while leaving centroid energies nearly unchanged. This enables future applications to scattering from other unstable nuclei accessible with radioactive beams.
