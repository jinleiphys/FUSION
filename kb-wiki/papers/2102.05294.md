# Detecting scale anomaly in chiral phase transition of QCD: new critical endpoint pinned down

> Violation of scale symmetry, scale anomaly, being a radical concept in quantum field theory, is of importance to comprehend the vacuum structure of QCD, and should potentially contribute to the chiral phase transition in thermal QCD, as well as the chiral and U(1) axial symmetry. Though it should be essential, direct evidence of scale anomalies has never been observed in the chiral phase transition. We propose a methodology to detect a scale anomaly in the chiral phase transition, which is an electromagnetically induced scale anomaly: apply a weak magnetic field background onto two-flavor massless QCD with an extremely heavy strange quark, first observe the chiral crossover; second, adjusting the strange quark mass to be smaller and smaller, observe the second-order chiral phase transition, and then the first-order one in the massless-three flavor limit. Thus, the second-order chiral phase transition, observed as the evidence of the quantum scale anomaly, is a new critical endpoint. It turns out that this electromagnetic scale anomaly gets most operative in the weak magnetic field regime, rather than a strong field region. We also briefly address accessibility of lattice QCD, a prospected application to dense matter system, and implications to astrophysical observations, such as gravitational wave productions provided from thermomagnetic QCD-like theories.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a scale anomaly can be detected in the chiral phase transition of QCD by applying a weak magnetic field background, which generates an electromagnetically induced scale anomaly that produces a new critical endpoint on the extended Columbia plot; this effect is most operative in the weak magnetic field regime rather than a strong field region, and the second-order chiral phase transition observed as evidence of the quantum scale anomaly constitutes the new critical endpoint.

## Method

The authors employ a Ginzburg-Landau description based on a linear sigma model with two and three quark flavors, incorporating the chiral invariant potential, the U(1) axial anomaly term (Kobayashi-Maskawa-'t Hooft determinant), and explicit symmetry breaking from quark masses. They analyze the effective potential at tree level (mean field level) to determine the order of the chiral phase transition, and use the Ward-Takahashi identity for scale symmetry in an electromagnetic field to derive the coupling between the chiral-singlet scalar meson and the electromagnetic field via the scale anomaly. The analysis considers the effect of a weak magnetic field background on the phase structure as a function of the strange quark mass, interpolating between the two-flavor massless limit and the three-flavor massless limit.

## Key numbers

- The smallest nonzero magnetic field achievable on the lattice at finite temperature is \(\sqrt{eB}_\text{min}\sim 0.1\) GeV, which is only for physical pion mass simulations.
- The magnetic field on the lattice is bounded by temperature via \(|eB|_\text{min} \propto T^2\).

## Context

This work extends the classic Columbia plot framework initiated by Pisarski and Wilczek (1984) and the lattice QCD studies by the Columbia group, which mapped the order of the QCD phase transition on the quark mass plane. The paper proposes that while the standard QCD scale anomaly leaves no distinct footprint on the Columbia plot due to its flavor universality, an electromagnetically induced scale anomaly becomes visible in a weak magnetic field, producing a new critical endpoint between the massless two-flavor and three-flavor limits. This differs from previously proposed critical points in strong magnetic fields by Cohen (2013) and Endrodi (2015), which arise from broadening of the quenched regime due to amplified quark mass gaps. The finding enables future lattice QCD tests in the weak magnetic field regime near the chiral limit, and has implications for dense matter systems and astrophysical observations such as gravitational wave production from thermomagnetic QCD-like theories.
