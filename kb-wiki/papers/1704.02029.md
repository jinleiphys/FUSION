# Universal few-body physics and cluster formation

> A recent rejuvenation of experimental and theoretical interest in the physics of few- body systems has provided deep, fundamental insights into a broad range of problems. Few-body physics is a cross-cutting discipline not restricted to conventional subject ar- eas such as nuclear physics or atomic or molecular physics. To a large degree, the recent explosion of interest in this subject has been sparked by dramatic enhancements of experimental capabilities in ultracold atomic systems over the past decade, which now permit atoms and molecules to be explored deep in the quantum mechanical limit with controllable two-body interactions. This control, typically enabled by magnetic or electromagnetically-dressed Fano-Feshbach resonances, allows in particular access to the range of universal few-body physics, where two-body scattering lengths far exceed all other length scales in the problem. The Efimov effect, where 3 particles experienc- ing short-range interactions can counterintuitively exhibit an infinite number of bound or quasi-bound energy levels, is the most famous example of universality. Tremendous progress in the field of universal Efimov physics has taken off, driven particularly by a combination of experimental and theoretical studies in the past decade, and prior to the first observation in 2006, by an extensive set of theoretical studies dating back to 1970. Because experimental observations of Efimov physics have usually relied on resonances or interference phenomena in three-body recombination, this connects naturally with the processes of molecule formation in a low temperature gas of atoms or nucleons, and more generally with N-body recombination processes. ## Key claim

This review argues that universal few-body physics, particularly the Efimov effect, is a cross-cutting discipline spanning nuclear, atomic, and molecular physics, and that the three-body parameter for homonuclear systems dominated by van der Waals interactions exhibits an approximate universality fixed by the van der Waals length, contrary to earlier theoretical expectations that this parameter would vary randomly between systems.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical and experimental progress in universal few-body physics, focusing on systems with finite-range interactions and emphasizing the hyperspherical coordinate framework. It surveys adiabatic hyperspherical methodology, the slow-variable discretization method, and applications to three-body recombination, atom-dimer scattering, Coulomb systems, chemical physics, and nuclear fragmentation processes, comparing results from ultracold atomic experiments with theoretical predictions.

## Key numbers

- The Efimov geometric scaling factor for homonuclear three-body recombination resonances is approximately $e^{\pi/s_0} \approx 22.7$ between successive resonances in the scattering length
- The three-body parameter for homonuclear systems dominated by van der Waals interactions satisfies $a_-^{(1)} \approx -10 \ell_{\rm{vdW}}$, where $\ell_{\rm{vdW}} \equiv [m C_6/(16\hbar^2)]^{1/4}$, fixed to within approximately 15%
- Nucleon-nucleon scattering lengths: $a_{nn}(S=0)=-18.9$ fm, $a_{np}(S=0)=-23.7$ fm, $a_{np}(S=1)=5.43$ fm, with the range of the nucleon-nucleon strong force $r_0\sim 1-2$ fm
- The Wannier-Rau-Peterkop threshold law for double escape gives $\sigma \propto E^\gamma$ where $\gamma = \frac{1}{4} [(\frac{100Z-9}{4Z-1})^{1/2}-1]$, with $\gamma=1.127$ for $Z=1$
- Three-body recombination rate coefficient for spin-polarized atomic hydrogen is of order $10^{-38}$ cm$^6$/s

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework developed by Thomas (1935) on the three-nucleon collapse and Efimov (1970) on infinite three-body bound states, connecting these nuclear physics predictions to ultracold atomic experiments beginning with the first observation by Kraemer et al. (2006). It synthesizes work from the hyperspherical coordinate methodology of Macek (1968), the nonperturbative recombination treatments of Nielsen and Macek (1999) and Esry et al. (1999), and the van der Waals universality interpretation of Wang et al. (2012) and Naidon et al. (2014), enabling future studies of N-body recombination, confinement-induced resonances, and ion-atom-atom recombination.
