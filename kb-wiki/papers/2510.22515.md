# An End-to-End Generative Diffusion Model for Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Heavy-ion collision physics has entered the high precision era, demanding theoretical models capable of generating huge statistics to compare with experimental data. However, traditional hybrid models, which combine hydrodynamics and hadronic transport, are computationally intensive, creating a significant bottleneck. In this work, we introduce DiffHIC, an end-to-end generative diffusion model, to emulate ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The model takes initial entropy density profiles and transport coefficients as input and directly generates two-dimensional final-state particle spectra. Our results demonstrate that DiffHIC achieves a computational speedup of approximately $10^5$ against traditional simulations, while accurately reproducing a wide range of physical observables, including integrated and differential anisotropic flow, multi-particle correlations, and momentum fluctuations. This framework provides a powerful and efficient tool for phenomenological studies in the high-precision era of heavy-ion physics.

## Key claim

DiffHIC, a conditional diffusion model, can emulate ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions end-to-end with a computational speedup of approximately 10^5 compared to traditional hybrid simulations, while accurately reproducing integrated and differential anisotropic flow, multi-particle correlations, and momentum fluctuations. The hedge is that the model's precision shows a slight decrease for higher-order multi-particle correlations, attributed to the finite 64x64 pixel resolution of the generated spectra.

## Method

The authors trained a conditional diffusion model (DiffHIC) using a U-Net architecture on 12,000 minimum-bias Pb-Pb collision events at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV generated by a traditional hybrid model (TRENTO initial conditions, (2+1)D MUSIC viscous hydrodynamics, Cooper-Frye particlization, and UrQMD hadronic transport) for each of three shear viscosity values (eta/s = 0.0, 0.1, 0.2). The model takes initial entropy density profiles and the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio as input and generates two-dimensional final-state charged particle momentum spectra by learning the reverse stochastic differential equation that transforms Gaussian noise into the data distribution.

## Key numbers

- Computational speedup of approximately 10^5: DiffHIC generates a single event in about 0.1 seconds on a single NVIDIA GeForce GTX 4090 GPU, compared to approximately 120 minutes for the traditional hybrid model on a single CPU core.
- Training dataset: 12,000 minimum-bias Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV for each of three shear viscosity values (eta/s = 0.0, 0.1, and 0.2).
- Validation dataset: 10,000 additional events per eta/s value across all centralities.
- Fine-tuning demonstration: a pre-trained DiffHIC model can be adapted with as few as 500 new events to incorporate new physics such as nuclear deformation in Uranium-Uranium collisions.

## Context

This work extends the line of machine-learning surrogate models for heavy-ion collisions, being the first application of a conditional diffusion model as an end-to-end emulator that directly maps initial conditions to final particle spectra, bypassing the computationally expensive hybrid approach combining hydrodynamics and hadronic transport (e.g., TRENTO + MUSIC + UrQMD). The model enables large-scale statistical studies requiring 10^9-10^10 events for high-precision analyses of QGP properties, nuclear structure effects, and the equation of state, which were previously computationally prohibitive. Future extensions include incorporating bulk viscosity, equation of state parameters, moving to 3D hydrodynamic simulations, and generating full particle clouds for greater kinematic detail.
