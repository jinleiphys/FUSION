# Investigation of the background in coherent $J/ψ$ production at the EIC

> Understanding various fundamental properties of nucleons and nuclei are among the most important scientific goals at the upcoming Electron-Ion Collider (EIC). With the unprecedented opportunity provided by the next-generation machine, the EIC might provide definitive answers to many standing puzzles and open questions in modern nuclear physics. Here we investigate one of the golden measurements proposed at the EIC, which is to obtain the spatial gluon density distribution within a lead ($Pb$) nucleus. The proposed experimental process is the exclusive $J/ψ$ vector-meson production off the $Pb$ nucleus - $e+Pb\rightarrow e'+J/ψ+Pb'$. The Fourier transformation of the momentum transfer $|t|$ distribution of the coherent diffraction is the transverse gluon spatial distribution. In order to measure it, the experiment has to overcome an overwhelmingly large background arising from the incoherent diffractive production, where the nucleus $Pb'$ mostly breaks up into fragments of particles in the far-forward direction close to the hadron-going beam rapidity. In this paper, we systematically study the rejection of incoherent $J/ψ$ production by vetoing products from these nuclear breakups - protons, neutrons, and photons, which is based on the BeAGLE event generator and the most up-to-date EIC Far-forward Interaction Region design. The achieved vetoing efficiency, the ratio between the number of vetoed events and total incoherent events, ranges from about 80% - 99% depending on $|t|$, which can resolve at least the first minimum of the coherent diffractive distribution based on the Sar$\it{t}$re model. Experimental and accelerator machine challenges as well as potential improvements are discussed.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that vetoing nuclear breakup products (protons, neutrons, and photons) from incoherent diffractive J/ψ production can achieve 80% to 99% rejection efficiency depending on momentum transfer |t|, which is sufficient to resolve at least the first minimum of the coherent diffractive distribution. The feasibility of this measurement depends critically on the far-forward detector design and beam pipe material choices, with the results based on the BeAGLE event generator and the EIC Conceptual Design Report interaction region layout.

## Method
The authors used the BeAGLE event generator to simulate 1.3 million incoherent e+Pb → e'+J/ψ+X events at 18 GeV electrons on 110 GeV per nucleon Pb nuclei, modeling nuclear breakup via the DPMJET intra-nuclear cascade and FLUKA evaporation. They applied a sequential veto procedure using four far-forward detector subsystems (B0 spectrometer, off-momentum detector, Roman Pots, and zero-degree calorimeter) to reject events containing protons, neutrons, or photons from nuclear breakup, and studied the impact of different beam pipe materials (beryllium, aluminum, stainless steel) on veto efficiency.

## Key numbers
- Vetoing efficiency ranges from about 80% to 99% depending on |t|, with the lowest efficiency at the lowest |t| values
- For the first diffractive minimum, roughly 90% vetoing efficiency is required; for the second minimum, 99%; for the third minimum, >99.8%
- With a beryllium beam pipe and veto step 7 (photon energy threshold >50 MeV), only 2.05% of incoherent events survive
- With a stainless steel beam pipe and veto step 7 (photon energy threshold >50 MeV), 5.58% of incoherent events survive
- The average neutron multiplicity from E665 data for Pb targets is 4.70415, used to tune the formation time parameter τ0
- Three τ0 values were used: 6 fm (f=0.42), 10 fm (f=0.24), and 14 fm (f=0.08), with 10 fm as the central value

## Context
This work extends the methodology developed for ultra-peripheral collisions at RHIC and LHC (Khachatryan 2016, Abelev 2012, Adam 2015, Adamczyk 2017, Acharya 2020) to the electron-ion collider environment, where the photon virtuality is nonzero and event-by-event control is improved. The study uses the Sartre model (Toll 2012) for coherent/incoherent cross section ratios and the BeAGLE generator (Tu 2020) for nuclear breakup simulation, and was conducted as part of the EIC Yellow Report detector design studies (AbdulKhalek 2021). The results enable future optimization of the EIC far-forward interaction region design, particularly at the proposed IP-8 location with a 35 mrad crossing angle and secondary focus for improved low-pT acceptance.
