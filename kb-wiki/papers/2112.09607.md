# Proposed Lunar Measurements of $r$-Process Radioisotopes to Distinguish Origin of Deep-sea 244Pu

> 244Pu has recently been discovered in deep-sea deposits spanning the past 10 Myr, a period that includes two 60Fe pulses from nearby supernovae. 244Pu is among the heaviest $r$-process products, and we consider whether it was created in the supernovae, which is disfavored by nucleosynthesis simulations, or in an earlier kilonova event that seeded 244Pu in the nearby interstellar medium that was subsequently swept up by the supernova debris. We discuss how these possibilities can be probed by measuring 244Pu and other $r$-process radioisotopes such as 129I and 182Hf, both in lunar regolith samples returned to Earth by missions such as Chang'e and Artemis, and in deep-sea deposits.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the recently detected deep-sea 244Pu, which appears coincident with two 60Fe pulses from nearby supernovae at approximately 3 and 7 Myr ago, could originate either directly from those supernovae (disfavored by nucleosynthesis simulations) or from an earlier kilonova event whose ejecta was subsequently swept up by the supernova debris, and argues that lunar regolith measurements of additional r-process radioisotopes can distinguish between these scenarios.

## Method

The authors constructed four nucleosynthesis models: two supernova models (a forced neutrino-driven wind model SA and a magnetohydrodynamic model SB) and two kilonova models (KA and KB, based on neutron-star merger dynamical ejecta and disk neutrino-driven wind calculations), all constrained by r-process abundances in metal-poor stars. They calculated the 60Fe/244Pu ratios and the yields of other r-process radioisotopes (93Zr, 107Pd, 129I, 135Cs, 182Hf, 236U, 237Np, 247Cm) for each model, then compared these predictions to the deep-sea measurements from Wallner (2021) and estimated the detectability of these isotopes in lunar regolith samples from future Chang'e and Artemis missions.

## Key numbers

- 244Pu detection in deep-sea ferromanganese crusts: 181 +/- 19 atoms above background (Wallner 2021)
- Observed interstellar 244Pu flux: 980 atoms cm^-2 Myr^-1 (Wallner 2021)
- 60Fe/244Pu ratio in deep-sea deposits: approximately 5 x 10^-5
- 60Fe half-life: 2.6 Myr; 244Pu half-life: approximately 80 Myr
- For supernova model SA at 3 Myr: 60Fe/244Pu = 9.2 x 10^-2, 129I/244Pu = 3.2 x 10^2, 182Hf/244Pu = 3.1
- For supernova model SB at 3 Myr: 60Fe/244Pu = 5.3 x 10^3, 129I/244Pu = 1.7 x 10^6, 182Hf/244Pu = 4.4 x 10^3
- For kilonova model KA at 10 Myr: 129I/244Pu = 69, 182Hf/244Pu = 0.43
- For kilonova model KB at 10 Myr: 129I/244Pu = 14, 182Hf/244Pu = 6.9 x 10^-2
- Chang'e-5 returned approximately 1.7 kg of lunar sample; Artemis will return approximately 100 kg
- Estimated sample mass needed for 244Pu detection in lunar regolith: approximately 10 g
- Lunar regolith gardening depth for material deposited 3 Myr ago: 3.8 cm; for 7 Myr ago: 5.7 cm

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Wang (2021) on possible signatures of rare supernova and kilonova r-process events, now confronting the firm 244Pu detection by Wallner (2021) alongside the two 60Fe pulses (the Pliocene event at approximately 3 Myr ago and the Miocene event at approximately 7 Myr ago, following Ertel (2022)). The paper builds on prior measurements of 60Fe in deep-sea deposits by Knie (1999, 2004), Wallner (2016), and others, as well as 60Fe detections in lunar regolith by Fimiani (2016). The two-step scenario (kilonova ejecta swept up by later supernova) was first estimated by Ellis (1996). The results enable future lunar sample analysis missions to distinguish between supernova and kilonova origins of r-process material by measuring 129I/244Pu and 182Hf/244Pu ratios, with 129I identified as the most promising additional isotope for detection across all scenarios.
