# Direct photon observables from hydrodynamics and implications on the initial temperature and EoS

> The expansion of the strongly interacting quark gluon plasma (sQGP) created in Au+Au collisions at RHIC can be described by hydrodynamical models. Hadrons are created after a freeze-out, thus their distribution describes the final state of the evolution. The earlier stages can be analyzed via penetrating probes like photon observables. These were measured in 2010 and 2011 by the PHENIX experiment. Here we analyze an analytic, 1+3 dimensional perfect relativistic hydrodynamic solution and calculate hadron and photon observables, such as transverse momentum spectra, elliptic flow and correlation (HBT) radii. We find that our model is not incompatible with the data, not even with the direct photon elliptic flow. From fitting the data, we find that early temperatures of the sQGP were well above the quark-hadron transition temperature, in the hottest point, the center of the fireball the temperature may have reached 507+-12 MeV. The equation of state of this quark matter can be described by an average sound speed of 0.36+-0.02. We also predict a photon source that is significantly larger in the out direction than in the side direction.

# Direct photon observables from hydrodynamics and implications on the initial temperature and EoS

## Key claim
The author finds that a 1+3 dimensional perfect relativistic hydrodynamic solution with ellipsoidal symmetry is "not incompatible" with PHENIX direct photon data, including the direct photon elliptic flow, and that thermal radiation is consistent with the measured direct photon observables. The key qualifier is that the model assumes thermal photon radiation even though direct photons may not be thermalized in the strongly interacting plasma, and the equation of state parameter is treated as an average, fixed value rather than a temperature-dependent function.

## Method
The author uses an analytic, 1+3 dimensional perfect relativistic hydrodynamic solution with ellipsoidal symmetry and isotropic Hubble-type expansion to calculate hadron and photon observables. Freeze-out parameters (expansion rates, freeze-out proper-time, central freeze-out temperature) are first determined from fits to PHENIX hadron data (invariant momentum distributions, HBT radii, elliptic flow), then the remaining free parameters (equation of state parameter κ and initial time t_i) are fitted to PHENIX direct photon data from 200 GeV Au+Au collisions. Photon observables are calculated from a thermal source function using a second-order saddlepoint approximation.

## Key numbers
- Central freeze-out temperature T_0 = 199 ± 3 MeV (from hadronic N_1 and HBT fit, 0-30% centrality)
- Central freeze-out temperature T_0 = 204 ± 7 MeV (from hadronic elliptic flow fit, 0-30% centrality)
- Freeze-out proper-time τ_0 = 7.7 ± 0.1 fm/c (from hadronic N_1 and HBT fit)
- Equation of state parameter κ = 7.9 ± 0.7 (from direct photon fit)
- Average sound speed c_s = 0.36 ± 0.02 (stat) ± 0.04 (syst)
- Initial time t_i maximum within 95% probability: 0.7 fm/c
- Lower bound on initial temperature at center of fireball: T_i > 507 ± 12 (stat) ± 90 (syst) MeV at 0.7 fm/c
- Eccentricity ε = 0.80 ± 0.02 (from hadronic N_1 and HBT fit) and ε = 0.34 ± 0.03 (from hadronic elliptic flow fit)
- Fit quality for photon N_1: χ² = 7 with 3 degrees of freedom (confidence level 7.2%)

## Context
This work extends the analytic hydrodynamic model of Csörgő (2003) and previous hadronic calculations by Csanád (2009) to direct photon observables, comparing to PHENIX measurements from 2010 and 2011. The extracted average sound speed c_s = 0.36 ± 0.02 agrees with lattice QCD calculations by Borsanyi (2010) and experimental results from hadronic data by Adare (2006) and Lacey (2006). The lower bound on initial temperature (above 507 MeV) is consistent with the 300-600 MeV range from other hydrodynamic models cited in Adare (2008). The work enables future extraction of a temperature-dependent equation of state κ(T) and predicts a photon source with significantly larger HBT radius in the out direction than in the side direction, which can be tested by future measurements.
