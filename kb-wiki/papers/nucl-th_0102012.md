# Nuclear fragmentation by tunneling

> Fragmentation of nuclear system by tunneling is discussed in a molecular dynamics simulation coupled with imaginary time method. In this way we obtain informations on the fragmenting systems at low densities and temperatures. These conditions cannot be reached normally (i.e. above the barrier) in nucleus-nucleus or nucleon-nucleus collisions. The price to pay is the small probability of fragmentation by tunneling but we obtain observables which can be a clear signature of such phenomena.

# Nuclear fragmentation by tunneling

## Key claim
Fragmentation of a nuclear system can occur via quantum tunneling through a collective potential barrier, producing fragments at low densities and temperatures that are otherwise unreachable in normal nucleus-nucleus or nucleon-nucleus collisions. The authors acknowledge that the calculated threshold energy of 7 MeV/nucleon for three-fragment events is unrealistically high compared to experimental data (which suggest approximately 3 MeV/nucleon), and that the spherical approximation used in the simulation breaks down during the tunneling process, making the calculated actions "quite unrealistic."

## Method
The authors combine a quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) model with an imaginary-time method to simulate sub-barrier radial expansion of a finite nuclear system. A one-dimensional collective coordinate for spherical expansion is introduced, and when the collective motion reaches a turning point, the dynamics is evolved in imaginary time using an inverse potential method coupled to the real-time motion of individual nucleons. Tunneling events are weighted by a probability calculated from the action integral between the two turning points, using the formula Π = 1/(1 + e^(2S)).

## Key numbers
- The system studied is ²³⁰U, compressed uniformly to excitation energies E* from 5 to 8 MeV/nucleon.
- The threshold energy E₀ for real (above-barrier) events with fragment multiplicity M=3 is 7.0 MeV/nucleon in the simulation, compared to an estimated 3 MeV/nucleon from Aladin data (Pochodzalla et al., 1995).
- The difference in total kinetic energy of large fragments between M=2 and M=3 events in real fragmentation is approximately 30 MeV, corresponding to the difference in their Q-values from a liquid-drop model.
- The collective coordinate momentum P(t) becomes zero at t = 100 fm/c, at which point the tunneling simulation begins.

## Context
This work extends the imaginary-time method previously applied to fusion and spontaneous fission problems by Bonasera and Kondratyev (1994) and Bonasera and Iwamoto (1997), where a one-dimensional collective coordinate was used to treat sub-barrier dynamics. The paper proposes a novel mechanism called "Quantum Drops", fragments formed via tunneling, and suggests that experimental signatures could be observed in proton-nucleus, gentle nucleus-nucleus, or cluster-cluster collisions. The approach is limited by the breakdown of the spherical approximation during tunneling and the unrealistically high threshold energy, which the authors note could be improved with a more suitable effective interaction. The work enables future experimental searches for tunneling fragmentation signatures, particularly through Dalitz plot analysis of fragment masses and fragment momentum distributions.
