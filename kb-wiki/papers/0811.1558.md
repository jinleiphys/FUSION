# Symmetry Energy in Nuclear Surface

> Interplay between the dependence of symmetry energy on density and the variation of nucleonic densities across nuclear surface is discussed. That interplay gives rise to the mass dependence of the symmetry coefficient in an energy formula. Charge symmetry of the nuclear interactions allows to introduce isoscalar and isovector densities that are approximately independent of the magnitude of neutron-proton asymmetry.

## Key claim

The symmetry coefficient in the Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula depends on mass number \(A\) due to the interplay between the density-dependence of symmetry energy in uniform matter and the variation of nucleonic densities across the nuclear surface, rather than being a fixed constant as usually assumed.

## Method

The authors perform Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations of half-infinite nuclear matter to examine isoscalar and isovector density profiles at different asymmetries. They then extract mass-dependent symmetry coefficients from experimental excitation energies of isobaric analog states (IAS) using tabulated data and microscopic corrections, and compare these to predictions from Thomas-Fermi (TF) theory.

## Key numbers

- Volume symmetry coefficient extracted from IAS data: \(a_a^V = 32.9 \, {\rm MeV}\)
- Surface symmetry coefficient extracted from IAS data: \(a_a^S = 11.3 \, {\rm MeV}\)
- Deduced symmetry coefficient values range from \(a_a \sim 9 \, {\rm MeV}\) for light \(A < 10\) nuclei to \(a_a \sim 22.5 \, {\rm MeV}\) for \(A > 200\)
- Favored value of symmetry energy at normal density: \(a_a^V = (30-33) \, {\rm MeV}\)
- Favored value of slope parameter: \(L \sim 80 \, {\rm MeV}\)
- Typical volume coefficient for Skyrme interactions: \(a_a^V \sim 30 \, {\rm MeV}\)
- Friedel oscillation wavelength: \(\lambda = \pi / k_F \simeq 2.36 \, {\rm fm}\)

## Context

This work extends the understanding of symmetry energy beyond the standard Bethe-Weizsacker formula by showing that the symmetry coefficient is mass-dependent, consistent with earlier Thomas-Fermi calculations by Danielewicz (2003, 2004). The results constrain the density dependence of symmetry energy, which is important for neutron star structure as discussed by Lattimer (2006). The approach enables future extraction of mass-dependent symmetry coefficients from spherical SHF calculations and model-independent understanding of asymmetry skins.
