# Manipulation of Giant Multipole Resonances via Vortex $γ$ Photons

> Traditional photonuclear reactions primarily excite giant dipole resonances, making the measurement of isovector giant resonances with higher multipolarties a great challenge. In this work, the manipulation of collective excitations of different multipole transitions in nuclei via vortex $γ$ photons has been investigated. We develop the calculation method for photonuclear cross sections induced by the vortex $γ$ photon beam using the fully self-consistent random-phase approximation plus particle-vibration coupling (RPA+PVC) model based on Skyrme density functional. We find that the electromagnetic transitions with multipolarity $J< m_γ$ are forbidden for vortex $γ$ photons due to the angular momentum conservation, with $m_γ$ being the projection of total angular momentum of $γ$ photon on its propagation direction. For instance, this allows for probing the isovector giant quadrupole resonance without interference from dipole transitions using vortex $γ$ photons with $m_γ=2$. The electromagnetic transitions with $J>m_γ$ are strongly suppressed compared with the plane-wave-$γ$-photon case, and even vanish at specific polar angles. Therefore, the giant resonances with specific multipolarity can be extracted via vortex $γ$ photons. Moreover, the vortex properties of $γ$ photons can be meticulously diagnosed by measuring the nuclear photon-absorption cross section. Our method opens new avenues for photonuclear excitations, generation of coherent $γ$ photon laser and precise detection of vortex particles, and consequently, has significant impact on nuclear physics, nuclear astrophysics and strong laser physics.

# Manipulation of Giant Multipole Resonances via Vortex γ Photons

## Key claim
Vortex γ photons with a well-defined total angular momentum projection mγ can selectively suppress or forbid electromagnetic transitions with multipolarity J < mγ, and strongly suppress transitions with J > mγ at specific polar angles, enabling the isolation of isovector giant quadrupole resonances without interference from dipole transitions, but only when the nucleus is positioned on the beam axis; off-axis the selection rules revert to the plane-wave case.

## Method
The authors developed a calculation method for photonuclear cross sections induced by vortex γ photon beams using the fully self-consistent random-phase approximation plus particle-vibration coupling (RPA+PVC) model based on the Skyrme density functional. They computed the ratio r(tw) = σ(tw)/σ(pl) of vortex to plane-wave cross sections for electric dipole (E1), quadrupole (E2), and octupole (E3) transitions in 208Pb, incorporating the Bessel-function and Wigner d-function dependence on the vortex parameters (mγ, polar angle θk, and impact parameter b). They also analyzed superposition states of two vortex γ photons with different topological charges.

## Key numbers
- For plane-wave γ photons on 208Pb: peak energy Eγ,tot = 12.80 MeV with total cross section σ(pl)tot = 648.03 mb; experimental values are Eγ,exp = 13.40 MeV and σexp = 641.50 mb.
- Individual plane-wave multipole peaks: E1 at Eγ,1 = 12.80 MeV with σ(pl)E1 = 635.76 mb; E2 at Eγ,2 = 24.20 MeV with σ(pl)E2 = 63.75 mb; E3 at Eγ,3 = 34.00 MeV with σ(pl)E3 = 6.49 mb.
- For vortex γ photons with mγ = 2, the E1 transition is forbidden on the beam axis, allowing isolation of the E2 contribution.
- For vortex γ photons with mγ = 1 at θk ≃ 60.03°, the E2 transition is forbidden and E3 suppressed, yielding a quasi-pure E1 cross section with r(tw)E1 = 1.13.
- For vortex γ photons with mγ = 2 at θk ≃ 70.56°, the E3 transition is forbidden, yielding a quasi-pure E2 cross section with r(tw)E2 = 1.19.
- For typical interatomic distances in crystals (0.1 nm), the ratio r(tw) is approximately 10−4 for Bessel-mode vortex states and approximately 0 for Bessel-Gauss modes.

## Context
This work extends the study of vortex photon interactions from atomic physics, where Schmiegelow (2016) and Lange (2022) demonstrated modified selection rules for atomic multipole transitions, to nuclear giant resonances. It addresses the longstanding challenge of measuring the isovector giant quadrupole resonance (IVGQR), which has been difficult to isolate using plane-wave photons due to overwhelming E1 dominance, and which previous studies by Dale (1992) and Henshaw (2011) attempted to extract via E1-E2 interference with linearly polarized γ-ray beams. The method enables future experiments with trapped single ions or solid targets using vortex γ beams generated via Compton scattering in ultra-intense laser facilities, and could inform the design of coherent zeptosecond γ photon lasers as proposed by Tkalya (2011) and Baldwin (1997).
