# Dynamical modeling of high energy heavy ion collisions

> We present theoretical approaches to high energy nuclear collisions in detail putting a special emphasis on technical aspects of numerical simulations. Models include relativistic hydrodynamics, Monte-Carlo implementation of k_T-factorization formula, jet quenching in expanding fluids, a hadronic transport model and the Vlasov equation for colored particles.

## Key claim

This paper presents a comprehensive review of theoretical and numerical approaches to modeling high-energy heavy ion collisions, with a special emphasis on the technical aspects of numerical simulations rather than on new physical results. The authors explicitly state that they are providing a review of existing methods rather than presenting original numerical findings.

## Method

The paper reviews and explains the numerical implementation of several key theoretical frameworks: relativistic ideal hydrodynamics solved via the piecewise parabolic method (PPM) in both Cartesian and relativistic (τ-ηₛ) coordinates; Monte-Carlo implementation of the k_T-factorization formula for initial conditions; jet quenching in expanding fluids; the hadronic transport model JAM; and the Vlasov equation for colored particles solved via the particle-in-cell method. For the hydrodynamic component, the authors detail the discretization scheme, operator splitting method, and the procedure for converting numerical solutions back to thermodynamic variables.

## Key numbers

- Initial time for hydrodynamic simulations: τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c
- Inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross sections: σ_in = 39.53 mb at √s = 130 GeV, 41.94 mb at √s = 200 GeV, and 61.36 mb at √s = 2760 GeV
- MC-Glauber parameters for RHIC Au+Au collisions: C = 19.8 and α = 0.14, fixed by comparison with PHOBOS multiplicity data at decoupling temperature T_dec = 100 MeV
- MC-Glauber parameters for LHC Pb+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV: C = 41.4 and α = 0.08, chosen to reproduce ALICE centrality dependence of multiplicity

## Context

This review extends and systematizes the integrated approach to heavy ion collisions first attempted by Hirano (2004), which combined three-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic simulations with CGC-based initial conditions and parton energy loss in expanding fluids. The paper provides technical details for implementing the numerical methods used in this line of work, including the PPM algorithm first applied to relativistic heavy ion collisions by Hirano (2000) and later extended to τ-ηₛ coordinates by Hirano (2001). The review enables practitioners to implement these modules, CGC initial conditions, relativistic hydrodynamics, parton energy loss, hadronic transport, and the Vlasov equation, in a unified framework for studying the space-time evolution of matter created in high-energy nuclear collisions.
