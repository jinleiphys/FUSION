# Periodic Orbits and Shell Structure in Octupole Deformed Potentials

> The effect of an octupole term in a quadrupole deformed single particle potential is studied from the classical and quantum mechanical view point. Whereas the problem is nonintegrable, the quantum mechanical spectrum nevertheless shows some shell structure in the superdeformed prolate case for particular, yet fairly large octupole strengths; for spherical or oblate deformation the shell structure disappears. This result is associated with classical periodic orbits which are found by employing the removal of resonances method; this approximation method allows determination of the shape of the orbit and of the approximate octupole coupling strength for which it occurs. The validity of the method is confirmed by solving numerically the classical equations of motion. The quantum mechanical shell structure is analysed using the particle number dependence of the fluctuating part of the total energy. In accordance with the classical result this dependence turns out to be very similar for a superdeformed prolate potential plus octupole term and a hyperdeformed prolate potential without octupole term. In this way the shell structure is explained at least for some few hundred levels. The Fourier transform of the level density further corroborates these findings.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in a quadrupole-deformed harmonic oscillator potential with an octupole term, shell structure persists in the quantum mechanical spectrum only for the superdeformed prolate case (b=2) at particular, fairly large octupole strengths, while for spherical or oblate deformation the shell structure disappears. The authors hedge that this result holds "at least for some few hundred levels" and that the classical approximation method works best for the prolate case where the system remains "quasi-integrable."

## Method

The authors studied classical and quantum motion in a potential combining quadrupole deformation (parameter b) with an octupole term (parameter λ) using cylindrical coordinates. For the classical analysis, they employed the "removal of resonances" method (secular perturbation theory) to average over fast degrees of freedom, reducing the nonintegrable two-degree-of-freedom system to an effective integrable one, and verified the approximation by numerically integrating the exact classical equations of motion. Quantum mechanically, they diagonalized the Hamiltonian in a basis of asymptotic quantum numbers (n⊥, nz, Λ) using matrices up to 1600×1600 dimensions, then extracted the fluctuating part of the total energy via a variant of Strutinsky's procedure to identify shell closures.

## Key numbers

- For prolate deformation b=2, the critical octupole strength is λc = 1/(2b²) = 1/8 = 0.125, above which the potential no longer binds.
- At λ/λc = 0.63 (with b=2), the classical frequency ratio ω⊥/ωz becomes 5:2, producing a quantum spectrum similar to a pure prolate oscillator with b=5/2.
- At λ/λc = 0.76 (with b=2), the frequency ratio becomes 3:1, producing a quantum spectrum similar to a pure prolate oscillator with b=3.
- The Fourier transform of the level density (Λ=0 only) yields periods T5:2 = 1.2 and T3:1 = 1.4 in units of the unperturbed (b=2, λ=0) period.
- For the oblate case (b=0.58), chaos onset occurs at λ/λc ≥ 0.25, a much smaller octupole strength than for the prolate case.
- The quantum calculations used 1600×1600 dimensional matrices, with variation less than 1% for the first 300 levels when λ ≤ 0.9λc and 0.5 ≤ b ≤ 2.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical periodic orbit theory of Gutzwiller (1971) and Balian and Bloch (1972) to nonintegrable octupole-deformed potentials, building on earlier shell-structure analyses by Mottelson (1976) and Nazmitdinov (1992) for quadrupole-deformed systems. The results provide a classical foundation for the quantum mechanical shell structure found previously by Heiss (1994) and suggest that superdeformed prolate nuclei and metallic clusters with octupole deformation should be more stable than their oblate counterparts, enabling future studies of supershell structure in deformed many-body systems.
