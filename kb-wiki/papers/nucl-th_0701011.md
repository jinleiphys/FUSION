# Eta meson production in nucleon-nucleon collisions within an effective Lagrangian model

> We investigate the pp --> pp eta and pn --> pn eta reactions within an effective Lagrangian model for laboratory kinetic energies ranging from very close to eta meson production threshold to about 10 GeV. Production amplitudes include contributions from the mechanism of excitation, propagation and decay of N*(1535), N*(1650), and N*(1710) baryonic resonances. The initial interaction between two incoming nucleons is modeled by the exchange of pi, rho, omega and sigma mesons where the vertex parameters are taken to be the same as those used in the previous applications of this model. Parameters of the resonance vertices also have been taken from our earlier studies wherever applicable. Calculations have been done for total as well as differential eta production cross sections. To describe the data for energies closer to the production threshold final state interactions among the out-going particles have been included by means of a generalized Watson-Migdal method. Terms corresponding to the excitation of N*(1535) resonance and pion exchange process dominate the cross sections. With a single set of vertex parameters our model is able to describe the available data well on total cross sections for beam energies ranging from close to threshold to upto 10 GeV.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a single set of vertex parameters within an effective Lagrangian model, including contributions from N*(1535), N*(1650), and N*(1710) resonances with meson exchanges (pi, rho, omega, sigma), can describe total cross section data for pp → ppη and pn → pnη reactions from near threshold to 10 GeV, but the description relies on a generalized Watson-Migdal method for final state interactions whose applicability to three-body final states is acknowledged as approximate.

## Method

The authors model η meson production in nucleon-nucleon collisions using an effective Lagrangian approach where the initial nucleon-nucleon interaction is described by π, ρ, ω, and σ meson exchanges with energy-dependent coupling constants fitted to NN elastic scattering T-matrices from 800 MeV to 4.00 GeV. The production amplitude proceeds through excitation, propagation, and decay of N*(1535), N*(1650), and N*(1710) baryonic resonances, with coupling constants determined from experimental decay branching ratios and vector meson dominance for ω couplings. Final state interactions among outgoing particles are included via a generalized Watson-Migdal method that treats pairwise interactions in the three-body final state, using ηN scattering parameters from a K-matrix fit to πN → ηN and γN → ηN data.

## Key numbers

- Total cross sections for pp → ppη are reproduced from near threshold to beam momenta up to about 10 GeV (corresponding to laboratory kinetic energies up to about 10 GeV)
- For the ηN scattering amplitude, the best description uses parameter set with scattering length a = 0.51 + i 0.26 fm, effective range r0 = -2.50 - i 0.310 fm, and shape parameter s = -0.20 - i 0.04 fm³
- The cut-off parameter λ_i^{N*} for resonance-nucleon-meson vertices is fixed at 1.2 GeV for all vertices
- N*(1535) resonance dominates the total cross section, with N*(1650) and N*(1710) providing smaller contributions
- For the NN sub-state final state interaction, the coefficient c_{ij} is set to unity; for the ηN sub-state, c_{ij} comes out to 1.07 with the same ξ_c cutoff

## Context

This work extends the effective Lagrangian model previously applied successfully to pion production (Engel 1996, Shyam 1998), associated kaon production (Shyam 1999), and dilepton production (Shyam 2003) in nucleon-nucleon collisions. It addresses the need for reliable extraction of nucleon resonance properties from hadronic production data, particularly for N*(1535) which has a large ηN branching ratio and lies near the η production threshold. The model enables future transport model studies of η meson production in heavy ion collisions, where elementary NNη cross sections are crucial input, and provides a framework for investigating η-nucleus bound states that have been discussed in recent workshops.
