# Illuminating dark matter admixed in neutron stars with simultaneous mass-radius constraints

> We investigate how simultaneous mass and radius measurements of massive neutron stars (NSs) can help constrain properties of dark matter (DM) possibly admixed in them. Within a fermionic DM model that interacts only through gravitation, along with a well-constrained nuclear matter equation of state, we show that the simultaneous mass and radius measurement of PSRJ0740+6620 reduces the uncertainty of DM central energy density by more than 50\% compared to the results obtained from using the two observables independently, while other DM parameters remain unconstrained. Additionally, we find that the DM fraction $f_D$ should be smaller than 2\% when constrained by the observed NS maximum mass alone, and it could be even smaller than 0.3\% with the simultaneous measurement of mass and radius, supporting the conclusion that only a small amount of DM exists in DM admixed neutron stars (DANS).

# Illuminating Dark Matter Admixed in Neutron Stars with Simultaneous Mass–Radius Constraints

## Key claim
Simultaneous mass and radius measurement of PSRJ0740+6620 reduces the uncertainty of dark matter central energy density by more than 50% compared to using the two observables independently, but only the lower radius limit of 12.2 km provides additional constraints; other dark matter parameters (coupling constants C_DS and C_DV) remain unconstrained regardless of measurement precision.

## Method
The authors solve two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations for a dark matter admixed neutron star (DANS) composed of nuclear matter described by a parameterized equation of state (PEOS) and fermionic dark matter interacting only through gravity with scalar and vector mediator fields. They construct a three-dimensional parameter space of dark matter coupling constants C_DS, C_DV, and the ratio of central energy densities epsilon_D^c/epsilon_N^c for four fixed dark matter masses (500, 1000, 1500, and 2000 MeV), then map constant surfaces corresponding to the observed maximum mass (2.08 solar masses) and the simultaneous mass-radius constraint (R=12.2 km for a 2.08 solar mass star) from PSRJ0740+6620 NICER observations.

## Key numbers
- The maximum mass of the nuclear matter PEOS alone is 2.13 solar masses, with radii R_2.08 = 12.25 km and R_1.4 = 12.73 km
- The upper limit for epsilon_D^c/epsilon_N^c from the maximum mass constraint alone increases from 0.15 to 2.45 as dark matter mass m_D increases from 500 to 2000 MeV
- Simultaneous mass-radius constraint reduces the upper limit of epsilon_D^c/epsilon_N^c by more than 50% for any m_D, to 0.05 for m_D = 500 MeV and 0.86 for m_D = 2000 MeV
- Using constraints from Das (2022) for C_DS = 3.90 GeV^-1 and C_DV = 11.88 GeV^-1, epsilon_D^c/epsilon_N^c = 0.07, 0.17, 0.26, and 0.37 for m_D = 500, 1000, 1500, and 2000 MeV respectively
- Dark matter fraction f_D is smaller than 2% when constrained by maximum mass alone, and smaller than 0.3% with simultaneous mass-radius measurement
- For m_D = 500 MeV, the largest f_D is 12.6% for maximum mass constraint and 4.8% for simultaneous constraint at C_DS = C_DV = 0
- Dark matter core size decreases from 8.00 km to 2.41 km as m_D increases from 500 to 2000 MeV at C_DS = C_DV = 0

## Context
This work extends previous studies of fermionic dark matter in neutron stars by Xiang (2014), Ellis (2018), and Routaray (2023) by systematically varying dark matter equation of state parameters and applying simultaneous mass-radius constraints from NICER observations of PSRJ0740+6620 (Miller 2021, Riley 2021). The nuclear matter equation of state is taken from Zhang (2025) and satisfies all current observational constraints including GW170817 tidal deformability limits (Abbott 2018). The results enable tighter constraints on dark matter fraction in neutron stars, supporting the conclusion that only a small amount of dark matter (f_D less than 0.3%) can exist in DANSs, and provide a methodology for constraining dark matter central energy density that can be applied to future neutron star observations.
