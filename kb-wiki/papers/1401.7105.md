# Size measurement of dynamically generated hadronic resonances with finite volume effect

> The structures of the hyperon resonance $Λ(1405)$ and the scalar mesons $σ$, $f_{0}(980)$, and $a_{0}(980)$ are investigated based on the coupled-channels chiral dynamics with finite volume effect. The finite volume effect is utilized to extract the coupling constant, compositeness, and mean squared distance between two constituents of a Feshbach resonance state as well as a stable bound state. In this framework, the real-valued size of the resonance can be defined from the downward shift of the resonance pole according to the decreasing finite box size $L$ on a given closed channel. As a result, we observe that, when putting the $\bar{K}N$ and $K\bar{K}$ channels into a finite box while other channels being unchanged, the poles of the higher $Λ(1405)$ and $f_{0}(980)$ move to lower energies while other poles do not show downward mass shift, which implies large $\bar{K}N$ and $K\bar{K}$ components inside higher $Λ(1405)$ and $f_{0}(980)$, respectively. Extracting structures of $Λ(1405)$ and $f_{0}(980)$ in our method, we find that the compositeness of $\bar{K}N$ ($K\bar{K}$) inside $Λ(1405)$ [$f_{0}(980)$] is 0.82-1.03 (0.73-0.97) and the mean distance between two constituents is evaluated as 1.7-1.9 fm (2.6-3.0 fm).

## Key claim

The spatial size of a hadronic resonance can be defined as a real-valued quantity by measuring the downward shift of the resonance pole as the finite box size \(L\) is decreased on a specific closed channel, rather than relying on complex-valued mean squared radii from scattering amplitudes.

## Method

The authors use a coupled-channels Bethe-Salpeter equation with the lowest-order \(s\)-wave chiral interaction to dynamically generate resonance poles for \(\Lambda(1405)\) and the scalar mesons \(\sigma\), \(f_{0}(980)\), and \(a_{0}(980)\). They then impose a finite cubic box with periodic boundary conditions on the \(\bar{K}N\) and \(K\bar{K}\) channels while leaving other channels unchanged, and track the shift of the resonance pole positions as a function of box size \(L\) from 3.0 fm to 7.0 fm. From the mass shift, they extract the coupling constant, compositeness, and mean squared distance between constituents using formulas derived for stable bound states, applied to the real part of the resonance pole.

## Key numbers

* Compositeness of \(\bar{K}N\) inside the higher \(\Lambda(1405)\): \(0.82\)–\(1.03\)
* Mean distance between constituents in \(\Lambda(1405)\): \(1.7\)–\(1.9\) fm
* Compositeness of \(K\bar{K}\) inside \(f_{0}(980)\): \(0.73\)–\(0.97\)
* Mean distance between constituents in \(f_{0}(980)\): \(2.6\)–\(3.0\) fm
* Root mean squared radius of higher \(\Lambda(1405)\): \(1.1\)–\(1.2\) fm
* Root mean squared radius of \(f_{0}(980)\): \(1.4\)–\(1.6\) fm
* Coupling constant \(g_{\bar{K}N,\mathrm{FV}}\) for higher \(\Lambda(1405)\): \(4.6\)–\(5.2\) GeV
* Coupling constant \(g_{K\bar{K},\mathrm{FV}}\) for \(f_{0}(980)\): \(2.7\)–\(3.1\) GeV

## Context

This work extends the chiral unitary approach to hadronic resonances, which had previously described \(\Lambda(1405)\) and the light scalar mesons phenomenologically (Kaiser 1995; Dobado 1992), by providing a method to extract real-valued spatial sizes. Earlier attempts to measure the spatial structure of \(\Lambda(1405)\) (Sekihara 2008) and \(\sigma\) (Albaladejo 2012) yielded complex-valued mean squared radii, which are difficult to interpret physically. The finite-volume technique introduced here, building on the formalism of Luscher (1985) and the authors' own prior work (Sekihara 2012), enables a clear interpretation of resonance size and compositeness, and can be applied to other Feshbach resonances in hadron physics.
