# Nucleon Resonance Transition Couplings to Vector Mesons

> Recent heavy ion experiments indicate modifications of the $ρ-$meson mass in medium. In the CERES experiments $ρ-$mesons are produced at $\sim$ normal nuclear matter density, where hadrons are more appropriate constituents than quarks. A collective "nuclear $ρ$", in which every nucleon is excited to the N(1520) state, with equal amplitude, enters in this description. At the higher densities reached by future experiments constituent quarks become the appropriate variables. Here the $ρ$ and $ω$ transition couplings to the nucleon resonances up to 1700 MeV, including the N(1520), are derived by means of the chiral quark model. The relevant coupling constants are expressed in terms of the corresponding vector coupling constants to nucleons. The quality of the model relations is tested by a calculation of the corresponding pion-resonance coupling constants, which are known from the empirical pion decay widths.

# Nucleon Resonance Transition Couplings to Vector Mesons

## Key claim

The chiral quark model can derive the ρ- and ω-meson transition couplings to nucleon resonances up to 1700 MeV, expressed in terms of the corresponding vector coupling constants to nucleons, but the single-quark operator approximation likely underestimates these couplings by factors of 1.5–2, similar to the known underestimation of pion-resonance transition couplings in the same approximation.

## Method

The authors used the chiral quark model with a covariant harmonic oscillator description of the 3-quark system, employing eigenfunctions of a mass operator with a linear confining interaction and a flavor-spin dependent hyperfine interaction. They compared matrix elements of generalized Rarita-Schwinger type Lagrangians for vector meson transitions to the corresponding quark model matrix elements, for off-mass-shell vector mesons with zero energy. The quality of the model was tested by first calculating pion-resonance coupling constants in the impulse approximation and comparing them to values determined from empirical pion decay widths.

## Key numbers

- The oscillator parameter ω is 311 MeV, as determined from the baryon spectrum.
- The empirical rms radius of the proton (0.8 fm) obtains with ω = 245 MeV.
- The pion-quark coupling constant f_πqq = 3/5 f_πNN ≈ 0.6, with f_πNN ≈ 1.
- From the Goldberger-Treiman relation for quarks, f_πqq ≈ 0.66 using g_A^q ≈ 0.88 and f_π = 93 MeV.
- Empirical πNΔ coupling for Δ(1232): f_πNΔ^(1232) = 2.2 ± 0.04; quark model gives 1.55 (underestimate by factor 1.5).
- Empirical πNΔ coupling for Δ(1600): f_πNΔ^(1600) = 0.51 ± 0.07; quark model gives 0.47 (underestimate by factor 1.1).
- Empirical πNN* coupling for N(1440): f_πNN*^(1440) = 0.39 ± 0.06; quark model gives 0.26 (underestimate by factor 1.5).
- Empirical πNN* coupling for N(1535): f_πNN*^(1535) = 0.36 ± 0.05; quark model within 30%.
- Empirical πNN* coupling for N(1520): f_πNN*^(1520) = 1.56 ± 0.06; quark model value is fairly close.
- Empirical πNN* coupling for N(1675): f_πNN*^(1675) = 0.10; quark model gives 0.09.
- The ρNN vector coupling constant ranges from g_ρNN = 2.97 (Nijmegen model) to 3.19 (Bonn model).
- The ωNN coupling constant is g_ωNN = 10.35 in the Nijmegen model and 15.85 in the Bonn model.
- The quark model gives g_ρNN*^(1520) = 4.5, yielding f_ρNN*^(1520) = 1.5, about half the value 3.2 from Friman et al.
- The quark model gives g_ωNN*^(1520) = 7.7, yielding f_ωNN*^(1520) = 2.6, somewhat less than half the value from Friman et al.

## Context

This work extends the chiral quark model approach of Glozman et al. to vector meson transition couplings, motivated by the CERES heavy ion experiments showing ρ-meson mass modifications in medium and the theoretical framework of Rapp and Wambach (1997) using nucleon isobar-hole excitations. The results are intended to constrain the treatment of resonance propagation in nuclear matter at densities near normal nuclear matter density, where hadronic variables are appropriate, and to connect to the scaling relations of Brown and Rho (1991) at higher densities. The derived coupling constants for subthreshold resonances, which cannot be determined from empirical decay widths, provide input for dynamical models of nuclear matter such as those of Peters et al. (1998) and Friman et al. (1999).
