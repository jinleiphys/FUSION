# The Ground State Aspects and the Impact of Shell Structures on the Stability of Es-Isotopes

> In this work, we have analyzed the nuclear structure and several prospective decay characteristics of the $^{240-259}$Es$_{99}$ isotopes. For this we use Relativistic Mean Field model (RMF) with NL-SH and NL3* force parameter in an axially deformed oscillator basis. In structural properties, we have analyzed binding energy (B.E.), skin thickness ($r_{np}$) , charge radius ($r_c$), one neutron separation energy ($S_{1n}$), two neutron separation energy ($S_{2n}$), differential variation of two neutron separation energy ($dS_{2n}$), the single particle energy and its variation with quadrupole deformation parameter of Es isotopes. We have also estimated the $α$-decay, $β$-decay and cluster decay half lives of Es isotopes to analyze the shell structure and also to predict the suitable decay mode among them. The $α$-decay half-life periods are calculated using the MUDL and AKRE formulae using both our calculated Q-values and empirically assessable Q-values. In a similar manner, we have computed the half-lives of cluster decay using Universal Decay Law and HOROI formula. A longer decay half-life indicates a shell stabilized parent nucleus, while a small parent half-life suggests the shell stability of the daughter. This study provides us the insights regarding the structural changes with the change in neutron number enabling us to predict shell closures and nuclear stability. We found a shell/sub-shell closure at N = 154 for the NL-SH parameter set. This research aids in our comprehension of Es isotopes' shell structure and decay mechanism.

# Wiki Page: Ground State Aspects and Shell Structure Effects on the Stability of Einsteinium Isotopes

## Key claim

The authors identify a shell/sub-shell closure at neutron number N = 154 for Einsteinium isotopes using the NL-SH parameter set in the Relativistic Mean Field model, but this finding is parameter-dependent as the NL3* parameter set does not consistently reproduce the same closure. The claim is hedged by noting that the shell effect is observed primarily in separation energies and single-particle energy gaps, while charge radii do not show a corresponding kink at N = 154.

## Method

The authors employed the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) model with NL-SH and NL3* force parameters in an axially deformed harmonic oscillator basis, using a maximum of 20 oscillator shells for both fermions and bosons. They calculated ground state properties including binding energy, charge radius, neutron skin thickness, one- and two-neutron separation energies, and quadrupole deformation parameters for the isotopic chain 240-259Es. Alpha-decay half-lives were computed using the Modified Universal Decay Law (MUDL) and AKRE formulas, beta-decay half-lives using a semi-empirical formula from Sobhani et al. (2023), and cluster decay half-lives using the Universal Decay Law and the Horoi scaling law, with Q-values derived from both calculated and experimental binding energies.

## Key numbers

- The highest binding energy per nucleon (B.E./A) for NL3* is 7.493 MeV at N = 142; for NL-SH it is 7.525 MeV at N = 144; experimentally it is 7.486 MeV at N = 144, identifying 243Es as the most stable isotope in the series.
- Standard deviation in B.E./A compared to experimental data is 0.00704 for NL3* and 0.03158 for NL-SH.
- At N = 154, the quadrupole deformation parameter β2 is 0.28 for NL-SH and 0.2989 for NL3*.
- The neutron drip line is predicted near N = 163 for NL3* and N = 161 for NL-SH, based on negative one-neutron separation energies.
- Sharp drops in two-neutron separation energy (S2n) are observed at N = 148 and N = 154 for both parameter sets, indicating possible shell/sub-shell closures at 247Es and 253Es.
- Single-particle energy gaps are identified at N = 126 (between 3p1/2 and 1i11/2), N = 138 (between 1i11/2 and 1j15/2), N = 154 (between 1j15/2 and 2g9/2), and N = 164 (between 2g9/2 and 2g7/2).

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical and experimental investigations of shell closures in the heavy and superheavy region, including the N = 152 shell gap first experimentally found in 250Cf by Ghiorso et al. (1954) and later predicted by various studies. The authors note that Ismail et al. (2010) predicted enhanced stabilities at N = 152 and 162 from alpha-decay investigations, and that their own prior work on Fermium (Dash et al., 2025) reproduced shell closures at N = 152 and 162. The present study specifically examines the N = 154 gap in Einsteinium isotopes, which differs from the N = 152 gap seen in FRDM and experimental data, and finds that charge radii do not show a kink at N = 154, consistent with the weak shell effects in this region reported by Warbinek et al. (2024). The results enable further investigation of shell structure in the actinide region and may inform future synthesis attempts of superheavy elements using Es targets.
