# Insights into the Origin of Mass

> Atomic nuclei are the core of everything we can see. At the first level of approximation, their atomic weights are simply the sum of the masses of all the nucleons they contain. Each nucleon has a mass $m_N \approx 1\,$GeV, i.e. approximately 2000-times the electron mass. The Higgs boson produces the latter, but what produces the nucleon mass? This is the crux: the vast bulk of the mass of a nucleon is lodged with the energy needed to hold quarks together inside it; and that is supposed to be explained by quantum chromodynamics (QCD), the strong-interaction piece within the Standard Model. This contribution canvasses the potential for a coherent effort in QCD phenomenology and theory, coupled with experiments at existing and planned facilities, to reveal the origin and distribution of mass by focusing on the properties of the strong-interaction Nambu-Goldstone modes. Key experiments are approved at JLab 12; planned with COMPASS++/AMBER at CERN; and could deliver far-reaching insights by exploiting the unique capabilities foreseen at an electron ion collider.

## Key claim

The Higgs boson accounts for only about 1-2% of the mass of everyday matter; the vast bulk of nucleon mass (approximately 98%) is generated dynamically by quantum chromodynamics (QCD) through the energy needed to hold quarks together, and this emergent mass is most cleanly expressed in the properties of the nearly-massless pion, which is a Nambu-Goldstone mode of dynamical chiral symmetry breaking.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework of QCD's trace anomaly and the dynamical generation of a gluon mass-scale (m0 ≈ 0.5 GeV), then uses continuum Schwinger function methods and lattice QCD simulations to compute dressed-quark propagators, parton distribution amplitudes, and parton distribution functions for pions and kaons. These calculations are compared with existing Drell-Yan experimental data from the E615 experiment and with lattice QCD results to expose the role of emergent mass versus Higgs-generated mass in different meson systems.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon mass mN ≈ 1 GeV, approximately 2000 times the electron mass
- Renormalization-group-invariant gluon mass-scale m0 ≈ 0.5 GeV ≈ mp/2
- Dynamical gluon mass implies gluons with wavelengths λ ≳ 0.5 fm decouple from the strong interaction
- Dressed-quark mass function in the infrared is approximately mp/3
- The Higgs-generated current masses of u- and d-quarks are more than two orders of magnitude smaller than the proton mass
- Pion valence-quark PDF at the hadronic scale: u^π(x; ζ=ζ_H) ∼ (1-x)^2
- At ζ=2 GeV, the gluon momentum fraction in the pion is 50% greater than that in the kaon
- Kaon gluon content at ζ_K=0.51 GeV is 5±5%, while pion gluon content is more than 30%

## Context

This work extends the DCSB paradigm developed over decades, building on foundational studies of the gluon propagator by Cornwall (1981) and subsequent continuum and lattice analyses. It directly addresses the tension between the E615 Drell-Yan experiment (Conway, 1989) which reported a (1-x)^1 behavior for the pion PDF, and later NLO analyses with soft-gluon resummation (Aicher, 2010) that reconciled the data with the QCD prediction. The paper enables future experimental tests at JLab 12, COMPASS++/AMBER at CERN, and a planned electron-ion collider to validate predictions for pion and kaon structure functions, which would provide definitive evidence for the mechanism of emergent mass generation in the Standard Model.
