# Multinucleon ejection model for Meson Exchange Current neutrino interactions

> A model is proposed to describe pairs or triples of nucleons ejected from nucleus as a result of Meson Exchange Current neutrino interaction. The model can be easily implemented in Monte Carlo neutrino event generators. It can provide a help in identifying true charge current quasielastic events and allow for better determination of the systematic error of neutrino energy reconstruction in neutrino oscillation experiments

# Multinucleon ejection model for Meson Exchange Current neutrino interactions

## Key claim

The paper proposes a model for the final-state nucleon kinematics in two-body current (meson exchange current) neutrino interactions that can be implemented in Monte Carlo event generators, but the model's predictions for nucleon observables depend strongly on the choice of underlying muon inclusive cross section model and on the treatment of Final State Interactions (FSI), with the microscopic model and Transverse Enhancement Model (TEM) giving significantly different results for high-momentum proton pairs.

## Method

The model selects two or three nucleons from a Fermi sphere of radius 220 MeV (for carbon), computes the hadronic system four-momentum by adding the selected nucleon momenta to the energy and momentum transferred by the neutrino, performs an isotropic distribution of final-state nucleons in the hadronic center-of-mass frame, and weights each event by the muon inclusive differential cross section from either a microscopic model (based on the Marteau approach with RPA and Δ self-energy effects) or the Transverse Enhancement Model (TEM). The model is implemented in the NuWro Monte Carlo event generator, which includes nucleon rescattering effects via a cascade model, and the energy balance accounts for a potential well depth of V = Ef + 8 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Fermi sphere radius for carbon target: 220 MeV
- Potential well depth: V = Ef + 8 MeV, with final-state nucleon energy reduced by 8 MeV
- In CC neutrino reactions, approximately 80% of final-state nucleons are protons and 20% are neutrons; for antineutrinos the composition is opposite
- TEM parameters: A = 6 GeV⁻² and B = 0.34 GeV² for the transverse enhancement function
- Multinucleon ejection contribution amounts to about 25% of the quasielastic (QE) cross section
- For 750 MeV muon neutrinos, the microscopic model predicts 1805 signal proton pairs with both momenta above 500 MeV/c (from 2.5×10⁵ generated events), while the TEM predicts 651 such pairs
- For 750 MeV muon neutrinos, the microscopic model predicts 4201 signal proton pairs with both momenta above 400 MeV/c, while the TEM predicts 2271
- For 750 MeV muon neutrinos, the microscopic model predicts 7185 signal proton pairs with both momenta above 300 MeV/c, while the TEM predicts 5457
- On average, the true neutrino energy is larger by approximately 150-200 MeV than the reconstructed energy E_rec^QE when two-body current contributions are present
- The probability to have a signal pair of protons with both momenta above 500 MeV/c is only 0.26-0.72%
- The TEM predicts the cross section to be similar to CCQE with MA = 1.3 GeV for energies up to about 700 MeV, and at Eν ~ 5 GeV corresponds to CCQE with MA ~ 1.15 GeV
- The sharp fall in the TEM differential cross section occurs at approximately 460 MeV energy transfer
- On average, proton momenta are reduced by approximately 100 MeV/c due to rescatterings

## Context

This work extends the theoretical discussion of multinucleon ejection in neutrino-nucleus scattering, which was previously addressed by the Marteau model (based on Ericson and Delorme), the MEChM model by Martini, Ericson, Chanfray and Marteau, the relativistic model by Nieves, Ruiz Simo and Vicente Vacas, and the Transverse Enhancement Model by Bodek. The paper provides a practical framework for implementing these theoretical predictions in Monte Carlo generators, enabling experimental searches for two-body current signals through observables such as proton pair counting and integrated hadronic kinetic energy. The model enables better determination of systematic errors in neutrino energy reconstruction for oscillation experiments like T2K, and the author notes that all microscopic models discussed are based on the Local Fermi Gas model, with arguments from Carlson (year not specified in text) indicating that a more realistic nucleus ground state model is needed for evaluating the two-body current contribution.
