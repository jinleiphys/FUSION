# How Well Do We Know the Beta-Decay of 16N and Oxygen Formation in Helium Burning

> We review the status of the 12C(a,g)16O reaction rate, of importance for stellar processes in a progenitor star prior to a super-nova collapse. Several attempts to constrain the p-wave S-factor of the 12C(a,g)16O reaction at Helium burning temperatures (200 MK) using the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of 16N have been made, and it is claimed that this S-factor is known, as quoted by the TRIUMF collaboration. In contrast reanalyses (by G.M. hale) of all thus far available data (including the 16N data) does not rule out a small S-factor solution. Furthermore, we improved our previous Yale-UConn study of the beta- delayed alpha-particle emission of \n16 by improving our statistical sample (by more than a factor of 5), improving the energy resolution of the experiment (by 20%), and in understanding our line shape, deduced from measured quantities. Our newly measured spectrum of the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of 16N is not consistent with the TRIUMF('94) data, but is consistent with the Seattle('95) data, as well as the earlier (unaltered !) data of Mainz('71). The implication of this discrepancies for the extracted astrophysical p-wave s-factor is briefly discussed.

## Key claim

The p-wave astrophysical S-factor ($S_{E1}$) for the $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ reaction is not yet known with sufficient accuracy for stellar evolution models, despite claims from the TRIUMF collaboration that it has been measured; a reanalysis of all available data (including the $^{16}N$ data) by G.M. Hale does not rule out a small S-factor solution, and the sign of the interference of the two $1^-$ states cannot be directly determined from beta-decay data on $^{16}N$.

## Method

The authors produced $^{16}N$ via the $^{15}N(d,p)^{16}N$ reaction using a 70 MeV $^{15}N$ beam on a deuterium gas target, then measured the beta-delayed alpha-particle energy spectrum using thin silicon surface barrier detectors in coincidence with fast plastic scintillator detectors for beta timing. They improved upon their previous Yale-UConn experiment by increasing statistics by more than a factor of five (to 292,000 events), improving energy resolution by 20% (to 200 keV at 2.36 MeV), and deducing the line shape from measured quantities including $\partial E/\partial x$ data and the measured time resolution.

## Key numbers

- Statistical sample improved by more than a factor of 5 over the previous Yale-UConn experiment, reaching 292,000 events.
- Energy resolution improved by 20% to 200 keV at 2.36 MeV.
- The TRIUMF collaboration quoted $S_{E1} = 81 \pm 21$ keV-b based on their R-matrix analysis.
- Hale's best fit for the TRIUMF $^{16}N$ data gave an E1 S-factor of approximately 20 keV-b.
- The new Yale-UConn data gave a reduced chi-squared of 1.4 when compared to the Seattle theory curve and 7.2 when compared to the TRIUMF theory curve.
- The unaltered Mainz (1971) data agreed with the Seattle data (chi-squared per data point of 2.5) and disagreed with the TRIUMF data (chi-squared per data point of 123).
- The TRIUMF data disagree with other data sets by up to a factor of 3.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing effort to determine the $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ reaction rate, which is critical for understanding helium burning in massive stars and the resulting carbon-to-oxygen ratio that determines whether a supernova progenitor leaves a neutron star or a black hole (Weaver 1993). The paper contradicts the strong claim by the TRIUMF collaboration (Azuma 1994) that the p-wave S-factor is now known, and instead supports the Seattle data (Zhao 1995) and the original Mainz data (Waffler 1971). The unresolved discrepancy among data sets means that additional low-energy data on the $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ reaction itself are needed to determine the sign of the interference of the two $1^-$ states and extract a reliable S-factor.
