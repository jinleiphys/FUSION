# Non-perturbative regularization and renormalization: simple examples from non-relativistic quantum mechanics

> We examine several zero-range potentials in non-relativistic quantum mechanics. The study of such potentials requires regularization and renormalization. We contrast physical results obtained using dimensional regularization and cutoff schemes and show explicitly that in certain cases dimensional regularization fails to reproduce the results obtained using cutoff regularization. First we consider a delta-function potential in arbitrary space dimensions. Using cutoff regularization we show that for $d \ge 4$ the renormalized scattering amplitude is trivial. In contrast, dimensional regularization can yield a nontrivial scattering amplitude for odd dimensions greater than or equal to five. We also consider a potential consisting of a delta function plus the derivative-squared of a delta function in three dimensions. We show that the renormalized scattering amplitudes obtained using the two regularization schemes are different. Moreover we find that in the cutoff-regulated calculation the effective range is necessarily negative in the limit that the cutoff is taken to infinity. In contrast, in dimensional regularization the effective range is unconstrained. We discuss how these discrepancies arise from the dimensional regularization prescription that all power-law divergences vanish. We argue that these results demonstrate that dimensional regularization can fail in a non-perturbative setting.

## Key claim

Dimensional regularization (DR) can fail in non-perturbative calculations because it discards all power-law divergences, and in certain non-perturbative settings these divergences cannot always be absorbed into counterterms through renormalization, leading to physical results that differ from those obtained using cutoff regularization.

## Method

The authors solve the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for zero-range delta-function potentials in non-relativistic quantum mechanics, first for a pure delta-function potential in arbitrary spatial dimensions and then for a delta function plus derivative-squared of a delta function in three dimensions. They compare the renormalized scattering amplitudes obtained using dimensional regularization versus those obtained using a momentum-cutoff regularization scheme, with renormalization conditions fixed by physical observables such as the scattering length and effective range.

## Key numbers

- For a delta-function potential in odd dimensions \(d \geq 5\), cutoff regularization yields a trivial renormalized scattering amplitude \(T^{\rm on}_{\rm cutoff}(E)=0\), while dimensional regularization yields a nontrivial amplitude \(1/T^{\rm on}_{\rm DR}(E)=1/C_{\rm DR} + i (2\pi\mu)/[\Gamma(d/2)(4\pi)^{d/2}] (2\mu E)^{d/2-1}\) that can be nonzero.
- For the delta-function-plus-derivative-squared potential in three dimensions, the cutoff-regularized on-shell T-matrix is \(1/T^{\rm on}_{\rm cutoff}(E) = -(\mu/(2\pi))(-1/a + r_e \mu E - i\sqrt{2\mu E})\) with the constraint \(r_e \leq 0\).
- For the same potential in three dimensions, dimensional regularization gives \(1/T^{\rm on}_{\rm DR}(E) = -(\mu/(2\pi))(1/(-a - a^2 r_e \mu E) - i\sqrt{2\mu E})\) with no constraint on the sign of \(r_e\).
- In two and three dimensions for the pure delta-function potential, both regularization schemes yield equivalent renormalized amplitudes.

## Context

This work extends earlier rigorous results by Friedman (1976) showing that delta-function potentials in dimensions \(d>3\) are trivial, and by Wigner (1955) establishing a bound on the effective range for finite-range potentials. The paper directly addresses the use of zero-range potentials in effective field theory treatments of the nucleon-nucleon interaction, as developed by Weinberg (1990, 1991) and Kaplan et al. (1996). The results demonstrate that dimensional regularization, while convenient in perturbative settings, can produce inequivalent physical predictions in non-perturbative calculations, and that cutoff regularization enforces the Wigner bound \(r_e \leq 0\) for any potential of the form considered, regardless of the number of derivative terms included.
