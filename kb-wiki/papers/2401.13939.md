# Estimation of the slope of nuclear symmetry energy via charge radii of mirror nuclei

> Charge radii of mirror nuclei are calculated by implementing pairing effects with the Hartree-Fock Bogoliubov approximation. Correlations between the difference of charge radii ($ΔR_{ch}$) and slope of nuclear symmetry energy (L) are examined for different mirror nuclei pairs of varying masses using 40 different Skyrme energy density functionals. $ΔR_{ch}-L $ correlations are found to be robust for the binding constraints imposed on density functionals. We observe that $ΔR_{ch}$ and $L$ show better correlations in relatively heavier pairs than those obtained in the lighter pairs. Our calculations impose a constraint on the slope of nuclear symmetry energy as -20 MeV $\leq L \leq$ 55 MeV with 68\% confidence band using available measurements on charge radii. This is a moderately soft symmetry energy, in contrast to stiff and soft symmetry energy indicated by PREX-II and CREX measurements of neutron skin thickness in $^{208}Pb$ and $^{48}Ca$, respectively. Our result is also in agreement with celestial constraints obtained from observational data for neutron stars.

## Key claim

The authors find that the difference in charge radii of mirror nuclei (\(\Delta R_{ch}\)) correlates with the slope of nuclear symmetry energy (L), but pairing effects weaken this correlation, contrary to earlier claims that \(\Delta R_{ch}\) is a clean probe of L. Using available charge radius measurements, they constrain L to between -20 MeV and 55 MeV at 68% confidence, indicating a moderately soft symmetry energy that lies between the stiff value from PREX-II and the soft value from CREX.

## Method

The authors performed Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with a density-dependent delta-pairing force for five mirror nuclei pairs (masses 14 to 60) using 40 different Skyrme energy density functionals spanning L from approximately -30 MeV to 160 MeV. They computed charge radii from point proton radii with a 0.8 fm finite-size correction, then analyzed \(\Delta R_{ch}\) versus L correlations via least-square linear fits and Pearson correlation coefficients, both with and without pairing effects and under binding energy constraints of \(\pm 10\) MeV from experimental values.

## Key numbers

- Pearson correlation coefficients for \(\Delta R_{ch}\) vs L with pairing: 0.66 for \(^{18}\text{Ne}-^{18}\text{O}\), 0.83 for \(^{44}\text{Cr}-^{44}\text{Ca}\), 0.8 for \(^{58}\text{Zn}-^{58}\text{Ni}\), and 0.8 for \(^{60}\text{Ge}-^{60}\text{Ni}\); without pairing these increase to 0.76, 0.9, 0.89, and 0.96 respectively.
- For the \(^{54}\text{Ni}-^{54}\text{Fe}\) pair, the Pearson correlation coefficient is 0.75, yielding a constraint on L of -20 MeV \(\leq L \leq\) 55 MeV at 68% confidence band, with a maximum L of 62 MeV at 95% confidence.
- Experimental charge radii differences used: \(\Delta R_{ch} = 0.082(9)\) fm for \(^{34}\text{Ar}-^{34}\text{S}\), 0.150(4) fm for \(^{36}\text{Ca}-^{36}\text{S}\), 0.063(3) fm for \(^{38}\text{Ca}-^{38}\text{Ar}\), and 0.049(4) fm for \(^{54}\text{Ni}-^{54}\text{Fe}\).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Brown (2017) and Yang and Piekarewicz (2015) who proposed \(\Delta R_{ch}\) as a probe of L, but directly addresses the criticism by Reinhard and Naraewicz (2017) and Huang et al. (2020) that pairing correlations weaken the correlation. The authors confirm that pairing reduces the correlation strength, but show that correlations remain robust under binding energy constraints and improve for heavier nuclei. Their derived L constraint of -20 to 55 MeV is consistent with the combined analysis of neutron skin measurements and neutron star properties by Lattimer et al. (2023), with ab initio chiral EFT calculations by Hu et al. (2022), and with the S\(\pi\)RIT collaboration results from Sn+Sn collisions (Estee et al., 2021). This enables future measurements of charge radii in heavier mirror nuclei to provide independent constraints on the symmetry energy slope, complementing neutron skin measurements from PREX-II and CREX.
