# Quark contribution to the nucleon polarizabilities and three-body forces

> We study the response of the nucleon, as a system of three bound (constituent) non relativistic quarks, to external (quasi static) electric and magnetic fields. The approach, based on a sum rule technique, is applied to a large class of two and three-body interquark potentials. Lower and upper bounds to the electric polarizability and para-magnetic susceptibility are explicitly calculated within a large variety of constituent models and their values related to the features of the interquark interaction picture. The rôle of three-body forces is investigated in details as well as the effects of SU(6) breaking terms in the potential model. Our results can be used to extract the mesonic contributions to the static polarizabilty and susceptibility. The quark degrees of freedom give a quite sizeable contributions to both and the meson cloud accounts roughly for 30% and 60% of the electric proton and neutron polarizability respectively. The quark contribution to the paramagnetic susceptibility is even higher and the mesonic effects are rather uncertain.

## Key claim

The quark contribution to nucleon electric polarizabilities and paramagnetic susceptibility is sizeable, but the meson cloud accounts for roughly 30% of the proton and 60% of the neutron electric polarizability; the quark contribution to the paramagnetic susceptibility is even larger, though mesonic effects remain rather uncertain.

## Method

The authors apply a sum-rule technique to a non-relativistic constituent quark model of the nucleon, calculating lower and upper bounds to the electric polarizability and paramagnetic susceptibility from positive moments of the strength distribution. They evaluate these bounds for a range of two-body and three-body interquark potentials, including harmonic oscillator, Isgur-Karl, and hypercentral three-body force models, using ground-state wave functions obtained by solving the Schrödinger equation exactly.

## Key numbers

- Proton Compton electric polarizability: \(\bar \alpha_E^{\rm p} = (12.1 \pm 0.8 \pm 0.5) \times 10^{-4} \, {\rm fm}^3\) (global experimental analysis)
- Proton Compton magnetic polarizability: \(\bar \beta_M^{\rm p} = (2.1 \pm 0.8 \pm 0.5) \times 10^{-4} \, {\rm fm}^3\) (global experimental analysis)
- Neutron Compton electric polarizability: \(\bar \alpha_E^{\rm n} = (12.3 \pm 1.5 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-4} \, {\rm fm}^3\) (n-Pb scattering experiment)
- Neutron Compton magnetic polarizability: \(\bar \beta_M^{\rm n} = (3.5 \pm 1.6 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-4} \, {\rm fm}^3\) (extracted using sum rule \(\bar \alpha_E^{\rm n} + \bar \beta_M^{\rm n} = (15.8 \pm 0.5) \times 10^{-4} \, {\rm fm}^3\))
- Static neutron electric polarizability from n-Pb scattering: \(\alpha_E^{\rm n} = (12.0 \pm 1.5 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-4} \, {\rm fm}^3\)
- Sum rule constraint for proton: \(\bar \alpha_E^{\rm p} + \bar \beta_M^{\rm p} \approx 14.2 \pm 0.5 \times 10^{-4} \, {\rm fm}^3\) (from photoabsorption cross section dispersion relation)
- Meson cloud accounts for roughly 30% of the proton electric polarizability and 60% of the neutron electric polarizability
- Non-energy-weighted sum rule for electric dipole: \(m_0(D'_z) = \frac{1}{3} e^2 \langle r_{\rm p}^2 \rangle_{\rm ch}\) (for both proton and neutron in SU(6) symmetric wave functions)
- Model-independent linear energy-weighted sum rule: \(m_1(D'_z) = e^2 / (3m)\)
- Non-energy-weighted sum rule for magnetic dipole: \(m_0(\mu_z) = \frac{8}{9} \mu_0^2\) with \(\mu_0 = e/(2m)\)

## Context

This work extends earlier constituent quark model studies of nucleon polarizabilities (e.g., Drechsel et al. 1984, Traini et al. 1994) by systematically including three-body forces and using a sum-rule approach to obtain rigorous bounds rather than model-dependent estimates. The hypercentral three-body force model follows the work of Ferraris, Giannini, Pizzo, Santopinto, and Tiator (1995), which was motivated by the need to reproduce the Roper resonance in the baryon spectrum. The results enable extraction of the mesonic (cloud) contribution to the static polarizabilities by subtracting the calculated quark contribution from experimental values, and they provide a benchmark for chiral perturbation theory and dispersion relation approaches.
