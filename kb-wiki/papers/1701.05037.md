# The relation between stretched-exponential relaxation and the vibrational density of states in glassy disordered systems

> Amorphous solids or glasses are known to exhibit stretched-exponential decay over broad time intervals in several of their macroscopic observables: intermediate scattering function, dielectric relaxation modulus, time-elastic modulus etc. This behaviour is prominent especially near the glass transition. In this Letter we show, on the example of dielectric relaxation, that stretched-exponential relaxation is intimately related to the peculiar lattice dynamics of glasses. By reformulating the Lorentz model of dielectric matter in a more general form, we express the dielectric response as a function of the vibrational density of states (DOS) for a random assembly of spherical particles interacting harmonically with their nearest-neighbours. Surprisingly we find that near the glass transition for this system (which coincides with the Maxwell rigidity transition), the dielectric relaxation is perfectly consistent with stretched-exponential behaviour with Kohlrausch exponents $0.56 < β< 0.65$, which is the range where exponents are measured in most experimental systems. Crucially, the root cause of stretched-exponential relaxation can be traced back to soft modes (boson-peak) in the DOS.

# The relation between stretched-exponential relaxation and the vibrational density of states in glassy disordered systems

## Key claim
Stretched-exponential relaxation in glasses is directly caused by quasi-localized boson-peak modes in the vibrational density of states (DOS), specifically when the system is near the Maxwell rigidity transition at coordination number Z=6. The claim is conditional on the system being a random assembly of spherical particles interacting harmonically with nearest-neighbors, and the stretched-exponential behavior is demonstrated for dielectric relaxation with Kohlrausch exponents in the range 0.56 < β < 0.65, which matches most experimental systems.

## Method
The authors reformulated the Lorentz model of dielectric matter to express the dielectric response as a function of the vibrational DOS for a random lattice of harmonically-bound spherical particles. They obtained the DOS by numerical diagonalization of model random networks generated from a dense Lennard-Jones liquid driven into a metastable glassy energy minimum, with harmonic springs replacing nearest-neighbor pairs and springs cut randomly to vary the mean coordination Z from 9 down to the isostatic limit Z=6. The dielectric function was then evaluated using a sum rule over the discrete DOS, and the time-dependent dielectric response was obtained via Fourier inversion, with temperature related to Z through thermal expansion using the relation Z = Z₀ exp(-α_T T).

## Key numbers
- Kohlrausch exponents extracted from the model: β = 0.56, 0.65, 0.60, 0.60 for Z = 6.1, 7, 8, 9 respectively
- Relaxation times τ = 5655, 155, 50, 25 (arbitrary units) for Z = 6.1, 7, 8, 9 respectively
- For glycerol, Z ≈ 6.02 at T = 184 K, close to the reported T_g for this material
- For Z = 7, 8, and 9, corresponding temperatures calculated as T = 144 K, 108 K, and 77 K respectively
- The lowest non-zero eigenfrequency for Z = 6.1 is ω_{p,min} = 0.019 (dimensionless)
- The boson peak frequency scales as ω_p^{BP} ∼ (Z - 6)
- The ascending side of the dielectric loss peak follows a power law ∼ ω^1

## Context
This work extends the Goldhaber-Teller model (originally developed for the giant dipole resonance in atomic nuclei by Teller and collaborators) to disordered dielectrics, providing a first-principles link between lattice dynamics and stretched-exponential relaxation. It contrasts with the two existing models that predict stretched-exponential behavior: the exciton-hole recombination model (Lifshitz, Luttinger, Procaccia) which gives β = 0.6 in 3D but is not straightforward to apply to glasses, and Mode-Coupling Theory (Goetze, Mazenko) which applies only above the glass transition. The results enable a direct connection between α-relaxation, the boson peak, and dynamical heterogeneity in glasses, and build on prior work relating the boson peak to the Ioffe-Regel crossover (Ioffe, Tanaka, Schirmacher, Parshin) and to the absence of inversion symmetry in the lattice (Milkus).
