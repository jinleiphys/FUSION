# Microscopic optical potentials for nucleon-nucleus scattering

> Microscopic optical potentials for nucleon-nucleus (NA) scattering obtained from the full folding of the effective g matrices, solutions of the Bruckner-Bethe-Goldstone equation, with the densities of the target, are applied to the case of neutron-nucleus scattering. Given that the optical potentials are specified in all two-body angular momentum, spin and isospin channels available to the NA scattering, the only difference in this model description of proton and neutron scattering observables for a given nucleus is in the inclusion of the Coulomb interaction. A priori microscopic optical model predictions for neutron and proton elastic scattering are compared with results from a phenomenological optical model and with data. New measurements are recommended to reduce discrepancies in the existing database, and to differentiate between different theoretical predictions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a fully microscopic optical potential derived from the same $g$ matrix (Bonn B interaction) can describe both proton and neutron elastic scattering observables for a range of nuclei at 65 MeV, with the only difference being the inclusion of the Coulomb interaction for protons. The authors hedge that while the microscopic model reproduces neutron scattering data well, the phenomenological model often gives a better representation of proton scattering observables, and that new measurements are needed to resolve discrepancies between existing neutron data sets.

## Method

The authors construct microscopic optical potentials by folding effective $g$ matrices, obtained as solutions of the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone equation using the Bonn B nucleon-nucleon interaction, with target densities from shell model calculations (using OXBASH) or Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (for $^{208}$Pb). The resulting nonlocal optical potentials are used in a Schrödinger equation to predict differential cross sections, analyzing powers, and integral cross sections for 65 MeV neutron and proton elastic scattering from $^{12}$C, $^{28}$Si, $^{40}$Ca, $^{56}$Fe, $^{90}$Zr, and $^{208}$Pb. These predictions are compared with experimental data and with results from a global phenomenological optical potential (Madland, 1997) based on a relativistic Lane model.

## Key numbers

- For $n$-$^{12}$C scattering at 65 MeV, the microscopic model reproduces the total cross section much better than the phenomenological model; the microscopic model predicts a smaller elastic cross section and larger reaction cross section than the phenomenological model.
- For $n$-$^{28}$Si scattering at 65 MeV, both models predict the total cross section to within 3%.
- For $n$-$^{90}$Zr scattering at 65 MeV, both models agree with the measured total cross section to within 1%.
- For $n$-$^{208}$Pb scattering between 60 and 200 MeV, the microscopic model shows excellent agreement with total cross section data of Finlay et al., to within 1.5%; the phenomenological model underpredicts the total cross section by at most 4% above 80 MeV but fails below 80 MeV.
- The microscopic model predicts the total reaction cross section for $n$-$^{208}$Pb to within 10% of measurement.
- For $^{208}$Pb, the neutron and proton analyzing powers at 65 MeV are significantly different but not completely out of phase, unlike the 100 MeV case where they were completely out of phase (Kozack and Madland, 1990; Karataglidis and Madland, 2001).

## Context

This work extends the application of microscopic $g$-folding optical potentials, previously successful for proton-nucleus scattering (reviewed in a 1998 reference), to neutron-nucleus scattering. It supports the earlier phenomenological finding of Kozack and Madland (1990) that the Coulomb interaction causes proton and neutron analyzing powers to differ significantly for heavy nuclei, but shows this effect is energy-dependent. The paper identifies discrepancies between two existing neutron scattering data sets (Hjort et al., 1994 and Ibaraki et al., 2000) and recommends new measurements of neutron elastic scattering cross sections and analyzing powers, particularly for $^{40}$Ca, $^{90}$Zr, and $^{208}$Pb, to differentiate between the microscopic and phenomenological models.
