# From high $p_\perp$ theory and data to inferring anisotropy of Quark-Gluon Plasma

> High $p_\perp$ theory and data are commonly used to study high $p_\perp$ parton interactions with QGP, while low $p_\perp$ data and corresponding models are employed to infer QGP bulk properties. On the other hand, with a proper description of high $p_\perp$ parton-medium interactions, high $p_\perp$ probes become also powerful tomography tools, since they are sensitive to global QGP features, such as different temperature profiles or initial conditions. This tomographic role of high $p_\perp$ probes can be utilized to assess the spatial anisotropy of the QCD matter. With our dynamical energy loss formalism, we show that a (modified) ratio of $R_{AA}$ and $v_2$ presents a reliable and robust observable for straightforward extraction of initial state anisotropy. We analytically estimated the proportionality between the $v_2/(1-R_{AA})$ and anisotropy coefficient $ε_{2L}$, and found surprisingly good agreement with full-fledged numerical calculations. Within the current error bars, the extraction of the anisotropy from the existing data using this approach is still inaccessible. However, with the expected accuracy improvement in the upcoming LHC runs, the anisotropy of the QGP formed in heavy ion collisions can be straightforwardly derived from the data. Such a data-based anisotropy parameter would present an important test to models describing the initial stages of heavy-ion collision and formation of QGP, and demonstrate the usefulness of high $p_\perp$ theory and data in obtaining QGP properties.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the ratio \(v_2/(1-R_{AA})\) of high transverse momentum (\(p_\perp\)) particles provides a reliable and robust observable for extracting the initial-state spatial anisotropy of the Quark-Gluon Plasma, but that extraction from existing data is still inaccessible within current error bars; it will become feasible with the expected accuracy improvements in upcoming LHC runs.

## Method

The authors use their dynamical energy loss formalism (integrated into the DREENA-B numerical framework) to compute high-\(p_\perp\) observables \(R_{AA}\) and \(v_2\) for Pb+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV, assuming 1D Bjorken expansion. They derive an analytical scaling relation showing that \(v_2/(1-R_{AA})\) asymptotically approaches a constant proportional to the path-length anisotropy parameter \(\varsigma\) at high \(p_\perp\) (above about 20 GeV). They then compare full numerical predictions to CMS, ALICE, and ATLAS data and extract the anisotropy coefficient \(\epsilon_{2L}\) from the asymptotic plateau.

## Key numbers

* The exponents governing energy loss scaling are \(a \approx 1.2\) (temperature dependence) and \(b \approx 1.4\) (path-length dependence), extracted from the dynamical energy loss formalism.
* The proportionality constant in the relation \(v_2/(1-R_{AA}) \approx 0.57 \varsigma\) is derived as \(\frac{1}{2}(b - a/c) \approx 0.57\), where \(c \approx 4.3\).
* For \(p_\perp \gtrsim 20\) GeV, the ratio \(v_2/(1-R_{AA})\) becomes independent of \(p_\perp\) and approaches the asymptotic value \(0.57 \varsigma\) to within 5% accuracy, as shown by full numerical calculations.
* The extracted \(\epsilon_{2L}\) band is narrower than the spread among different initial-state model predictions (e.g., MC-Glauber vs. MC-KLN), providing resolving power to distinguish between models.

## Context

This work extends the use of high-\(p_\perp\) probes beyond studying parton-medium interactions to inferring bulk QGP properties, specifically spatial anisotropy. It contrasts with the common approach where low-\(p_\perp\) data and models are used for bulk properties, and high-\(p_\perp\) data are reserved for parton energy loss studies. The proposed method is complementary to initial-state model calculations such as MC-Glauber (Miller et al.), EKRT (Eskola et al.), IP-Glasma (Schenke et al.), and MC-KLN (Kharzeev et al.), which give notably different eccentricity predictions. The approach enables a data-driven test of initial-stage models once high-\(p_\perp\) measurements achieve sufficient precision in future LHC runs.
