# Reexamine the nuclear chiral geometry from the orientation of the angular momentum

> The paradox on the previous interpretation for the nuclear chiral geometry based on the effective angle has been clarified by reexamining the system with the particle-hole configuration $π(1h_{11/2})^1 \otimes ν(1h_{11/2})^{-1}$ and rotor with deformation parameter $γ=30^\circ$. It is found that the paradox is caused by the fact that the angular momentum of the rotor is much smaller than those of the proton and the neutron near the bandhead. Hence, it does not support a chiral rotation interpretation near the bandhead. The nuclear chiral geometry based on the effective angle makes sense only when the angular momentum of the rotor becomes comparable with those of the proton and the neutron at the certain spin region.

## Key claim

The paradox that effective angles between the proton, neutron, and rotor angular momenta appear near 90 degrees at the bandhead (suggesting chiral geometry) is resolved by showing that the rotor angular momentum is much smaller than those of the proton and neutron near the bandhead; therefore, the effective-angle-based chiral geometry interpretation is valid only when the rotor angular momentum becomes comparable to those of the valence particles at higher spin.

## Method

The particle-rotor model (PRM) was used to study a system with one proton particle in the \(h_{11/2}\) orbital and one neutron hole in the \(h_{11/2}\) orbital coupled to a triaxial rigid rotor with deformation parameters \(\beta = 0.23\) and \(\gamma = 30.0^\circ\). The effective angles between angular momenta, the normalized orientation parameter, and azimuthal plots of the total angular momentum distribution were calculated as functions of spin to examine the angular momentum geometry.

## Key numbers

- The effective angles \(\theta_{pn}\), \(\theta_{Rp}\), and \(\theta_{Rn}\) are about \(120^\circ\) at \(I=0\hbar\) and drop to about \(90^\circ\) at the bandhead \(I=8\hbar\).
- The normalized orientation parameter \(o\) reaches its maximal value at \(I=15\)–\(17\hbar\), corresponding to the smallest energy difference \(\Delta E\) between doublet bands and the static chirality region.
- At the static chiral region (\(15 \leq I \leq 17\hbar\)), the effective angles \(\theta_{pn}\), \(\theta_{Rp}\), and \(\theta_{Rn}\) are about \(70^\circ\), not \(90^\circ\).
- The rotational frequency \(\hbar\omega\) is negative below \(I=8\hbar\), near zero at the bandhead, and shows a kink at \(I=13\hbar\) marking the onset of aplanar rotation.
- At the static chiral region, the projection of the rotor angular momentum along the total spin \(R_I\) is approximately equal to the projection of the particle angular momentum \(J_I\) (\(R_I \approx J_I\)).

## Context

This work resolves a paradox in the interpretation of nuclear chiral geometry based on effective angles, as previously used by Starosta et al. (2001, 2002) and Wang et al. (2007). It extends the understanding that chiral rotation (static chirality) exists only above a certain critical frequency, consistent with earlier findings by Olbratowski et al. (2004, 2006) and Zhao et al. (2017). The results clarify that the effective-angle method is reliable only when the rotor angular momentum is comparable to those of the valence particles, enabling more accurate identification of chiral doublet bands in future experimental and theoretical studies.
