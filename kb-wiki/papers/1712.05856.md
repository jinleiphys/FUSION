# Revisiting the role of the $(n,γf)$ process in the low-energy fission of $^{235}$U and $^{239}$Pu

> The $(n,γf)$ process is reviewed in light of modern nuclear reaction calculations in both slow and fast neutron-induced fission reactions on $^{235}$U and $^{239}$Pu. Observed fluctuations of the average prompt fission neutron multiplicity and average total $γ$-ray energy below 100 eV incident neutron energy are interpreted in this framework. The surprisingly large contribution of the M1 transitions to the pre-fission $γ$-ray spectrum of $^{239}$Pu is explained by the dominant fission probabilities of 0$^+$ and $2^+$ transition states, which can only be accessed from compound nucleus states formed by the interaction of $s$-wave neutrons with the target nucleus in its ground state, and decaying through M1 transitions. The impact of an additional low-lying M1 scissors mode in the photon strength function is analyzed. We review experimental evidence for fission fragment mass and kinetic energy fluctuations in the resonance region and their importance in the interpretation of experimental data on prompt neutron data in this region. Finally, calculations are extended to the fast energy range where $(n,γf)$ corrections can account for up to 3\% of the total fission cross section and about 20\% of the capture cross section.

# Revisiting the role of the $(n,\gamma f)$ process in the low-energy fission of $^{235}$U and $^{239}$Pu

## Key claim

The $(n,\gamma f)$ process, where a compound nucleus emits a gamma ray before fissioning, can explain observed fluctuations in prompt fission neutron multiplicity and total gamma-ray energy below 100 eV incident neutron energy, but its contribution is significant only when the direct fission width is small (e.g., for $1^+$ resonances in $^{240}$Pu$^*$), and the effect is masked when direct fission dominates (e.g., for $0^+$ resonances in $^{240}$Pu$^*$ and $3^-$ resonances in $^{236}$U$^*$).

## Method

The authors calculated the $(n,\gamma f)$ width using an integral over gamma-ray energy that combines the radiative capture width (for E1 and M1 transitions only) with the fission probability at the residual excitation energy, assuming at most one gamma ray is emitted prior to fission. They used the QPVR (Quasi-Particle, Vibrational and Rotational) level density model with Nilsson single-particle states, a double-humped fission barrier with R-matrix fission probabilities, and photon strength functions including E1 giant resonance, M1 giant resonance, and an additional low-lying M1 scissors mode. Two alternative models for the vibrational resonances were employed: a secondary well vibration model and a tertiary well vibration model.

## Key numbers

- For $^{240}$Pu$^*$ $0^+$ resonances: average two-step fission width $\langle \Gamma_{\gamma f}\rangle = 2.8\pm 9.2$ meV (Shcherbakov 1990), compared to average direct fission width $\langle \Gamma_f\rangle = 2270$ meV (Trochon 1970)
- For $^{240}$Pu$^*$ $1^+$ resonances: average two-step fission width $\langle \Gamma_{\gamma f}\rangle = 1.91\pm 0.81$ meV (Shcherbakov 1990), compared to average direct fission width $\langle \Gamma_f\rangle = 33.7\pm 5$ meV (Trochon 1970)
- For $^{236}$U$^*$ $3^-$ resonances: average two-step fission width $\langle \Gamma_{\gamma f}\rangle = 0.87\pm 0.89$ meV (Shcherbakov 1990), compared to average direct fission width $\langle \Gamma_f\rangle = 180\pm 18$ meV (Moore 1978)
- For $^{236}$U$^*$ $4^-$ resonances: average two-step fission width $\langle \Gamma_{\gamma f}\rangle = 0.32\pm 0.13$ meV (Shcherbakov 1990), compared to average direct fission width $\langle \Gamma_f\rangle = 91\pm 11$ meV (Moore 1978)
- Total radiation width calculated for $^{240}$Pu$^*$ $0^+$ resonances: 37.9 meV; for $1^+$ resonances: 38.5 meV (without scissors mode)
- Average capture width for $^{240}$Pu$^*$ from Mughabghab (2006): $43.0\pm 4.0$ meV
- Average capture width for $^{236}$U$^*$ from Mughabghab (2006): $38.1\pm 1.7$ meV
- In the fast energy range, $(n,\gamma f)$ corrections account for up to 3% of the total fission cross section and about 20% of the capture cross section
- Fission barrier parameters for $^{240}$Pu$^*$: inner barrier height $V_A = 5.65$ MeV with $\hbar\omega_A = 1.05$ MeV; outer barrier height $V_B = 5.23$ MeV with $\hbar\omega_B = 0.6$ MeV; secondary well ground-state energy $E_{II,G} = 2.95$ MeV
- Fission barrier parameters for $^{236}$U$^*$: inner and outer barrier heights both $V_A = V_B = 5.56$ MeV; tertiary well ground-state energy $5.37$ MeV
- M1 scissors mode parameters used: mode strength $0.34$ mb.MeV, width $\Gamma_{M1,sc} = 0.65$ MeV, energy near 2 MeV

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of the $(n,\gamma f)$ process originally predicted by Lynn (1965) and Stavinsky (1965), and builds on experimental evidence from correlation measurements by Shackleton (1972), Ryabov (1973), Frehaut (1974), Howe (1976), and Moore (1978). The calculations use modern double-humped fission barrier models and the QPVR level density approach of Bouland (2013), and incorporate recent findings on the M1 scissors mode from Guttormsen (2012), Ullmann (2014), and Mumpower (2017). The results enable improved nuclear data evaluations for reactor applications, particularly for understanding fluctuations in prompt fission neutron multiplicity in the resonance region of $^{239}$Pu and $^{235}$U that impact criticality benchmarks, as emphasized by the WPEC Subgroup 34 and an IAEA coordinated research project (Capote 2016).
