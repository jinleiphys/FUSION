# Production of n-rich nuclei in red giant stars

> We outline a partial historical summary of the steps through which the nucleosynthesis phenomena induced by {\it slow} neutron captures (the {\it s-process}) were clarified, a scientific achievement in which Franz Käppeler played a major role. We start by recalling the early phenomenological approach, which yielded a basic understanding of the subject even before models for the parent stellar evolutionary stages were developed. Through such a tool, rough limits for the neutron density and exposure were set, and the crucial fact was understood that more than one nucleosynthesis component is required to account for solar abundances of $s$-process nuclei up to the Pb-Bi region. We then summarize the gradual understanding of the stellar processes actually involved in the production of nuclei from Sr to Pb (the so-called {\it Main Component}, achieved in the last decade of the past century and occurring in red giants of low and intermediate mass, ($M \lesssim$ 8 $M_{\odot}$), populating, in the {\it HR} diagram, the {\it Asymptotic Giant Branch} or {\it AGB} region. We conclude by giving some details on more recent research concerning mixing mechanisms inducing the activation of the main neutron source, $^{13}$C($α$,n)$^{16}$O.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the main neutron source for the slow neutron capture process (s-process) in low-mass Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB) stars is the $^{13}$C($\alpha$,n)$^{16}$O reaction, but this requires a physical mechanism for mixing protons from the envelope into the He-rich intershell region, a process that is not included in standard stellar models and remains an area of active research.

## Method
The paper provides a historical review of the phenomenological approach to the s-process, which uses exponential distributions of neutron exposures to fit the solar $\sigma N$ curve, and then summarizes the evolution of stellar models for thermally-pulsing AGB stars. It details the shift from the $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n)$^{25}$Mg neutron source to the $^{13}$C($\alpha$,n)$^{16}$O source for low-mass stars and describes recent magneto-hydrodynamical (MHD) models exploring magnetic buoyancy as a mechanism for the required extra-mixing of protons during third dredge-up (TDU) episodes.

## Key numbers
- The $^{13}$C($\alpha$,n)$^{16}$O neutron source burns at a temperature of $T \lesssim 1.5 \times 10^8$ K (approximately 12 keV), while the $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n)$^{25}$Mg source requires higher temperatures of $T \gtrsim 3 \times 10^8$ K.
- The duration of a third dredge-up (TDU) episode is just a few centuries, normally from 1 to 4, depending on stellar mass and pulse number.
- The mass of the He-rich layer polluted by protons via magnetic buoyancy is normally around 0.004 to 0.005 solar masses ($M_{\odot}$).
- The luminosity function of Galactic carbon stars from GAIA confirms their luminosities are in line with theoretical predictions for AGB stars below about 3 $M_{\odot}$ ($M_{bol} \simeq -5$).

## Context
This review extends the foundational work on the s-process by Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler and Hoyle (1957) and the phenomenological approach of Clayton et al. (1961) and Seeger et al. (1965). It specifically addresses the crisis identified by Iben (1981) that low-mass AGB stars, which are the primary s-process sites, are too cool for the $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n)$^{25}$Mg source, and it summarizes the subsequent search for a physical mixing mechanism to activate the $^{13}$C($\alpha$,n)$^{16}$O source, as first suggested by Iben and Renzini (1982). The work enables future studies to move beyond parameterized models of extra-mixing and toward a self-consistent, physically motivated understanding of neutron capture nucleosynthesis in AGB stars.
