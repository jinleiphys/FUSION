# Monte-Carlo Approach to Calculating the Fragmentation Functions in NJL-Jet Model

> Recent studies of the fragmentation functions using the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio (NJL) - Jet model have been successful in describing the quark fragmentation functions to pions and kaons. The NJL-Jet model employs the integral equation approach to solve for the fragmentation functions in quark-cascade description of the hadron emission process, where one assumes that the initial quark has infinite momentum and emits an infinite number of hadrons. Here we introduce a Monte Carlo (MC) simulation method to solve for the fragmentation functions,, that allows us to relax the above mentioned approximations. We demonstrate that the results of MC simulations closely reproduce the solutions of the integral equations in the limit where a large number of hadrons are emitted in the quark cascade. The MC approach provides a strong foundation for the further development of the NJL-Jet model that might include many more hadronic emission channels with decays of the possible produced resonances, as well as inclusion of the transverse momentum dependence (TMD), all of which are of considerable importance to the experimental studies of the transverse structure of hadrons.

## Key claim

The authors introduce a Monte Carlo (MC) simulation method for calculating fragmentation functions within the NJL-Jet model that relaxes the assumptions of infinite initial quark momentum and infinite number of emitted hadrons, while demonstrating that the MC results closely reproduce the integral equation solutions in the limit of a large number of emitted hadrons.

## Method

The authors replace the previously used coupled integral equations for fragmentation functions with a Monte Carlo simulation that randomly samples elementary fragmentation probabilities (calculated within the NJL model) at each hadron emission vertex in a quark cascade. The simulation tracks the flavor and light-cone momentum fraction of the initial quark remaining after each emission, recording the type and momentum fraction transferred to each emitted hadron. The fragmentation chain stops after a predefined number of emitted hadrons (N_Links) or when the remnant quark's momentum fraction falls below a threshold (z_Min), and the fragmentation functions are extracted by averaging over many simulations (N_Sims).

## Key numbers

- The MC simulation with N_Links = 8 and N_Links = 20 produces solutions for the fragmentation function z D_u^{π+}(z) that are virtually identical, with discrepancy from the integral equation solution only occurring at "vanishingly small values of z"
- The solution with N_Links = 1 (equivalent to the elementary fragmentation function z d̂_u^{π+}(z)) is peaked at z ~ 0.8
- The MC approach can reliably use solutions with N_Links ≥ 8

## Context

This work extends the NJL-Jet model developed by Ito (2009) and Matevosyan (2010), which used integral equations to solve for fragmentation functions under the assumptions of infinite momentum and infinite hadron emission. The MC approach enables future extensions that were impractical with integral equations, including inclusion of vector meson and nucleon-antinucleon channels, strong decays of produced resonances, and transverse momentum dependence (TMD) of fragmentation functions, all relevant to experimental studies of hadron transverse structure.
