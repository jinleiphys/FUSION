# Hadronic molecules

> A large number of experimental discoveries especially in the heavy quarkonium sector that did not at all fit to the expectations of the until then very successful quark model led to a renaissance of hadron spectroscopy. Among various explanations of the internal structure of these excitations, hadronic molecules, being analogues of light nuclei, play a unique role since for those predictions can be made with controlled uncertainty. We review experimental evidences of various candidates of hadronic molecules, and methods of identifying such structures. Nonrelativistic effective field theories are the suitable framework for studying hadronic molecules, and are discussed in both the continuum and finite volumes. Also pertinent lattice QCD results are presented. Further, we discuss the production mechanisms and decays of hadronic molecules, and comment on the reliability of certain assertions often made in the literature.

## Key claim
Hadronic molecules, defined as QCD bound states of two hadrons analogous to the deuteron, are a unique class of exotic hadrons for which predictions can be made with controlled uncertainty, but many claimed candidates remain challenged by kinematic explanations such as threshold cusp effects or triangle singularities, and further experimental and theoretical studies are necessary to settle which states indeed correspond to S-matrix poles.

## Method
The authors review experimental evidence for candidate hadronic molecules across light meson, open heavy-flavor, charmonium, bottomonium, and baryon sectors, compiling masses, widths, quantum numbers, and distances to nearby S-wave thresholds from published experimental data and the Particle Data Group. They formulate nonrelativistic effective field theories (NREFT) for studying hadronic molecules in both continuum and finite volume, discuss lattice QCD results, and analyze production mechanisms and decays while critiquing common assertions in the literature about molecular identification.

## Context
This review extends the line of work on exotic hadron spectroscopy following the renaissance triggered by discoveries in the heavy quarkonium sector that contradicted quark model expectations, building on earlier reviews by Klempt (2007, 2009), Brambilla (2010), Olsen (2014), Oset (2016), Chen (2016), Esposito (2016), Lebed (2016), Hosaka (2016), Dong (2017), and Olsen (2017). The paper provides a systematic framework using Weinberg's compositeness criterion (1962, 1963) and nonrelativistic effective field theories to distinguish hadronic molecules from compact multiquark states, enabling future lattice QCD calculations and experimental searches to test molecular assignments for candidates such as $X(3872)$, $Z_c(3900)$, and $Z_b(10610)$.
