# Stochastic Mean-Field Theory and Applications to Multinucleon Transfer and Kinetic Energy Dissipation Processes in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> In this Review article, a brief description of the stochastic mean-field theory (SMF) for describing reaction dynamics in low-energy heavy-ion collisions at bombarding energies in the vicinity of the Coulomb barrier is presented. In these collisions, as a result of strong Pauli blocking, binary nucleon collisions do not have a significant effect on the dissipation and fluctuations. At low energies, the mean-field fluctuations, due to initial correlations, have a dominant effect on fluctuations of macroscopic variables. The SMF theory proposes the determination of an ensemble of single-particle density matrices by specifying random initial fluctuations according to a distribution law. Employing an ensemble of single-particle density matrices, not only the mean values but also the distribution functions of the one-body observables can be determined. If the di-nuclear structure is maintained in heavy-ion collisions, such as deep inelastic collisions and fast quasi-fission reactions, a much simpler description of the reaction mechanism can be derived in terms of several macroscopic variables such as mass and charge asymmetry, and relative linear and relative angular momentum. In this case, by geometric projection of the SMF equations, it is possible to derive the quantal Langevin equations for macroscopic variables. As an application of quantal transport description, an analysis of multinucleon transfers and kinetic energy dissipation and fluctuations is presented for selected quasi-fission reactions.

# Stochastic Mean-Field Theory and Applications to Multinucleon Transfer and Kinetic Energy Dissipation Processes in Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The stochastic mean-field (SMF) theory provides a microscopic framework for describing fluctuations in low-energy heavy-ion collisions by incorporating initial correlations through an ensemble of single-particle density matrices, but only when binary nucleon collisions are suppressed by strong Pauli blocking and the di-nuclear structure is maintained throughout the collision. The approach enables derivation of quantal Langevin equations for macroscopic variables through geometric projection of the SMF equations, though the linearized treatment is strictly valid only for small amplitude fluctuations around the mean-field trajectory.

## Method

The SMF theory generates an ensemble of initial single-particle density matrices by specifying random initial fluctuations according to uncorrelated Gaussian distributions, with mean values and variances chosen to reproduce the quantum mechanical expectation values and variances of one-body observables at the initial time. Each member of the ensemble is evolved using the time-dependent self-consistent mean-field Hamiltonian, producing an ensemble of trajectories from which both mean values and distribution functions of one-body observables can be extracted. For reactions where a di-nuclear structure is maintained, the SMF equations are projected onto macroscopic variables (neutron and proton numbers of fragments, relative linear and angular momentum) to derive coupled quantal Langevin equations, which are then linearized around the mean TDHF trajectory to obtain a Fokker-Planck description for the fragment mass and charge distributions.

## Key numbers

- Bombarding energies considered are in the vicinity of the Coulomb barrier, at energies per nucleon in the range of nucleonic binding energy (8-10 MeV/nucleon), where Pauli blocking suppresses binary collisions
- The smoothing function used in the window dynamics employs a Gaussian with dispersion κ = 1.0 fm, taken to be on the order of the lattice spacing
- The SMF theory was originally proposed in 2008 by Ayik, with subsequent demonstration that it captures many-body correlations beyond one-body fluctuations through the BBGKY hierarchy (Lacroix, 2016)
- Applications to quasi-fission reactions include systems such as ⁶⁰Ni+⁶⁰Ni (Williams, 2018) and ¹⁷⁶Yb+¹⁷⁶Yb (Godbey, 2020), where mass and charge dispersions were calculated and compared with available data

## Context

This review extends the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) approach, which accurately describes mean reaction paths and average fragment properties but severely underestimates fragment mass and charge dispersions. The SMF theory provides an alternative to the time-dependent random-phase approximation (TDRPA) developed by Balian and Vénéroni (1981), which improves mean-field descriptions but cannot currently be applied to asymmetric collisions. The SMF approach enables calculation of probability distributions for multinucleon transfer products in quasi-fission reactions, with potential applications to producing neutron-rich isotopes along the N=126 magic number for r-process nucleosynthesis studies and to reaching the island of stability in superheavy nuclei.
