# Lorentz-symmetry test at Planck-scale suppression with nucleons in a spin-polarized $^{133}$Cs cold atom clock

> We introduce an improved model that links the frequency shift of the $^{133}\text{Cs}$ hyperfine Zeeman transitions $\vert F = 3, m_F> \longleftrightarrow \vert F = 4, m_F >$ to the Lorentz-violating Standard-Model Extension (SME) coefficients of the proton and neutron. The new model uses Lorentz transformations developed to second order in boost and additionally takes the nuclear structure into account, beyond the simple Schmidt model used previously in SME analyses, thereby providing access to both proton and neutron SME coefficients including the isotropic coefficient $\tilde{c}_{TT}$. Using this new model in a second analysis of the data delivered by the FO2 dual Cs/Rb fountain at Paris Observatory and previously analysed in arXiv:hep-ph/0601024v1, we improve by up to 12 orders of magnitude the present maximum sensitivities (see arXiv:0801.0287v9) on the $\tilde{c}_{Q}$, $\tilde{c}_{TJ}$ and $\tilde{c}_{TT}$ coefficients for the neutron and on the $\tilde{c}_{TT}$ coefficient for the proton, reaching respectively $10^{-20}$, $10^{-17}$, $10^{-13}$ and $10^{-15}$ GeV.

## Key claim

The paper presents an improved model for linking hyperfine transition frequency shifts in a $^{133}$Cs atomic fountain clock to Lorentz-violating Standard-Model Extension (SME) coefficients of the proton and neutron, using Lorentz transformations to second order in boost and accounting for nuclear structure beyond the simple Schmidt model. The authors claim improvements of up to 12 orders of magnitude on previous maximum sensitivities for several SME coefficients, but these results depend on the specific nuclear model used and the assumption that no Lorentz violation is present.

## Method

The authors re-analyzed data from the FO2 dual Cs/Rb fountain clock at Paris Observatory, previously analyzed in Wolf (2006), using Ramsey interferometry on spin-polarized $^{133}$Cs atoms in $\ket{F=3,m_F} \longleftrightarrow \ket{F=4,m_F}$ transitions with $m_F = +3, -3, 0$. They developed a new model incorporating Lorentz transformations to second order in boost (including Earth's orbital boost $\beta_\oplus \sim 10^{-4}$ and lab boost $\beta_l \sim 10^{-6}$) and used self-consistent relativistic mean field (SCRMF) theory to calculate nuclear momentum quadrupole moments, enabling separate constraints on proton and neutron SME coefficients including the isotropic coefficient $\tilde{c}_{TT}$.

## Key numbers

- Sensitivity on neutron $\tilde{c}_Q$ coefficient: $10^{-20}$ GeV
- Sensitivity on neutron $\tilde{c}_{TJ}$ coefficient: $10^{-17}$ GeV
- Sensitivity on neutron $\tilde{c}_{TT}$ coefficient: $10^{-13}$ GeV
- Sensitivity on proton $\tilde{c}_{TT}$ coefficient: $10^{-15}$ GeV
- Earth's orbital boost magnitude: $\beta_\oplus \sim 10^{-4}$ (ratio of velocity to speed of light)
- Lab boost due to Earth's rotation: $\beta_l \sim 10^{-6}$
- Overall scaling factor for SME coefficients: $9/7h = 3.1 \times 10^{23}$ Hz/GeV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Wolf (2006) by including second-order boost terms that give access to the isotropic $\tilde{c}_{TT}$ coefficient, which was previously inaccessible in first-order analyses and was among the least constrained SME coefficients from non-gravitational experiments. The use of SCRMF theory goes beyond the simple Schmidt model used in prior SME analyses by Kostelecky (1999) and Bluhm (2003), enabling separate constraints on proton and neutron coefficients. The results challenge expected Planck-scale suppression factors of $10^{-17}$ GeV from quantum gravity phenomenology and enable future experiments to set limits on the cut-off energy scale $E$ in effective field theory approaches.
