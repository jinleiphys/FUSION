# Phase diagram of helically imbalanced QCD matter

> We discuss the influence of a helicity imbalance on the phase diagram of dense QCD at finite temperature. We argue that the helical chemical potential is a thermodynamically relevant quantity in theories with the mass gap generation. Using the linear sigma model coupled to quarks, we show that the presence of the helical density substantially affects the phase diagram of dense quark matter. A moderate helical density makes the chiral phase transition softer while shifting the critical endpoint towards lower temperatures and higher baryon chemical potentials. As the helical density increases, the segment of the first-order transition disappears, and the chiral transition becomes a soft crossover. At even higher helical chemical potentials, the first-order transition reappears again at the zero-density finite-temperature transition and extends into the interior of the phase diagram. This evolution of the chiral transition reflects the existence of a thermodynamic duality between helical and vector (baryonic) chemical potentials. We also show that the presence of the helicity imbalance of quark matter increases the curvature of the chiral pseudocritical line in QCD.

## Key claim

The helical chemical potential is thermodynamically consistent for massive fermions (unlike the axial chemical potential), and its presence substantially alters the QCD phase diagram: moderate helical density softens the chiral phase transition and shifts the critical endpoint toward lower temperatures and higher baryon chemical potentials, while at higher helical chemical potentials the first-order transition reappears, reflecting a thermodynamic duality between helical and vector (baryonic) chemical potentials.

## Method

The authors used the linear sigma model coupled to quarks (LSMq), a low-energy effective model of QCD containing a doublet of light quarks and the chiral fields σ and π. They introduced a helical chemical potential μ_H into the Lagrangian alongside the vector (baryonic) chemical potential μ_V, computed the thermodynamic potential via the Matsubara formalism, and determined the phase diagram by analyzing the chiral condensate as a function of temperature, baryon chemical potential, and helical chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- The vector density at small mass and finite temperature is n_V = μ_V T²/3 + μ_V³/(3π²) − μ_V m²/(2π²) + O(m⁴)
- The helical density at small mass and finite temperature is n_H = μ_H T²/3 + μ_H³/(3π²) − μ_H m²/(2π²) + O(m⁴)
- For massless fermions at zero temperature, the axial density is n_A = μ_A³/(3π²)
- For massive fermions at zero temperature and high axial chemical potential (μ_A ≫ m), the axial density diverges logarithmically as n_A = μ_A³/(3π²) + (m² μ_A/π²) ln(Λ_UV/m) + ...
- In the presence of both vector and helical chemical potentials, the densities at small mass are n_V = μ_V T²/3 + (μ_V³ + 3 μ_V μ_H²)/(3π²) − μ_V m²/(2π²) + O(m⁴) and n_H = μ_H T²/3 + (μ_H³ + 3 μ_H μ_V²)/(3π²) − μ_H m²/(2π²) + O(m⁴)

## Context

This work extends the study of axial charge effects in QCD matter (Ruggieri 2011, Chernodub 2011, Gatto 2011) to the less-explored helical quantum number, which is conserved for massive fermions unlike chirality. The authors show that the helical chemical potential is thermodynamically consistent even with mass gap generation, in contrast to the axial chemical potential which leads to divergences for massive fermions as shown by Marco (Ref. [ref:Marco] in the paper). The results enable future studies of helical transport phenomena in heavy-ion collisions and connect to the helical vortical effects discussed by Ambrus (2019).
