# Effective interaction approach to the Fermi hard-sphere system

> The formalism based on correlated basis functions and the cluster expansion technique has been recently employed to derive an effective interaction from a realistic nuclear hamiltonian. To gauge the reliability of this scheme, we perform a systematic comparison between the results of its application to the Fermi hard-sphere system and the predictions obtained from low-density expansions, as well as from other many-body techniques. The analysis of a variety of properties, including the ground state energy, the effective mass and the momentum distribution, shows that the effective interaction approach is remarkably accurate, thus suggesting that it may be employed to achieve a consistent description of the structure and dynamics of nuclear matter in the density region relevant to astrophysical applications.

# Effective interaction approach to the Fermi hard-sphere system

## Key claim

The effective interaction derived from correlated basis functions (CBF) and the cluster expansion technique, when applied to the Fermi hard-sphere system, yields remarkably accurate results for ground state energy, effective mass, and momentum distribution compared to low-density expansions and other many-body techniques, suggesting it may be employed for nuclear matter descriptions in the density region relevant to astrophysical applications, provided the correlation range is adjusted to reproduce the Fermi Hyper-Netted Chain (FHNC) ground state energy at the two-body cluster level.

## Method

The authors derived an effective interaction for the Fermi hard-sphere system using the CBF formalism, where correlated states are obtained from non-interacting Fermi gas states through a correlation operator, and the effective interaction is defined by equating the expectation value of the Hamiltonian in the correlated ground state (evaluated at the two-body cluster level) to the expectation value of the effective interaction in the Fermi gas ground state. The correlation range was adjusted so that the two-body cluster approximation reproduces the FHNC ground state energy, and the resulting effective interaction was then used in second-order perturbation theory to compute the self-energy, from which the single-particle spectrum, effective mass, and momentum distribution were obtained via Dyson's equation.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy expansion: \( E_0 = \frac{k_F^2}{2m} \left[ \frac{3}{5} + \frac{2}{\pi}c + \frac{12}{35\pi^2}(11 - 2\ln 2)c^2 + 0.78c^3 + \frac{32}{9\pi^3}(4\pi - 3\sqrt{3})c^4\ln c + O(c^4) \right] \), where \( c = k_F a \) with hard-core radius \( a = 1 \) fm, mass \( m = 1 \) fm\(^{-1}\), and degeneracy \( \nu = 4 \)
- At \( c = 0.2 \), the difference in the dimensionless quantity \( \zeta \) (defined by \( E_0 = \frac{3k_F^2}{10m}(1+\zeta) \)) between FHNC and low-density expansion is less than 5%, translating to an energy difference of less than 1%; at \( c = 0.3 \), the difference in \( \zeta \) is less than 7% (energy difference less than 2%)
- Effective mass at \( k = k_F \) from low-density expansion: \( m^\star(k_F)/m = 1 + \frac{24}{15\pi^2}(7\ln 2 - 1)c^2 \), implying \( m^\star/m > 1 \) for all \( c \)
- Momentum distribution discontinuity \( Z \) at \( k = k_F \): values of 0.92, 0.72, and 0.28 for \( c = 0.3 \), 0.5, and 0.7, respectively
- Correlation range for the effective interaction is sizeably smaller (by approximately 35–40%) than the variational correlation range from FHNC minimization over the whole range of \( c \)

## Context

This work extends the CBF effective interaction approach previously developed by Shannon and Benhar (unreferenced in text) and Benhar and Valli (unreferenced in text) for nuclear matter, applying it to the simpler Fermi hard-sphere system to test its reliability. The results are compared against low-density expansions by Huang and Yang (1950s) and Bishop (unreferenced in text), as well as variational FHNC calculations by Fantoni et al. (unreferenced in text) and Sartor and Mahaux (unreferenced in text) for the momentum distribution. The agreement with low-density expansions at small \( c \) and the demonstration that the effective interaction approach captures correlation effects beyond the variational FHNC method for the momentum distribution suggest this scheme can be extended to realistic nuclear Hamiltonians for astrophysical applications requiring consistent descriptions of nuclear matter structure and dynamics.
