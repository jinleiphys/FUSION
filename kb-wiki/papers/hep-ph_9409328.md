# Summary of Heavy Ion Theory

> Can we study hot QCD using nuclear collisions? Can we learn about metallic hydrogen from the impact of comet Shoemaker-Levy 9 on Jupiter? The answer to both questions may surprise you! I summarize progress in relativistic heavy ion theory reported at DPF `94 in the parallel sessions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that relativistic heavy ion collisions can be used to study hot QCD, but the interpretation of signals such as J/ψ suppression and disoriented chiral condensates is complicated by multiple background contributions and theoretical uncertainties, making definitive conclusions about deconfinement and chiral symmetry restoration dependent on future experimental and theoretical progress.

## Method

The author summarizes theoretical talks from the DPF '94 parallel sessions on relativistic heavy ion theory, covering global collision dynamics at AGS (√s ~ 5 AGeV) and SPS (√s ~ 20 AGeV) energies, specific probes including open charm production, direct photon production, J/ψ suppression, and disoriented chiral condensates, and draws analogies to the comet Shoemaker-Levy 9 impact on Jupiter to illustrate the complexity of extracting equation-of-state information from collision observables.

## Key numbers

- Lattice QCD simulations show a critical temperature Tc ~ 150 MeV for the transition from hadronic to quark-gluon degrees of freedom
- The transition interval is |T - Tc| < 5 MeV, indicating an abrupt transformation
- RHIC and LHC collision energies are √s = 200 and 5500 GeV per nucleon, respectively
- Minijets with transverse momenta pT > 2 GeV may produce up to 50% of particle production in Au+Au at RHIC
- Minijet formation time scales as pT^{-1} ~ 0.1 fm
- The chiral transition critical temperature is Tc ~ 140 MeV
- DCC domain sizes in the linear sigma model are estimated at ~1-3 fm

## Context

This conference summary extends the work of Matsui and Satz on J/ψ suppression as a deconfinement probe, Rajagopal and Wilczek (1993) on disoriented chiral condensates, and lattice QCD simulations demonstrating the high-temperature phase transition. It enables future experimental searches at RHIC (1999) and LHC, and theoretical development of first-principles constraints on parton distributions in large nuclei as pursued by Ayala, McLerran, Venugopalan, and Jalilian-Marian, as well as the operator-product-expansion approach to J/ψ-nucleon cross sections by Satz and Kharzeev.
