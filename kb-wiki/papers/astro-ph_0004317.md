# The Properties of Matter in White Dwarfs and Neutron Stars

> White dwarfs and neutron stars are stellar objects with masses comparable to that of our sun. However, as the endpoint stages of stellar evolution, these objects do not sustain any thermonuclear burning and therefore can no longer support the gravitational load of their own mass by generating thermal pressure. Rather, matter in their interiors is compressed to much higher densities than commonly found in normal stars, and pressure is created by degenerate fermion kinetic energy and particle interactions. As a result, white dwarfs and neutron stars offer unique cosmic laboratories for studying matter at very high densities. In this review we discuss the basic properties of condensed matter at extreme densities and summarize the extent to which these properties can be examined by observations of compact objects.

## Key claim
This review argues that the equation of state (EoS) of cold, degenerate matter in compact objects is well understood up to neutron drip density (approximately 4 x 10^11 gm/cm^3), but remains an active field of research at supernuclear densities (above approximately 2.8 x 10^14 gm/cm^3), where the EoS is model-dependent and not yet derivable from first principles like QCD.

## Method
The paper surveys the theoretical construction of the cold, zero-temperature equation of state (EoS) for matter spanning fifteen orders of magnitude in density. It distinguishes between the electron-degenerate regime (below neutron drip), where the EoS is governed by an ideal fermion gas with electrostatic and neutronization corrections, and the nucleon-dominated regime (above neutron drip), where the EoS relies on phenomenological nuclear models such as the liquid-drop model, variational methods (e.g., WFF, APR), and relativistic mean field approximations (e.g., SerWal79). The review connects these microscopic properties to macroscopic stellar structure via the condition of hydrostatic equilibrium.

## Key numbers
- The neutron drip density is approximately 4 x 10^11 gm/cm^3, marking the transition between electron-dominated and nucleon-dominated matter (Baym, Pethick and Sutherland, 1971).
- The transition between a nonrelativistic and extremely relativistic electron gas occurs at a density of approximately 10^6 gm/cm^3 for matter with Y_e = 0.5.
- The density of atomic nuclei (saturation density) is approximately 2.8 x 10^14 gm/cm^3.
- The speed of sound in a neutron star is expected to be several tens of percent of the speed of light, with typical values around 10^10 cm/s.
- The adiabatic index (Gamma) for a neutron star is in the range of 2-3, compared to 4/3 for a relativistic white dwarf.

## Context
This review extends the foundational work on white dwarf structure by Chandrasekhar (1983 Nobel prize) and Salpeter (1961), and the standard catalyzed EoS of Baym, Pethick and Sutherland (1971) and Baym, Bethe and Pethick (1971). It contrasts the well-understood physics of white dwarfs with the open questions in neutron star interiors, where the EoS remains uncertain. The paper enables future work by summarizing the current state of the art, including modern EoS models like APR (Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall, 1998), and identifying the need for observational constraints from neutron star masses and radii to discriminate between different supernuclear EoS models.
