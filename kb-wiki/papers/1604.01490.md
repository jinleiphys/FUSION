# Radii of neutron drops probed via the neutron skin thickness of nuclei

> Multi-neutron systems are crucial to understanding the physics of neutron-rich nuclei and neutron stars. Neutron drops, neutrons confined in an external field, are investigated systematically in both non-relativistic and relativistic density functional theories and with ab initio calculations. We demonstrate a strong linear correlation, which is universal in the realm of mean-field models, between the rms radii of neutron drops and the neutron skin thickness of Pb-208 and Ca-48; i.e., the difference between the neutron and proton rms radii of a nucleus. Due to its high quality, this correlation can be used to deduce the radii of neutron drops from the measured neutron skin thickness in a model-independent way, and the radii obtained for neutron drops can provide a useful constraint for realistic three neutron forces. This correlation, together with high- precision measurements of the neutron skin thicknesses of Pb-208 and Ca-48, will have an enduring impact on the understanding of multi-neutron interactions, neutron-rich nuclei, neutron stars, etc.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates a strong linear correlation between the root-mean-square (rms) radii of neutron drops and the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb and 48Ca, which is universal across many mean-field models. This correlation can be used to deduce neutron drop radii from measured neutron skin thicknesses, but the authors note that the correlation only holds when the central density of the neutron drop is close to nuclear saturation density (approximately 0.16 fm^-3).

## Method

The authors performed systematic calculations of neutron drops (neutrons confined in an external harmonic oscillator potential) using six relativistic density functionals (NL3, PK1, DD-ME2, PKDD, PC-PK1, DD-PC1) and compared them with non-relativistic density functionals and ab initio quantum Monte Carlo calculations. They computed the neutron skin thickness (difference between neutron and proton rms radii) for 208Pb and 48Ca using the same functionals, then established linear correlations between these skin thicknesses and the rms radii of neutron drops with 8, 14, and 20 neutrons. They also calculated the slope L of the symmetry energy from the equation of state of pure neutron matter using the auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo method with several nuclear Hamiltonians (AV8', AV8'+UIX, and local chiral forces at N2LO).

## Key numbers

- Pearson's correlation coefficient r = 0.95 for the linear correlation between neutron skin thickness of 208Pb and rms radius of 20 neutrons in a harmonic oscillator potential with hbar-omega = 10 MeV
- Pearson's correlation coefficient r = 0.97 for the same correlation with 48Ca
- The 16% accuracy in neutron skin thickness from electric dipole polarizability measurements leads to approximately 2.5% accuracy in the deduced rms radius R of the neutron drop
- For 48Ca, an estimated neutron skin thickness of 0.16 +/- 0.01 fm leads to a deduced neutron drop radius R = 3.04 +/- 0.04 fm (1.3% accuracy)
- The first PREX experiment gave neutron skin thickness of 208Pb as 0.33 +0.16/-0.18 fm
- Central density of the neutron drops was verified to be within 0.16 +/- 0.02 fm^-3
- Pearson's correlation coefficient r = 0.92 for the correlation between the slope L of the symmetry energy and the rms radius of 20 neutrons

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutron drops using quantum Monte Carlo methods (Pudliner 1996, Gandolfi 2011) and density functional theories, and provides the first relativistic study of neutron drops. The correlation between neutron skin thickness and the slope L of the symmetry energy had been demonstrated within mean-field models by Roca-Maza (2011), and the authors here establish a new correlation between neutron drop radii and L, validated by both density-functional and ab initio calculations. The results enable model-independent extraction of neutron drop radii from upcoming high-precision measurements of neutron skin thicknesses, particularly the PREX-II and CREX experiments at Jefferson Lab, which will provide constraints for realistic three-neutron forces.
