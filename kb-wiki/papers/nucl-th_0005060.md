# Properties of Strange Hadronic Matter in Bulk and in Finite Systems

> The hyperon-hyperon potentials due to a recent SU(3) Nijmegen soft-core potential model are incorporated within a relativistic mean field calculation of strange hadronic matter. We find considerably higher binding energy in bulk matter compared to several recent calculations which constrain the composition of matter. For small strangeness fractions, matter is dominated by a composition of nucleons, Lambdas, and Xis, and the calculated binding energy closely follows that calculated by using the hyperon potentials of our previous calculations. For larger strangeness fractions, the calculated binding energy increases substantially beyond any previous calculation due to a phase transition into Sigma and Xi dominated matter. We also compare bulk matter calculations with finite system calculations, again highlighting the consequences of reducing the Coulomb destabilizing effects in finite strange systems.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that when the composition of strange hadronic matter is allowed to adjust self-consistently under chemical equilibrium (rather than being artificially constrained), the SU(3)-extended Nijmegen soft-core potential model NSC97 predicts substantially higher binding energies than previously reported, including a phase transition to deeply bound NΣΞ-dominated matter at high strangeness fractions.

## Method

The authors performed relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations for bulk strange hadronic matter composed of the full SU(3) octet of baryons (N, Λ, Σ, Ξ), incorporating hyperon-hyperon interactions from the Nijmegen soft-core potential model NSC97 by adjusting the σ* meson coupling constants to reproduce the hyperon binding energy curves of Stoks and Lee (1999). They compared constrained calculations (with fixed equal hyperon fractions, as done by Stoks and Lee) against unconstrained calculations where the baryon composition was determined self-consistently by enforcing chemical equilibrium via the Hugenholtz–van Hove theorem. Finite system calculations were also performed for multi-strange nuclei built on a ¹⁰⁰Sn core, including Coulomb effects.

## Key numbers

- For model N (NSC97-inspired) with unconstrained equilibrium, the binding energy per baryon reaches a minimum of E/A = -79 MeV at strangeness fraction f_S = 1.45, compared to the constrained calculation which gives approximately -10 MeV higher at f_S = 1.
- In model 2 with U_Ξ = -28 MeV, the minimum binding energy per baryon is E/A = -24.6 MeV at f_S = 1.3; for U_Ξ = -18 MeV, E/A = -21.5 MeV at f_S = 1.3; for U_Ξ = -10 MeV, E/A = -19.6 MeV at f_S = 1.4.
- In model 1 with U_Ξ = -28 MeV, the minimum binding energy per baryon is E/A = -17.4 MeV at f_S = 0.6.
- Pure Ξ matter in model 2 has binding energy E/A = -8.9 MeV at f_S = 2 for all three U_Ξ values.
- For finite systems in model 2, the binding energy per baryon reaches E/A = -19 MeV at f_S = 1.3, close to the bulk value.
- For finite systems in model 1, the strangeness-richest object (f_S = 0.375) has E/A = -9.5 MeV with Coulomb, compared to E/A = -8.3 MeV for ¹⁰⁰Sn.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous RMF calculations of strange hadronic matter (Schaffner-Bielich et al., 1993, 1994) which predicted metastable strange systems with f_S ~ 1 and f_Q ~ 0. It directly addresses the conflicting results of Stoks and Lee (1999), who used a Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach with the SU(3)-extended NSC97 potential and found only weak binding for NΛΞ systems. The authors show that the discrepancy arises because Stoks and Lee artificially constrained the hyperon fractions, preventing the system from reaching its true minimum-energy configuration. The results enable future studies of metastable multi-strange hadronic systems and their possible role in neutron star interiors, while also highlighting the need for improved experimental constraints on hyperon-hyperon interactions, particularly the ΛΛ interaction which the NSC97 model underestimates relative to double-Λ hypernuclear data (Dover, 1991; Aoki, 1991).
