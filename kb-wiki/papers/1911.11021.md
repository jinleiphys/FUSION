# Recent advances in the description of reactions involving exotic nuclei

> In this contribution to the proceedings of the International Nuclear Physics Conference 2019, I review recent developments made in reaction models used to analyse data measured at radioactive-ion beam facilities to study exotic nuclear structures. I focus in particular on reactions like elastic scattering and breakup, which are used to study halo nuclei. Although these peculiar nuclei challenge usual nuclear-structure models, some can now be computed ab initio. This brief review illustrates the progresses made in nuclear-reaction theory in the last few years to improve the description of the projectile within reaction models. I dedicate this contribution to the memory of Mahir Hussein, who has significantly contributed to this field and who passed away in May this year.

## Key claim

Although the abstract states that *ab initio* models can now describe halo nuclei, the paper's actual claim is that these *ab initio* predictions can be tested against experimental data only when coupled to a precise reaction model, and that the success of such tests depends on the specific reaction and target: for example, the *ab initio* predictions for 11Be are validated by Coulomb breakup on Pb but are insufficient to describe resonant breakup on C without including core excitation or an effective three-body force.

## Method

The paper reviews three recent developments in reaction theory for halo nuclei. First, the Microscopic Continuum-Discretised Coupled Channel (MCDCC) model, which embeds a fully microscopic cluster description of the projectile into the CDCC framework, treating both bound and continuum states microscopically and building optical potentials via folding from nucleon-nucleus global potentials. Second, the extended CDCC (X-CDCC) model, which includes the internal structure of the projectile core (e.g., as a rigid rotor) and its excited states within the coupled-channel calculation. Third, the coupling of a Halo Effective Field Theory (Halo EFT) description of the projectile, at next-to-leading order (NLO) and beyond, into a precise few-body reaction code, allowing the short-range physics to be controlled by a Gaussian regulator of width sigma.

## Key numbers

*   Elastic scattering of 7Li on Pb at 27 MeV: MCDCC calculations including breakup (solid lines) achieve excellent agreement with experiment without fitting parameters; calculations considering only bound states (dashed lines) miss the data.
*   Elastic scattering of 6He on Pb at 22 MeV: MCDCC calculations including breakup (solid lines) achieve excellent agreement with experiment without fitting parameters.
*   Breakup of 19C on p at 70A MeV: calculations including core excitation reproduce the data nearly perfectly; a calculation assuming the 5/2+ resonance as a simple d5/2 single-neutron resonance (core in 0+ ground state) misses the experimental cross section by an order of magnitude.
*   Breakup of 11Be on p at 63.7A MeV: the full X-CDCC calculation (solid black line) shows significant differences from a calculation with dynamical core excitation switched off (green dash-dotted line) in the energy range of the 5/2+ and 3/2+ resonances (at E approximately 1.3 MeV and 3 MeV).
*   Breakup of 11Be on Pb at 69A MeV: a NLO Halo-EFT description reproduces the data excellently, with no sensitivity to the Gaussian width sigma, confirming the reaction is purely peripheral.
*   Breakup of 11Be on C at 67A MeV: the NLO Halo-EFT calculation reproduces the general shape but misses breakup strength at E approximately 1.3 MeV and 3 MeV; adding a d5/2 resonance and an effective three-body force can reproduce the resonant peaks.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on few-body reaction models for halo nuclei, which began with the development of CDCC by Austern (1987) and semiclassical and eikonal approaches. It specifically highlights improvements beyond the simple few-body picture: the MCDCC model of Descouvemont and Hussein (2013) provides a fully microscopic projectile description but is currently limited to elastic scattering; the X-CDCC model of Summers, Nunes, and Thompson (2006), later used by the Sevilla-Lisbon collaboration (Lay 2016, de Diego 2014, Moro 2019), shows that core excitation dominates resonant breakup; and the Halo-EFT approach of Capel, Phillips, and Hammer (2018) enables direct tests of *ab initio* predictions (e.g., Calci 2016) against data, revealing that Coulomb breakup is peripheral while nuclear-dominated breakup requires additional degrees of freedom. These advances enable more reliable extraction of nuclear-structure information from reactions at radioactive-ion beam facilities.
