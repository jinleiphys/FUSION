# Spectral and transport properties from lattice QCD

> In these lecture notes we will discuss recent progress in extracting spectral and transport properties from lattice QCD. We will focus on results of probes of the thermal QCD medium as well as transport coefficients which are important ingredients for hydrodynamic and transport models that describe the evolution of the produced medium. These include electromagnetic probes, like the rates of emitted photons and dileptons, quarkonium spectral functions as well as transport coefficients like the electrical conductivity or heavy flavor diffusion coefficients of the quark gluon plasma (QGP). All these real time quantities are encoded in the vector meson spectral function. A direct determination of the spectral functions is not possible in Euclidean lattice QCD calculations but they can be analytically continued from imaginary to real time. Therefore it is possible to relate the spectral function to the corresponding Euclidean correlation functions. In the following sections we will discuss the procedure to determine the required correlation functions and the extraction of the spectral functions from lattice QCD correlators. We will illustrate the concepts and methods to obtain spectral functions and related physical observables from continuum extrapolated correlation functions. We will focus here on results obtained from continuum extrapolated lattice correlation functions, which requires large and fine lattices, which so far was only possible in quenched approximation. We will only give a brief introduction to lattice QCD and refer to the textbooks [1,2,3,4] and lecture notes [5] for more detailed introductions to lattice field theory. For the topics addressed in this lecture note we also like to refer to the overview articles on QCD thermodynamics and the QCD phase transition [5,6,7] and quarkonium in extreme conditions [8].

## Key claim

These lecture notes present recent progress in extracting spectral and transport properties from lattice QCD, but the results shown for continuum extrapolated correlation functions are obtained only in the quenched approximation, where dynamical fermionic degrees of freedom are neglected, because large and fine lattices required for continuum extrapolation have so far only been possible in this approximation.

## Method

The authors compute vector meson correlation functions on the lattice in the quenched approximation, perform continuum extrapolations in \(1/N_{\tau}^2\) for all distances \(\tau T\) down to around \(\tau T\simeq 0.1\), and then reconstruct spectral functions from these continuum extrapolated correlators using a phenomenologically inspired Ansatz with a Breit-Wigner transport peak and a perturbatively motivated high-frequency part. Three different Ansatze for the spectral function are fitted to the continuum data at temperatures \(T=1.1, 1.3\) and \(1.5 T_c\) to assess systematic uncertainties, including one with a flat low-frequency behavior motivated by AdS/CFT correspondence and another incorporating five-loop vacuum perturbation theory.

## Key numbers

* Thermal dilepton rates and electrical conductivity are extracted from continuum extrapolated vector meson correlation functions at three temperatures: \(T=1.1 T_c\), \(T=1.3 T_c\), and \(T=1.5 T_c\).
* The continuum extrapolated correlator ratio \(G_{ii}/G_{V}^{\rm free}\) approaches unity at \(\tau\rightarrow0\) and shows deviations up to around \(50\%\) at large \(\tau\).
* The electrical conductivity results incorporate systematic uncertainties estimated from the minimum and maximum of the two Ansatze \(\rho_{ans}\) and \(\rho_R\).
* The thermal dilepton rate obtained from \(\rho_{\text{R}}\) shows enhancement in the intermediate region \(\omega/T\sim2\) compared to hard thermal loop (HTL) calculations.
* The quark number susceptibility \(\chi_q\) is used for normalization, defined as \(\chi_q = \int_0^\beta d\tau\int d^3x\langle V^0(\tau,\vec{x})V^0(0)\rangle\).

## Context

This work extends lattice QCD studies of real-time properties of the quark-gluon plasma, building on earlier quenched calculations of vector meson correlators and spectral functions. The methodology enables extraction of transport coefficients including electrical conductivity and heavy flavor diffusion coefficients via Kubo formulas, and provides input for hydrodynamic and transport models describing heavy-ion collision evolution. The results are compared to hard thermal loop calculations by Braaten (1989) and to AdS/CFT predictions by Teaney (2006), while the review by Aarts (2020) summarizes the current status of electrical conductivity determinations in both quenched and full QCD.
