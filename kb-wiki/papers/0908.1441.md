# Poincaré invariant quantum theory, J. Lab lectures

> Lectures on Poincare invariant quantum theory presented at TJNAF.

## Key claim

Poincaré invariant quantum mechanics provides a framework for constructing quantum mechanical models of systems with a finite number of degrees of freedom that are consistent with Poincaré invariance, cluster properties, and a spectral condition, suitable for describing reactions in the few GeV region where effective field theory is not generally applicable and direct QCD solutions are not available.

## Method

The paper constructs unitary representations of the Poincaré group on Hilbert spaces of square-integrable functions of momentum and spin projection, using Wigner's classification of irreducible representations by mass and spin. It derives explicit matrix elements for these representations via a factorization theorem that decomposes any Poincaré transformation into a sequence of inverse boosts, rotations, translations, and boosts, and then constructs Clebsch-Gordan coefficients for decomposing tensor products of irreducible representations into mutually orthogonal irreducible subspaces.

## Key numbers

- The Poincaré group has 10 independent one-parameter subgroups: 3 rotations, 3 rotationless Lorentz boosts, 3 spatial translations, and 1 time translation.
- The irreducible representations are classified by two polynomial invariants: \(M^2 = H^2 - \mathbf{P}\cdot\mathbf{P}\) and \(W^2\), with spin given by \(j^2 := W^2/M^2\) when \(M^2 \neq 0\).
- For massive particles with \(M^2 > 0\) and \(P^0 > 0\), the little group is \(SU(2)\); for massless particles with \(M^2 = 0\) and \(P^0 > 0\), the little group is \(E(2)\).
- The Jacobian relating momentum-space volume elements under Lorentz transformations is \(\vert {\partial \mathbf{p} \over \partial \bLam p }\vert = {\omega (\mathbf{p}) \over \omega (\bLam p )}\).

## Context

This work extends the foundational classification of unitary representations of the Poincaré group by Wigner (1939) and the subsequent work by Bargmann (1954) on ray representations and covering groups. It provides a practical framework for constructing relativistic quantum mechanical models without the full machinery of local quantum field theory, addressing the difficulty noted by Streater and Wightman (1965) that locality violations on one scale propagate to all scales. The approach enables construction of models for reactions in the few GeV region, complementing effective field theory approaches at lower energies and quasipotential approaches such as the Gross equation at higher energies.
