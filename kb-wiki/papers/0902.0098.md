# Band terminations in density functional theory

> The analysis of the terminating bands has been performed in the relativistic mean field framework. It was shown that nuclear magnetism provides an additional binding to the energies of the specific configuration and this additional binding increases with spin and has its {\it maximum} exactly at the terminating state. This suggests that the terminating states can be an interesting probe of the time-odd mean fields {\it provided that other effects can be reliably isolated.} Unfortunately, a reliable isolation of these effects is not that simple: many terms of the density functional theories contribute into the energies of the terminating states and the deficiencies in the description of those terms affect the result. The recent suggestion \cite{ZSW.05} that the relative energies of the terminating states in the $N \neq Z, A\sim 44$ mass region given by $ΔE$ {\it provide unique and reliable constraints on time-odd mean fields and the strength of spin-orbit interaction} in density functional theories has been reanalyzed. The current investigation shows that the $ΔE$ value is affected also by the relative placement of the states with different orbital angular momentum ${\it l}$, namely, the placement of the $d$ (${\it l}=2$) and $f$ (${\it l}=3$) states. This indicates the dependence of the $ΔE$ value on the properties of the central potential.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the relative energies of terminating states in the \(A\sim 44\) mass region, previously claimed to provide unique constraints on time-odd mean fields and spin-orbit interaction strength in density functional theories, are also affected by the relative placement of states with different orbital angular momentum \(l\) (specifically the \(d\) and \(f\) states), indicating dependence on the central potential properties rather than solely on spin-orbit and time-odd fields.

## Method

The author performed cranked relativistic mean field (CRMF) calculations for the ground state band of \(^{20}\)Ne using the NL1 parametrization, comparing results with and without nuclear magnetism (NM). The study then analyzed terminating states in the \(A\sim 44\) mass region using CRMF calculations with NL1 and NL3 parametrizations, comparing the energy difference \(\Delta E = E(d_{3/2}^{-1}\,f_{7/2}^{n+1})-E(f_{7/2}^{n})\) between terminating states with \(d_{3/2}^{-1}\,f_{7/2}^{n+1}\) and \(f_{7/2}^{n}\) configurations against experimental data and against predictions from the Nilsson potential with \(N\)-dependent parameters.

## Key numbers

- In \(^{20}\)Ne, nuclear magnetism provides additional binding that reaches its maximum at the terminating state with spin \(I_{max}=8^+\)
- The \(f_{7/2}-d_{3/2}\) splitting from CRMF calculations without nuclear magnetism reaches \(\Delta E= 7.57\) MeV, exceeding the experimental value by 37%
- Including nuclear magnetism decreases the difference between calculation and experiment by 1.14 MeV
- For the Nilsson potential with \(N\)-dependent parameters, the \(\Delta E^{Nil-N-dep}\) value using \(\mu_N=0\) is \(0.415 \hbar \omega_0\) for protons, while using the full parameter set gives \(0.334 \hbar \omega_0\), a difference of approximately 25% corresponding to about 1.4 MeV
- The experimental \(\Delta E^{exp}\) is approximately 5.5 MeV
- Deformation polarization effects change \(\Delta E\) by approximately 0.25 MeV from \(n=0\) to \(n=3\)
- The difference between spherical RMF results and the full calculation for all nuclei under study does not exceed 40 keV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of terminating bands from the cranked Nilsson-Strutinsky approach (CNS) and previous Skyrme density functional theory studies by ZSW (2005) and BWSMG (2006), who proposed the "TS-method" for constraining time-odd mean fields and spin-orbit interaction. The paper contradicts the claim in ZSW (2005) that the \(\Delta E\) value provides unique constraints, showing instead that it depends on multiple DFT terms including the central potential's treatment of orbital angular momentum. The findings enable future work to properly account for all contributing terms when using terminating states as probes of time-odd mean fields, and highlight the need for global fits rather than perturbative adjustments of individual coupling constants.
