# Exploring proton rich systems and Coulomb induced instabilities

> The thermodynamic properties of proton rich systems are explored in a mean field approach which is generated from a Skyrme interaction. The addition of Coulomb interactions result in asymmetries which modify the chemical and mechanical instability of the system and its equilibrium properties. These properties are studied for systems with proton fraction $y$ on the proton richer side of the valley of $β$-stability as well as the neutron rich side. Coulomb induced instabilities lead to proton diffusion processes on the proton richer side and also large asymmetries in chemical and mechanical instabilities and coexistence curves. Considering the whole range of $0 \le y \le 1$, we can study how the symmetry about $y=1/2$ is broken by asymmetric interaction and we can also explicitly show that the role between proton and neutron is exchanged around $y_E$. It is shown that there are two asymmetric coexistence surfaces in $(y, P, T)$ space, one for $y < y_E$ and another for $y > y_E$ and touching each other at $y_E$. These asymmetries in instabilities show up as new branches, one for $y < y_E$ and one for $y > y_E$, and thus form a closed loop in pressure versus $ρ$ for both chemical instability and coexistence regions. The branch of $y > 1/2 > y_E$ was not previously investigated since only the $y < 1/2$ region is usually considered. In our simplified model, mechanical instability is still symmetric around a point $y_E \ne 1/2$ even with Coulomb forces present.

## Key claim

The paper extends previous mean-field studies of nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions to the full range of proton fraction \(0 \le y \le 1\), revealing that Coulomb interactions break the symmetry around \(y=1/2\) and produce a second coexistence branch for \(y > y_E\) that was missed in earlier work restricted to \(y \le 1/2\). The authors emphasize that their simplified Skyrme-plus-Coulomb model is intended to qualitatively illustrate the main physical phenomena, and they expect no major qualitative changes with more complicated interactions.

## Method

The authors use a mean-field approach with a Skyrme interaction energy density including volume attraction (\(\rho^2\)), short-range repulsion (\(\rho^3\)), nuclear symmetry energy (\(\propto (2y-1)^2 \rho^2\)), and a simplified Coulomb term (\(C y^2 \rho^2\)) treated as constant. They add a kinetic energy density with first-order degeneracy corrections and derive analytic expressions for pressure and chemical potentials. Mechanical instability boundaries are obtained from \((dP/d\rho)_{y,T}=0\) (a quadratic equation), while chemical instability boundaries come from \((d\mu_q/dy)_{P,T}=0\) (a cubic equation), and coexistence surfaces are constructed by equating chemical potentials in liquid and gas phases at fixed \(P\) and \(T\).

## Key numbers

- The Skyrme parameters used are \(t_0 = -1089\) MeV fm\(^3\) and \(t_3 = 17480.4\) MeV fm\(^6\), with \(\gamma = 2\) as the spin degeneracy factor.
- At \(T = 10\) MeV, the coexistence loops intersect at \(y_E = 0.41\) when Coulomb and surface terms are included, shifted from \(y_E = 1/2\) when Coulomb is absent.
- The symmetry energy parameter is \(a_S = \frac{2}{3} (\frac{1}{2} + x_0) a_0\), with calculations shown for \(x_0 = -1/6\) (smaller symmetry energy) and \(x_0 = 1/2\) (larger symmetry energy).
- The critical point occurs when \(b^2 = a c\) with critical density \(\rho_c = -b/a\), where \(a = 6 a_3\) and \(b\) contains the Coulomb and symmetry terms.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Lee and Mekjian, 2003) that focused on \(0 \le y \le 1/2\), and it builds on the pioneering mean-field work of Muller and Serot on symmetry energy effects, Baran et al. on chemical instability and isospin distillation, and Colonna et al. on finite-nuclei Coulomb and surface effects. The results enable future studies of proton-rich systems relevant to rare isotope accelerator experiments and nuclear astrophysics, particularly for understanding isospin fractionization and the role of Coulomb-induced proton diffusion in the liquid-gas phase transition.
