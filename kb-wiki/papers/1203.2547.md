# Hypernuclei, dibaryon and antinuclei production in high energy heavy ion collisions: Thermal production vs. Coalescence

> We study the production of (hyper-)nuclei and di-baryons in most central heavy Ion collisions at energies of $E_{lab}=1-160 A$ GeV. In particular we are interested in clusters produced from the hot and dense fireball. The formation rate of strange and non-strange clusters is estimated by assuming thermal production from the intermediate phase of the UrQMD-hydro hybrid model and alternatively by the coalescence mechanism from a hadronic cascade model. Both model types are compared in detail. For most energies we find that both approaches agree in their predictions for the yields of the clusters. Only for very low beam energies, and for di-baryons including $Ξ$'s, we observe considerable differences. We also study the production of anti-matter clusters up to top RHIC energies and show that the observation of anti-$^4He$ and even anti-$^4_ΛHe$ is feasible. We have found a considerable qualitative difference in the energy dependence of the strangeness population factor $R_H$ when comparing the thermal production with the coalescence results.

# Hypernuclei, dibaryon and antinuclei production in high energy heavy ion collisions: Thermal production vs. Coalescence

## Key claim
The thermal production approach (UrQMD-hydro hybrid model) and the coalescence mechanism (DCM-QGSM cascade model) agree well in their predictions for most hypernuclei and dibaryon yields across beam energies from 1 to 160 A GeV, but show considerable differences at very low beam energies and for dibaryons containing Xi hyperons, as well as a qualitative difference in the energy dependence of the strangeness population factor R_H.

## Method
The authors compared two distinct theoretical approaches for cluster production in central Au+Au and Pb+Pb collisions. The first approach used the UrQMD hybrid model, which couples a hadronic cascade to a hydrodynamic evolution, with clusters produced thermally via the Cooper-Frye freeze-out procedure. The second approach used the Dubna Cascade Model (DCM) combined with the Quark-Gluon String Model (QGSM) for high energies, where clusters were formed via a coalescence mechanism that requires proximity in both momentum and coordinate space, with coalescence parameters fixed by fitting experimental data at 11.5 A GeV.

## Key numbers
- Beam energy range studied: E_lab = 1 to 160 A GeV for most central collisions (impact parameter b < 3.4 fm)
- Coalescence momentum parameters used: p_C(d) = 90 MeV/c, p_C(t) = 108 MeV/c, p_C(3He) = 108 MeV/c, p_C(alpha) = 115 MeV/c
- For double Xi dibaryon states, the difference between thermal and coalescence predictions is as large as one order of magnitude
- The DCM model produces approximately a factor of 5 fewer Xi hyperons than the UrQMD hybrid model
- Yields of most hypernuclei show a maximum or saturation around 10-20 A GeV beam energy
- At 20 A GeV, the yield of Lambda-N dibaryon varies from 4.4 x 10^-4 to 2.1 per event depending on the coalescence momentum parameter p_C (5 to 90 MeV/c)
- At 20 A GeV, the yield of Xi-Xi dibaryon varies from less than 10^-6 to 7.2 x 10^-4 per event depending on p_C
- The switching density for the hydrodynamic to transport transition corresponds to roughly T = 170 MeV at mu_B = 0
- Final state interactions reduce the deuteron yield by only about 20 percent

## Context
This work extends previous studies of hypernuclei production in heavy ion collisions, including the thermal model approach of Andronic (2008) and the coalescence approach of Schaffner-Bielich (1999) using the RQMD model at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV. The paper builds on the recent experimental discoveries of the anti-hypertriton by the STAR collaboration (2010) and the anti-alpha by STAR (2011). The comparison between thermal and coalescence approaches enables an assessment of theoretical uncertainties and suggests that the double ratio R_H may serve as a probe of baryon-strangeness correlations, potentially sensitive to the onset of deconfinement at future FAIR and NICA facilities.
