# Quark Gluon Plasma Diagnostics in a Successive Equilibrium Scenario

> The relativistic Fokker Planck equation has been used to study the evolution of the quark distribution in the quark gluon phase expected to be formed in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. The effect of thermal masses for quarks and gluons is incorporated to take account of the in-medium properties. We find that the kinetic equilibrium is achieved before the system reaches the critical temperature of quark hadron phase transition. We find that chemical equilibrium is not achieved during this time. We have evaluated the electromagnetic probes of quark gluon plasma from the non-equilibrated quark gluon phase and compared them with those in completely equilibrated scenario. The hard QCD production rates for the electromagnetic ejectiles as well as the heavy quark production rates are also calculated.

## Key claim

Kinetic (thermal) equilibrium of quarks is achieved before the system reaches the critical temperature of the quark-hadron phase transition, but chemical equilibrium is not achieved during this time; the paper qualifies that this result applies specifically to the central rapidity region at RHIC and LHC energies under the assumption of complete transparency and scaling hydrodynamics.

## Method

The authors used the relativistic Fokker-Planck equation to model the temporal evolution of the quark distribution function, treating the gluons as an equilibrated heat bath that drives the quarks toward equilibrium via Brownian motion. The friction parameter was related to the partonic energy loss (dE/dx), and inelastic processes (gg ↔ qq̅ and g ↔ qq̅) were incorporated via a relaxation time approximation. The resulting non-equilibrium quark distributions were then used to compute electromagnetic probe spectra (photons, dileptons, diphotons) and compared with fully equilibrated scenarios and hard QCD production rates.

## Key numbers

- At RHIC: initial gluon thermalisation time τ_g = 0.3 fm/c, initial temperature T_g(τ_g) = 500 MeV, initial u/d quark density = 0.7 fm⁻³
- At LHC: initial gluon thermalisation time τ_g = 0.25 fm/c, initial temperature T_g(τ_g) = 660 MeV, initial u/d quark density = 2.8 fm⁻³
- Kinetic equilibrium for quarks is achieved by τ ≈ 3 fm/c at RHIC and τ ≈ 2 fm/c at LHC, well before the critical temperature T_c = 160 MeV
- The cooling law is parameterised as T = α/τ^β with α = 0.33, β = 0.352 at RHIC and α = 0.4077, β = 0.355 at LHC
- At LHC, pre-equilibrium photons dominate over equilibrium photons for p_T > 7 GeV; at RHIC, equilibrium photons dominate up to p_T = 8 GeV, with pre-equilibrium dominating above 8 GeV
- At LHC, pre-equilibrium dileptons dominate over equilibrium dileptons for all invariant masses M; at RHIC, pre-equilibrium dominates for M < 2.5-3.0 GeV and equilibrium dominates for M > 3.0 GeV

## Context

This work extends the earlier parton cascade approach of Geiger (1990s) and the Brownian motion framework of Svetitsky (1988) for heavy quarks, applying the Fokker-Planck equation to light quarks in a gluonic heat bath. The authors build on the observation by Shuryak that gluons thermalise faster than quarks due to larger gg cross-sections, and use the Bjorken scaling hydrodynamics framework. The paper enables quantitative estimates of how pre-equilibrium emission affects electromagnetic QGP signals, showing that the competing effects of high initial temperature versus low quark density produce different signatures at RHIC and LHC energies, which future data should distinguish among various models (HIJING, parton cascade, Shuryak's estimates).
