# Constraining neutron star matter from the slope of the mass-radius curves

> We analyse the implications of information about local derivatives from the mass-radius diagram in neutron star matter. It is expected that the next generation of gravitational wave and electromagnetic detectors will allow the determination of the neutron star radius and mass with a small uncertainty. Observations of neutron stars clustered around a given neutron star mass allow the estimation of local derivatives in the $M(R)$ diagram, which can be used to constrain neutron star properties. From a model-independent description of the neutron star equation of state, it is shown that a $M(R)$ curve with a negative slope at 1.4$M_\odot$ predicts a $2M_\odot$ neutron star radius below 12 km. Furthermore, a maximum mass below 2.3$M_\odot$ is obtained if the $M(R)$ slope is negative in the whole range of masses above $1M_\odot$, and a maximum mass above 2.4$M_\odot$ requires the $M(R)$ slope to be positive in some range of masses. Constraints on the mass-radius curve of neutron stars will place strong constraints on microscopic models.

# Constraining neutron star matter from the slope of the mass-radius curves

## Key claim

Using a model-independent piecewise polytropic description of the neutron star equation of state, the authors show that the sign of the local slope dM/dR in the mass-radius diagram can distinguish between two classes of equations of state and constrain neutron star properties, but the results should be considered indicative due to uncertainties of approximately 1-1.5% in radius calculations from crust modeling.

## Method

The authors constructed 40435 model-independent equations of state using five connected piecewise polytropic segments with randomly sampled transition densities and adiabatic indices, imposing consistency with chiral effective field theory at low densities and causality. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to generate mass-radius sequences and divided the dataset into two subsets: those with dM/dR negative for all masses above 1 solar mass (3076 equations of state) and those with dM/dR not always negative (37359 equations of state). Astrophysical constraints from NICER observations of PSR J0740+6620 and PSR J0030+0451, plus the GW170817 tidal deformability limit, were applied as hard cutoffs, reducing the sample to 2493 and 19243 equations of state respectively.

## Key numbers

- A negative slope dM/dR at 1.4 solar masses predicts a 2 solar mass neutron star radius below 13 km; with astrophysical constraints, the 90% credible interval for R(2.0 solar masses) is 10.77 to 11.89 km for the negative-slope set
- For equations of state with dM/dR negative over the whole mass range above 1 solar mass, the maximum mass lies between 2.01 and 2.20 solar masses at 90% credible interval (with astrophysical constraints)
- A maximum mass above 2.4 solar masses requires the mass-radius slope to be positive in some range of masses; the maximum mass for the dM/dR not always negative set reaches 2.69 solar masses with astrophysical constraints
- For equations of state with dM/dR negative at both 1.2 and 1.8 solar masses, the radius of a 2 solar mass star is constrained to 10.5 to 12.5 km
- The 50% credible interval for the renormalized trace anomaly at the central density of the maximum mass star is -0.057 (+0.049, -0.052) for the negative-slope set and +0.008 (+0.066, -0.073) for the other set, with astrophysical constraints

## Context

This work extends model-independent studies of neutron star equations of state using piecewise polytropic parameterizations (Annala 2017) and Gaussian process approaches (Ecker 2024) by focusing specifically on the information content of the local slope of the mass-radius curve. The analysis distinguishes between equations of state that produce backbending in the M(R) diagram, such as those from the DD2, DDME2, NL3omega-rho, FSU2H, and BigApple models, and those with monotonic negative slope like SLy4, SLy9, SFHo, FSU, and FSU2. The results enable future gravitational wave and electromagnetic detectors to constrain microscopic nuclear models by measuring the slope of the mass-radius curve at one or two masses, and the authors connect their findings to the conjecture of Fujimoto (2022) regarding the positive renormalized trace anomaly.
