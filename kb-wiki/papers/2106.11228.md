# Unraveling the Flux-Averaged Neutrino-Nucleus Cross Section

> The interpretation of the nuclear cross sections measured using accelerator neutrino beams involve severe difficulties, arising primarily from the average over the incoming neutrino flux. The broad energy distribution of the beam particles hampers the determination of the energy transfer to the nuclear target, the knowledge of which is needed to pin down the dominant reaction mechanism. Overcoming this problem requires the development of a theoretical approach suitable to describe neutrino interactions at energies ranging from hundreds of MeV to few GeV. In this paper, it is argued that the approach based on the factorisation of the nuclear cross section provides a consistent framework for the calculation of neutrino-nucleus interactions in both the quasi elastic and inelastic channels. The near-degeneracy between theoretical models based on different assumptions, and the use of electron scattering data to advance the understanding of neutrino-nucleus cross sections are also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the factorisation approach to nuclear cross sections, combined with the Green's function formalism and spectral functions constrained by electron scattering data, provides a consistent framework for calculating neutrino-nucleus interactions across both quasi elastic and inelastic channels. However, the author emphasises that models based on conceptually different assumptions can yield similar results when compared to flux-averaged data, implying that agreement between theory and experiment may be accidental and that the sources of this degeneracy must be identified.

## Method

The author reviews the factorisation ansatz for neutrino-nucleus cross sections, where the nuclear response tensor is expressed as a convolution of the nucleon spectral function (describing momentum and energy distributions of nucleons in the nucleus) with elementary neutrino-nucleon cross sections. The approach is applied to charged-current quasi elastic scattering, two-nucleon emission processes involving meson-exchange currents, resonance production (including the P33(1232) and second resonance region states), and deep inelastic scattering, using spectral functions derived from (e,e'p) data and the Local Density Approximation. The author compares predictions from different theoretical models (Nieves et al. 2004, Megias et al. 2016) against MiniBooNE flux-averaged double-differential cross section data to illustrate the degeneracy problem.

## Key numbers

- Mean energy of MiniBooNE neutrino flux: ⟨Eν⟩ = 880 MeV
- Momentum transfer threshold for long-range correlations: |q| ≪ π/d ∼ 400 MeV
- Reduction of occupation probability for valence shell model states due to correlations: ∼30-35%
- Spectroscopic factor for carbon P-state protons from (e,e'p) data: ZP = 0.625 (reducing average from 4 to 2.5 protons)
- Spectroscopic factor from LDA spectral function: ZP = 0.64 (within ∼2% of experimental value)
- Continuum strength from JLab (e,e'p) experiment: 0.61 ± 0.06, compared to theoretical value 0.64
- ∼20% difference at the peak between spectral-function-based 1p1h cross section and local Fermi gas model predictions
- Minimum Q² for parton distributions used in DIS calculations: Q²min = 0.8 GeV²

## Context

This paper extends the factorisation approach previously developed for electron-nucleus scattering (Benhar et al. 2006) to neutrino interactions, addressing the flux-averaging problem that complicates interpretation of accelerator neutrino data. The work directly confronts the inadequacy of the Relativistic Fermi Gas Model exposed by MiniBooNE data (CCQE 2002) and the degeneracy between models such as those of Nieves et al. (2004) and Megias et al. (2016), which achieve similar agreement with data through different combinations of reaction mechanisms. The approach enables future work on disentangling 1p1h and 2p2h contributions, constraining axial-vector form factors, and extending calculations to heavier targets relevant to DUNE and other next-generation oscillation experiments.
