# Baryon Resonance Extraction from piN Data using a Unitary Multichannel Model

> A unitary multi-channel approach, first developed by the Carnegie-Mellon- Berkeley group, is applied to extract the pole positions, masses, and partial decay widths of nucleon resonances. Input used are the partial wave amplitudes for the transitions from piN to eight possible final baryon-meson final states. Because the model preserves analyticity, resonances are found as poles in the T matrix. This approach is designed to handle inelastic threshold openings with minimal model dependence. Although many of the known states have the same properties as in previous analyses, others change significantly. We pay particular attention to S11(1535) which is very close to the eta threshold.

## Key claim

The authors apply the Carnegie-Mellon-Berkeley (CMB) unitary multichannel model to extract baryon resonance pole positions, masses, and partial decay widths from modern partial wave amplitudes, finding that while many known states agree with previous analyses, some weakly excited resonances change significantly, particularly the S11(1535) resonance, which is very close to the eta threshold and whose extracted properties depend critically on proper treatment of threshold effects and channel coupling within the unitarity constraint.

## Method

The CMB model parameterizes transition amplitudes from piN to eight possible final baryon-meson states (piN, etaN, piDelta, rhoN, sigmaN, piN*(1440), KLambda, and omegaN) using a Dyson equation formalism with bare intermediate states dressed by self-energies computed via subtracted dispersion relations. The authors fit the VPI single-energy elastic partial wave amplitudes (SM95 analysis) and the VPI-KSU inelastic amplitudes from Manley et al. (1984) for piN -> pi pi N reactions, using the same piN -> etaN data and weights as Batinic et al. Resonances are identified as poles of the T-matrix in the complex energy plane, found by solving det(H(s)) = 0.

## Context

This work revives the CMB approach of Cutkosky et al. (1979) with updated input data, using the VPI single-energy amplitudes (SM95) and the VPI-KSU inelastic amplitudes from Manley et al. (1984), which include 30 percent more data than the original CMB analysis. The results are compared with the Kent State University (KSU) analysis by Manley and Saleski, the VPI analysis, the Karlsruhe-Helsinki (KH) analysis, and the Particle Data Group (PDG) compilations; the authors note that the CMB model's analyticity-preserving formalism is particularly suited for resonances near inelastic thresholds, unlike K-matrix approaches that cannot be analytically continued to find poles. This work enables future analyses of new exclusive data from Jefferson Lab, Bonn, and Brookhaven, and the authors plan to address how to differentiate between resonance poles, bound state or virtual poles, and poles that have moved across inelastic threshold cuts.
