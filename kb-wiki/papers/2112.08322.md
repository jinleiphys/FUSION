# Gorkov algebraic diagrammatic construction formalism at third order

> Background. The Gorkov approach to self-consistent Green's function theory has been formulated in [V. Somà, T. Duguet, C. Barbieri, Phys. Rev. C 84, 064317 (2011)]. Over the past decade, it has become a method of reference for first-principle computations of semi-magic nuclear isotopes. The currently available implementation is limited to a second-order self-energy and neglects particle-number non-conserving terms arising from contracting three-particle forces with anomalous propagators. For nuclear physics applications, this is sufficient to address first-order energy differences, ground-state radii and moments on an accurate enough basis. However, addressing absolute binding energies, fine spectroscopic details of $N\pm1$ particle systems or delicate quantities such as second-order energy differences associated to pairing gaps, requires to go to higher truncation orders. Purpose. The formalism is extended to third order in the algebraic diagrammatic construction (ADC) expansion with two-body Hamiltonians. Methods. The expansion of Gorkov propagators in Feynman diagrams is combined with the algebraic diagrammatic construction up to the third order as an organization scheme to generate the Gorkov self-energy. Results. Algebraic expressions for the static and dynamic contributions to the self-energy, along with equations for the matrix elements of the Gorkov eigenvalue problem, are derived. It is first done for a general basis before specifying the set of equations to the case of spherical systems displaying rotational symmetry. Workable approximations to the full self-consistency problem are also elaborated on. The formalism at third order it thus complete for a general two-body Hamiltonian. Conclusion. Working equations for the full Gorkov-ADC(3) are now available for numerical implementation.

## Key claim

The authors derive the complete algebraic expressions for the Gorkov self-energy at third order in the algebraic diagrammatic construction (ADC) expansion, limited to Hamiltonians containing up to two-body forces, thereby providing the formal foundation for numerical implementation of Gorkov-ADC(3) that is expected to improve upon the current second-order implementation's inability to accurately compute absolute binding energies and pairing gaps.

## Method

The expansion of Gorkov propagators in Feynman diagrams is combined with the algebraic diagrammatic construction up to third order as an organization scheme to generate the Gorkov self-energy. Algebraic expressions for the static and dynamic contributions to the self-energy, along with equations for the matrix elements of the Gorkov eigenvalue problem, are derived first for a general basis before specifying the set of equations to the case of spherical systems displaying rotational symmetry. Workable approximations to the full self-consistency problem are also elaborated on.

## Key numbers

* The currently available Gorkov SCGF implementation is limited to a second-order self-energy, which can grasp around 90% of correlation energy and predict accurate trends of nuclear binding energies and radii with varying proton-neutron asymmetry.
* The formalism is extended to third order (ADC(3)) for a general two-body Hamiltonian, making working equations available for numerical implementation.
* Applications of the second-order Gorkov formalism have covered complete isotopic and isotonic chains around O, Ca and Ni, eventually stretching to heavier isotopes up to A=140 (Arthuis 2020).

## Context

This work extends the Gorkov approach to self-consistent Green's function theory originally formulated by Soma, Duguet and Barbieri (2011), which had been limited to a second-order self-energy (ADC(2)) and neglected particle-number non-conserving terms from three-particle forces. The third-order truncation, or ADC(3), resums full Tamm-Dancoff series of ladder and rings diagrams among other terms and has become a method of reference for closed-shell nuclei and molecules, providing chemical accuracy predictions for binding energies and ionization potentials (Danovich 2011). This extension enables future numerical implementations that can address absolute binding energies, fine spectroscopic details of N±1 particle systems, and second-order energy differences associated to pairing gaps, as well as assess the rate at which good particle number is restored when going to higher truncation orders.
