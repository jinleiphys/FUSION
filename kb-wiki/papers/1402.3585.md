# Stabilizing Canonical-Ensemble Calculations in the Auxiliary-Field Monte Carlo Method

> Quantum Monte Carlo methods are powerful techniques for studying strongly interacting Fermi systems. However, implementing these methods on computers with finite-precision arithmetic requires careful attention to numerical stability. In the auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method, low-temperature or large-model-space calculations require numerically stabilized matrix multiplication. When adapting methods used in the grand-canonical ensemble to the canonical ensemble of fixed particle number, the numerical stabilization increases the number of required floating-point operations for computing observables by a factor of the size of the single-particle model space, and thus can greatly limit the systems that can be studied. We describe an improved method for stabilizing canonical-ensemble calculations in AFMC that exhibits better scaling, and present numerical tests that demonstrate the accuracy and improved performance of the method.

## Key claim

The paper presents an improved method for numerically stabilized auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) calculations in the canonical ensemble that reduces the computational scaling from O(N_s^4) to O(N_s^3), where N_s is the number of single-particle states, making canonical-ensemble calculations competitive with grand-canonical calculations for large model spaces.

## Method

The authors replace the standard approach of performing a separate matrix decomposition for each term in the discrete Fourier sum used for particle-number projection with a single stabilized diagonalization of the row-stratified matrix D B A derived from the stabilized decomposition U = A D B of the propagator. The eigenvalues and eigenvectors of U are then used to compute the canonical-ensemble partition function and one-body densities in O(N_s^3) operations. The method relies on the QR algorithm with balancing as implemented in LAPACK to stably diagonalize the ill-conditioned but row-stratified matrix.

## Key numbers

- The standard method scales as O(N_s^4) while the improved method scales as O(N_s^3), where N_s is the number of single-particle states.
- For a 20-atom cold Fermi gas at T = 0.1 hbar omega with N_max = 9 (N_s = 220), the standard method required 26973.2 seconds per two samples while the new method required 1300.4 seconds per two samples.
- For N_max = 3 (N_s = 20), both methods gave identical energy expectation values of 47.14058966 hbar omega, with the standard method taking 8.6 seconds and the new method taking 2.1 seconds per two samples.
- The QR stabilization method was numerically stable for condition numbers up to approximately 10^300 in test matrices.
- Calculations at N_max = 11 (N_s = 364) were practical with the new method but would have been impractical with the standard method.

## Context

This work extends the stabilized matrix multiplication techniques developed for grand-canonical AFMC calculations by Loh et al. (1992) to the canonical ensemble, where particle-number projection via Fourier sums was previously implemented by Alhassid et al. (2008). The method was applied by the authors to compute signatures of the superfluid phase transition in a finite-size cold atomic trapped Fermi gas (Gilbreth and Alhassid, 2013). The approach is general and applicable to other AFMC calculations where the canonical ensemble is important, such as finite nuclei.
