# Interpretable deep learning for nuclear deformation in heavy ion collisions

> The structure of heavy nuclei is difficult to disentangle in high-energy heavy-ion collisions. The deep convolution neural network (DCNN) might be helpful in mapping the complex final states of heavy-ion collisions to the nuclear structure in the initial state. Using DCNN for supervised regression, we successfully extracted the magnitude of the nuclear deformation from event-by-event correlation between the momentum anisotropy or elliptic flow ($v_2$) and total number of charged hadrons ($dN_{\rm ch}/dη$) within a Monte Carlo model. Furthermore, a degeneracy is found in the correlation between collisions of prolate-prolate and oblate-oblate nuclei. Using the Regression Attention Mask algorithm which is designed to interpret what has been learned by DCNN, we discovered that the correlation in total-overlapped collisions is sensitive to only large nuclear deformation, while the correlation in semi-overlapped collisions is discriminative for all magnitudes of nuclear deformation. The method developed in this study can pave a way for exploration of other aspects of nuclear structure in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
Using a deep convolutional neural network (DCNN) for supervised regression, the authors successfully extracted the magnitude of nuclear deformation parameters from the event-by-event correlation between elliptic flow ($v_2$) and charged hadron multiplicity ($dN_{\rm ch}/d\eta$) within a Monte Carlo model, but a degeneracy was found between collisions of prolate and oblate nuclei that prevented extraction of the sign of the deformation parameters.

## Method
The authors used the Trento Monte Carlo model to generate initial conditions for heavy-ion collisions of deformed uranium nuclei with deformation parameters $\beta_2 \in [-0.5, 0.5]$ and $\beta_4 \in [-0.2, 0.2]$, then mapped initial-state spatial eccentricity to final-state elliptic flow $v_2$ via a heuristic equation with added fluctuations. A 34-layer deep residual neural network with squeeze-excitation blocks was trained for supervised regression to predict $|\beta_2|$ and $|\beta_4|$ from 56x56 pixel images of the two-dimensional $(dN_{\rm ch}/d\eta, v_2)$ correlation distributions. The Regression Attention Mask algorithm, adapted from Grad-CAM by removing the ReLU activation, was used to identify the most discriminative regions in the input images.

## Key numbers
- 51 values of $\beta_2$ in the range $[-0.5, 0.5]$ and 51 values of $\beta_4$ in the range $[-0.2, 0.2]$ were used, giving 2601 groups of deformed uranium nuclei.
- 100,000 collisions were simulated per group, with the 50% highest-entropy events selected (centrality 0-50%).
- The heuristic mapping from eccentricity to $v_2$ used coefficients $k_2=0.2$, $k_2'=0.1$, with $\pm 10\%$ uniformly-distributed random fluctuations added.
- The "toe" region (central collisions, small $v_2$) was found sensitive only to large nuclear deformation $|\beta_2| > 0.17$.
- The "ankle" region (semi-overlapped collisions, $dN_{\rm ch}/{dY}|_{\rm normed}\approx 0.5$) was discriminative for all magnitudes of nuclear deformation.

## Context
This work extends prior studies that used nuclear deformation parameters from low-energy Coulomb excitation (Morinaga 1963, Coulomb Excitation 1986) as input for heavy-ion collision models (Heinz 2004, Kuhlman 2005, Filip 2013), and addresses the difficulty of disentangling nuclear structure from complex final states noted by Alvioli (2011, 2018) and Denicol (2014). The method enables future exploration of other nuclear structure aspects such as neutron skin, charge distribution, and alpha clustering, and could be extended to full (3+1)D hydrodynamic simulations using GPU-parallelized codes like CLVisc (Pang 2018) or GPU-VH (Bazow 2016).
