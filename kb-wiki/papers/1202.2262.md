# Quark-to-gluon composition of the quark-gluon plasma in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We study the evolution of the quark-gluon composition of the plasma created in ultra-Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions (uRHIC's) employing a partonic transport theory that includes both elastic and inelastic collisions plus a mean fields dynamics associated to the widely used quasi-particle model. The latter, able to describe lattice QCD thermodynamics, implies a "chemical" equilibrium ratio between quarks and gluons strongly increasing as $T\rightarrow T_c$, the phase transition temperature. Accordingly we see in realistic simulations of uRHIC's a rapid evolution from a gluon dominated initial state to a quark dominated plasma close to $T_c$. The quark to gluon ratio can be modified by about a factor of $\sim 20$ in the bulk of the system and appears to be large also in the high $p_T$ region. We discuss how this aspect, often overflown, can be important for a quantitative study of several key issues in the QGP physics: shear viscosity, jet quenching, quarkonia suppression. Furthermore a bulk plasma made by more than $80\%$ of quarks plus antiquarks provides a theoretical basis for hadronization via quark coalescence.

## Key claim

The quark-to-gluon composition of the quark-gluon plasma created in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions evolves rapidly from a gluon-dominated initial state to a quark-dominated plasma near the phase transition temperature \(T_c\), with the ratio \(N_{q+\bar{q}}/N_g\) changing by about a factor of \(\sim 20\) in the bulk of the system, but this result depends on modeling the plasma as a massive quasi-particle system that reproduces lattice QCD thermodynamics, not as a massless gas.

## Method

The authors employed a Boltzmann-Vlasov transport theory that includes both elastic and inelastic (gluon-to-quark conversion, \(gg \leftrightarrow q\bar{q}\)) collisions, coupled with a mean-field dynamics from a quasi-particle model (QPM) that gives temperature-dependent masses to quarks and gluons. The inelastic cross sections were evaluated at leading-order pQCD with finite masses and a running coupling \(\alpha_s(Q)\) with \(Q^2 = (\pi T)^2\). The transport equations were solved numerically on a 3D lattice using test-particle methods for \(Au+Au\) collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) AGeV and \(Pb+Pb\) collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=5.5\) ATeV, with initial conditions from a Glauber model and Boltzmann-Juttner plus mini-jet distributions.

## Key numbers

- The quark-to-gluon ratio \(R_{qg} = N_{q+\bar{q}}/N_g\) evolves from an initial value of 0.25 to a final value of \(\sim 4\) for \(Au+Au\) at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) AGeV, corresponding to a change by a factor of \(\sim 20\).
- For \(Pb+Pb\) at \(\sqrt{s}=5.5\) ATeV, the final ratio is moderately smaller than for \(Au+Au\).
- At freeze-out, the plasma is composed of more than \(80\%\) quarks plus antiquarks.
- The initial maximum temperature at the fireball center is \(T_0 = 340\) MeV for RHIC (with initial time \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm/c) and \(T_0 = 600\) MeV for LHC (with \(\tau_0 = 0.3\) fm/c).
- In the massless case, the system reaches chemical equilibrium with \(R_{qg} \sim 2\) in less than 1 fm/c.
- Changing the initial ratio by a factor of two affects the final ratio by less than \(10\%\).

## Context

This work extends the quasi-particle model (QPM) approach previously developed by Plumari et al. (2011) to describe lattice QCD thermodynamics, and applies it for the first time in a full dynamical transport simulation of heavy-ion collisions. The results contradict the common assumption of a gluon-dominated plasma used in many jet quenching models and provide a theoretical basis for the quark coalescence hadronization mechanism that explains the baryon/meson anomaly and quark-number scaling of elliptic flow observed at RHIC (Greco et al., 2003; Fries et al., 2003). The findings have implications for quantitative studies of shear viscosity, jet quenching, and quarkonia suppression, and suggest that the QGP exhibits even stronger deviations from perturbative behavior than previously thought.
