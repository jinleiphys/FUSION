# Searching for Scalar Dark Matter in Atoms and Astrophysical Phenomena: Variation of Fundamental Constants

> We propose to search for scalar dark matter via its effects on the electromagnetic fine-structure constant and particle masses. Scalar dark matter that forms an oscillating classical field produces `slow' linear-in-time drifts and oscillating variations of the fundamental constants, while scalar dark matter that forms topological defects produces transient-in-time variations of the constants of Nature. These variations can be sought for with atomic clock, laser interferometer and pulsar timing measurements. Atomic spectroscopy and Big Bang nucleosynthesis measurements already give improved bounds on the quadratic interaction parameters of scalar dark matter with the photon, electron, and light quarks by up to 15 orders of magnitude, while Big Bang nucleosynthesis measurements provide the first such constraints on the interaction parameters of scalar dark matter with the massive vector bosons.

# Searching for Scalar Dark Matter in Atoms and Astrophysical Phenomena: Variation of Fundamental Constants

## Key claim
Scalar dark matter that interacts quadratically with Standard Model matter can produce variations of the electromagnetic fine-structure constant and particle masses, and existing atomic spectroscopy and Big Bang nucleosynthesis measurements already constrain these interactions by up to 15 orders of magnitude, with Big Bang nucleosynthesis providing the first constraints on scalar interactions with massive vector bosons. The claim is conditional on scalar dark matter existing in the ultralight mass range (sub-eV) and forming either a coherently oscillating classical field or topological defects.

## Method
The authors derive how quadratic couplings of a scalar field to photons, fermions, and massive vector bosons alter the fine-structure constant and particle masses, then apply constraints from Big Bang nucleosynthesis measurements of primordial helium-4 abundance, atomic dysprosium spectroscopy, and supernova energy-loss bounds. They also propose search strategies using atomic clock comparisons, laser interferometry, and pulsar timing measurements to detect oscillating or transient variations of fundamental constants.

## Key numbers
- Scalar dark matter mass range considered: $m_\phi \sim 10^{-24} - 10^{-20}$ eV
- Stellar energy-loss bounds on quadratic coupling scales: $\Lambda'_\gamma \gtrsim 3 \times 10^{3}$ GeV, $\Lambda'_p \gtrsim 15 \times 10^{3}$ GeV, $\Lambda'_e \gtrsim 3 \times 10^{3}$ GeV
- Fifth-force bound on proton quadratic coupling: $\Lambda'_p \gtrsim 2 \times 10^{3}$ GeV
- Atomic spectroscopy and Big Bang nucleosynthesis measurements improve bounds on quadratic interaction parameters by up to 15 orders of magnitude
- Pulsar rotation periods range: $T \simeq 1$ ms to 10 s

## Context
This work extends the search for dark matter beyond traditional WIMP scattering experiments, which are fourth-power in interaction strength, to ultralight scalar candidates that produce first-power effects through variations of fundamental constants. The authors build on prior proposals for scalar dark matter solving structure formation problems (Gruzinov 2000, Marsh 2013, Schive 2014) and on existing constraints from supernova energy-loss and fifth-force searches (Olive 2008). The paper enables new experimental searches using atomic clocks, laser interferometers, and pulsar timing arrays, with the first laboratory search for oscillating fine-structure variation already performed in atomic dysprosium (Budker 2015).
