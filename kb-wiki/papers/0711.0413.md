# Non-inertial effects in fusion reactions of astrophysical interest

> We discuss the effects of non-inertial motion in reactions occurring in laboratory, stars, and elsewhere. It is demonstrated that non-inertial effects due to large accelerations during nuclear collisions might have appreciable effects nuclear and atomic transitions. We also explore the magnitude of the corrections induced by strong gravitational fields on nuclear reactions in massive, compact stars, and the neighborhood of black holes.

# Non-inertial effects in fusion reactions of astrophysical interest

## Key claim
Non-inertial effects due to large accelerations during nuclear collisions can produce appreciable corrections to nuclear and atomic transitions, but the effect is negligible in stellar environments except near black holes; the correction term arises only when relativistic definitions of the center of mass are used, and its magnitude depends on the square of the acceleration.

## Method
The authors derive a relativistic Hamiltonian for a many-body system in an accelerated frame to order 1/c², using the Pryce (1948) definition of the relativistic center of mass. They apply first-order perturbation theory with the resulting non-inertial correction term H_nin = (A²/c²) Σ m_i z_i² to calculate energy shifts in atomic levels, modifications to Coulomb barrier penetrability via the WKB approximation, and transition probabilities in Coulomb excitation. The acceleration A is replaced by the local gravitational field g for stellar applications.

## Key numbers
- Two lead nuclei in a head-on collision at 500 MeV center-of-mass kinetic energy reach a closest distance of 19.4 fm and experience an acceleration of ~10²⁷ m/s².
- For proton fusion on chlorine (Z=17) at E=0.1 MeV, the non-inertial effect becomes visible for accelerations of order g = 4×10²⁷ m/s², which is 26 orders of magnitude larger than Earth's gravity and 15 orders larger than at a neutron star surface (assuming M_ns = M_sun, R_ns = 10 km).
- The energy shift of the 2p₁/₂ level in hydrogen equals the Lamb shift (4.372×10⁻⁶ eV) for A ≈ 10²¹ m/s², 9 orders of magnitude larger than gravity at a neutron star surface.
- For a typical fusion reaction with E=1 MeV, Z₁=Z₂=10, A₁=A₂=20, the Coulomb radius is 144 fm and the acceleration is 6.2×10²⁵ m/s².
- For a halo state with separation energy S=100 keV, the non-inertial energy shift is 0.024 eV; for larger nuclear systems it reaches 1-10 eV.
- For lead-lead collision at 500 MeV, the ratio of non-inertial to Coulomb excitation probabilities is |a_if^nin / a_if|² = (0.0093X)², where X is the number of nucleons participating in the transition.
- Non-inertial corrections to the polarization potential can increase the cross sections for D(d,p)T and ⁶Li(d,α)⁴He by up to 10%.

## Context
This work extends the relativistic treatment of accelerated many-body systems following the center-of-mass definition of Pryce (1948) and the formalism of Landau and Lifshitz. It contradicts the common belief that non-inertial effects in laboratory nuclear reactions are negligible. The authors identify the triple-alpha reaction forming carbon via the ⁸Be resonance (width 5.57±0.25 eV, resonance energy 91.84±0.04 keV) as a possible case where non-inertial effects could broaden shallow nuclear states. The paper also suggests that non-inertial corrections to the polarization potential might help resolve the longstanding discrepancy between experimental and theoretical electron screening energies in fusion reactions, previously studied by Bateman (1997) and Rolfs (2001).
