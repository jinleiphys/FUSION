# Detection of Antineutrinos for Non-Proliferation

> We discuss the feasibility of using the detection of electron antineutrinos produced in fission to monitor the time dependence of the plutonium content of nuclear power reactors. If practical such a scheme would allow world-wide, automated monitoring of reactors and, thereby, the detection of certain proliferation scenarios. For GW$_e$ power reactors the count rates and the sensitivity of the antineutrino spectrum (to the core burn-up) suggest that monitoring of the gross operational status of the reactor from outside the containment vessel is feasible. As the plutonium content builds up in a given burn cycle the total number of antineutrinos steadily drops and this variation is quite detectable, assuming fixed reactor power. The average antineutrino energy also steadily drops, and a measurement of this variation would be very useful to help off set uncertainties in the total reactor power. However, the expected change in the antineutrino signal from the diversion of a significant quantity (SQ) of plutonium, which would typically require the diversion of as little as a single fuel assemblies in a GW$_e$ reactor, would be very difficult to detect.

## Key claim

Antineutrino monitoring can detect gross deviations from a reactor's declared operational mode (such as replacing 2/3 of irradiated fuel with fresh fuel) from outside the containment vessel, but the expected change in the antineutrino signal from the diversion of a significant quantity of plutonium (as little as a single fuel assembly in a GW_e reactor) would be very difficult to detect, requiring better than 1% accuracy in the antineutrino detection rate.

## Method

The authors performed coupled burn-up calculations using the ERPI/CELL and CINDER-2 codes to model the time-dependent fission fractions and antineutrino spectra for 2.7% and 4.2% enriched pressurized water reactors (PWRs) over their fuel cycles. They computed cumulative antineutrino spectra from thermal neutron induced fission of 235U, 239Pu, and 241Pu and fast neutron induced fission of 238U using England and Rider evaluated cumulative fission product yields and beta decay branching ratios from ENDF/B-VI. The expected antineutrino event rates were then calculated for a 1 cubic meter liquid scintillator detector situated 24 m from the core of a 3.4 GW_t reactor, and the changes in count rate were evaluated for normal fuel cycling and various diversion scenarios.

## Key numbers

- A 3.4 GW_t power reactor emits about 10^26 antineutrinos per day.
- For a 1 cubic meter liquid scintillator detector situated 24 m from the core of a 3.4 GW_t reactor, the expected signal is 2740 antineutrino events per day.
- The drop in the number of antineutrinos over one fuel cycle in a 2.7% enriched PWR operating in equilibrium is about 5%.
- A gross misuse scenario where 2/3 of irradiated fuel is replaced with fresh fuel (instead of the regular 1/3) would lead to a 10% shift in the antineutrino count rate.
- The threshold for the antineutrino-proton charge-current reaction is 1.8 MeV, and the cross section at the mean fission antineutrino energy is about 10^-42 cm^2.
- The detected antineutrino spectrum peaks at about 3.8 MeV when folded with the detection cross section.
- The 2.7% enriched PWR corresponds to a 3 year discharge neutron exposure of 30 GW days per metric ton of uranium (GWd/MTU).

## Context

This work extends earlier feasibility studies on antineutrino detection for reactor monitoring, including the ongoing experimental study at the San Onofre reactor by Bernstein et al. and the historical reactor antineutrino experiments by Reines and Cowan. The paper builds on large-scale neutrino detector technologies developed for particle physics (Super-Kamiokande, SNO, KamLAND, LSND) and prior reactor antineutrino spectrum measurements such as CHOOZ. The results enable assessment of whether antineutrino monitoring can address specific safeguards scenarios, concluding that while gross misuse is detectable, diversion of a single fuel assembly containing a significant quantity of plutonium is unlikely to be observable.
