# Parallel scattering, saturation, and generalized Abramovskii-Gribov-Kancheli (AGK) theorem in the EPOS4 framework, with applications for heavy-ion collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ of 5.02 TeV and 200 GeV

> Ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions will first realize many nucleon-nucleon scatterings, happening instantaneously and therefore necessarily in parallel, due to the short collision time. An appropriate quantum mechanical tool to treat that problem is S-matrix theory, and it has been known for a long time how to derive a simple geometric probabilistic picture, still widely used, and here the Abramovskii-Gribov-Kancheli (AGK) theorem plays a crucial role. All this is done in a scenario where energy conservation is not taken care of, but this is needed, in particular for Monte Carlo simulations. When introducing energy-momentum sharing properly, the AGK theorem does not apply anymore, nor do simple geometric concepts such as binary scaling. I will discuss this (very serious) problem, and how it can be solved, in the EPOS4 framework. When connecting the multiple Pomeron approach (for parallel scatterings) and perturbative QCD, one is actually forced to implement in a very particular way saturation scales, in order to get an approach free of contradictions. One recovers a generalized AGK theorem (gAGK), valid at large $p_{t}$ (larger than the relevant saturation scales). I discuss how gAGK is related to factorization (in proton-proton scatterings) and binary scaling (in heavy-ion collisions). I will show some applications, using this new approach as an initial condition for hydrodynamical evolutions, for heavy-ion collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ of 5.02 TeV and 200 GeV, to get some idea about the energy dependence.

## Key claim

The standard AGK theorem and geometric concepts like binary scaling break down when energy-momentum sharing is properly introduced into the parallel scattering formalism; a generalized AGK theorem (gAGK) valid at large transverse momentum can be recovered only by implementing saturation scales in a particular way within the EPOS4 framework, and the regularization of the "sign problem" (negative probabilities) requires an ad hoc modification of the g-functions that nevertheless preserves the large-impact-parameter behavior.

## Method

The author develops a multiple scattering formalism based on S-matrix theory and the Gribov-Regge (GR) approach, extended to include energy-momentum sharing (referred to as GR+). The T-matrix is expressed in terms of products of sub-T-matrices (Pomerons), and the inelastic cross section is obtained via the optical theorem in impact parameter representation. The functions G(x+,x-,b) representing cut Pomerons are parametrized as sums of powers (x+x-)βN, and the vertices are parametrized as V(x)=xα_remn, allowing analytic integration. To solve the sign problem (negative values of the probability-like quantity W_AB at small impact parameters), the author introduces a "g-factorization" modification of the g functions, and then connects the multiple Pomeron approach to perturbative QCD by implementing saturation scales as lower limits of virtualities for DGLAP evolutions.

## Key numbers

* For PbPb collisions at √s_NN = 5.02 TeV and AuAu collisions at 200 GeV, results are shown using the new approach as initial condition for hydrodynamical evolutions.
* The sign problem appears below b = 1 fm, where W_11(1,1) becomes negative, reaching roughly -0.4 at b = 0.
* The parametrization of G functions uses a sum over N = 1 to 4 terms of the form α_N (x+x-)β_N, with coefficients depending on impact parameter b.

## Context

This paper is the fourth in a series of publications about the EPOS4 framework (Werner 2023), extending the earlier Gribov-Regge approach (Gribov 1967, 1968; Abramovsky 1973) which did not include energy-momentum conservation. The work addresses the problem that standard AGK cancellations and binary scaling fail when energy-momentum sharing is introduced, a difficulty noted in prior work by Drescher (2000). The solution builds on saturation concepts from Gribov (1983), McLerran (1993), and others, and enables event-class-dependent calculations for heavy-ion collisions that are compatible with both QCD and probabilistic interpretations.
