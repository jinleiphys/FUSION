# Hydrodynamics at RHIC

> The hydrodynamical models used to describe the evolution of heavy-ion collisions are briefly reviewed and their results compared with recent RHIC data.

## Key claim

Hydrodynamical models successfully reproduce single-particle RHIC data at low transverse momentum, but the simultaneous description of HBT radii remains unsuccessful, suggesting that the collision system behaves like a thermal system for some observables while the HBT puzzle points to unresolved issues in freeze-out, hadronization, or initial state modeling.

## Method

The paper reviews ideal-fluid hydrodynamical models applied to heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, where the evolution is determined by solving conservation laws with an equation of state (EoS) and specified initial conditions. The models assume boost-invariant longitudinal flow and cylindrical symmetry for central collisions, with freeze-out treated via the Cooper-Frye algorithm at a chosen temperature. Comparisons are made to RHIC data on charged particle multiplicity, transverse energy, pT spectra, elliptic anisotropy, and HBT correlations, using various EoSs including first-order phase transition and crossover constructions.

## Key numbers

- Maximum initial temperature T_max = 328 MeV and energy density epsilon_max = 21.4 GeV/fm^3 at initial time tau_0 = 0.6 fm/c in central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 130 GeV (Heinz 2001)
- Average energy density at tau = 1 fm/c: <epsilon> = 5.4 GeV/fm^3, consistent with experimental estimates
- Phase transition temperature T_c approximately 155-175 MeV from lattice QCD
- Freeze-out temperatures in Pb+Pb at SPS vary between 100 and 140 MeV in different calculations; Heinz (2001) uses T_f = 128 MeV with chemical freeze-out at T_chem = 165 MeV
- Calculated transverse energy at RHIC is 10-20% larger than experimental value (Phenix)
- Elliptic anisotropy data reaches hydrodynamical limit in central and semicentral collisions; best overall description uses LH8 EoS with smaller latent heat
- HBT ratio R_out/R_side from calculations is larger than one, while experimental value is of order one

## Context

This review extends earlier hydrodynamical work at SPS energies (Rischke 1998, Sollfrank 1996) and compares with RHIC data from Phenix (Velkovska 2001), STAR (Adler 2001), and Phobos (Back 2000, 2001). The paper contrasts the EoS constructions of Teaney (2001) using bag model speed of sound with explicit latent heat parameters, and Zschiesche (2001) using parametrized sigma-omega models. The HBT puzzle is identified as the key open question, with calculations from Zschiesche (2001) and Heinz (2001) unable to reproduce the data, while elliptic anisotropy favors a first-order phase transition and HBT radii favor a crossover transition. Future work should refine freeze-out procedures, hadronization, and initial state modeling to resolve this contradiction.
