# Effect of mass asymmetry on the mass dependence of balance energy

> We demonstrate the role of the mass asymmetry on the balance energy (Ebal) by studying asymmetric reactions throughout the periodic table and over entire colliding geometry. Our results, which are almost independent of the system size and as well as of the colliding geometries indicate a sizeable effect of the asymmetry of the reaction on the balance energy.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the mass asymmetry of colliding nuclei has a sizeable, systematic effect on the balance energy (Ebal) which is almost independent of the total system mass and colliding geometry, contrary to earlier studies that did not control for asymmetry and thus saw scatter around the average power-law mass dependence.

## Method

The author used the Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) model to simulate heavy-ion collisions for four fixed total masses (A_TOT = 40, 80, 160, 240) at four asymmetry parameters (η = 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7) for peripheral collisions (b/b_max = 0.5) over incident energies from 90 to 500 MeV/nucleon. The balance energy was extracted from the directed transverse momentum as a function of incident energy, using a momentum-dependent soft equation of state (SMD) with standard energy-dependent Cugnon cross-section.

## Key numbers

- Power-law exponent τ for Ebal ∝ A^τ was -0.34, -0.36, -0.35, and -0.34 for asymmetry parameters η = 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, and 0.7, respectively.
- The variation in Ebal for η varying between 0.1 and 0.7 was approximately 32, 35, 19, and 19 MeV for total masses A_TOT = 40, 80, 160, and 240, respectively.
- For large asymmetries (η = 0.7), the effect of asymmetry on Ebal can be 15% with momentum-dependent interactions (MDI) and 40% without MDI.
- For lighter masses, variation of η can result in about 35 MeV change in Ebal.

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of balance energy by controlling for mass asymmetry, which earlier studies (e.g., Chugh and Puri) had not done, explaining why individual Ebal values in prior work deviated from the average power-law fit. The results enable future experiments and calculations to account for asymmetry when extracting the nuclear equation of state from flow observables, and show that the effect of asymmetry is uniform across system masses and impact parameters.
