# Large-scale configuration interaction description of the structure of nuclei around $^{100}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb

> In this contribution I would like to discuss briefly the recent developments of the nuclear configuration interaction shell model approach. As examples, we apply the model to calculate the structure and decay properties of low-lying states in neutron-deficient nuclei around $^{100}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb that are of great experimental and theoretical interests.

## Key claim

The nuclear configuration interaction shell model, despite severe computational limitations on the number of orbitals that can be included, can now describe heavy nuclei around \(^{100}\)Sn and \(^{208}\)Pb with the help of Petaflops supercomputers, provided that efficient truncation schemes based on monopole energy ordering are applied.

## Method

The author reviews the configuration interaction shell model approach, where an effective Hamiltonian expressed in terms of single-particle energies and two-body matrix elements is diagonalized within a finite model space. Truncation of the shell-model basis is achieved by ordering partitions according to their total monopole energy and retaining only the lowest-energy configurations, which is justified because the Hamiltonian is dominated by the diagonal monopole channel. Large-scale calculations are then performed for neutron-deficient nuclei around \(^{100}\)Sn and \(^{208}\)Pb, including studies of binding energies, excitation energies, pairing gaps, and alpha clustering.

## Key numbers

- The state-of-the-art configuration interaction algorithms can diagonalize matrices with dimension up to \(2\times10^{10}\) (or \(\sim10^{9}\) with three-body interaction or identical particles only).
- For Pb isotopes heavier than \(^{196}\)Pb, the difference between calculated and experimental shell-model energies is less than 100 keV.
- The largest deviation in Pb isotope calculations appears for \(^{194}\)Pb, where the calculation overestimates the shell-model energy by 300 keV.
- For the isotopes \(^{200,194}\)Pb, convergence of the truncation calculation can be reached with only about 10% of the total M-scheme wave function.

## Context

This work extends the successful shell-model description of nuclei below \(^{100}\)Sn to heavier systems around \(^{100}\)Sn and \(^{208}\)Pb, which were previously inaccessible to standard shell-model calculations. The truncation scheme based on monopole energy ordering (Qi 2016) builds on earlier importance truncation methods and seniority coupling schemes (Talmi 1993). The results enable microscopic studies of the astrophysical r-process third peak via the \(N=126\) isotones, and provide a framework for understanding alpha clustering and neutron-proton correlation in \(N\sim Z\) nuclei, building on prior work by Seweryniak (2006) and Liddick (2006).
