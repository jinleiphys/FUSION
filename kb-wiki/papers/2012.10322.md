# Resonant Shattering Flares as Multimessenger Probes of the Nuclear Symmetry Energy

> The behaviour of the nuclear symmetry energy near saturation density is important for our understanding of dense nuclear matter. This density dependence can be parameterised by the nuclear symmetry energy and its derivatives evaluated at nuclear saturation density. In this work we show that the core-crust interface mode of a neutron star is sensitive to these parameters, through the (density-weighted) shear-speed within the crust, which is in turn dependent on the symmetry energy profile of dense matter. We calculate the frequency at which the neutron star quadrupole ($\ell = 2$) crust-core interface mode must be driven by the tidal field of its binary partner to trigger a Resonant Shattering Flare (RSF). We demonstrate that coincident multimessenger timing of an RSF and gravitational wave chirp from a neutron star merger would enable us to place constraints on the symmetry energy parameters that are competitive with those from current nuclear experiments.

## Key claim

The paper shows that coincident multimessenger timing of a Resonant Shattering Flare (RSF) and the gravitational wave chirp from a neutron star merger could constrain the nuclear symmetry energy parameters \(J\), \(L\), and \(K_{\rm sym}\) with precision competitive with current nuclear experiments, but only if the crust-core interface mode frequency can be precisely measured from the gravitational wave frequency at the time of the RSF.

## Method

The authors use an extended Skyrme mean field model to generate neutron star equations of state parameterized by the symmetry energy parameters \(J\), \(L\), and \(K_{\rm sym}\), combined with a compressible liquid drop model for the crust and piecewise polytropes for the core at supersaturation densities. They solve the linearized Newtonian perturbation equations (in the Cowling approximation) for the quadrupole (\(\ell=2\)) crust-core interface mode frequency, applying jump conditions at the crust-core boundary and boundary conditions at the surface and center, for a grid of stellar models with fixed maximum mass (2.2 \(M_\odot\)) and averaged moment of inertia at 1.4 \(M_\odot\).

## Key numbers

* For a neutron star with symmetry energy parameters \(J=30\) MeV, \(L=50\) MeV, and \(K_{\rm sym}=-80\) MeV, the quadrupole crust-core interface mode frequency is \(f=134.3\) Hz.
* Varying \(J\) from 25 to 35 MeV (holding other parameters fixed) changes the stellar radius by 0.21 km, the crust-core transition radius by \(-0.23\) km, and the density-weighted average shear speed by \(3.8\times 10^6\) cm/s.
* Varying \(L\) from 20 to 70 MeV changes the stellar radius by 0.47 km, the crust-core transition radius by 0.48 km, and the average shear speed by \(-4.9\times 10^6\) cm/s.
* Varying \(K_{\rm sym}\) from \(-200\) to 40 MeV changes the stellar radius by 0.87 km, the crust-core transition radius by 0.79 km, and the average shear speed by \(1.8\times 10^6\) cm/s.
* The reference EOS with \(J=30\) MeV, \(L=50\) MeV, \(K_{\rm sym}=-80\) MeV gives a 1.4 \(M_\odot\) neutron star with radius \(R_*=11.92\) km, crust-core transition radius \(R_{\rm cc}=10.95\) km, and average shear speed \(\bar{c}_t=4.8\times 10^6\) cm/s.
* The RSF resonance window duration is \(t_{\rm res} \sim 0.1\) seconds.

## Context

This work extends the Resonant Shattering Flare mechanism proposed by Tsang (2012) by explicitly linking the crust-core interface mode frequency to the nuclear symmetry energy parameters \(J\), \(L\), and \(K_{\rm sym}\) through the density-weighted shear speed in the crust. It builds on prior work showing that crust properties and shear waves are sensitive to the symmetry energy (Newton 2013, Steiner 2009, Sotani 2012, 2013), and that the symmetry energy can be constrained by nuclear experiments (Tsang 2009, Lattimer 2013) and astrophysical observations (Steiner 2010, 2013). A coincident RSF and gravitational wave detection would enable constraints competitive with these existing methods, providing a new multimessenger probe of dense matter.
