# Jet lag effect and leading hadron production

> We propose a solution for the long standing puzzle of a too steeply falling fragmentation function for a quark fragmenting into a pion, calculated by Berger [1] in the Born approximation. Contrary to the simple anticipation that gluon resummation worsens the problem, we find good agreement with data. Higher quark Fock states slow down the quark, an effect which we call jet lag. It can be also expressed in terms of vacuum energy loss. As a result, the space-time development of the jet shrinks and the $z$-dependence becomes flatter than in the Born approximation. The space-time pattern is also of great importance for in-medium hadronization.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the long-standing puzzle of the too-steeply falling fragmentation function for a quark fragmenting into a pion, calculated by Berger in the Born approximation, can be resolved by including higher Fock states that slow down the quark (the "jet lag" effect), contrary to the simple anticipation that gluon resummation would worsen the problem.

## Method

The authors start from Berger's Born approximation for the fragmentation function \(q \to \pi q\), which gives a \((1-z)^2\) dependence. They then incorporate gluon resummation by decomposing the initial bare quark into Fock states containing a quark and multiple gluons, where the quark in higher Fock states carries only a fraction of the total momentum. The fragmentation function is obtained by convoluting the Born term with the quark momentum distribution within these Fock states, including a Sudakov suppression factor that bans gluon radiation with energy above \(E(1-z)\), and adding both perturbative gluon radiation and nonperturbative string energy loss (with two models, STRING-I and STRING-II) to compute the effective fractional momentum \(\tilde z\) of the retarded quark.

## Key numbers

* The Born approximation fragmentation function scales as \(\partial D^{(Born)}_{\pi/q}(z)/\partial k^2 \propto (1-z)^2/k^4\).
* The coherence length for pion radiation is \(L^\pi_c = 2Ez(1-z)/[k^2 + z^2 m_q^2 + (1-z)m_\pi^2]\).
* The soft cutoff for gluon radiation is fixed at \(\lambda = 0.7\ \text{GeV}\).
* The string tension for nonperturbative energy loss is taken at its static value \(\kappa = 1\ \text{GeV/fm}\).
* The gluon correlation radius from the stochastic vacuum model is \(a = 0.3-0.35\ \text{fm}\).
* The rate of radiative energy loss at small \(1-z < LQ^2/2E\) is \(dE/dL = -(4\alpha_s E/3\pi L)(1-z)\).
* The maximal length available for radiation is \(L_{max} = E/2\lambda^2\).

## Context

This work extends the Born approximation calculation of Berger (1980) for the quark-to-pion fragmentation function, which was known to disagree with phenomenological fits such as KKP and BKK. The paper introduces the "jet lag" effect as a mechanism to flatten the \(z\)-dependence, and it provides a space-time picture of jet development that is crucial for understanding in-medium hadronization, such as hadron attenuation in semi-inclusive deep-inelastic scattering (SIDIS) off nuclei and hadron quenching in heavy ion collisions.
