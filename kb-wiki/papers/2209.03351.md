# Neutron stars as extreme laboratories for gravity tests

> Neutron stars are versatile in their application to studying various important aspects of fundamental physics, in particular strong-field gravity tests and the equation of state for super-dense nuclear matter at low temperatures. However, in many cases these two objectives are degenerate to each other. We discuss how pulsar timing and gravitational waves provide accurate measurements of neutron star systems and how to effectively break the degeneracy using tools like universal relations. We also present perspectives on future opportunities and challenges in the field of neutron star physics.

## Key claim

Neutron stars can simultaneously test strong-field gravity and probe the equation of state of super-dense nuclear matter, but these two objectives are degenerate with each other; the degeneracy can be broken using universal relations among neutron star observables that are nearly independent of the equation of state.

## Method

The authors review how pulsar timing (using radio telescopes to record pulse arrival times from rotating neutron stars over decades) and gravitational-wave observations (from merging binary neutron stars) provide complementary measurements of neutron star systems. They describe how universal relations such as the I-Love-Q relation (connecting moment of inertia, tidal Love number, and quadrupole moment) can be used to test gravity theories without contamination from nuclear physics uncertainties, by measuring any two of the three quantities independently.

## Key numbers

- The double pulsar PSR J0737-3039 has an orbital period of 0.1022515592973 ± 0.0000000000010 days, enabling multiple relativistic gravity tests including orbital decay measurements that exceed all previous results by orders of magnitude.
- Heavy pulsars with masses ~2 solar masses have ruled out "soft" equations of state whose maximum neutron star mass is smaller than 2 solar masses.
- The I-Love-Q universal relations show an equation-of-state variation of ~1% or smaller, which is small enough for gravity tests given that typical measurement errors of I-Love-Q are of order 10%.
- For neutron stars with similar masses (~1.4 solar masses), combining observations from N events can improve measurement accuracy by a factor of sqrt(N).

## Context

This perspective article extends the work on neutron star gravity tests from binary pulsar studies (Damour 2007, Shao 2016) and gravitational-wave observations (LIGO/Virgo 2017). It builds on the I-Love-Q universal relations discovered by Yagi and Yunes (2013) and their application to parity-breaking gravity tests, which was recently confirmed by Silva et al. (2020) using LIGO/Virgo Love number measurements and NICER x-ray observations. The paper enables future multi-messenger approaches combining pulsar timing, gravitational waves, and black hole shadow observations (Event Horizon Telescope 2022) to test gravity, and identifies open questions about extending universal relations to neutron stars with different masses and developing model-independent parameterized tests.
