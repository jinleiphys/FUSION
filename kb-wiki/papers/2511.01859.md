# Uncertainties in the production of iron-group nuclides in core-collapse supernovae from Monte Carlo variations of reaction rates

> Core-collapse supernovae, occurring at the end of massive star evolution, produce heavy elements, including those in the iron peak. Although the explosion mechanism is not yet fully understood, theoretical models can reproduce optical observations and observed elemental abundances. However, many nuclear reaction rates involved in explosive nucleosynthesis have large uncertainties, impacting the reliability of abundance predictions. To address this, we have previously developed a Monte Carlo-based nucleosynthesis code that accounts for reaction rate uncertainties and has been applied to nucleosynthesis processes beyond iron. Our framework is also well suited for studying explosive nucleosynthesis in supernovae. In this paper, we investigate 1D explosion models using the "PUSH method", focusing on progenitors with varying metallicities and initial masses around $M_\mathrm{ZAMS} = 16 M_{\odot}$. Detailed post-process nucleosynthesis calculations and Monte Carlo analyses are used to explore the effects of reaction rate uncertainties and to identify key reaction rates in explosive nucleosynthesis. We find that many reactions have little impact on the production of iron-group nuclei, as these elements are primarily synthesized in the nuclear statistical equilibrium. However, we identify a few "key reactions" that significantly influence the production of radioactive nuclei, which may affect astrophysical observables. In particular, for the production of ${}^{44}$Ti, we confirm that several traditionally studied nuclear reactions have a strong impact. However, determining a single reaction rate is insufficient to draw a definitive conclusion.

## Key claim

While many nuclear reactions have little impact on iron-group production because these elements are primarily synthesized in nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE), a few "key reactions" significantly influence the production of radioactive nuclei such as Ti-44; however, the authors caution that determining a single reaction rate is insufficient to draw a definitive conclusion about Ti-44 production.

## Method

The authors used the PUSH method to generate 1D explosion models of 16 solar-mass progenitors at solar and sub-solar metallicities (models s16, w16, and u16), then performed post-process nucleosynthesis calculations with the PizBuin Monte Carlo framework. They varied 7,992 nuclear reactions and decays using temperature-dependent uncertainty factors (e.g., factors of 2 for (n,gamma) and 1/10 for (alpha,gamma) lower limits) and computed weighted Pearson correlation coefficients between rate variations and final abundances, identifying key reactions as those with |r_corr| >= 0.65.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear reaction network includes 615 nuclides with 7,992 nuclear decays and reactions, ranging up to mass number A = 80.
- The s16 model produces 0.2621 solar masses of Ni-56, w16 produces 0.2047 solar masses, and u16 produces 0.4307 solar masses.
- For the s16 model, Ca-41 production shows a correlation coefficient of -0.67 with the Ar-38 + alpha <-> n + Ca-41 reaction at Level 1.
- For the s16 model, Cr-48 production shows a correlation coefficient of -0.82 with the Cr-48 + alpha <-> p + Mn-51 reaction at Level 1.
- For the s16 model, Mn-52 production shows a correlation coefficient of -0.70 with the Fe-52 + alpha reaction at Level 1.
- The uncertainty factors for (n,gamma) and (p,gamma) reactions have an upper limit of 2 and lower limit of 1/2 and 1/3 respectively; (alpha,gamma) reactions have upper limit 2 and lower limit 1/10.
- Weak-decay rates (beta decay and electron capture) are assigned a 30% uncertainty, corresponding to a factor of 1.3 for the upper limit and 1/1.3 for the lower limit.
- The Monte Carlo analysis used k = 10,000 iterations per zone.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Monte Carlo nucleosynthesis framework (Rauscher 2016, Nishimura 2017) which had been applied to nucleosynthesis processes beyond iron, now applying it to explosive nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae for iron-group nuclides. The study uses the PUSH method explosion models from Paper V (Ebinger 2022) and contrasts with traditional single-rate variation studies (e.g., Thielemann 1998, Hoffman 1999, Iliadis 2002) by simultaneously varying many rates to better assess the importance of specific reactions within the ensemble. The results enable identification of which nuclear reactions most urgently require experimental measurement to reduce uncertainties in supernova nucleosynthesis predictions, particularly for radioactive nuclei observable in supernova remnants and gamma-ray astronomy.
