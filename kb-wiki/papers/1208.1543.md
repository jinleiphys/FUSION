# Nuclear evaporation process with simultaneous multiparticle emission

> The nuclear evaporation process is reformulated by taking into account simultaneous multiparticle emission from a hot compound nucleus appearing as an intermediate state in many nuclear reaction mechanisms. The simultaneous emission of many particles is particularly relevant for high excitation energy of the compound nucleus.These channels are effectively open in competition with the single particle emissions and fission in this energy regime. Indeed, the inclusion of these channels along the decay evaporating chain shows that the yield of charged particles and occurrence of fission are affected by these multiparticle emission processes of the compounded nucleus, when compared to the single sequential emission results. The effect also shows a qualitative change in the neutron multiplicity of different heavy compound nucleus considered. This should be an important aspect for the study of spallation reaction in Acceleration Driven System (ADS) reactors. The majority of neutrons generated in these reactions come from the evaporation stage of the reaction, the source of neutron for the system. A Monte Carlo simulation is employed to determine the effect of these channels on the particle yield and fission process. The relevance of the simultaneous particle emission with the increasing of excitation energy of the compound nucleus is explicitly shown.

## Key claim

The paper reformulates the nuclear evaporation process to include simultaneous multiparticle emission channels, showing that these channels become relevant for compound nucleus excitation energies above about 150 MeV, but the effect on neutron yield is modest while charged particle yields and fission occurrence are significantly altered.

## Method

The authors extend the Weisskopf evaporation formalism by deriving decay widths for simultaneous emission of two and three particles (neutrons, protons, alpha particles) using a Tomasini-inspired N-particle phase space approach with Fermi's Golden Rule. A Monte Carlo simulation of the evaporative decay chain is implemented, sampling among single, double, triple emission, and fission channels for each decay step, with 600 thousand repetitions per calculation. The method is applied to compound nuclei 208Pb and 200Hg for excitation energies from a few MeV to 300 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Neutron multiplicity for p+Pb at 800 MeV beam energy: single sequential emission gives 13.35, simultaneous multiparticle emission gives 13.08, compared to experimental upper bound 12.0 ± 6.57 and lower bound 6.5 ± 0.7
- Neutron multiplicity for p+Pb at 1200 MeV: single sequential emission gives 9.47, simultaneous multiparticle emission gives 10.23, compared to experimental upper bound 12.12 ± 7.13 and lower bound 8.3 ± 0.8
- Neutron multiplicity for p+Pb at 1600 MeV: single sequential emission gives 7.8, simultaneous multiparticle emission gives 9.16, compared to experimental lower bound 10.1 ± 1.0
- Charged particle yields (protons and alphas) increase by approximately one order of magnitude when multiparticle emission channels are included for both 208Pb and 200Hg
- The threshold excitation energy for significant multiparticle emission effects is approximately 150 MeV for 208Pb
- The ternary emission channels p p alpha and p alpha alpha show branching ratios one order of magnitude greater than single charged particle emission and double emission including charged particles for 208Pb

## Context

This work extends the conventional Weisskopf (1937) sequential evaporation model by incorporating simultaneous multiparticle emission, building on the earlier statistical approach of Tomasini (1957) which used N-particle phase space for microstate representation. The results are compared with the Liege group calculations (Cugnon) for neutron yields and with experimental data from Leray and Letourneau for p+Pb reactions. The work is motivated by applications to Accelerator Driven System (ADS) reactors, where spallation reactions at beam energies around 1 GeV produce compound nuclei with excitation energies high enough for multiparticle emission to compete with sequential decay. The authors note that coupling this new evaporation code with intranuclear cascade calculations to simulate the full spallation process is a work in progress.
