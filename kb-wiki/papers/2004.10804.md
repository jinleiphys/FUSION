# Fission of $^{240}$Pu with symmetry-restored density functional theory

> Nuclear fission plays an important role in fundamental and applied science, from astrophysics to nuclear engineering, yet it remains a major challenge to nuclear theory. Theoretical methods used so far to compute fission observables rely on symmetry-breaking schemes where basic information on the number of particles, angular momentum, and parity of the fissioning nucleus is lost. In this work, we analyze the impact of restoring broken symmetries in the benchmark case of $^{240}$Pu.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that restoring broken symmetries (particle number, angular momentum, and parity) in density functional theory calculations of fission in $^{240}$Pu significantly modifies the potential energy surface and fission fragment mass distributions, but the effect on the pre-scission energy is negligible; the authors caution that a phenomenological rotational energy correction with a fixed quenching factor of 0.7 is insufficient for configurations with quadrupole moments below approximately 150 b, where discrepancies up to 2.5 MeV arise.

## Method

The authors first generated a set of axially symmetric Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) configurations for $^{240}$Pu using the SkM* Skyrme functional with constraints on quadrupole ($q_{20}$) and octupole ($q_{30}$) moments, solving in a deformed harmonic oscillator basis of $N_{\rm max}=31$ incomplete shells. They then projected each HFB configuration onto good values of particle numbers ($N=146$, $Z=94$), angular momentum ($J=0$ or $J=1$), and parity ($\pi=+$ or $-$) using a technique for rotational symmetry restoration in incomplete bases originally proposed by Robledo (1994). The projected energies were used to construct one-dimensional least-energy fission pathways and two-dimensional potential energy surfaces, which were then fed into the FELIX solver to compute primary fission fragment mass distributions via the Gaussian overlap approximation of the time-dependent generator coordinate method.

## Key numbers

- Inner barrier height: HFB = 9.37 MeV, PNP = 8.78 MeV, PNP&AMP = 8.05 MeV; the empirical value is approximately 6.1 MeV with about 1 MeV uncertainty.
- Fission isomer energy: HFB = 2.67 MeV, PNP = 2.27 MeV, PNP&AMP = 1.02 MeV; the empirical value is $2.25 \pm 0.20$ MeV.
- Outer barrier height: HFB = 6.75 MeV, PNP = 6.28 MeV, PNP&AMP = 4.58 MeV; the empirical value is 5.15 MeV.
- Pre-scission energy: HFB = 11.68 MeV, PNP = 10.88 MeV, PNP&AMP = 11.85 MeV.
- The 1D pathway covered quadrupole moments from $21 \le q_{20} \le 397$ b with steps of $\Delta q_{20} = 4$ b, using 95 configurations.
- The 2D PES covered $20 \le q_{20} \le 567$ b and $0 \le q_{30} \le 70$ b$^{3/2}$, with 1150 configurations within 20 MeV of the ground state.
- For numerical convergence, $N_{\varphi} = 9$ gauge angles and $26 \le N_{\beta} \le 30$ rotational angles were used.

## Context

This work extends the early study by Bender and co-workers (2004) who examined symmetry restoration only along the reflection-symmetric pathway and up to moderate deformations, by covering the entire fission pathway from ground state to scission. It also builds on the HFB calculations of Schunck (2014) for the same nucleus. The results enable future development of a projected theory of collective inertia and provide a framework for modeling different fission channels (e.g., neutron-induced versus photofission) that symmetry-breaking approaches cannot distinguish. The technique is extendable to configuration mixing schemes for nuclear structure studies at radioactive beam facilities.
