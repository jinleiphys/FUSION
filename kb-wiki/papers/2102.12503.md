# Axions: From Magnetars and Neutron Star Mergers to Beam Dumps and BECs

> We review topics in searches for axion-like-particles (ALPs), covering material that is complementary to other recent reviews. The first half of our review covers ALPs in the extreme environments of neutron star cores, the magnetospheres of highly magnetized neutron stars (magnetars), and in neutron star mergers. The focus is on possible signals of ALPs in the photon spectrum of neutron stars and gravitational wave/electromagnetic signals from neutron star mergers. We then review recent developments in laboratory-produced ALP searches, focusing mainly on accelerator-based facilities including beam-dump type experiments and collider experiments. We provide a general-purpose discussion of the ALP search pipeline from production to detection, in steps, and our discussion is straightforwardly applicable to most beam-dump type and reactor experiments. We end with a selective look at the rapidly developing field of ultralight dark matter, specifically the formation of Bose-Einstein Condensates (BECs). We review the properties of BECs of ultralight dark matter and bridge these properties with developments in numerical simulations, and ultimately with their impact on experimental searches.

# Axions: From Magnetars and Neutron Star Mergers to Beam Dumps and BECs

## Key claim
This review covers axion-like-particle (ALP) searches across astrophysical and laboratory settings, emphasizing that while astrophysical environments provide extreme conditions for ALP production, laboratory-based searches can set more conservative and model-independent constraints because environmental parameters are under greater control.

## Method
The authors review ALP production and detection in three distinct regimes: neutron star cores, magnetar magnetospheres, and neutron star mergers (astrophysical signals); beam-dump, reactor, and collider experiments (laboratory searches); and ultralight dark matter Bose-Einstein Condensates (BECs). For laboratory searches, they decompose the signal rate calculation into three sequential probabilities: production of ALPs at a source, transportation from source to detector, and detection at the detector, providing explicit cross-section formulas for Primakoff and Compton-like processes.

## Key numbers
- Beam-dump experiments listed include CCM (0.8 GeV proton beam, 1.5×10²² POT/yr, 7 t LAr detector at 20 m), COHERENT (1 GeV proton beam, 1.5×10²³ POT/yr, 14.6 kg CsI[Na] at 19.3 m and 24 kg LAr at 28.4 m), and JSNS² (3 GeV proton beam, 3.8×10²² POT/yr, 17 t Gd-LS at 24 m)
- Reactor experiments listed include CONNIE (3.95 GW thermal power, 52 g Skipper CCD at 30 m), CONUS (3.9 GW, 3.76 kg Ge at 17.1 m), and MINER (0.001 GW, 4 kg Ge+Si at 1–2.25 m)
- Future expected limits shown for DUNE-GAr (one year exposure), LDMX-vis. and LDMX-inv. (10¹⁶ EOTs of 8 GeV beam), NA62 (10¹⁸ POTs), NA64 (5×10¹² EOTs), SeaQuest (Phase I), and SHiP (2×10²⁰ POTs)
- Reactor experiments are not sensitive to ALP masses m_a ≳ 10 MeV due to limited photon energy in the reactor core
- The ALP-to-photon conversion probability in a magnetic field is approximately P_det^conv ≈ (g_{aγγ} B L_B / 2)² in the limit m_a, g_{aγγ} → 0
- Belle-II searched for ALPs using data corresponding to 445 pb⁻¹ and set new limits with no evidence found

## Context
This review complements other recent ALP reviews by covering neutron star mergers and BEC formation alongside laboratory searches. It extends discussions of beam-dump experiments (E137, E141, CHARM, NuCal, NOMAD, NA64) and reactor experiments, and connects to specific anomalies including PVLAS (Zavattini 2005), EDGES (Bowman 2018), and Xenon1T (Aprile 2020) that have motivated ALP model-building. The work enables future searches by providing a unified framework for calculating ALP signal rates across different experimental settings, and by identifying parameter space where scattering and conversion channels complement decay-based searches.
