# Three-level mixing model for nuclear chiral rotation: Role of planar component

> Three- and two-level mixing models are proposed to understand the doubling of states at the same spin and parity in triaxially-deformed atomic nuclei with odd numbers of protons and neutrons. The Particle-Rotor Model for such nuclei is solved using the newly proposed basis which couples angular momenta of two valence nucleons and the rotating triaxial mean-field into left-handed $|\mathcal{L}\rangle$, right-handed $|\mathcal{R}\rangle$, and planar $|\mathcal{P}\rangle$ configurations. The presence and the impact of the planar component is investigated as a function of the total spin for mass A$\approx$130 nuclei with the valence h$_{11/2}$ proton particle, valence h$_{11/2}$ neutron hole and the maximum difference between principle axes allowed by the quadrupole deformation of the mean field. It is concluded that at each spin value the higher-energy member of a doublet of states is built on the anti-symmetric combination of $|\mathcal{L}\rangle$ and $|\mathcal{R}\rangle$ and is free of the $|\mathcal{P}\rangle$ component, indicating that it is of pure chiral geometry. For the lower-energy member of the doublet, the contribution of the $|\mathcal{P}\rangle$ component to the eigenfunction first decreases and then increases as a function of the total spin. This trend as well as the energy splitting between the doublet states are both determined by the Hamiltonian matrix elements between the planar ($|\mathcal{P}\rangle$) and non-planar ($|\mathcal{L}\rangle$ and $|\mathcal{R}\rangle$) subspaces of the full Hilbert space.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the higher-energy member of a chiral doublet band is built from the anti-symmetric combination of left-handed and right-handed configurations and contains no planar component, making it of pure chiral geometry; the lower-energy member's planar component first decreases and then increases as a function of total spin, and the energy splitting between doublet states is determined by Hamiltonian matrix elements coupling the planar and non-planar subspaces.

## Method

The Particle-Rotor Model (PRM) for triaxially deformed odd-odd nuclei in the A≈130 mass region was solved using a newly proposed basis that explicitly classifies states into left-handed (|L⟩), right-handed (|R⟩), and planar (|P⟩) subspaces prior to diagonalization. Three-level and two-level mixing models were constructed by simplifying each subspace to a single basis state, allowing analytic diagonalization of the Hamiltonian. The effective interaction C between planar and non-planar subspaces was extracted as a function of total spin I and compared with the energy splitting between chiral doublet bands.

## Key numbers

- The deformation parameters used were β=0.18 and γ=90°, with coupling constant 0.24 MeV/ℏ² and irrotational flow moments of inertia J₃=4J₁=4J₂=J₀ with J₀=30 ℏ²/MeV.
- At spin I=15, the lowest-energy solutions in the planar subspace are separated from those in the left/right subspaces by a sizable ≈5 MeV energy gap.
- The energy difference between states of the same spin in the doublet band is less than 200 keV in a large spin window around the most degenerate level at spin 15.
- The magnitude of the effective interaction C between planar and non-planar subspaces is approximately 1 MeV throughout the whole spin region.
- The turning point for C extracted from the three-level model is at I≈15, and for the two-level model at I≈14.

## Context

This work extends the Particle-Rotor Model approach to chiral doublet bands originally developed by Frauendorf (1997) and Starosta (2001), and builds on the basis proposed by Starosta (2017) that explicitly classifies handedness prior to diagonalization. The three-level mixing model provides an analytic framework analogous to the Double Square Potential Well model for parity doublets, enabling interpretation of the energy splitting and planar component admixture in chiral doublet bands. The results enable reexamination of fingerprints of chiral doublets, such as electromagnetic transitions and angular momentum geometries, by distinguishing the different planar components in the two members of each doublet.
