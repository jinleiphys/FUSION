# Dimer Effective Field Theory

> While chiral perturbation theory for mesons is characterized by a momentum expansion in $Q/Λ_χ$ with $Λ_χ\sim 1$ GeV, existing formulations of effective theory for nucleon-nucleon scattering deviate from data at $Q\sim 300$ MeV or lower. We offer heuristic evidence that unsuspected nonanalytic structure exists in the complex momentum plane obstructing the effective field theory expansion in the spin-triplet channels, associated with the peak of the angular momentum barrier whose energy in low partial waves satisfies $k=\sqrt{ME} \sim 300$ MeV. With this motivation, we construct a meromorphic function of $k^2$ we call the $C$-matrix, for which the radius of convergence of its Taylor expansion in $k^2$ is equivalent to that of the momentum expansion of the effective field theory. Thus the range of validity of the effective theory is directly related to the pole structure of the $C$-matrix. We uncover that pole structure and confirm that it is the source of the obstruction. The systematic inclusion of dimer fields as propagating degrees of freedom in the effective theory to account for those poles results in cut-off insensitive fits at order $Q^0$ to most of the lower partial wave phase shifts up to the pion production threshold, using only the one pion exchange part of the long-range nucleon-nucleon interaction. Our theory should be applicable to the singular potentials regularly found in atomic physics as well.

# Dimer Effective Field Theory

## Key claim

The paper argues that the premature failure of existing nucleon-nucleon effective field theories at momenta around 300 MeV is caused by unsuspected nonanalytic structure in the complex momentum plane, arising from the angular momentum barrier peak in spin-triplet channels. By introducing propagating "dimer" fields to account for these poles, the authors achieve cutoff-insensitive fits to lower partial wave phase shifts up to the pion production threshold using only one-pion exchange at leading order.

## Method

The authors construct a meromorphic function of $k^2$ called the $C$-matrix, whose Taylor expansion radius of convergence is equivalent to that of the effective field theory momentum expansion, and whose pole structure directly determines the theory's range of validity. They identify the pole locations analytically by studying the classical stationary solution of a particle in an effective potential $U(r) = -g/r^3 + \ell^2/M r^2$ for large angular momentum $\ell$, then confirm these poles numerically for lower partial waves. Propagating dimer fields (baryon number 2) are systematically added to the effective theory to remove these poles and extend the derivative expansion convergence, with fits performed at order $Q^0$ including only the one-pion exchange part of the long-range nucleon-nucleon interaction.

## Key numbers

- $\Lambda_{NN} = 8\pi f_\pi^2/(g_A^2 M) \simeq 300$ MeV, the scale introduced in Kaplan (1996) that sets the nonanalyticity scale in spin-triplet channels
- $f_\pi = 132$ MeV, the pion decay constant used in defining $\Lambda_{NN}$
- The critical momentum for nonanalyticity at large $\ell$ scales as $k_\star(\ell) = \ell^3 \Lambda_{NN} / [\sqrt{27} |2(-1)^\ell + 1|]$, derived from summing perturbative contributions to all orders
- The ${}^3P_0$ phase shift fit from the dimer effective theory at $O(Q^0)$ shows good agreement up to $p = 405$ MeV, corresponding to lab kinetic energy $T_\text{lab} = 350$ MeV
- The regulator scale $\mu$ is varied from 100 MeV to 1500 MeV in the ${}^3P_0$ fit, demonstrating relative insensitivity
- The Fermi momentum at nuclear density is $k_F \sim 265$ MeV, cited as context for the theory's potential applicability
- The classical stationary solution energy $E_{cl} = 4\ell^6/(27M^2 g^2)$ yields momentum $k_{cl}$ matching $k_\star$ from the all-orders summation

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Birse (2005), who showed that the $1/r^3$ singular potential in spin-triplet channels causes nonanalytic behavior at a critical momentum $k_c \propto \Lambda_{NN} \ell^3$, and the ladder diagram calculations of Kaplan (2020) which confirmed the breakdown of the KSW expansion at these scales. The paper addresses the cutoff sensitivity problems identified by Nogga, Timmermans, and van Kolck (2005), who found that Weinberg's power counting required "promoting" contact interactions to lower order than expected. The dimer approach enables accurate computations for nuclei and nuclear matter potentially well beyond nuclear density, and should be applicable to singular potentials found in atomic physics.
