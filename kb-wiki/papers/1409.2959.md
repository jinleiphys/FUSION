# Anthropic considerations in nuclear physics

> In this short review, I discuss the sensitivity of the generation of the light and the life-relevant elements like carbon and oxygen under changes of the parameters of the Standard Model pertinent to nuclear physics. Chiral effective field theory allows for a systematic and precise description of the forces between two, three, and four nucleons. In this framework, variations under the light quark masses and the electromagnetic fine structure constant can also be consistently calculated. Combining chiral nuclear effective field theory with Monte Carlo simulations allows to further calculate the properties of nuclei, in particular of the Hoyle state in carbon, that plays a crucial role in the generation of the life-relevant elements in hot, old stars. The dependence of the triple-alpha process on the fundamental constants of Nature is calculated and some implications for our anthropic view of the Universe are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the production of life-relevant elements like carbon and oxygen is fine-tuned under variations of the light quark mass and the electromagnetic fine structure constant, but this conclusion depends on the specific nuclear physics framework used; the anthropic interpretation is only supported if the resonance condition for the Hoyle state splits markedly under small parameter changes, which can now be tested via computer simulations rather than remaining a purely philosophical statement.

## Method

The author combines chiral effective field theory (EFT) to derive nuclear forces between two, three, and four nucleons with Monte Carlo nuclear lattice simulations to solve the non-relativistic many-body problem for nuclei up to carbon-12. The quark mass dependence of nuclear forces is calculated through explicit and implicit dependences in the chiral Lagrangian, using the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation to connect pion and quark masses. The response of the triple-alpha process to variations in the light quark mass and the fine structure constant is then computed by evaluating how the energies of the Hoyle state and the helium-4 plus beryllium-8 threshold shift under these parameter changes.

## Key numbers

- The Hoyle state (0₂⁺) in carbon-12 is calculated at NNLO to be at -84(3) MeV, compared to the experimental value of -84.5 MeV; the ground state (0₁⁺) is -92(3) MeV versus -92.2 MeV experimentally.
- The K-factors for the quark mass dependence of the deuteron binding energy is K^q_BE(deut) = -0.86^{+0.45}_{-0.50}, and for the singlet NN scattering length it is K^q_1S0 = 2.3^{+1.9}_{-1.8}.
- The constraint from Big Bang nucleosynthesis on the variation of the Higgs vacuum expectation value (and thus the light quark mass) is |δv/v| = |δm_q/m_q| ≤ 0.9%.
- The K-factors for the pion mass dependence are K^q_Mπ = 0.494^{+0.009}_{-0.013}, K^q_Fπ = 0.048 ± 0.012, and K^q_mN = 0.048^{+0.002}_{-0.006}.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on anthropic fine-tuning in nuclear physics, particularly the work of Hoyle (1954) who predicted the Hoyle state for carbon production, and the model-based studies of Oberhummer (2000) on the triple-alpha process. It also builds on the chiral EFT framework of Weinberg (1990) and the nuclear lattice simulation methods developed by Epelbaum, Lee, and collaborators. The results enable future ab initio tests of whether the resonance condition for carbon production is robust or fine-tuned under parameter variations, moving beyond the philosophical anthropic principle to quantitative physics.
