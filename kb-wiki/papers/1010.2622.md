# Zeno Paradox for Bohmian Trajectories: The Unfolding of the Metatron

> We analyse the track of an α-particle passing through a cloud chamber using the Bohm theory and show that the resulting classical track has its origins in the quantum Zeno effect. By assuming the ionised gas molecules reveal the positions of the α-particle on its trajectory and using these positions in a short time propagator technique developed by de Gosson, we show it is the failure of the quantum potential to develop quickly enough that leads to the appearance of the classical trajectory. Bohm and Hiley have already argued that it is this failure of the quantum potential to develop appropriately that prevents an Auger electron from undergoing a transition if continuously watched. This allows us to conclude that, in general, it is the suppression of the quantum potential that accounts for the quantum Zeno effect.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the quantum Zeno effect in Bohmian mechanics arises from the suppression of the quantum potential when a particle's position is monitored at sufficiently short time intervals, causing the Bohmian trajectory to become classical. The authors qualify that this holds when successive positions can be defined in a short enough time without significantly deflecting the particle, and the quantum potential is absent from the short-time approximations up to order O((t-t0)^2).

## Method

The authors analyze an alpha particle passing through a cloud chamber using the Bohm theory, where ionised gas molecules reveal the particle's positions along its trajectory. They apply a short-time propagator technique developed by de Gosson to derive approximate solutions to Bohm's equation of motion, showing that the quantum potential does not appear in the leading-order terms. Using the Lie-Trotter formula, they demonstrate that the composition of these short-time approximations converges to the classical Hamiltonian flow, proving that continuous monitoring produces a classical trajectory.

## Key numbers

- The short-time expansion for the action S is given as S(x,x0;t,t0) = sum_j m(x_j - x0,j)^2/(2(t-t0)) - V_tilde(x,x0)(t-t0) + O((t-t0)^2), where V_tilde is the average potential along the line segment
- The Hessian determinant satisfies det(S_x,x0) = (m/(t-t0))^n + O(t-t0)
- The short-time Bohmian trajectory solutions are x^Psi(t) = x0 + (p0/m)(t-t0) + O((t-t0)^2) and p^Psi(t) = p0 - grad_x V(x0)(t-t0) + O((t-t0)^2)
- The time interval between successive ionisations is taken as Delta t = t/N, with N assumed very large (approximately 10^6 to 10^8)
- The difference between the exact flow and the approximate flow at each step is O(Delta t^2), leading to a total error of NO(Delta t^2) = O(Delta t) which vanishes as N goes to infinity

## Context

This work extends the earlier argument by Bohm and Hiley (1993) that continuous observation prevents an Auger electron from undergoing a transition due to the failure of the quantum potential to develop. The authors contrast their approach with the prevailing decoherence-based view of the classical limit, noting that decoherence merely destroys off-diagonal density matrix elements without explaining how classical equations of motion arise, a criticism previously made by Primas. The mathematics builds on the symplectic and metaplectic representation theory developed by de Gosson and Hiley (2010), and the paper enables a Bohmian understanding of how classical trajectories emerge from quantum dynamics under continuous monitoring.
