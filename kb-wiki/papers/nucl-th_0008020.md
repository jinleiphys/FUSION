# Bound-Free Electron-Positron Pair Production in Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> We study the bound-free electron-positron pair production in relativistic heavy ion collisions to different bound states in a full Plane Wave Born Approximation (PWBA) calculation. Exact Dirac wave functions are used for both the bound electron and the free positron in the final states. Results for the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider RHIC as well as the forthcoming Large Hadron Collider (LHC) are given. This process is one of the dominant beam loss processes and can become critical for the operation with heavy ions. A simple parameterization is given as well. We compare our results with calculations of other groups.

# Bound-Free Electron-Positron Pair Production in Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

## Key claim
The authors present a full Plane Wave Born Approximation (PWBA) calculation of bound-free electron-positron pair production using exact Dirac wave functions for both the bound electron and free positron, finding that higher-order effects in the projectile charge (neglected here) are small (at the 1% level) and tend to decrease the cross section, so the predictions should be very reliable. The cross sections are larger than those quoted in the ALICE technical report, and the capture into the 2p1/2 state increases rapidly with nuclear charge Z, becoming larger than the 3s state for heavy ions.

## Method
The total cross section for bound-free pair production per electron state was calculated using the PWBA (equivalent to the semiclassical straight line approximation) with exact Dirac-Coulomb wave functions for both the bound electron and the free positron in the final state. The angular integration was performed analytically, and the resulting radial integrals involving rapidly oscillating Bessel functions were evaluated using recursion relations for the extreme kinematic limits relevant to RHIC and LHC. Cross sections were computed for symmetrical collisions (ZP = ZT = Z) and scaled with ZP^2 for asymmetrical cases.

## Key numbers
- For Au-Au collisions at RHIC (gamma_c = 250, gamma_T = 2e4): cross section of 93 barn (using interpolation from Becker et al.), 83 barn (from Baltz et al. parameterization), 89 barn (Baltz et al. including nonperturbative contributions), 90 barn (Aste et al. equivalent photon method), and 72 barn (Rhoades-Brown et al. plane wave method)
- For Pb-Pb collisions at LHC (gamma_c = 7500, gamma_T = 1.8e7): cross section of 226 barn (Becker et al. interpolation), 206 barn (extrapolated from Au results using Z^7 scaling), 195 barn (extrapolated from U results), and 222 barn (Aste et al. equivalent photon method)
- For Au-U collisions at RHIC with capture on U: 165 barn (b_min = 2 lambda_c) and 182 barn (b_min = lambda_c) from Agger et al.
- For Au-Au collisions at AGS (10.8 GeV/A, gamma_T = 12.6): experimental cross section of 8.8 barn; calculation gives 11.86 barn for 1s-state only
- For Pb beam at CERN SPS (158 GeV/A, gamma_T = 168) on gold target: experimental value of 44.3 barn; calculation gives 45 barn
- For Au-U collisions at Bevalac (1 GeV/A): experimental cross section of 2.19(0.25) barn
- Cross section for capture into 2p1/2 state contributes about 6.5% of total for the heaviest cases
- Cross section for s-states scales as 1/n^3, with sum over all s states = zeta(3) * sigma_1s = 1.202 * sigma_1s

## Context
This work extends earlier PWBA calculations by Becker et al. (1987) and Anholt and Becker (1987) to higher energies and includes capture to higher atomic shells, while also improving upon the Sommerfeld-Maue wave function approximation used by Bertulani and Baur (1988) which gave results lower by a factor of two. The results agree well with equivalent photon method calculations by Aste et al. (1994) and Agger and Soerensen (1997) when the cutoff parameter is chosen as the Compton wavelength of the electron. The calculations are critical for understanding beam loss and localized beampipe heating at RHIC and LHC, as discussed by Klein (2000), and enable reliable predictions for luminosity loss in heavy ion collider operations.
