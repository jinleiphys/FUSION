# Secondary bow with ripples in $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C rainbow scattering

> We report, {for the first time, the emergence of} a secondary bow with ripples in $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C nuclear rainbow scattering. This finding was achieved by studying the experimental angular distributions in $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C scattering at incident energies $E_L$= 240 and 300 MeV, utilizing an extended double-folding model. This model accurately describes all diagonal and off-diagonal coupling potentials derived from the microscopic wave functions for $^{12}$C. Although the observed angular distributions of rainbow scattering at large angles (approaching $90^\circ$) are complicated by the symmetrization of two identical bosonic nuclei, the Airy minimum, associated with a dynamically generated secondary bow with ripples, is clearly identified at approximately 77$^\circ$ for 240 MeV in the fall-off region of the primary nuclear rainbow. This {finding}, along with previous findings in the $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C and $^{13}$C+$^{12}$C systems, {reinforces} the concept of a secondary bow in nuclear rainbow scattering.

## Key claim

The authors report the first observation of a secondary bow with ripples in the symmetric $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C nuclear rainbow scattering system, which was previously not expected because semiclassical theory allows only one rainbow extremum for a mean-field nuclear potential. The secondary bow is identified through coupled-channel calculations that reproduce experimental angular distributions at large angles up to 90 degrees, where previous mean-field calculations had failed.

## Method

The authors used an extended double-folding model with coupled-channel calculations that incorporate all diagonal and off-diagonal coupling potentials derived from microscopic three-alpha cluster wave functions for $^{12}$C using the resonating group method. The calculations included six-channel couplings of the $0^+$, $2^+$, $3^-$ (9.64 MeV), and $4^+$ (14.08 MeV) states of $^{12}$C, using the DDM3Y-FR effective nucleon-nucleon interaction with a normalization factor $N_R$ for the real potential and a Woods-Saxon volume-type imaginary potential.

## Key numbers

* Airy minimum of the primary nuclear rainbow $A1^{(P)}$ at 43 degrees for $E_L$ = 240 MeV and at 33 degrees for $E_L$ = 300 MeV
* Airy minimum of the secondary bow $A1^{(S)}$ at 77 degrees for $E_L$ = 240 MeV and at 63 degrees for $E_L$ = 300 MeV
* Normalization factor $N_R$ = 1.13 for the real potential at 240 MeV and $N_R$ = 1.20 at 300 MeV
* Imaginary potential strength $W$ = 18 MeV for $E_L$ = 240 MeV and $W$ = 19 MeV for $E_L$ = 300 MeV, with fixed radius $R_W$ = 5.6 fm and diffuseness $a_W$ = 0.7 fm
* Secondary bow emerges at around $E_L$ = 210 MeV, with $A1^{(S)}$ at 88 degrees and $M1^{(S)}$ at 108 degrees at 220 MeV
* At 360 MeV, the calculation predicts $A1^{(P)}$ at around 25 degrees and $A1^{(S)}$ at around 54 degrees

## Context

This work extends the concept of a dynamically generated secondary bow previously demonstrated in the asymmetric $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C system (Ohkubo 2014) and the $^{13}$C+$^{12}$C system (Ohkubo 2015) to the symmetric $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C system, which had been extensively studied but not understood at large angles beyond 60 degrees. The finding resolves a long-standing discrepancy between theory and experiment in $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C rainbow scattering that persisted despite intensive calculations by Khoa (2016) and Phuc (2021), and it enables predictions for secondary bows in other systems involving $^{12}$C such as $^{12}$C+$^{13}$N, $^{12}$C+$^{14}$C, and $^{12}$C+$_\Lambda^{13}$C.
