# LHC and B physics probes of neutrinoless double beta decay in supersymmetry without R-parity

> In the event of an observation of neutrinoless double beta decay, a relevant question would be: what lepton number violating physics is responsible for the decay? The exchange of Majorana neutrinos and/or supersymmetric particles may contribute. We point out that measurements of supersymmetric signals at the LHC, including single slepton production, could be used to help bound some supersymmetric processes contributing to neutrinoless double beta decay. LHC information about the supersymmetric spectrum could be combined with Bd-Bd bar mixing data in order to bound a competing neutrinoless double beta decay process involving sbottom exchange.

## Key claim

If neutrinoless double beta decay is observed, the responsible lepton number violating physics could be supersymmetric particles rather than Majorana neutrino exchange; LHC measurements of single slepton production combined with B-physics data on B_d-B_dbar mixing can bound specific supersymmetric contributions to the decay, but the relative importance of different contributions depends on the unknown supersymmetric mass spectrum.

## Method

The authors consider supersymmetric models with R-parity violation that conserves baryon number but allows lepton number violation, focusing on two specific trilinear RPV coupling products: lambda'_113 lambda'_131 and lambda'_111 lambda'_111. They compute the contributions of these couplings to neutrinoless double beta decay in germanium-76, including both direct RPV diagrams and the neutrino mass contribution generated by the same couplings, and compare the resulting bounds with constraints from B_d-B_dbar mixing and projected LHC sensitivity to single selectron production. The supersymmetric mass spectra are generated using the SOFTSUSY spectrum generator under minimal supergravity (mSUGRA) boundary conditions with A_0=0, tan(beta)=10, and positive mu.

## Key numbers

- Current experimental lower limit on the half-life of germanium-76 neutrinoless double beta decay: T^{0nu beta beta}_{1/2} >= 1.9 x 10^25 years (Heidelberg-Moscow experiment)
- If only Majorana neutrino exchange contributes, the current half-life limit translates to |m_{beta beta}| <= 460 meV
- For the direct lambda'_113 lambda'_131 contribution dominating, the half-life limit gives lambda'_113 lambda'_131 <= 2 x 10^{-8} (Lambda_SUSY / 100 GeV)^3
- For the direct lambda'_111 contribution dominating, the half-life limit gives lambda'_111 <= 5 x 10^{-4} (m_{tilde{f}} / 100 GeV)^2 (m_{tilde{g}/tilde{chi}} / 100 GeV)^{1/2}
- Upper bound from B_d-B_dbar mixing: lambda'_113 lambda'_131 <= 4.0 x 10^{-8} (m^2_{tilde{nu}_e} / (100 GeV)^2)
- In the mSUGRA parameter space studied (M_0 = 40-1000 GeV, M_{1/2} = 40-1000 GeV), the bound from B_d-B_dbar mixing is more stringent than that from neutrinoless double beta decay for the lambda'_113 lambda'_131 channel, yielding predicted half-life lower limits of approximately 10^26 to 10^27 years

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of R-parity violating contributions to neutrinoless double beta decay by Hirsch (1995), Faessler (1998), and Pas (1998), correcting certain terms in the effective Lagrangian at the quark level that were incorrect in the literature. It goes beyond the scope of Wodecki (1999) by focusing on what can be inferred from different experimental measurements about the mechanism producing the decay, rather than just calculating nuclear matrix elements. The results show that if the direct lambda'_113 lambda'_131 contribution is the only source of neutrinoless double beta decay, the half-life must be larger than the current experimental limit in most of the mSUGRA parameter space, meaning that an observed signal would likely require additional contributions. The analysis enables future work combining LHC measurements of sparticle masses and single slepton production with B-physics data to disentangle the underlying lepton number violating mechanism.
