# Thermal and atomic effects on coupled-channels heavy-ion fusion

> Stellar nuclear fusion reactions take place in a hot, dense plasma within stars. To account for the effect of these environments, the theory of open quantum systems is used to conduct pioneering studies of thermal and atomic effects on fusion probability at a broad range of temperatures and densities. Since low-lying excited states are more likely to be populated at stellar temperatures and increase nuclear plasma interaction rates, a 188Os nucleus was used as a target that interacts with an inert 16O projectile. Key results showed thermal effects yield an average increase in fusion probability of 15.5% and 36.9% for our test nuclei at temperatures of 0.1 and 0.5 MeV respectively, compared to calculations at zero temperature. Thermal effects could be tested in a laboratory using targets prepared in excited states as envisaged in facilities exploiting laser-nucleus interactions.

## Key claim

Thermal population of low-lying nuclear excited states before a heavy-ion collision, modeled via Boltzmann factors in an open quantum system framework, produces an average increase in sub-barrier fusion probability of 15.5% and 36.9% for the 16O + 188Os system at plasma temperatures of 0.1 MeV and 0.5 MeV respectively, compared to zero-temperature coupled-channels calculations.

## Method

The authors used the coupled-channels density-matrix method based on the Lindblad master equation for open quantum systems, coupling the radial motion of the projectile to the ground and first excited state (155 keV, 2+) of the 188Os target. Thermal effects were introduced by weighting the initial density matrix with Boltzmann factors for the target excited state population, while atomic effects (nuclear plasma interactions such as NEEC and NEET) were included via additional Lindblad transition rates calculated with the ISOMEX code. Fusion probabilities were computed by averaging over Gaussian and Coulomb wave packets with different initial mean energies, using an energy projection technique.

## Key numbers

- Average increase in sub-barrier fusion probability of 15.5% for a plasma temperature of 0.1 MeV, compared to zero-temperature calculations
- Average increase in sub-barrier fusion probability of 36.9% for a plasma temperature of 0.5 MeV, compared to zero-temperature calculations
- The 188Os target has a low-lying 2+ rotational excited state at 155 keV with deformation parameter beta_2 = 0.184
- The uncoupled Coulomb barrier height for the 16O + 188Os system is 71.7 MeV
- Woods-Saxon potential parameters used: V_0 = 60.64 MeV, R_0 = 1.2 fm, a_0 = 0.63 fm
- Nuclear plasma interaction rates for 188Os at density 10 g/cm^3 and temperature 0.1 MeV: excitation rate 7.3 x 10^8 s^-1, de-excitation rate 6.9 x 10^8 s^-1
- Nuclear plasma interaction rates for 188Os at density 10^5 g/cm^3 and temperature 1 MeV: excitation rate 1.6 x 10^10 s^-1, de-excitation rate 3.8 x 10^9 s^-1
- Atomic effects (NPIs) produced a negligible increase in fusion probability of less than 10^-6 percent at all temperatures and densities studied

## Context

This work extends the coupled-channels density-matrix method previously developed by Lee et al. (2022) to include thermal and atomic environmental effects on heavy-ion fusion for the first time. It builds on earlier Hauser-Feshbach statistical model studies of thermal population of excited states in neutron capture by PhysRevC.82.015803 and PhysRevC.82.015804, but applies a dynamical quantum treatment to heavy-ion fusion. The negligible atomic effects contrast with expectations from earlier work on NEEC and NEET processes, and the authors suggest that future experiments exploiting laser-nucleus interactions as envisaged by Buervenich (2006) could test the predicted thermal enhancement using targets prepared in excited states.
