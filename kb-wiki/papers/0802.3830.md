# WMAP Haze: Directly Observing Dark Matter?

> In this paper we show that dark matter in the form of dense matter/antimatter nuggets could provide a natural and unified explanation for several distinct bands of diffuse radiation from the core of the Galaxy spanning over 12 orders of magnitude in frequency. We fix all of the phenomenological properties of this model by matching to x-ray observations in the keV band, and then calculate the unambiguously predicted thermal emission in the microwave band, at frequencies smaller by 10 orders of magnitude. Remarkably, the intensity and spectrum of the emitted thermal radiation are consistent with--and could entirely explain--the so-called "WMAP haze": a diffuse microwave excess observed from the core of our Galaxy by the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe (WMAP). This provides another strong constraint of our proposal, and a remarkable nontrivial validation. If correct, our proposal identifies the nature of the dark matter, explains baryogenesis, and provides a means to directly probe the matter distribution in our Galaxy by analyzing several different types of diffuse emissions.

## Key claim

The authors propose that dark matter consists of dense matter/antimatter nuggets formed at the QCD phase transition, and that the thermal microwave emission from these nuggets could entirely explain the "WMAP haze", a diffuse microwave excess from the Galactic core, with no free parameters, as the model is fixed by matching to keV-scale x-ray observations.

## Method

The authors calculate the thermal bremsstrahlung emissivity from the positron electrosphere surrounding antimatter nuggets, using a Boltzmann gas approximation for the positron density profile. They fix the phenomenological parameters (suppression factor f and energy fraction g) by matching to observed 511 keV positronium emission from INTEGRAL/SPI and diffuse keV x-ray emission from Chandra, then compute the predicted microwave spectrum at frequencies 10 orders of magnitude lower. The equilibrium temperature of the nuggets is determined by balancing the annihilation energy deposition rate against the thermal emission rate.

## Key numbers

- The observed relation between dark matter and baryonic matter densities is Ω_DM ≈ 5Ω_B, with the ratio of antibaryons in antinuggets to baryons in nuggets to visible baryons approximately 3:2:1.
- The nuggets carry baryon charge |B| ≈ 10^20 to 10^33, giving them nuclear densities and interaction cross-section σ/M ≈ 10^-13 to 10^-9 cm^2/g.
- The suppression factor for proton annihilation relative to electron annihilation is f·g ∼ 6 × 10^-3, where f ∼ 10^-1 accounts for proton reflection and 1/10 < g < 1/2 accounts for the fraction of 2 GeV proton annihilation energy released at hot spots.
- The equilibrium temperature of the nuggets is T ∼ 1 eV, determined by the balance F_tot ∼ 10^9 GeV/(cm^2·s) × (T/eV)^(4+1/4) matching the annihilation energy flux F_ann ∼ 10^9 GeV/(cm^2·s) for typical parameters.
- The total surface emissivity of the nuggets is F_tot ∼ 10^-6 F_BB compared to black-body radiation at the same temperature, with a spectral shape that produces a hard low-frequency tail due to logarithmic dependence h(ω/T).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous proposal (Forbes and Zhitnitsky, 2006) that dark matter consists of compact composite objects formed at the QCD phase transition, which can explain baryogenesis and several diffuse emissions from the Galactic core. It directly addresses the "WMAP haze" anomaly first identified by Finkbeiner (2003, 2004) and later studied by Hooper (2007, 2008) and Dobler (2007), who suggested WIMP annihilation as a possible explanation. The authors' model differs from WIMP-based explanations by predicting a thermal microwave spectrum with a characteristically hard low-frequency tail, whereas WIMP models require heavy particles (m ∼ 100 GeV) and produce different spectral predictions. The work enables direct observational tests by correlating the morphology of 511 keV, keV x-ray, and microwave emissions along the same lines of sight.
