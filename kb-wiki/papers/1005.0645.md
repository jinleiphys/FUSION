# Dissecting the role of initial collision geometry for jet quenching observables in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> The observation of large azimuthal anisotropy or $v_2$ for hadrons above $p_T>5$ GeV/$c$ in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm nn}}=200$ GeV has been a longstanding challenge for jet quenching models based on perturbative QCD (pQCD). Using a simple jet absorption model, we seek to clarify the situation by exploring in detail how the calculated $v_2$ varies with choices of the collision geometry as well as choices of the path length dependence and thermalization time $τ_0$ in the energy loss formula. Besides the change of eccentricity due to distortion from gluon saturation or event-by-event fluctuation, we find that the $v_2$ is also sensitive to the centrality dependence of multiplicity and the relative size between the matter profile and the jet profile. We find that the $v_2$ calculated for the naive quadratic path length dependence of energy loss, even including eccentricity fluctuation and the gluon saturation, is not enough to describe the experimental data at high $p_T$ ($\sim$ 6 GeV/$c$) in Au+Au collisions. However, it can match the full centrality dependence of $v_2$ data if higher power path length dependence of energy loss is allowed. We also find that the calculated $v_2$ is sensitive to the assumption of the early time dynamics but generally increases with $τ_0$, opposite to what one expects for elliptic flow. This study attests to the importance of confining the initial geometry, possibly by combining jet quenching $v_2$ with elliptic flow and other jet quenching observables, for proper interpretation of the experimental data.

# Dissecting the role of initial collision geometry for jet quenching observables in relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
The large azimuthal anisotropy (v₂) for hadrons above pT > 5 GeV/c in Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV cannot be explained by the naive quadratic path-length dependence of energy loss (ΔE ∝ l²) even when eccentricity fluctuations and gluon saturation (CGC geometry) are included; however, the full centrality dependence of v₂ can be matched if a higher-power path-length dependence of energy loss is allowed. The calculated v₂ is sensitive to the early-time dynamics and generally increases with thermalization time τ₀, opposite to what is expected for elliptic flow.

## Method
The authors used a simple jet absorption model where back-to-back jet pairs are generated according to the binary collision density profile and propagated through a medium whose density is given by various matter profiles (participant density, collision density, two-component Glauber, and CGC gluon density from MC-KLN). The survival probability is e^(−κI) with path-length integrals Im = ∫ l^(m−1) ρ dl for m = 1–4, corresponding to different energy-loss path-length dependencies (ΔE ∝ l, l², l³, l⁴). The absorption coefficient κ was tuned independently for each configuration to reproduce RAA ≈ 0.18 for the 0%–5% most central collisions, and v₂ was calculated as ⟨e^(−κI) cos2(φ−Ψpart)⟩.

## Key numbers
- PHENIX π⁰ v₂ data at pT > 6 GeV/c in Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV are compared with calculations; pQCD models (AMY, ASW, HT) undershoot the v₂ value by as much as a factor of 2.
- The CGC geometry (MC-KLN) increases eccentricity by 10%–30% relative to Glauber geometry, but its transverse RMS size is 4%–8% smaller than the participant profile.
- The collision density profile ρ₁ has a ∼15% narrower RMS size than the participant profile ρ₀, leading to a ∼50% suppression of v₂ in central collisions.
- The fractional increase of v₂ from participant-plane fluctuations is about 15% for mid-centrality collisions and significantly larger for central and peripheral collisions.
- Higher-order harmonics (v₄) are less than 10% of the v₂ value for all running modes.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies by Drees (2003) and the MC-KLN model by Drescher and Nara (2006), which showed that collision geometry modifications (gluon saturation and event-by-event fluctuations) give 15%–30% corrections to elliptic flow in hydrodynamic calculations. The paper contradicts the assumption that the quadratic path-length dependence from pQCD radiative energy loss (ΔE ∝ l²) can explain high-pT v₂, and instead supports stronger path-length dependencies (ΔE ∝ l³ or l⁴) as suggested by AdS/CFT calculations by Gubser (2008) and Dominguez (2008). The results enable future work to combine jet quenching v₂ with elliptic flow and other observables (RAA, IAA, v₂^IAA) to constrain the initial collision geometry and distinguish between pQCD and strongly-coupled energy-loss mechanisms.
