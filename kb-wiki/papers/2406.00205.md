# Inhomogenuous instabilities at large chemical potential in a rainbow-ladder QCD model

> In this work we continue our efforts to study the existence of a phase with an inhomogeneous, i.e., spatially varying, chiral condensate in QCD. To this end we employ a previously established method of stability analysis of the two-particle irreducible effective action in a truncation that corresponds to a rainbow-ladder approximation of the quark-gluon interaction of QCD. If the analysis is restricted to homogeneous phases, the phase diagram features a first-order chiral transition in the lower-temperature regime. Performing the stability analysis along the lower-chemical-potential border of the corresponding spinodal region, we find that below a certain temperature the homogeneous chirally symmetric solution is unstable against inhomogeneous condensation. We argue that this instability may persist to chemical potentials above the homogeneous first-order phase boundary, in which case it signals the existence of an inhomogeneous ground state. Our methodology is also applicable for more sophisticated truncations of the QCD effective action.

## Key claim

The authors find that, within a rainbow-ladder truncation of QCD, the homogeneous chirally symmetric quark matter solution becomes unstable against inhomogeneous (spatially varying) chiral condensation below a certain temperature along the lower-chemical-potential border of the spinodal region; they argue this instability may persist to chemical potentials above the homogeneous first-order phase boundary, signaling an inhomogeneous ground state, but note this is an exploratory study and the result depends on the truncation scheme.

## Method

The authors perform a stability analysis of the two-particle irreducible (2PI) effective action for QCD, truncated at two-loop order in a rainbow-ladder approximation of the quark-gluon interaction. They expand the free energy around a homogeneous, chirally symmetric stationary point of the quark propagator and examine the second-order variation with respect to small, inhomogeneous (momentum off-diagonal) perturbations of the quark self-energy, using a test function with a Gaussian form in momentum space whose parameters are fixed by a saddle-point condition to handle the complex nature of the propagator at finite chemical potential. The gluon propagator and quark-gluon vertex are modeled via three different effective running couplings, including the Maris-Tandy model, and the analysis is performed at finite temperature and chemical potential in the chiral limit.

## Key numbers

* The instability toward inhomogeneous condensation is found below a certain temperature along the lower-chemical-potential border of the spinodal region; the specific temperature value is not given as a single number in the provided text.
* The renormalization scale for the quark propagator is set at \(\zeta = 19\) GeV, where the renormalization condition \(S^{-1}(\zeta) = (ip + m)|_{p=\zeta}\) is imposed.
* The Maris-Tandy model effective coupling contains parameters \(\eta\), \(\Lambda_\text{MT}\), \(\Lambda_\text{QCD}\), \(\gamma_m\), \(\tau\), and \(m_t\), but no numerical values for these parameters are provided in the excerpt.

## Context

This work extends the stability analysis method previously developed by the same group (Motta et al., 2023) for homogeneous chiral symmetry breaking to the case of inhomogeneous fluctuations, and applies it to QCD for the first time in this context. It builds on earlier explicit studies of inhomogeneous phases in QCD using Dyson-Schwinger equations (Muller et al., 2013), which found an inhomogeneous chiral density wave favored over homogeneous phases, but the present method does not require assuming a specific form of inhomogeneity. The results are relevant for understanding the QCD phase diagram at large chemical potential, where lattice QCD is not applicable due to the sign problem (Berger, 2019; Nagata, 2021), and the methodology is designed to be applicable to more sophisticated truncations of the QCD effective action in future work.
