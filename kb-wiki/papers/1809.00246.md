# Neutron-antineutron oscillations from lattice QCD

> Fundamental symmetry tests of baryon number violation in low-energy experiments can probe beyond the Standard Model (BSM) explanations of the matter-antimatter asymmetry of the universe. Neutron-antineutron oscillations are predicted to be a signature of many baryogenesis mechanisms involving low-scale baryon number violation. This work presents first-principles calculations of neutron-antineutron matrix elements needed to accurately connect measurements of the neutron-antineutron oscillation rate to constraints on $|ΔB|=2$ baryon number violation in BSM theories. Several important systematic uncertainties are controlled by using a state-of-the-art lattice gauge field ensemble with physical quark masses and approximate chiral symmetry, performing nonperturbative renormalization with perturbative matching to the $\overline{\text{MS}}$ scheme, and studying excited state effects in two-state fits. Phenomenological implications are highlighted by comparing expected bounds from proposed neutron-antineutron oscillation experiments to predictions of a specific model of post-sphaleron baryogenesis. Quantum chromodynamics is found to predict at least an order of magnitude more events in neutron-antineutron oscillation experiments than previous estimates based on the "MIT bag model" for fixed BSM parameters. Lattice artifacts and other systematic uncertainties that are not controlled in this pioneering calculation are not expected to significantly change this conclusion.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD predicts that neutron-antineutron oscillation matrix elements are 4 to 8 times larger than previous MIT bag model estimates, implying that experiments should observe 16 to 64 times more oscillation events for fixed beyond-the-Standard-Model parameters; however, this conclusion comes from a pioneering calculation using a single lattice spacing and limited statistics, and lattice artifacts and other uncontrolled systematic uncertainties are not expected to significantly change this conclusion.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD calculations on a 48³×96 ensemble with physical quark masses (mπ = 139.2(4) MeV), using Möbius Domain Wall fermions with approximate chiral symmetry and a lattice spacing a = 0.1141(3) fm. They computed two-point and three-point correlation functions for neutron and antineutron states on 30 gauge configurations, extracting ground-state matrix elements for seven six-quark operators using two-state fits with optimal shrinkage and variable projection methods. Nonperturbative renormalization was performed in the RI-MOM scheme with perturbative matching to the MSbar scheme at 2 GeV and 700 TeV.

## Key numbers

- Pion mass: mπ = 139.2(4) MeV; lattice spacing: a = 0.1141(3) fm; lattice volume: L ≈ 5.45 fm with mπL = 3.86
- Renormalized matrix element for Q1 at MSbar(2 GeV): −46(13) × 10⁻⁵ GeV⁶; for Q2: 95(17) × 10⁻⁵ GeV⁶; for Q3: −50(12) × 10⁻⁵ GeV⁶; for Q5: −1.06(48) × 10⁻⁵ GeV⁶
- LQCD matrix elements are 4.2 times (Q1), 7.5 times (Q2), 5.1 times (Q3), and −0.8 times (Q5) the MIT bag model A results at 2 GeV
- Finite volume effects estimated from chiral perturbation theory to be less than 1%
- Residual operator mixing in the chiral basis is O(10⁻³) or smaller
- Higher-order matching uncertainties estimated at less than 7%

## Context

This work provides the first lattice QCD calculation of neutron-antineutron six-quark matrix elements, replacing previous estimates based on the MIT bag model from Rao and Shrock (1982). The results improve upon preliminary lattice QCD results from Buchoff et al. (2012) by using physical quark masses and nonperturbative renormalization, and upon Syritsyn et al. (2015) by analyzing excited state effects. These matrix elements enable robust connections between experimental bounds on neutron-antineutron oscillation times (currently τnnbar > 0.89 × 10⁸ s from the ILL experiment) and constraints on baryon number violation in beyond-the-Standard-Model theories, and have been used in effective field theory studies of baryogenesis such as Grojean et al. (2018).
