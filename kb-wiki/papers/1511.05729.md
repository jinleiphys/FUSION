# Recent developments in the eikonal description of the breakup of exotic nuclei

> The study of exotic nuclear structures, such as halo nuclei, is usually performed through nuclear reactions. An accurate reaction model coupled to a realistic description of the projectile is needed to correctly interpret experimental data. In this contribution, we briefly summarise the assumptions made within the modelling of reactions involving halo nuclei. We describe briefly the Continuum-Discretised Coupled Channel method (CDCC) and the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) in particular and present a comparison between them for the breakup of 15C on Pb at 68AMeV. We show the problem faced by the models based on the eikonal approximation at low energy and detail a correction that enables their extension down to lower beam energies. A new reaction observable is also presented. It consists of the ratio between angular distributions for two different processes, such as elastic scattering and breakup. This ratio is completely independent of the reaction mechanism and hence is more sensitive to the projectile structure than usual reaction observables, which makes it a very powerful tool to study exotic structures far from stability.

## Key claim

The eikonal approximation can be extended to low beam energies (down to 20A MeV) by applying a semiclassical correction that replaces the transverse impact parameter with the distance of closest approach on a classical Coulomb trajectory, and the ratio of angular distributions for different reaction processes (e.g., breakup and elastic scattering) is nearly independent of the reaction mechanism and thus more sensitive to projectile structure than usual observables.

## Method

The authors compare the Continuum-Discretised Coupled Channel method (CDCC) and the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) for the breakup of 15C on Pb at 68A MeV and 20A MeV. They solve the three-body Schrodinger equation for a two-body projectile (core plus halo neutron) interacting with a structureless target via optical potentials. The DEA factorizes the wave function as a plane wave times a slowly varying factor, neglecting second-order derivatives, while CDCC expands the wave function in eigenstates of the projectile Hamiltonian and discretizes the continuum into bins. A semiclassical correction replaces the transverse impact parameter b with the distance of closest approach b' on a classical Coulomb trajectory to extend DEA to lower energies.

## Key numbers

- Breakup of 15C on Pb at 68A MeV: CDCC and DEA produce nearly identical energy distributions and angular distributions, with only a "tiny shift" observed in the angular distribution.
- Breakup of 15C on Pb at 20A MeV: DEA predicts a breakup cross section too large and too forward-focused compared to CDCC; the semiclassical correction brings DEA into "perfect agreement" with CDCC.
- Uncertainty in the core-target optical potential V_cT can lead to up to a factor of 2 variation in the breakup cross section, as shown in Ref. CGB04.
- For 11Be on Pb at 69A MeV and on C at 67A MeV, the ratio of breakup to summed cross sections is nearly identical for both targets and in excellent agreement with the REB form factor.
- For 11Be on C at 20A MeV, the ratio method remains valid and results obtained with different V_cT potentials produce "nearly identical" ratios.

## Context

This work extends the comparison between CDCC (Kam86) and DEA (BCG05) for halo-nucleus breakup, previously studied at intermediate energies, down to lower beam energies where the standard eikonal approximation fails. The semiclassical correction (FOC14) builds on earlier work by BW81 and BD04. The ratio method (CJN11, CJN13) is based on the Recoil Excitation and Breakup model (REB) of JAT97 and aims to remove dependence on uncertain optical potentials, which can cause factor-of-2 uncertainties in breakup cross sections (CGB04). The method enables more reliable extraction of nuclear structure information for exotic nuclei far from stability.
