# What has been learnt from the analysis of the low-energy pion-nucleon data during the past three decades?

> Over twenty-five years ago, two analyses of the pion-nucleon ($πN$) data at low energy (i.e., for pion laboratory kinetic energy $T \leq 100$ MeV) reported on the departure of the extracted scattering amplitudes, corresponding to the two elastic-scattering reactions $π^\pm p \to π^\pm p$ and to the $π^- p$ charge-exchange reaction $π^- p \to π^0 n$, from the triangle identity, which these amplitudes fulfil if the isospin invariance holds in the hadronic part of the $πN$ interaction. This discrepancy indicates that \emph{at least one} of the following assumptions is not valid: first, that the absolute normalisation of the bulk of the low-energy $πN$ datasets is correct; second, that any residual contributions to the corrections, which aim at the removal of the effects of electromagnetic (EM) origin from the measurements, are not significant; and third, that the isospin invariance holds in the hadronic part of the $πN$ interaction. In view of the incompatibility of the results of the various schemes of removal of the so-called trivial EM effects at the $πN$ threshold ($T = 0$ MeV), the likelihood of residual effects of EM origin in the extracted $πN$ scattering amplitudes (from the data in the scattering region, i.e., above the $πN$ threshold) must be reassessed. This work emphasises the importance of the development of a unified scheme for the determination of reliable EM corrections, \emph{applicable at the $πN$ threshold and in the scattering region}, in providing a resolution to the established discrepancy at low energy.

## Key claim

Over twenty-five years, analyses of low-energy pion-nucleon data (pion laboratory kinetic energy T ≤ 100 MeV) have consistently revealed a departure from the triangle identity that would hold if isospin invariance were valid in the hadronic part of the πN interaction; this discrepancy implies that at least one of three assumptions fails: correct absolute normalisation of the low-energy datasets, negligible residual electromagnetic corrections, or isospin invariance in the hadronic interaction.

## Method

The author reviews the ETH πN project's approach, which models the hadronic part of the πN interaction using an isospin-invariant model with Feynman diagrams (including t-channel meson exchanges and s- and u-channel nucleon and Δ(1232) exchanges), then includes electromagnetic corrections from the NORDITA programme and from Rasche and Woolcock (1976b, 1982). The analysis uses a database of 1711 datapoints (200 datasets) for π±p elastic scattering and π−p charge-exchange reactions, fitting s- and p-wave K-matrix elements via polynomial parameterisation to extract scattering amplitudes and test the triangle identity.

## Key numbers

- The low-energy πN database comprises 1711 datapoints across 200 datasets, including 1504 differential cross section measurements, 136 analysing power measurements, 27 partial-total cross sections, 12 total-nuclear cross sections, 10 total cross sections, 18 coefficients in Legendre expansions, 2 strong shift measurements, and 2 total decay width measurements.
- The strong shift ε1s of the ground state in pionic hydrogen is known with statistical uncertainty below 2 per-mille and systematic uncertainty below 0.5% in the earlier experiment (Schroeder 2001), and with both uncertainties at the 1 per-mille level in the most recent experiment (Hennebach 2014).
- The corrected ε1s result from Schroeder (2001) is −7.085(13)(34) eV, and from Hennebach (2014) is −7.0858(71)(64) eV, both requiring a +0.0055 eV correction due to updated physical constants.
- The total decay width Γ1s of the ground state in pionic hydrogen from the final result (Hirtl 2021) is 0.856(16)(22) eV.
- The π−p CX database has increased from 46 to 337 datapoints since the analysis of Gibbs (1995).

## Context

This work extends the pioneering analysis of Gibbs (1995), who first reported discrepancies between extracted and reconstructed charge-exchange amplitudes using hadronic potentials, and the model-independent polynomial K-matrix approach of Fettes (1997). The review contradicts the assumption that isospin invariance holds in the hadronic πN interaction at low energy, and emphasises the need for a unified scheme of electromagnetic corrections applicable both at threshold and in the scattering region, as the existing NORDITA corrections (Tromborg 1976, 1977, 1978) and threshold corrections (Rasche and Woolcock 1976b, 1982; Oades 2007) remain incompatible with each other.
