# Sequential fission of highly excited compound nuclei in a 4D Langevin approach

> In highly dissipative collisions between heavy ions, the optimal conditions to investigate different de-excitation channels of hot nuclei such as evaporation, fission or multifragmentation are well known. One crucial issue remains the excitation energy region where fission gives way to multifragmentation. In this paper, the onset of multi-fragment exit channels is investigated in terms of sequential fission. For the first time, the dynamical approach based on solving Langevin transport equations in multidimensional collective coordinate space is used to follow the de-excitation of highly excited (up to E* =223-656 MeV) 248Rf compound nuclei. The sequential fission model we propose contains two steps: (1) time evolution of the compound nucleus up to either scission or residue formation, followed by (2) dynamical calculations of each primary fragment separately. This procedure allows to obtain from one to four cold fragments correlated with the light particles emitted during the de-excitation process. Experimental data measured with the INDRA detector for the 129Xe+ natSn reaction at beam energies 8, 12 and 15 MeV/nucleon provide strong constraints for this sequential fission scenario.

## Key claim

For the first time, a dynamical approach based on solving Langevin transport equations in multidimensional collective coordinate space is used to follow the de-excitation of highly excited (up to E* = 223–656 MeV) 248Rf compound nuclei, treating both primary and secondary fission steps in a coherent and fully dynamical way; the model can account at least qualitatively for the N-fragment exit channels observed experimentally, but the assumption of complete fusion forming a spherical compound nucleus likely does not hold for all reactions studied.

## Method

The sequential fission model proceeds in two steps: (1) time evolution of the compound nucleus up to either scission or residue formation, solved via 4D Langevin equations in collective coordinates (elongation, neck size, mass asymmetry, and angular momentum projection onto the fission axis), with the driving potential given by the Helmholtz free energy from the finite range liquid drop model; (2) dynamical calculations of each primary fragment separately, using the same Langevin approach, to determine whether they undergo secondary fission or end as evaporation residues. Light particle evaporation (n, p, d, t, α, 3He) and γ-ray emission are included via a Monte Carlo Hauser-Feschbach statistical code throughout both steps.

## Key numbers

- Compound nucleus excitation energies studied: E* = 223, 471, and 656 MeV, corresponding to complete fusion of 129Xe + 119Sn at beam energies 8, 12, and 15 MeV/nucleon.
- Primary fission probability for 248Rf at E* = 223 MeV (circles), 471 MeV (squares), and 656 MeV (triangles) shown for different viscosity parametrizations; secondary fission probability per primary fission fragment also shown for the same energies.
- Mean multiplicities for hydrogen (Z=1) and helium (Z=2) isotopes at 8 MeV/nucleon: experimental 1.6 and 0.9; model with ks=0.25 gives 1.4 and 0.6.
- Mean multiplicities at 12 MeV/nucleon: experimental 4.1 and 2.6; model with ks=0.25 gives 5.2 and 1.6.
- Mean multiplicities at 15 MeV/nucleon: experimental 5.3 and 3.5; model with ks=0.25 gives 7.5 and 2.2.
- Approximately 10^5 trajectories generated for the primary fission step and ~5 × 10^3 complete calculations performed for each excitation energy/viscosity combination.

## Context

This work extends dynamical fission modeling into the excitation energy region where fission gives way to multifragmentation, a regime previously inaccessible to standard fission models. The approach builds on prior Langevin-based fission models (Nadtochy 2012, 2014; Mazurek 2017) and is compared with INDRA data for 129Xe + natSn reactions (Chbihi 2013; Manduci 2016; Gruyer 2015). The sequential fission model enables investigation of nuclear viscosity with increasing temperature while minimizing potential energy surface effects, and the authors plan to couple it with the HIPSE code (Lacroix 2004) to include pre-equilibrium emission in future work.
