# Complete set of observables in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction -- Controversy solved

> The long-standing debate over whether the complete set of observables in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction consists of eight or merely four elements continues to persist. From the perspective of amplitude analysis, it is argued that all eight observables are necessary to completely determine the others. On the other hand, proponents of partial-wave analysis, working with theoretically precise data of infinite accuracy, claim that only four observables are needed. However, this claim is not acceptable from an experimental viewpoint, as all data in the real world contain some uncertainty. This paper illustrates that the controversy is artificial and is due to additional mathematical assumptions used in partial-wave analysis. Our research advances this discussion by moving from exact synthetic numerical data to also synthetic, but more realistic data in partial-wave analysis and shows that the claimed reduction in observables is unjustified. Consequently, the final conclusion is that the complete set of observables in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction, whether using amplitude analysis or partial-wave analysis with practical data, must consist of eight observables.

# Complete set of observables in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction -- Controversy solved

## Key claim

The long-standing debate over whether the complete set of observables in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction requires eight or only four observables is artificial, arising from additional mathematical assumptions used in partial-wave analysis. When realistic data with experimental uncertainty are used, the complete set must consist of eight observables regardless of whether amplitude analysis or partial-wave analysis is employed.

## Method

The author replicated the truncated partial-wave analysis fits of the Mainz/Bonn/GWU collaborations (Wunderlich 2014, Workman 2017, Wunderlich 2019) using synthetic numerical data of infinite precision, first with a fixed multipole phase (Re[E0+] > 0, Im[E0+] = 0) for truncation orders up to Lmax = 3. The analysis was then extended to infinite partial-wave analysis by fixing the overall amplitude phase (Im[F1(W,theta)] = 0) using three constraint equations on the CGLN amplitude expansion coefficients, and further to include energy analyticity via a simplified Pietarinen expansion model. Finally, the synthetic data were randomized with a 0.1% systematic error to test the effect of realistic experimental uncertainty.

## Key numbers

- For truncated partial-wave analysis with Lmax = 2 and fixed E0+ multipole phase, a set of four carefully selected observables constitutes a complete set, confirming the results in Table III of Workman (2017).
- The author confirmed these results for Lmax = 3, with the ability to go even higher in L if needed.
- When the overall F1 amplitude phase was fixed instead of a single multipole phase, the number of observables needed for a complete set reduced from four to three.
- With explicit energy analyticity introduced via a simplified Pietarinen expansion model (using n=1 for F1, n=2 for F2, n=3 for F3, n=4 for F4), only a single observable (differential cross-section) was required to uniquely determine all four CGLN amplitudes.
- A randomization of 0.1% of the maximum value for each observable, plus a relative error of 0.1% of the precise numerical value, caused the reduction in observable count to disappear, restoring the requirement of eight observables.

## Context

This work resolves a controversy between amplitude analysis, which has long held that eight observables are necessary (Barker, Donnachie, and Storrow 1975; Keaton and Workman 1996; Chiang and Tabakin 1997; Nakayama 2019), and partial-wave analysis claims by the Mainz/Bonn/GWU collaborations (Wunderlich 2014, Workman 2017, Wunderlich 2019) that only four or five observables suffice. The author demonstrates that the reduction in required observables is an artifact of using idealized data of infinite precision, and that with realistic experimental uncertainties the complete set remains eight observables. This enables experimentalists to proceed with confidence that eight measurements are indeed necessary for a complete experiment in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction.
