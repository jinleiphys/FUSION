# Colliding localized, lumpy holographic shocks with a granular nuclear structure

> We apply a recent and simple technique which speeds up the calculation of localized collisions in holography to study more realistic models of heavy ion collisions via the gauge/gravity duality. The initial data takes into account the lumpy nuclear structure of real heavy ions and the projectiles' aspect ratio mimics the Lorentz contraction of nuclei during RHIC collisions. At the hydrodynamization time of the central region of the quark gluon plasma developed during the collision, we find that most of the vorticity three vector's absolute value is deposited far away from the hydrodynamized part of the plasma. Only the relativistic corrections to the thermal vorticity in the hydrodynamized region are non-negligible. We compare the transverse flow after the collision determined in this work with previous results, without granular initial conditions and determine the proper energy density and fluid velocity in a hydrodynamized subregion of the plasma.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that holographic collisions of localized shocks with realistic Lorentz contraction (γ=100) and granular nuclear structure can be computed using a transverse derivative expansion, and find that at the hydrodynamization time of the central region, most of the system's vorticity is located well outside the hydrodynamized part of the plasma, with only relativistic corrections to thermal vorticity being non-negligible in the hydrodynamized region.

## Method

The authors solve five-dimensional Einstein equations in asymptotically AdS₅ spacetime using a characteristic formulation, expanding the equations to first order in transverse derivatives to reduce computational cost. Initial data is constructed from a Woods-Saxon probability distribution for nucleon positions (with parameters R=6.5 fm, a=0.66 fm skin thickness, γ=100 Lorentz boost factor) and Gaussian profiles for individual nucleons (w=1 fm transverse size), with a minimal nucleon separation d_min=0.4 fm. The time evolution uses Fourier grids with N_x=N_y=40 and N_z=256 spatial points, a two-domain Chebyshev grid with N_u=2×21 radial points, and a fourth-order Runge-Kutta algorithm with time step δt=7.5×10⁻⁴ fm/c.

## Key numbers

- Lorentz contraction factor γ=100, corresponding to RHIC collision energies
- Nuclear radius R=6.5 fm, skin thickness a=0.66 fm, minimal nucleon distance d_min=0.4 fm
- Nucleon transverse size w=1 fm, leading to μ=1.1 GeV
- Impact parameter |b|=4.5 fm along the x direction
- Time evolution from t₀=-0.144 fm/c to t₁=0.144 fm/c, requiring approximately three weeks on a decade-old multi-core desktop computer with 128 Gb memory
- Maximum energy density reaches 170% of initial peak at t=0.034 fm/c after collision
- At t=0.144 fm/c, energy density averaged over central region (|x⊥|<2.5 fm) falls off approximately as ∝ t⁻⁰·⁹
- Background energy density equal to 7% of peak projectile energy density added for numerical stability
- Hydrodynamization threshold defined as residual Δ<0.15

## Context

This work extends the holographic shockwave collision calculations of Chesler and Yaffe (2015) and earlier work by the same authors (2022) by incorporating realistic granular nuclear structure from a Woods-Saxon distribution, rather than smooth Gaussian projectiles. The transverse derivative expansion technique developed in Waeber and Yaffe (2022) enables simulations with realistic Lorentz contraction (γ=100) that would be computationally infeasible with exact methods. The results enable future studies of how initial state fluctuations affect early-stage quark-gluon plasma dynamics, vorticity evolution, and odd azimuthal flow moments v_{2i+1} observed in experiments.
