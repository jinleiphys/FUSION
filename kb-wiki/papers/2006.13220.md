# Dynamical chemistry: non-equilibrium effective actions for reactive fluids

> We present two approaches for describing chemical reactions taking place in fluid phase. The first method mirrors the usual derivation of the hydrodynamic equations of motion by relating conserved---or to account for chemical reactions, non-conserved---currents to local-equilibrium parameters. The second method involves a higher-brow approach in which we attack the same problem from the perspective of non-equilibrium effective field theory (EFT). Non-equilibrium effective actions are defined using the in-in formalism on the Schwinger-Keldysh contour and are therefore capable of describing thermal fluctuations and dissipation as well as quantum effects. The non-equilibrium EFT approach is especially powerful as all terms in the action are fully specified by the symmetries of the system; in particular the second law of thermodynamics does not need to be included by hand, but is instead derived from the action itself. We find that the equations of motion generated by both methods agree, but the EFT approach yields certain advantages. To demonstrate some of these advantages we construct a quadratic action that is valid to very small distance scales---much smaller than the scales at which ordinary hydrodynamic theories break down. Such an action captures the full thermodynamic and quantum behavior of reactions and diffusion at quadratic order. Finally, taking the low-frequency and low-wavenumber limit, we reproduce the linearized version of the well-known reaction-diffusion equations as a final coherence check.

## Key claim

The paper presents two approaches for describing chemical reactions in fluids, a standard equations-of-motion method and a non-equilibrium effective field theory (EFT) approach, and claims that both yield the same equations of motion, but the EFT approach offers advantages including systematic inclusion of quantum and thermal fluctuations and automatic satisfaction of the second law of thermodynamics, provided that the characteristic time-scale of reactions is much longer than the collision time of molecules in the fluid.

## Method

The first method derives hydrodynamic equations by relating conserved and non-conserved currents to local-equilibrium parameters, using conservation of the stress-energy tensor and (non-)conservation equations for particle-number currents of N species. The second method constructs a non-equilibrium effective action on the Schwinger-Keldysh contour using the in-in formalism, where the field content is doubled and symmetries (including chemical shift symmetries for approximate U(1) charges) fully specify all terms in the action. The EFT approach incorporates dynamical KMS symmetries derived from thermal equilibrium conditions, from which the second law of thermodynamics follows automatically rather than being imposed by hand.

## Key numbers

- The system contains N species of particles with k ≤ N allowed chemical reactions, where the number of genuinely conserved charges is N minus the number of independent reaction constraints.
- For the explicit example of hydrogen, oxygen, and water with reaction 2H₂ + O₂ ↔ 2H₂O, there are exactly two conserved charges: total hydrogen atoms (N_H = 2Q_H₂ + 2Q_H₂O) and total oxygen atoms (N_O = 2Q_O₂ + Q_H₂O).
- The quadratic action constructed in the EFT approach is valid to distance scales "much smaller than the scales at which ordinary hydrodynamic theories break down" (no specific numerical scale given).
- In the low-frequency and low-wavenumber limit, the EFT reproduces the linearized version of the well-known reaction-diffusion equations.

## Context

This work extends the non-equilibrium EFT framework developed by H. Liu and collaborators (2018-2020) for hydrodynamics to include chemical reactions, building on the understanding that hydrodynamic modes corresponding to conserved charges possess chemical shift symmetries as described by Nicolis (2011). The paper enables systematic treatment of reactive fluids that accounts for thermal fluctuations and quantum effects, with applications to condensed matter physics, nuclear physics, and large-scale cosmology, and addresses the theoretical point that unlike ordinary hydrodynamics, there is no non-trivial regime in which entropy does not increase even at leading order.
