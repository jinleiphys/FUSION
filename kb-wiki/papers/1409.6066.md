# Synthesize Neutron-Drip-Line-Nuclides with Free-Neutron Bose-Einstein Condensates Experimentally

> We first show a possible way to create a new type of matter, free-neutron Bose-Einstein condensate by the ultracold free-neutron-pair Bose-Einstein condensation and then determine the neutron drip line experimentally. The Bose-Einstein condensation of bosonic and fermionic atoms in atomic gases was performed experimentally and predicted theoretically early. Neutrons are similar to fermionic atoms. We found free neutrons could be cooled to ultracold neutrons with very low energy by other colder neutral atoms which are cooled by the laser. These neutrons form neutron pairs with spin zero, and then ultracold neutron-pairs form Bose-Einstein condensate. Our results demonstrate how these condensates can react with accelerated ion beams at different energy to synthesize very neutron-rich nuclides near, on or/and beyond the neutron drip line, to determine the neutron drip line and whether there are long-life nuclide or isomer islands beyond the neutron drip line experimentally. Otherwise, these experimental results will confirm our prediction that is in the whole interacting region or distance of nuclear force in all energy region from zero to infinite, Only repulsive nuclear force exists among identical nucleons and only among different nucleons exists attractive nuclear force.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that free neutrons can be cooled to ultracold temperatures (around 10^{-7} eV) by laser-cooled neutral atoms, form spin-zero neutron pairs, and then undergo Bose-Einstein condensation; this condensate could then be used as a target for accelerated ion beams to synthesize neutron-rich nuclides near, on, or beyond the neutron drip line. The author explicitly hedges that existing nuclear theory is not adequate to confirm the existence of such a bound dineutron state at these ultralow energies, and that only experiments can verify the prediction.

## Method

The author outlines a multi-step experimental pathway: first, laser cooling of neutral atoms (e.g., hydrogen, lithium, sodium, or rubidium) to create a cold atomic gas; second, sympathetic cooling of free neutrons by these colder atoms to produce ultracold neutrons with energies around 10^{-7} eV; third, magnetic trapping of the ultracold neutrons followed by evaporative cooling to reach degeneracy; fourth, formation of spin-zero neutron pairs via strong interaction near the boundary of the nuclear force or via magnetic interaction; and fifth, Bose-Einstein condensation of these neutron pairs. The resulting condensate would then be used as a target for accelerated ion beams to synthesize neutron-rich nuclides.

## Key numbers

- Free neutron lifetime is about 896 s (spin 1/2 hbar).
- The binding energy of deuterium is 2.2246 MeV, corresponding to a nearest nucleon-nucleon distance of 1.526 fm under the uncertainty principle.
- At a distance of 10 fm, the binding energy needed for two neutrons decreases to 52 keV under the uncertainty principle.
- The target ultracold neutron energy is on the order of 10^{-7} eV, which is 10-13 orders of magnitude lower than the typical 1-50 MeV energy region of conventional nuclear theory.
- Cold neutrons at energy 10^{-3} eV can be obtained from reactor sources; ultracold neutrons at 10^{-7} eV can be obtained by traditional methods (Steyerl turbine, beryllium converter, superfluid helium, solid deuterium).

## Context

This paper extends ideas from atomic-physics Bose-Einstein condensation experiments (Anderson 1995, Davis 1995, Regal 2003, Greiner 2003) to the nuclear domain, proposing that fermionic neutrons can be paired into bosonic molecules analogously to fermionic atom pairing via Feshbach resonances. The work aims to open a new "zero energy" nuclear physics field, enabling experimental determination of the neutron drip line and searches for long-lived nuclides or isomer islands beyond it, which cannot be reliably predicted by existing nuclear theory. The author explicitly contrasts this approach with conventional nuclear structure and reaction theories (e.g., mean-field approaches, scattering phase shifts) that operate at MeV energies and cannot describe the ultralow-energy regime or the boundary region of the nuclear force.
