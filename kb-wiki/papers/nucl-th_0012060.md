# Effective Field Theory in Nuclear Physics

> The Electromagnetic and Hadronic Physics sub-community of nuclear physics held a town hall meeting at Jefferson Lab during November 30 to December 4 of 2000. This is is our combined contribution to the white paper that will result from this meeting.

# Effective Field Theory in Nuclear Physics

## Key claim

Effective field theory (EFT) provides the most general description of low-energy QCD consistent with all underlying symmetries, but a fully consistent and convergent power-counting scheme for multi-nucleon systems with pions has not yet been established, requiring ingredients from both the Weinberg and KSW counting schemes.

## Method

The authors summarize the state of chiral perturbation theory (χPT) for mesons and single nucleons, and review the extension to multi-nucleon systems via nuclear chiral perturbation theory (NχPT). They compare two competing power-counting schemes: Weinberg's method, which computes NN potentials using χPT organization and is intrinsically numerical, and KSW counting, where pions contribute at subleading orders and allows analytic results but fails to converge in spin-triplet channels at higher orders.

## Key numbers

- The nd scattering length computed at next-to-next-to-leading order in EFT is a₃/₂^EFT = 6.33 ± 0.05 fm, compared to the experimental value a₃/₂^expt = 6.35 ± 0.02 fm.
- The cross section σ_np for np → dγ is now described at the ∼1% level by a compact analytic expression in the pionless EFT (EFT(π/)).
- The χPT prediction for the π⁺ electric polarizability α_E is 2.8 × 10⁻⁴ fm³, while experimental values range from 2.1 ± 1.1 to 6.8 ± 1.4 × 10⁻⁴ fm³.
- The χPT prediction for the radiative pion decay quantity h_V(m_π⁻¹) is 0.027, compared to the experimental value 0.029 ± 0.017.
- The χPT prediction for r_V/h_V in π⁺ → e⁺ν_e e⁺e⁻ is 2.6, compared to the experimental value 2.3 ± 0.6.
- The EFT(π/) calculation of νd breakup cross sections requires one unknown coefficient L_{1,A}, with values of 5.6 fm³ and 0.94 fm³ needed to match two different potential model calculations.

## Context

This white paper summarizes the findings of the Electromagnetic and Hadronic Physics sub-community town hall meeting at Jefferson Lab in November-December 2000. It extends the foundational work of Weinberg (1967, 1990) on chiral perturbation theory and its application to nuclear systems, and the development of heavy baryon χPT by Jenkins and Manohar (1991). The paper identifies key open questions including convergence issues in the baryon sector, the need for a consistent power-counting scheme for multi-nucleon systems, and the importance of high-precision low-energy experiments to determine fundamental constants such as the nucleon anapole moment and neutron polarizabilities.
