# Supersonic and subsonic shock waves in the unitary Fermi gas

> We investigate shock waves in the unitary Fermi gas by using the zero-temperature equations of superfluid hydrodynamics. We obtain analytical solutions for the dynamics of a localized perturbation of the uniform gas. These supersonic bright and subsonic dark solutions produce, after a transient time, an extremely large (divergent) density gradient: the shock. We calculate the time of formation of the shock and also simulate the space-time behavior of the waves by solving generalized hydrodynamic equations, which include a reliable dispersive regularization of the shock. We find that the shock spreads into wave ripples whose properties crucially depend on the chosen initial configuration.

# Supersonic and subsonic shock waves in the unitary Fermi gas

## Key claim

The unitary Fermi gas at zero temperature admits analytical solutions for localized density perturbations that evolve into shock waves, but these solutions are only reliable when the normal fluid fraction is negligible (temperature T much smaller than the critical temperature Tc ~ 0.2 TF), and the shock formation time and subsequent dispersive wave ripples depend crucially on whether the initial perturbation is bright (supersonic) or dark (subsonic).

## Method

The author solves the zero-temperature equations of superfluid hydrodynamics for a uniform unitary Fermi gas with a localized Gaussian density perturbation along one spatial axis, obtaining analytical implicit solutions for the density field via the method of characteristics. After the shock formation time, the equations are regularized by adding a quantum gradient term (with parameter λ = 1/4) to the hydrodynamic equations, and the resulting 1D nonlinear Schrödinger equation is solved numerically using a Crank-Nicolson finite-difference predictor-corrector algorithm.

## Key numbers

- Bulk sound velocity: c_s = √(ξ/3) v_F, where ξ ≃ 0.4 is the universal parameter and v_F is the Fermi velocity.
- Critical temperature of the normal-superfluid transition: T_c ≃ 0.2 T_F, where T_F = ε_F/k_B is the Fermi temperature.
- For T < 0.1 T_F, the normal fraction is below 10%, justifying the zero-temperature approach.
- Mach number of the perturbation: M = 4(1+η)^(1/3) - 3, where η is the amplitude parameter of the initial Gaussian perturbation ρ(z,0) = 1 + 2η exp(-z²/(2σ²)).
- Shock formation time: T_s = σ / [2 c_s ((1+η)^(1/3) - 1)] for bright perturbations (η > 0).
- Numerical simulations with σ/l_F = 18 and η = 0.3 (supersonic) and η = -0.2 (subsonic) show relative differences within 5% for the velocity V and within 20% for the breaking time T_s compared to analytical formulas.

## Context

This work extends the study of shock waves from atomic Bose-Einstein condensates (experimentally observed by Hau, Cornell, Hoefer, and Davis around 2000-2006, and theoretically studied by Damski, Zak, and others) to the unitary Fermi gas, motivated by recent experiments on colliding 6Li Fermi gas clouds by Thomas (2011). The author notes that while Thomas's experiment used hydrodynamic equations with a phenomenological viscous term, Bulgac (2011) showed that zero-temperature time-dependent Bogoliubov-de Gennes equations reproduce the experimental results without viscosity. This paper provides analytical predictions for a cleaner method to produce shock waves using laser-induced perturbations rather than cloud collisions, and enables future experimental tests of dispersive shock wave dynamics in strongly interacting Fermi superfluids.
