# Possibility of cold nuclear compression in antiproton-nucleus collisions

> We study the dynamical response of the oxygen-16 nucleus to an incident antiproton using the Giessen Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck microscopic transport model with relativistic mean fields. A special emphasis is put on the possibility of a dynamical compression of the nucleus induced by the moving antiproton. Realistic antibaryon coupling constants to the mean meson fields are chosen in accordance with empirical data. Our calculations show that an antiproton embedded in the nuclear interior with momentum less than the nucleon Fermi momentum may create a locally compressed zone in the nucleus with a maximum density of about twice the nuclear saturation density. To evaluate the probability of the nuclear compression in high-energy antiproton-nucleus collisions, we adopt a two-stage scheme. This scheme takes into account the antiproton deceleration due to the cascade of antiproton-nucleon rescatterings inside the nucleus (first stage) as well as the nuclear compression by the slow antiproton before its annihilation (second stage). With our standard model parameters, the fraction of antiproton annihilation events in the compressed zone is about $10^{-5}$ for $\bar p ^{16}$O collisions at $p_{\rm lab}=3-10$ GeV/c. Finally, possible experimental triggers aimed at selecting such events are discussed.

# Possibility of cold nuclear compression in antiproton-nucleus collisions

## Key claim

An antiproton embedded in the nuclear interior with momentum below the nucleon Fermi momentum may create a locally compressed zone in the nucleus reaching about twice the nuclear saturation density, but the fraction of antiproton annihilation events occurring in such a compressed zone is only about 10^{-5} for antiproton-oxygen-16 collisions at laboratory momenta of 3-10 GeV/c under standard model parameters.

## Method

The authors used the Giessen Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (GiBUU) microscopic transport model with relativistic mean fields, employing a two-stage scheme. In the first stage, standard GiBUU simulations tracked antiproton deceleration and rescattering inside an oxygen-16 nucleus until annihilation, recording the annihilation point coordinates and momenta for each event. In the second stage, an antiproton was initialized inside the nucleus at those recorded phase-space points using a Gaussian spatial distribution, and the nuclear compression response was simulated without annihilation while computing the antiproton survival probability via the annihilation width integrated over time.

## Key numbers

- Maximum nucleon density reached in the compressed zone: about twice the nuclear saturation density (2 rho_0), achieved within approximately 10 fm/c for antiprotons with initial momentum 0.3 GeV/c moving toward the nuclear center.
- Fraction of antiproton annihilation events in the compressed zone: about 10^{-5} for antiproton-oxygen-16 collisions at laboratory momenta of 3-10 GeV/c with standard model parameters (antinucleon coupling reduction factor xi = 0.22).
- Antiproton survival probability until the moment of maximum density (2 rho_0): approximately 10^{-2} for favorable initializations (antiproton moving toward nuclear center at 0.3 GeV/c with Gaussian width sigma_r = 1 fm).
- Nuclear matter incompressibility from the NL3 parameterization: K = 272 MeV, with nucleon effective mass m_N^* = 0.6 m_N at saturation density rho_0 = 0.148 fm^{-3}.
- Real part of antiproton optical potential at nuclear center: about -150 MeV for xi = 0.22.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of dynamical compression by a stopped antiproton (Larionov 2008) to the case of a moving antiproton, and builds on earlier predictions of strong compressional effects in antiproton-nucleus systems (Buer venich 2002, Mishustin 2004). The results are relevant for future antiproton beams at the Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research (FAIR) in Darmstadt. The paper also relates to the broader discussion of strongly-bound hadron-nucleus systems, including lambda-hypernuclei where core shrinkage of about 19 percent was observed experimentally (PhysRevLett.86.1982), and the ongoing theoretical debates about kaon-nuclei and antiproton-nuclei systems. The two-stage perturbative approach enables evaluation of rare compression events that would be diluted in standard ensemble-averaged transport calculations.
