# Can X(3915) be the tensor partner of the X(3872)?

> It has been proposed recently (Phys. Rev. Lett. 115 (2015), 022001) that the charmoniumlike state named X(3915) and suggested to be a $0^{++}$ scalar, is just the helicity-0 realisation of the $2^{++}$ tensor state $χ_{c2}(3930)$. This scenario would call for a helicity-0 dominance, which were at odds with the properties of a conventional tensor charmonium, but might be compatible with some exotic structure of the $χ_{c2}(3930)$. In this paper, we investigate, if such a scenario is compatible with the assumption that the $χ_{c2}(3930)$ is a $D^*\bar D^*$ molecular state - a spin partner of the $X(3872)$ treated as a shallow bound state. We demonstrate that for a tensor molecule the helicity-0 component vanishes for vanishing binding energy and accordingly for a shallow bound state a helicity-2 dominance would be natural. However, for the $χ_{c2}(3930)$, residing about 100 MeV below the $D^*\bar D^*$ threshold, there is no a priori reason for a helicity-2 dominance and thus the proposal formulated in the above mentioned reference might indeed point at a molecular structure of the tensor state. Nevertheless, we find that the experimental data currently available favour a dominant contribution of the helicity-2 amplitude also in this scenario, if spin symmetry arguments are employed to relate properties of the molecular state to those of the X(3872). We also discuss what research is necessary to further constrain the analysis.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether the X(3915) state, conventionally assigned as a scalar, could instead be the helicity-0 component of the tensor state χc2(3930) under the assumption that χc2(3930) is a D*D̅* molecular state and a spin partner of the X(3872). The authors find that while a shallow tensor molecule would naturally exhibit helicity-2 dominance, the X(3915) lies about 100 MeV below threshold, so helicity-0 dominance is not ruled out a priori; however, using spin symmetry to relate the molecular state to the X(3872), the available experimental data favor helicity-2 dominance, which is at odds with the proposal that X(3915) is the helicity-0 realization of the tensor state.

## Method

The authors decompose the amplitude for γγ → X2 (a 2++ D*D̅* molecule) into four gauge-invariant tensor structures and express the helicity-0 and helicity-2 amplitudes in terms of these structures. They compute the helicity-0 amplitude from loop diagrams involving D* mesons, using the X2D*D̅* coupling constant derived from the X(3872) binding energy via heavy-quark spin symmetry (HQSS) relations. The helicity-2 amplitude requires a counterterm that cannot be fixed without additional data, so the authors compare the predicted helicity-0 contribution to the two-photon decay width with experimental angular distributions to extract the ratio of helicity amplitudes.

## Key numbers

- The X(3915) lies approximately 100 MeV below the D*D̅* threshold (mass threshold not given numerically, but binding energy is about 100 MeV).
- The X(3872) binding energy is taken as EB = (3871.69 ± 0.17) MeV minus the D0D̅*0 threshold (the exact numerical binding energy is not quoted in the truncated text, but the formula uses EB in MeV).
- The predicted helicity-0 two-photon decay width of the X2 molecule is Γ0(X2 → γγ) ≈ 0.033 √EB keV, where EB is the X(3872) binding energy in MeV.
- The magnetic moment parameters used are β⁻¹ = 379 MeV and mc = 1863 MeV.
- The isospin traces for magnetic vertices yield factors of approximately 18 and 14 for the squared magnetic moment matrices, compared to about -2.4 and 1 for electric vertices.

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Zhou et al. (2015) that the X(3915) could be the helicity-0 component of the tensor χc2(3930) if the tensor state has an exotic structure, contradicting the conventional helicity-2 dominance seen in charmonia (Krammer, 1977; Li, 1990). The authors test this proposal within the molecular interpretation where the χc2(3930) is a D*D̅* molecule and a spin partner of the X(3872), building on HQSS predictions by Nieves (2012) and Guo (2013) that such a 2++ state should exist. The results suggest that if X(3915) is the molecular partner of X(3872), it is more likely a scalar state, and additional data on angular distributions are needed to resolve the ambiguity.
