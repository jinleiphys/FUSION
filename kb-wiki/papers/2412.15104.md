# Symmetry breaking effects in pion couplings to constituent quark currents

> Pseudoscalar and axial neutral and charged pion-constituent quark coupling constants are investigated with nondegenerate quark masses in different kinematical points, off shell and on shell pions and constituent quarks. By considering a large quark mass expansion of a quark determinant in the presence of local pion field and of constituent quark background currents, gluonic effects are considered by means of an effective gluon propagator that dresses quark currents. For the neutral pion, mixing effects are introduced by means of the pion mixing to states $P_0$ and $P_8$, that give rise to the $π^0-η-η'$ meson mixing, and mixing of quark currents via corresponding mixing interactions. The relative behavior of charged and neutral pion coupling constants to quarks may be nearly the same - in the framework of the constituent quark model - as the pion-nucleon coupling constants if mixings are introduced. A very small pion coupling to strange quark current is also obtained. The dependence of the positive and negative pion-constituent quark coupling constant on the non-degeneracy of quark masses, for emission and absorption processes, is identified.

# Symmetry breaking effects in pion couplings to constituent quark currents

## Key claim
The relative behavior of charged and neutral pion-constituent quark coupling constants may be nearly the same as the pion-nucleon coupling constants if flavor mixings (π⁰-η-η' meson mixing and quark current mixing) are introduced, but this result is conditional on the framework of the constituent quark model and the specific renormalization condition chosen (fixing the pseudoscalar charged pion coupling to 13 at one kinematical point).

## Method
The author derives pseudoscalar and axial pion-constituent quark coupling constants from a large quark mass expansion of a quark determinant in the presence of local pion fields and constituent quark background currents, with gluonic effects incorporated via an effective gluon propagator that dresses quark currents. For the neutral pion, mixing effects are introduced through π⁰-η-η' meson mixing and flavor mixing of quark currents via corresponding mixing interactions derived from flavor symmetry breaking in quark polarization. The coupling constants are calculated at different kinematical points (on shell and off shell pions and constituent quarks) using three different sets of constituent quark masses (A₁, A₂, A₃) and a longitudinal confining gluon propagator with M_g = 500 MeV.

## Key numbers
- Renormalization condition: pseudoscalar charged pion coupling constant fixed to G^{ps}_{π±}(K²=M², Q²=0) = 13 (off shell pion, on shell quark)
- Set A₁ quark masses: M_u = 389 MeV, M_d = 399 MeV, M_s = 600 MeV
- Set A₂ quark masses: M_u = 307 MeV, M_d = 319 MeV, M_s = 349 MeV
- Set A₃ quark masses: M_u = 340 MeV, M_d = 350 MeV, M_s = 550 MeV
- Effective gluon mass: M_g = 500 MeV
- The axial coupling g_A for charged pions at (M², 0) yields approximately 1 without further conditions for the parameter sets considered

## Context
This work extends previous analyses of pion-constituent quark couplings in the degenerate mass limit (Braghin, EPJA 2016; PRD 2019) by incorporating isospin and flavor symmetry breaking effects through nondegenerate up, down, and strange quark masses. It builds on investigations of isospin symmetry breaking in pseudoscalar pion-nucleon couplings from Granada and Bochum groups, which found very small ISB effects (e.g., g_{π⁰nn} ≈ 13.397 vs g_{π⁰pp} ≈ 13.227). The approach also incorporates meson mixing mechanisms from Feldmann (review) and instanton-induced interactions, as well as flavor mixing interactions derived in earlier work (Braghin, PRD 2021; JPG 2022). The results enable more precise assessment of how and why the constituent quark model works for describing pion couplings to hadrons, though the relation between these quark-level couplings and nucleon-level couplings remains not fully unambiguous due to confinement and the dynamical character of Bethe-Salpeter and Faddeev equations.
