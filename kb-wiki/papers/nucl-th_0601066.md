# A new method of measurement of the velocities of solar neutrinos

> A new method of measurement of the velocities of solar electron antineutrinos is proposed. The method is based on the assumption, that if the neutrino detector having a shape of a pipe and providing a proper angular resolution, is directed onto the optical "image" of the sun, then it would detect solar neutrinos with velocities $V_{\widetildeν_{e}}$ $=c$. Here c is the velocity of light. It is expected that the less is the value of $V_{\widetilde ν_{e}}$, the larger would be the angular lagging of the "image" of these neutrinos relative to the position of the optical "image" of the sun. Therefore, one can detect solar neutrinos with different energies by changing the angle between the axis of a detector pipe and the direction to the "image" of the sun. Also, the method gives unique possibility to check hypotheses predicting an existence of solar neutrinos with $V_{\widetildeν_{e}}>c$. In this case the "image" of such solar neutrinos on the sky should pass the "image" of the sun.

## Key claim

A pipe-shaped neutrino detector with angular resolution comparable to the solar disk (≤0.5°) could measure solar electron antineutrino velocities by detecting the angular offset between the neutrino "image" and the optical image of the sun, under the assumption that neutrinos travel at or near the speed of light.

## Method

The proposed method uses a pipe-shaped detector filled with liquid scintillator containing cadmium (or gadolinium), with photomultiplier tubes arranged in radial sections. Solar electron antineutrinos are detected via the inverse beta decay reaction \(\widetilde{\nu}_e + p \rightarrow n + e^+\), with delayed coincidence between two annihilation gamma quanta and neutron capture gamma quanta from the Cd(n,γ) reaction. The detector is pointed at the optical image of the sun, and the angle β between the detector axis and the solar direction is varied; the measured angular lag or lead of the neutrino signal relative to the solar position yields the neutrino velocity.

## Key numbers

- The angular velocity of the optical image of the sun relative to Earth is ω = 0.00417 deg·s⁻¹.
- For a neutrino velocity of \(10^8\) m·s⁻¹, the expected angular lag β is approximately 4°; for \(3 \times 10^7\) m·s⁻¹, β is approximately 18°.
- For hypothetical superluminal neutrinos (\(V > c\)), the maximum lead angle β′ is close to 2° (about 4 solar diameters).
- The estimated counting rate for a pipe detector of length 11.5 m and diameter 0.1 m is \(N_{\widetilde{\nu}_e} \approx 10^{-4}\) s⁻¹, corresponding to about \(3 \times 10^3\) events per year.
- The absorption cross section for electron antineutrinos on protons is \(\sigma_a(\widetilde{\nu}_e, p) = 1.2 \times 10^{-43}\) cm².
- The assumed solar electron antineutrino flux at Earth is \(\phi \approx 5 \times 10^{10}\) cm⁻²·s⁻¹.
- The detection efficiency \(\epsilon_{\widetilde{\nu}_e}\) is estimated at approximately 0.3.

## Context

This paper proposes a novel geometric method for measuring solar neutrino velocities, contrasting with existing large-scale detectors such as the Davis chlorine detector (615 tons of C₂Cl₄, threshold 0.816 MeV) and the SNO detector (1000 tons of D₂O), which are not designed for velocity or low-energy spectrometry. The method aims to provide experimental data for testing the Standard Solar Model and, uniquely, to search for hypothetical superluminal neutrinos as discussed in prior work (reference [4] in the paper), which would contradict the theory of relativity. If successful, the approach could enable velocity-resolved studies of solar antineutrino groups and either confirm relativistic predictions or challenge fundamental physics.
