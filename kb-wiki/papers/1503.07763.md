# Physics Opportunities with Meson Beams

> Over the past two decades, meson photo- and electro-production data of unprecedented quality and quantity have been measured at electromagnetic facilities worldwide. By contrast, the meson-beam data for the same hadronic final states are mostly outdated and largely of poor quality, or even nonexistent, and thus provide inadequate input to help interpret, analyze, and exploit the full potential of the new electromagnetic data. To reap the full benefit of the high-precision electromagnetic data, new high-statistics data from measurements with meson beams, with good angle and energy coverage for a wide range of reactions, are critically needed to advance our knowledge in baryon and meson spectroscopy and other related areas of hadron physics. To address this situation, a state of-the-art meson-beam facility needs to be constructed. The present paper summarizes unresolved issues in hadron physics and outlines the vast opportunities and advances that only become possible with such a facility.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the full potential of modern high-precision meson photo- and electroproduction data cannot be realized without correspondingly high-quality meson-beam data for the same hadronic final states, which are currently outdated, poor, or nonexistent; a new state-of-the-art meson-beam facility is therefore critically needed to provide the hadronic complement to the electromagnetic program.

## Method

The authors review the current status of baryon and meson spectroscopy, summarizing unresolved issues such as the "missing resonances" problem and the limitations of existing pion- and kaon-induced reaction data. They survey the major coupled-channel analysis efforts (ANL-Osaka, Julich-Athens-Washington, Bonn-Gatchina, GWU/INS SAID, Kent State, Giessen, MAID, and others) and document the available world data for key reactions including elastic pi N scattering, pi N to eta N, pi N to K Lambda, and their photoproduction counterparts, using figures to show data coverage as a function of center-of-mass energy up to 2.5 GeV.

## Key numbers

- The center-of-mass energy range up to 2.5 GeV is identified as rich in opportunities for physics with pion and kaon beams.
- For pi- p to eta n, most available data come from measurements published in the 1970s, evaluated as unreliable above W = 1620 MeV.
- Precise new data for pi- p to eta n from the Crystal Ball Collaboration extend only up to the peak of the first S11 resonance at 1535 MeV.
- For pi- p to pi0 n, there are no A and R polarization data available at all, and very few P data.
- The world data on pi N to eta N, K Lambda, K Sigma date back more than 20 or 30 years, as collected in Ronchen (2012).

## Context

This white paper extends the arguments made at the 2012 workshop "Physics with Secondary Hadron Beams in the 21st Century" and responds to the DNP Town Meeting on QCD and Hadron Physics (2015), which noted that meson beams are being considered. It directly addresses the "missing resonances" problem identified by Koniuk and Isgur (1980), where quark models and lattice QCD predict far more baryon states than observed in pi N scattering. The paper argues that new meson-beam data would enable coupled-channel analyses to rigorously determine resonance pole positions and statistical significances, complementing photoproduction programs at JLab, MAMI, ELSA, SPring-8, and BEPC, and would provide input for chiral perturbation theory low-energy constants used in nuclear lattice calculations of the Hoyle state by Epelbaum (2011, 2013).
