# Classical mechanics technique for quantum linear response

> It is shown that the lowest excitation energies of a quantum many-fermion system in the random phase approximation (RPA) can be obtained by minimizing an effective classical energy functional. The minimum can be found very efficiently using generalized Lanczos technique. Application of the new technique to molecular spectra allows to compute excited states at the expense comparable to the ground-state calculations. As an example, the first-principle RPA excitation spectrum of C60 molecule is computed taking into account all 240 valence electrons in the full valence space of the molecule. The results match linear absorption experiment within percents.

## Key claim

The lowest excitation energies of a quantum many-fermion system in the random phase approximation (RPA) can be obtained by minimizing an effective classical energy functional, with the minimum found efficiently using a generalized Lanczos technique; the method allows computation of excited states at an expense comparable to ground-state calculations, and the first-principle RPA excitation spectrum of C60 matches linear absorption experiment within percents.

## Method

The RPA eigenvalue problem is transformed into Hamiltonian equations of motion for classical oscillations by substituting T = A + B and K = A - B, where A and B are the particle-particle interaction matrices. The lowest excitation frequency is obtained by minimizing the total energy (kinetic plus potential) over all phase-space configurations {p,q} normalized by (pq)=1, using a generalized Lanczos recursion that generates biorthogonal basis vectors and reduces the problem to a smaller 2n x 2n eigenvalue problem with the same structure. Higher-frequency modes are found sequentially by choosing initial vectors orthogonal to previously found modes, with oblique projection to correct for loss of orthogonality.

## Context

This work extends the Thouless minimum principle (Thouless, 1961) for RPA eigenvalues by reformulating it as a classical Hamiltonian minimization problem, enabling efficient computation via generalized Lanczos recursion. It improves upon prior approaches including the Rettrup-type Davidson algorithm (Rettrup, 1982), the symplectic Lanczos method (Mei, 1986; Benner and Fassbender, 1997), and the density-matrix-spectral-moments algorithm (Tretiak, Chernyak, and Mukamel, 1997), which all faced performance limitations for large systems. The method enables full-space RPA calculations for molecules like C60 that were previously intractable, and opens the possibility of performing realistic molecular-dynamics simulations of photochemical reactions in large biological molecules by computing excited-state adiabatic surfaces at computational cost comparable to ground-state calculations.
