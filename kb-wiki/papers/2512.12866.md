# Local Quantum Friction with Pairing: Unitary Dissipation in Large Fermi Systems

> We present a unitary framework for dissipative quantum dynamics that can be efficiently applied to large-scale Fermi systems. The method introduces local Hermitian operators that emulate frictional forces while strictly preserving the unitarity of time evolution. Unlike approaches based on the Lindblad equation, our formulation scales favorably with system size and can be seamlessly integrated into time-dependent density functional theory frameworks. We demonstrate that energy dissipation arises from the damping of particle currents and pairing-field fluctuations. Furthermore, we develop a variant of the scheme that allows the particle number to vary in time, enabling controlled density scans. The method is generic and versatile, as illustrated by applications to spin-imbalanced unitary Fermi gases and to nuclear matter in the neutron-star crust. The framework can be naturally extended to include stochastic noise, providing a foundation for studying fluctuation-dissipation dynamics and thermalization in strongly interacting Fermi superfluids.

## Key claim

The paper presents a unitary framework for dissipative quantum dynamics in large Fermi systems that extends the local quantum friction (LQF) algorithm to the pairing channel, claiming improved cooling efficiency over the original LQF method which only removed energy from hydrodynamic flow. The authors demonstrate that energy dissipation arises from damping both particle currents and pairing-field fluctuations, and that the method can be extended to allow controlled variation of particle number over time.

## Method

The authors implement dissipative dynamics within the time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) formalism by constructing a Hermitian cooling potential U that is diagonal in position space, consisting of a mean-field dissipative potential U_diss proportional to the divergence of the current density and a dissipative pairing field Delta_diss derived from the off-diagonal block of the commutator [H, R]. The cooling potential is constructed to guarantee energy reduction (dE/dt ≤ 0) while preserving unitarity, and a variant with a specific phase choice for Delta_diss allows particle number conservation or controlled variation. The method is applied to spin-imbalanced unitary Fermi gases and nuclear matter in neutron-star crusts using the time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA).

## Key numbers

* The dissipative mean-field potential takes the form U_diss_sigma(r) = -(alpha/(m rho_0)) * (nabla dot j_sigma(r)), where alpha is a dimensionless dissipative constant and rho_0 is a characteristic density such as nuclear saturation density
* The cooling potential for the pairing channel is constructed as Delta_diss(r) = -i * tilde_beta(r) * B(r), where B(r) contains contributions from kinetic terms and pairing fields
* For large-scale simulations of quantum turbulence, the method requires evolving M = 10^5 to 10^6 three-dimensional quasiparticle wave functions to capture relevant physics
* The computational cost of applying the evolution operator scales as O(M N log N) for spatial derivatives using spectral methods, while Gram-Schmidt reorthogonalization would scale as O(M^2 N), becoming prohibitive when M >> log N

## Context

This work extends the local quantum friction algorithm originally introduced by Bulgac (2013) and further developed by Huang and Bulgac, which only removed energy from hydrodynamic flow through density and current variations. The new pairing-channel dissipation addresses the limitation that the original LQF algorithm could stall for large-scale problems because large fermionic systems have many internal degrees of freedom associated with quasiparticle wavefunctions. The framework can be naturally extended to include stochastic noise, providing a foundation for studying fluctuation-dissipation dynamics and thermalization in strongly interacting Fermi superfluids, and is directly applicable to TDSLDA studies used in nuclear physics and cold-atom communities.
