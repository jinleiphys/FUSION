# The Shape of Hadrons

> This colloquium addresses the issue of the shape of hadrons and in particular that of the proton. The concept of shape in the microcosm is critically examined. Special attention is devoted to properly define the meaning of shape for bound-state systems of near massless quarks. The ideas that lead to the expectation of non-sphericity in the shape of hadrons, the calculations that predict it, and the experimental information obtained from recent high-precision measurements are examined. Particular emphasis is given to the study of the electromagnetic transition between the nucleon and its first excited state, the $Δ$(1232)-resonance. The experimental evidence is is critically examined and compared with lattice calculations, as well as with effective-field theories and phenomenological models.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while hadrons are expected to exhibit non-spherical shapes due to non-central (tensor) interactions among quarks, the empirical evidence for such deformation is limited and derives almost exclusively from the detailed study of the electromagnetic transition between the nucleon and its first excited state, the Δ(1232) resonance; the concept of shape itself requires careful redefinition for relativistic bound-state systems of nearly massless quarks, where the number of constituents is not constant due to virtual pair production.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical foundations for defining shape in relativistic quantum systems, introducing light-front transverse charge densities as the appropriate framework for hadrons, where the J⁺ current component in a Drell-Yan frame (q⁺ = 0) yields a positive-definite density operator. They examine experimental information from elastic electron scattering form factors for spin-1 particles (W boson and deuteron) and focus on the γ*N → Δ transition, comparing experimental measurements of quadrupole amplitudes with lattice QCD calculations, effective-field theories, and phenomenological quark models.

## Key numbers

- Proton charge radius: 0.8768 (69) × 10⁻¹⁵ m
- Deuteron quadrupole moment G_Q^d(0) = 25.83 ± 0.03, compared to the natural spin-1 point-particle value G_Q(0) = -1
- Deuteron magnetic dipole moment G_M^d(0) = 1.71
- W boson anomalous couplings from LEP data: κ_γ = 0.973 (+0.044/-0.045), λ_γ = -0.028 (+0.020/-0.021), consistent with point-particle values G_M(0) = 2 and G_Q(0) = -1
- Electron size limit: smaller than 10⁻¹⁸ m in diameter

## Context

This colloquium extends the line of inquiry initiated by Glashow (1979), who first conjectured non-spherical hadron amplitudes based on color spin-spin interactions between quarks, and by Isgur, Karl, and Koniuk (1981), who provided indirect empirical evidence for deformation. The paper contrasts the successful determination of nuclear shapes (e.g., ¹⁵⁴Gd via electron scattering by Hersman and collaborators in 1986) with the much more limited empirical knowledge of hadron shapes, which remains confined to the N→Δ transition. The work enables future lattice QCD calculations to predict quadrupole moments of decuplet baryons such as the Ω⁻ and Δ⁺, and motivates new precision measurements at facilities like MAMI.
