# Exactness of the normal-ordered two-body truncation of three-nucleon forces

> Reference-state-based many-body methods start from Hamiltonians that are normal ordered with respect to the reference state. In low-energy nuclear physics applications normal-ordered Hamiltonians consisting of two- and three-nucleon forces are usually truncated at the two-body rank with residual three-nucleon operators being discarded. Benchmark computations have shown that this truncation is accurate, but we lack an understanding about why it works. We show that the normal-ordered two-body truncation is exact for zero-range three-body forces when nuclei are computed using the coupled cluster with singles and doubles method. As the nuclear three-nucleon force is short ranged and a three-body contact is a leading term in effective field theories of quantum chromodynamics, our result provides an analytical basis for the popular normal-ordered two-body approximation.

## Key claim

The normal-ordered two-body truncation of three-nucleon forces is exact for zero-range three-body forces when nuclei are computed using the coupled cluster with singles and doubles (CCSD) method, but only for reference states where hole and particle states reside on different lattice sites; corrections arise from finite-range forces and from three-particle–three-hole excitations.

## Method

The authors prove analytically that for zero-range (on-site) three-nucleon forces, the residual three-body matrix elements that survive normal ordering cannot contract with the cluster amplitudes in the CCSD equations, because hole and particle states occupy disjoint lattice sites. They illustrate this with lattice Hamiltonians from leading-order pionless effective field theory, performing exact diagonalizations and CCSD computations for ²H, ³He, and ⁴He on 4³ lattices at three different lattice spacings (2.5, 2.0, and 1.7 fm), adjusting the two-body coupling v and three-body coupling w to reproduce the ²H and ⁴He ground-state energies.

## Key numbers

- For ⁴He at lattice spacing a = 2.5 fm: exact ground-state energy = -29.70 MeV, CCSD energy = -29.11 MeV (CCSD captures about 90% of correlation energy)
- For ⁴He at a = 2.0 fm: exact = -29.45 MeV, CCSD = -27.73 MeV
- For ⁴He at a = 1.7 fm: exact = -28.97 MeV, CCSD = -26.41 MeV
- For ³He at a = 2.5 fm: exact = -16.32 MeV, CCSD = -15.22 MeV (CCSD captures 83% of correlation energy)
- For ³He at a = 2.0 fm: exact = -14.84 MeV, CCSD = -12.18 MeV (78% of correlation energy)
- For ³He at a = 1.7 fm: exact = -10.82 MeV, CCSD = -4.80 MeV (71% of correlation energy)
- ²H exact energies: -2.48 MeV (a = 2.5 fm), -2.53 MeV (a = 2.0 fm), -2.33 MeV (a = 1.7 fm)

## Context

This work provides the first analytical explanation for why the normal-ordered two-body approximation, which has been standard in coupled-cluster computations since Hagen (2007) and Roth (2012), works so accurately in practice. The result extends to even-even N=Z nuclei with mass number A=4n and to other nuclei where lattice sites hold up to four nucleons. The authors note that for finite-range forces (e.g., from pion exchange with range ~1.4 fm), the on-site contributions dominate, explaining the accuracy seen in earlier benchmarks; residual three-nucleon forces first contribute at seventh order in perturbation theory for zero-range forces. This analytical insight enables confidence in the continued use of the normal-ordered two-body approximation for medium-mass and heavy nuclei, and clarifies where corrections will arise (finite-range forces and triples excitations).
