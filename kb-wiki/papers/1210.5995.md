# A method for extracting the resonance parameters from experimental cross section

> The matrix elements of the multi-channel Jost matrices are written in such a way that their dependencies on all possible odd powers of channel momenta are factorized explicitly. As a result the branching of the Riemann energy surface at all the channel thresholds is represented in them via exact analytic expressions. The remaining single-valued functions of the energy are expanded in the Taylor series near an arbitrary point on the real axis. Using the thus obtained Jost matrices, the $S$-matrix is constructed and then the scattering cross section is calculated, which therefore depends on the Taylor expansion coefficients. These coefficients are considered as the adjustable parameters that are optimized to fit a given set of experimental data. After finding the coefficients, the resonances are located as zeros of the Jost matrix determinant at complex energies. Within this approach the $S$-matrix has proper analytic structure. This enables us not only to locate multi-channel resonances but also to reproduce their partial widths as well as the scattering cross section in the channels for which the data are not available.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method for extracting multi-channel resonance parameters from experimental cross-section data by parametrizing the Jost matrices via Taylor expansions of their single-valued components, thereby preserving the correct analytic structure (branching points at all channel thresholds) without embedding resonance poles by hand. The method is demonstrated to work for short-range interactions, but the authors note that extension to Coulomb forces and relativistic kinematics would require significant modifications and are not rigorously justified.

## Method

The authors factorize the multi-channel Jost matrices into explicit odd-power momentum factors (which contain all threshold branching) and single-valued matrices A(E) and B(E). These single-valued matrices are expanded in Taylor series around a real energy point E0, and the expansion coefficients serve as real fitting parameters. The S-matrix is constructed from the Jost matrices, and the χ² function (plus a symmetry-enforcing term) is minimized using the MINUIT program to fit experimental cross-section data. After optimization, resonances are located as complex zeros of the determinant of the incoming Jost matrix, and partial widths are obtained from the Jost matrix elements at the resonance energy.

## Key numbers

- Single-channel test (potential V(r)=7.5r²e⁻ʳ, ℓ=0): first resonance recovered as ℰ_fit₁ = 3.426388 − i/2·0.025531, compared to exact ℰ_exact₁ = 3.426390 − i/2·0.025549; second resonance recovered as ℰ_fit₂ = 4.821657 − i/2·2.036732, compared to exact ℰ_exact₂ = 4.834807 − i/2·2.235753.
- Single-channel statistical analysis (1000 variations): ℰ_fit₁ = (3.426309 ± 0.005361) − i/2·(0.025921 ± 0.010444); ℰ_fit₂ = (3.810114 ± 0.448626) − i/2·(1.457056 ± 1.090140).
- Two-channel Noro-Taylor model (thresholds E₁=0, E₂=0.1, ℓ₁=ℓ₂=0): second resonance recovered as ℰ_fit₂ = 7.250742 − i/2·1.513332, with partial widths Γ₁_fit=0.347732 and Γ₂_fit=1.165600.
- Two-channel statistical analysis (1000 variations): ℰ_fit₂ = (7.251593 ± 0.292781) − i/2·(1.085568 ± 0.537132); Γ₁_fit=0.346348±0.343585, Γ₂_fit=0.739220±0.516272.
- Fitting used M=5 (six terms in Taylor series) with 12 parameters for single-channel and 48 parameters for two-channel; achieved χ²=1.5×10⁻⁶ (single) and 𝒳²=1.9×10⁻⁴ (two-channel).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier theoretical derivations of Jost matrix structure (Rakityansky and Elander, 2009, 2011) into a practical data-analysis tool. It addresses the long-standing difficulty of constructing multi-channel S-matrices with correct branching at all thresholds for three or more channels, which traditional approaches such as the Dalitz-Tuan representation (Badalyan) and the unitary isobar model (Lee, Tiator) cannot handle easily. The method enables extraction of resonance parameters and partial widths, as well as prediction of cross sections in experimentally inaccessible channels, for systems with short-range interactions; the authors note that Coulomb corrections could be approximated using the C₀(η) factor approach of Arndt, and relativistic kinematics could be introduced by replacing non-relativistic channel momenta with relativistic ones.
