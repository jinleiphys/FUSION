# Glauber model for heavy ion collisions from low energies to high energies

> The Glauber model is extensively applied to heavy ion collision for describing a number of interaction processes over a wide range of energies from near the Coulomb barrier to higher energies. The model gives the nucleus-nucleus interaction in terms of interaction between the constituent nucleons with a given density distribution. The model is a semiclassical model picturing the nuclear collision in the impact parameter representation where the nuclei move along the collision direction in a straight path. In these lectures we derive this model and discuss its applications in variety of problems in nuclear and high energy physics.

# Glauber model for heavy ion collisions from low energies to high energies

## Key claim

The Glauber model, a semiclassical straight-line trajectory approach, can describe nucleus-nucleus collisions from near the Coulomb barrier to ultrarelativistic energies, provided that at low energies the straight-line trajectory is replaced by the trajectory evaluated at the distance of closest approach under the Coulomb potential (Coulomb-modified Glauber model). The model treats the collision as a superposition of independent nucleon-nucleon interactions, with the caveat that at low energies the surface region dominates and the eikonal approximation remains valid because V/E << 1 in that region.

## Method

The nucleus-nucleus scattering matrix S(b) is constructed from the overlap integral T(b) of the z-integrated nuclear densities of projectile and target, multiplied by the nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross section σ_NN. In the optical limit, |S(b)|^2 = exp(-T(b) σ_NN AB), from which the reaction cross section σ_R = 2π ∫ b db (1 - |S(b)|^2) is obtained. For low-energy collisions, the impact parameter b is replaced by the distance of closest approach r_c calculated from the Coulomb trajectory, and the overlap integral T(r_c) is evaluated along the straight line at that distance. The model is extended to compute participant and spectator numbers, forward energy, excitation energy, charged-particle multiplicity, and J/ψ production cross sections using binomial distributions derived from the same geometrical overlap.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross section at high energies: σ_NN = 3.2 fm^2
- Range parameter for the Gaussian profile function: r_0^2 = 0.439 fm^2
- Equilibrium nuclear matter density: ρ_0 = 0.14 fm^{-3}
- J/ψ absorption cross section: σ_abs = 6.2 mb
- Parameterization of pp/nn cross section (fm^2): σ_pp = σ_nn = 1.373 - 1.504/β + 0.876/β^2 + 6.867 β^2, where β is the projectile nucleon velocity
- Parameterization of np cross section for E_N > 10 MeV (fm^2): σ_np = -7.067 - 1.818/β + 2.526/β^2 + 11.385 β
- Parameterization of np cross section for E_N < 10 MeV (fm^2): σ_np = 273/((1 - 0.0553 E_N)^2 + 0.35 E_N) + 1763/((1 + 0.334 E_N)^2 + 6.8 E_N)
- Charged-particle multiplicity in pp collisions: <n_ch> = 0.88 + 0.44 ln(s) + 0.118 (ln(s))^2, where √s is the center-of-mass energy

## Context

This lecture note reviews and extends the Glauber model framework originally developed for high-energy hadron-nucleus scattering, applying it systematically to heavy-ion collisions from low to ultrarelativistic energies. The Coulomb-modified version addresses the limitation of the straight-line assumption at low energies, which is critical for systems such as ¹⁶O + ²⁰⁸Pb near the Coulomb barrier. The participant-spectator formalism connects to Monte Carlo event generators including FRITIOF (based on the LUND string model), IRIS, VENUS, HIJET, and RQMD, and provides the geometric basis for interpreting J/ψ suppression data from NA38, NA50, and NA51 experiments. The work enables consistent treatment of nuclear geometry across different models, which is necessary before drawing conclusions about rescattering or quark-gluon plasma formation.
