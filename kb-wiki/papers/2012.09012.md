# Recent advances in the quantification of uncertainties in reaction theory

> Uncertainty quantification has become increasingly more prominent in nuclear physics over the past several years. In few-body reaction theory, there are four main sources that contribute to the uncertainties in the calculated observables: the effective potentials, approximations made to the few-body problem, structure functions, and degrees of freedom left out of the model space. In this work, we illustrate some of the features that can be obtained when modern statistical tools are applied in the context of nuclear reactions. This work consists of a summary of the progress that has been made in quantifying theoretical uncertainties in this domain, focusing primarily on those uncertainties coming from the effective optical potential as well as their propagation within various reaction theories. We use, as the central example, reactions on the doubly-magic stable nucleus $^{40}$Ca, namely neutron and proton elastic scattering and single-nucleon transfer $^{40}$Ca(d,p)$^{41}$Ca. First, we show different optimization schemes used to constrain the optical potential from differential cross sections and other experimental constraints; we then discuss how these uncertainties propagate to the transfer cross section, comparing two reaction theories. We finish by laying out our future plans.

## Key claim

While the abstract states that the work illustrates features obtained when modern statistical tools are applied to nuclear reactions, the paper primarily summarizes progress in quantifying uncertainties from the effective optical potential and their propagation within reaction theories, using reactions on $^{40}$Ca as a central example, without presenting new experimental data or definitive conclusions on all four uncertainty sources.

## Method

The authors used standard $\chi^2$ minimization with covariance matrix propagation (frequentist methods) and Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling to optimize the nine free parameters of Woods-Saxon optical potentials for neutron, proton, and deuteron elastic scattering on $^{40}$Ca. They then propagated the resulting parameter uncertainties to calculate the $^{40}$Ca(d,p)$^{41}$Ca single-neutron transfer cross section using both the distorted-wave Born approximation (DWBA) and the adiabatic wave approximation (ADWA). The frequentist fits were performed with both uncorrelated and correlated $\chi^2$ functions, where the correlated $\chi^2$ included a model covariance matrix between scattering angles.

## Key numbers

- For $^{40}$Ca(n,n) at 13.9 MeV, $^{40}$Ca(p,p) at 14.5 MeV, $^{40}$Ca(p,p) at 26.3 MeV, and $^{40}$Ca(d,d) at 30.0 MeV, the 95% confidence intervals from correlated fits were at least as large as (and in most cases larger than) those from uncorrelated fits, even though the $\chi^2$ values were at least 5 times smaller in the correlated best fit.
- The $^{40}$Ca(d,p)$^{41}$Ca transfer cross section was calculated at a deuteron energy of 28.4 MeV for the ground state.
- For all elastic scattering angular distribution data and total reaction cross sections, the experimental uncertainty was taken to be 10%; for vector analyzing powers, the uncertainty was also 10% except where the value was less than 5% of the maximum value across all angles, in which case the uncertainty was taken as 5% of that maximum value.
- The Bayesian priors for each optical potential parameter were independent Gaussians centered at the Becchetti-Greenlees parameter values with a width of 100% of those values.

## Context

This work extends the group's earlier studies on uncertainty quantification in few-body reactions, which began with Lovell (2015) using standard $\chi^2$ minimization and found only 10-30% differences between optical model parameterizations for reactions on exotic nuclei. It builds on King (2019) which compared frequentist and Bayesian methods for reactions on stable targets, and on Lovell (2017) and Lovell (2018) which developed frequentist and Bayesian frameworks respectively. The paper enables future work to incorporate uncertainties from structure functions and degrees of freedom left out of the model space, and to apply these statistical tools to more complex reaction models beyond the simple optical potential parameterization.
