# Semi-Classical Description of Antiproton Capture on Atomic Helium

> A semi-classical, many-body atomic model incorporating a momentum-dependent Heisenberg core to stabilize atomic electrons is used to study antiproton capture on Helium. Details of the antiproton collisions leading to eventual capture are presented, including the energy and angular momentum states of incident antiprotons which result in capture via single or double electron ionization, i.e. into [He$^{++}\,\bar p$ or He$^{+}\,\bar p$], and the distribution of energy and angular momentum states following the Auger cascade. These final states are discussed in light of recently reported, anomalously long-lived antiproton states observed in liquid He.

## Key claim

The paper presents a semi-classical Monte Carlo model that reproduces the capture of antiprotons into both doubly ionized (He++ pbar) and singly ionized (He+ pbar) states, and finds that approximately 22.3% of singly ionized systems survive to 10^5 atomic units of time, which is much larger than the experimentally observed 3.6% metastable fraction surviving beyond about 1 microsecond, suggesting additional decay mechanisms beyond the scope of the model.

## Method

The authors used a semi-classical, many-body atomic model with a momentum-dependent Heisenberg core to stabilize atomic electrons, solving Hamilton's classical equations of motion for the antiproton and two electrons in the field of a helium nucleus. Monte Carlo calculations of 4,000 collision sequences (approximately 41,000 individual He-antiproton collisions) were performed, with the antiproton launched at 80 eV initial energy and randomized impact parameters, following sequential collisions until capture occurred. The final antiproton states were analyzed in terms of energy and angular momentum distributions for both single and double electron ionization channels.

## Key numbers

- Reduced mass of antiproton in atomic units: M* = 1469
- Principal quantum number of circular states: n0 = sqrt(M*) ≈ 38
- 916 out of 4,000 collision sequences (approximately 23%) ended with capture via single electron ionization into He+ pbar; the remaining 77% resulted in capture via double ionization into He++ pbar
- Survival probability fit: P(t) = [22.3 + 82.6 exp(-t/2051) + 7.67 exp(-t/57,650)]%, giving 22.3% survival at 10^5 a.u.
- Experimentally observed metastable population: 3.6% survival beyond about 1 microsecond (approximately 4 x 10^10 a.u.)
- Heisenberg core parameters: alpha = 1.0, xi_H = 2.767, giving ground state energy of -2.78 a.u. at electron radius 0.63 a.u. (compared to exact -2.9037 a.u. at mean radius 0.59 a.u.)
- Antiproton capture cross section saturates at pi b_max^2 for energies approximately 0.1 a.u. or lower, with b_max = 3.0 a.u.

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposals by Condo (1964) and Russell (1969-1972) and the specific metastable circular state hypothesis of Yamazaki et al. (1989) for long-lived antiproton states observed in liquid helium by Iwasaki et al. (1991). The semi-classical approach using a Heisenberg core, originally developed by Kirschbaum and Wilets (1980), provides new details on the energy and angular momentum distributions of captured antiprotons that can be used by those who have calculated Born-Oppenheimer states (Yamazaki and Ohtsuki, 1992) to compute annihilation rates from antiproton wave functions at the helium nucleus. The results enable further study of impurity effects on slowing and capture, and invite investigation of the longer time-scale decay dynamics beyond the 10^5 a.u. computational limit.
