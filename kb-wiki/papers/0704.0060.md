# Coulomb excitation of unstable nuclei at intermediate energies

> We investigate the Coulomb excitation of low-lying states of unstable nuclei in intermediate energy collisions ($E_{lab}\sim10-500$ MeV/nucleon). It is shown that the cross sections for the $E1$ and $E2$ transitions are larger at lower energies, much less than 10 MeV/nucleon. Retardation effects and Coulomb distortion are found to be both relevant for energies as low as 10 MeV/nucleon and as high as 500 MeV/nucleon. Implications for studies at radioactive beam facilities are discussed.

# Coulomb excitation of unstable nuclei at intermediate energies

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that accurate Coulomb excitation cross sections for unstable nuclei at intermediate energies (10–500 MeV/nucleon) require inclusion of both retardation effects and Coulomb distortion, which are relevant across the entire energy range; simpler non-relativistic or pure relativistic treatments can yield cross sections that differ by up to 30–40% from the correct values, and the approximate analytical formulas fail badly for E2 transitions at low and intermediate energies.

## Method
The authors used the formalism of Bertulani (2003) to calculate Coulomb excitation cross sections via the code COULINT, which computes orbital integrals S(πλ,μ) along Coulomb trajectories corrected for retardation. They compared these exact calculations with approximate analytical formulas based on the relativistic theory of Winther and Alder (1979) for E1, E2, and M1 transitions. Cross sections were computed for numerous light and medium-mass projectiles incident on Pb targets at bombarding energies from 10 to 500 MeV/nucleon, using reduced transition probabilities B(πλ) from the TUNL nuclear data evaluation database and other published sources.

## Key numbers
- Exact calculations differ from non-relativistic and relativistic treatments by up to 30–40% for experimental observables (cross sections, gamma-ray angular distributions)
- For E1 transitions, the approximate formulas work within 20% at 10 MeV/nucleon and 5% at 50 MeV/nucleon
- For the 13.09 MeV state in ¹⁶O, the approximate cross section is a factor of 10 smaller than the exact calculation at 10 MeV/nucleon, dropping to 10% difference at 100 MeV/nucleon
- For ¹¹Be on Pb at 43 MeV/nucleon, the experimental cross section for the E1 transition (B(E1)=0.115 e²fm²) is 191±26 mb, while the exact calculation gives 328 mb and the approximate gives 323 mb
- For ³²Mg on Pb at 49.2 MeV/nucleon, the experimental E2 cross section (B(E2)=454 e²fm⁴) is 91.7±14.4 mb, while the exact calculation gives 137 mb and the approximate gives 128 mb
- For ⁵⁴Ni on Pb, the E2 cross section at 10 MeV/nucleon is 1319 mb, decreasing to 43.1 mb at 500 MeV/nucleon
- For ¹¹Be on Pb, the E1 cross section at 10 MeV/nucleon is 1128 mb, decreasing to 69.6 mb at 500 MeV/nucleon

## Context
This work extends the formalism of Bertulani (2003) for Coulomb excitation at intermediate energies, where neither the non-relativistic treatment of Alder and Winther (1975) nor the relativistic treatment of Winther and Alder (1979) and Bertulani and Baur (1985) is appropriate. The paper provides systematic cross section predictions for planning experiments at radioactive beam facilities (LNS/Catania, NSCL/MSU, GSI, GANIL, RIKEN). The authors note that many existing experimental data on unstable nuclei have been analyzed with codes lacking relativistic dynamics, as first addressed by Bertulani (2005), leading to incorrect electromagnetic transition probabilities. The results enable improved planning of Coulomb excitation experiments and highlight the need for proper treatment of relativistic effects, particularly for E2 transitions and high-lying states.
