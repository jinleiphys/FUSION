# Non-Markovian character and irreversibility of real-time quantum many-body dynamics

> The presence of pairing correlations within the time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) extension to superfluid systems, is tantamount to the presence of a quantum collision integral in the evolution equations, which leads to an obviously non-Markovian behavior of the single-particle occupation probabilities, unexpected in a traditional quantum extension of kinetic equations. The quantum generalization of the Boltzmann equation, based on a collision integral in terms of phase-space occupation probabilities, is the most used approach to describe nuclear dynamics and which by construction has a Markovian character. By contrast, the extension of TDDFT to superfluid systems has similarities with the Baym and Kadanoff kinetic formalism, which however is formulated with much more complicated evolution equations with long-time memory terms and non-local interactions. The irreversibility of quantum dynamics is properly characterized using the canonical wave functions/natural orbitals, and the associated canonical occupation probabilities, which provide the smallest possible representation of any fermionic many-body wave function. In this basis, one can evaluate the orbital entanglement entropy, which is an excellent measure of the non-equilibrium dynamics of an isolated system. To explore the phenomena of memory effects and irreversibility, we investigate the use of canonical wave functions/natural orbitals in nuclear many-body calculations, assessing their utility for static calculations, dynamics, and symmetry restoration. As the number of single-particle states is generally quite large, it is highly desirable to work in the canonical basis whenever possible, preferably with a cutoff. We show that truncating the number of canonical wave functions can be a valid approach in the case of static calculations, but that such a truncation is not valid for time-dependent calculations...

## Key claim

The presence of pairing correlations in time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) for superfluid systems introduces a quantum collision integral that produces non-Markovian behavior in single-particle occupation probabilities, contrary to the Markovian character assumed in traditional quantum kinetic equations based on the Boltzmann equation.

## Method

The authors used the time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) equations to simulate nuclear fission dynamics, representing the many-body wave function in the canonical basis of natural orbitals. They evaluated the orbital entanglement entropy from the canonical occupation probabilities as a measure of irreversibility, and tested the validity of truncating the canonical basis by comparing static and time-dependent calculations with the full basis.

## Key numbers

- The total number of proton and neutron quasi-particle states on a spatial lattice of typical size 30×30×60 is 216,000 (2×2×NxNyNz).
- At any time during time evolution, the normal and anomalous densities can be represented with comparable numerical accuracy using about 5,000 canonical states or less.
- For static calculations of 240Pu, accurate binding energies were reproduced with at most 400 neutron orbitals and 300 proton orbitals.
- The anomalous density matrix κ(ξ,ζ) diverges as 1/|r−r'| in the limit |r−r'|→0 for local pairing potentials.
- The occupation probabilities of canonical states at large momenta follow n(p)∝1/p4.

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent density functional theory framework for superfluid systems developed by Bulgac and collaborators, contrasting with the Markovian quantum Boltzmann equation approach commonly used in nuclear dynamics. The authors show that truncating the canonical basis, which was proposed as a valid approach for static calculations by Chen (2022), fails for time-dependent calculations because it violates continuity equations, energy conservation, and misrepresents dynamics. The findings enable proper characterization of irreversibility in nuclear fission and other non-equilibrium superfluid dynamics through orbital entanglement entropy.
