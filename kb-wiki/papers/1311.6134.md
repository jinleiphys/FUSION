# Isospin-asymmetric nuclear matter

> This study uses classical molecular dynamics to simulate infinite nuclear matter and study the effect of isospin asymmetry on bulk properties such as energy per nucleon, pressure, saturation density, compressibility and symmetry energy. The simulations are performed on systems embedded in periodic boundary conditions with densities and temperatures in the ranges $ρ$=0.02 to 0.2 fm$^{-3}$ and T = 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5 MeV, and with isospin content of $x=Z/A$=0.3, 0.4 and 0.5. The results indicate that symmetric and asymmetric matter are self-bound at some temperatures and exhibit phase transitions from a liquid phase to a liquid-gas mixture. The main effect of isospin asymmetry is found to be a reduction of the equilibrium densities, a softening of the compressibility and a disappearance of the liquid-gas phase transition. A procedure leading to the evaluation of the symmetry energy and its variation with the temperature was devised, implemented and compared to mean field theory results.

## Key claim
Classical molecular dynamics simulations show that isospin asymmetry reduces equilibrium densities, softens compressibility, and can suppress the liquid-gas phase transition in infinite nuclear matter, but the procedure used to extract the symmetry energy from the CMD data has intrinsic variability that limits its accuracy to "ball park" estimates rather than exact values.

## Method
The authors used classical molecular dynamics (CMD) with Pandharipande pair potentials to simulate infinite nuclear matter systems of 2000 nucleons in cubic cells with periodic boundary conditions. The Nosé-Hoover equations of motion were integrated to maintain canonical ensemble conditions at fixed temperatures, and systems were studied at densities from 0.02 to 0.2 fm⁻³, temperatures of 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 MeV, and isospin contents x = Z/A = 0.3, 0.4, and 0.5. The energy per nucleon, pressure, saturation density, compressibility, and symmetry energy were extracted from the equilibrium configurations.

## Key numbers
- Saturation density for symmetric matter (x=0.5) varies from ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ at T→0 to 0.12 fm⁻³ at T=5 MeV
- Saturation density for x=0.4 matter varies from ρ₀ ≈ 0.12 fm⁻³ at T=1 MeV to 0.09 fm⁻³ at T=5 MeV
- Compressibility is reduced by about 50% as x drops from 0.5 to 0.4 or from 0.4 to 0.3
- Compressibility is further reduced by about 30% as T increases from 1 to 5 MeV for x=0.4 and 0.5 systems
- The average standard deviation of energy per nucleon measurements is 0.036 MeV across 200 thermodynamically independent configurations
- The Pandharipande potentials use a cutoff radius of r_c = 5.4 fm
- The cold nuclear matter saturation density is ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ with binding energy E(ρ₀) = -16 MeV/nucleon and compressibility of about 250 MeV

## Context
This work extends previous CMD studies of nuclear matter at low temperatures (López et al., 2013) to higher temperatures and varying isospin content, and compares results to relativistic Hartree calculations (Chen et al.) and mean-field theory. The findings confirm predictions from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock and relativistic mean-field calculations at zero temperature (Tanihata), and the temperature dependence of the symmetry energy agrees with the modified Seyler-Blanchard model (de Lima and Randrup, 1998) but disagrees with virial expansion results (Horowitz and Schwenk, 2006) and self-consistent model calculations (Xu et al., 2007). The study enables future investigations of transport properties such as speed of sound and diffusion coefficients, as well as extension to lower temperatures where crystalline structures exist.
