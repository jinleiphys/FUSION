# Effects of shell structure in reactions leading to the same compound nucleus or different isotopes

> The role of the entrance channel in fusion-fission reactions was studied by the theoretical analysis of the experimental evaporation residue excitation functions for reactions leading to the same compound nucleus. The evaporation residues cross sections for xn-channels were calculated in the frame of the combined dinuclear system concept and advanced statistical model. The revealed differences between experimental data on the evaporation residues in the ^{40}Ar+^{176}Hf, ^{86}Kr + ^{130}Xe and ^{124}Sn + ^{92}Zr reactions leading to the ^{216}Th^* compound nucleus are explained by the different spin distributions of compound nuclei which are formed. It is shown that the intrinsic fusion barrier B^*_{fus} and size of potential well are different for every entrance channel.

## Key claim

The observed differences in evaporation residue cross sections for reactions leading to the same compound nucleus (216Th*) are explained by the different spin distributions of the compound nuclei formed, which arise from entrance-channel-dependent intrinsic fusion barriers B*_fus and potential well sizes that are influenced by nuclear shell structure rather than by liquid-drop binding energies alone.

## Method

The authors combined the dinuclear system concept (DNS) with an advanced statistical model (ASM) to calculate capture, fusion, and evaporation residue cross sections. Capture probabilities were obtained by solving dynamical equations of motion for relative distance and orbital angular momentum with friction coefficients, while fusion probabilities were determined from the ratio of level densities depending on the intrinsic fusion barrier B*_fus and quasi-fission barrier B_qf. The driving potential was calculated using binding energies from the mass table of Audi and Wapstra (1995) and compared with liquid-drop model predictions.

## Key numbers

- For reactions leading to 216Th*: maximum evaporation residue for 40Ar+176Hf is twelve times larger than for 86Kr+130Xe and three times larger than for 124Sn+92Zr.
- Intrinsic fusion barriers B*_fus for reactions leading to 216Th*: 2.31 MeV (40Ar+176Hf), 12.31 MeV (86Kr+130Xe), 9.87 MeV (124Sn+92Zr).
- Quasi-fission barriers B_qf for reactions leading to 216Th*: 7.12 MeV (40Ar+176Hf), 2.35 MeV (86Kr+130Xe), 1.35 MeV (124Sn+92Zr).
- Fusion hindrance factor P_CN for reactions leading to 216Th*: 0.580 (40Ar+176Hf), 0.014 (86Kr+130Xe), 0.081 (124Sn+92Zr).
- For reactions leading to 222Th*: B*_fus = 7.52 MeV, B_qf = 4.05 MeV, P_CN = 0.027 (86Kr+136Xe).
- Evaporation residue in 86Kr+136Xe is about 500 times larger than in 86Kr+130Xe.
- Neutron separation energies: S_n = 7.808 MeV for 222Th*, S_n = 8.701 MeV for 216Th*.
- Ratio (Gamma_n/Gamma_f)_222Th* / (Gamma_n/Gamma_f)_216Th* ranges from 1.3 to 4.2 for the 1n-channel, 2.2 to 9.9 for the 2n-channel, 3.0 to 1.9e5 for the 3n-channel, and 2.7e4 to 9.1e5 for the 4n-channel.

## Context

This work extends the DNS concept developed by Adamian et al. (1997) and the advanced statistical model described by Sagawa (1998) to explain experimental evaporation residue data from Oganessian et al. (1996) for 86Kr+130,136Xe, Vermeulen et al. (1984) and Clerc et al. (1984) for 40Ar+176Hf, and Sahm et al. (1985) for 124Sn+92Zr. The results demonstrate that shell effects in binding energies, not liquid-drop model predictions, are essential for correctly reproducing the observed differences between reactions leading to the same compound nucleus, and that the neutron-rich 136Xe target produces a larger fusion cross section and higher survival probability due to a wider potential well and lower intrinsic fusion barrier. This enables improved predictions for optimal entrance channels in the synthesis of super-heavy elements.
