# Emergence of hydrodynamical behavior in expanding quark-gluon plasmas

> We use a set of simple angular moments to solve the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation for a boost invariant longitudinally expanding gluonic plasma. The transition from the free streaming regime at early time to the hydrodynamic regime at late time is well captured by the first two-moments, corresponding to the monopole and quadrupole components of the momentum distribution, or equivalently to the energy density and the difference between the longitudinal and the transverse pressures. We relate this property to the existence of fixed points in the infinite hierarchy of equations satisfied by the moments. These fixed points are already present in the two-moment truncations and are only moderately affected by the coupling to higher moments. Collisions contribute to a damping of all the non trivial moments. At late time, when the hydrodynamic regime is entered, only the monopole and quadrupole moments are significant and remain strongly coupled, the decay of the quadrupole moment being delayed by the expansion, causing in turn a delay in the full isotropization of the system. The two-moment truncation contains second order viscous hydrodynamics, in its various variants, and third order hydrodynamics, together with explicit values of the relevant transport coefficients, can be easily obtained from the three-moment truncation.

## Key claim

The transition from free streaming to hydrodynamics in an expanding gluonic plasma is captured by the first two angular moments (monopole and quadrupole) of the momentum distribution, and this property arises from fixed points in the infinite moment hierarchy that are already present in the two-moment truncation and only moderately affected by coupling to higher moments.

## Method

The authors solve the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation for a boost-invariant, longitudinally expanding gluonic plasma, using a set of angular moments (L-moments) defined as integrals of the distribution function weighted by Legendre polynomials P_{2n}(cosθ). They truncate the infinite hierarchy of moment equations at the two-moment level (keeping only L_0 and L_1, corresponding to energy density and pressure anisotropy) and compare results to the exact solution of the kinetic equation. The analysis covers both constant relaxation time and conformal (τ_R T = constant) cases, with isotropic and anisotropic initial conditions parameterized by a deformation parameter ξ.

## Key numbers

- The two-moment truncation reproduces the exact kinetic solution for the pressure ratio P_L/P_T across four different initial conditions, with the largest deviations occurring when the collision rate is small (r_0 = τ_0/τ_R = 0.07) and initial longitudinal pressure is very small.
- For a flat momentum distribution (ξ → ∞), the asymptotic ratio of moments is L_1/L_0 = A_1 = -1/2, corresponding to vanishing longitudinal pressure.
- The coefficients in the moment hierarchy include a_0 = 4/3, a_1 = 38/21, b_1 = 8/15, c_0 = 2/3, c_1 = -12/35.
- At late time in free streaming, all moments decay as 1/τ and are proportional to each other with constants A_n = P_{2n}(0) = (-1)^n (2n-1)!!/(2n)!!, giving A_1 = -1/2 and A_2 = 3/8.
- The hydrodynamic regime sets in when P_L/P_T ≈ 0.6, corresponding to L_1/L_0 ≈ 0.15 (a 15% correction to the leading moment).

## Context

This work extends the moment-based approach introduced by Blaizot and Yan (2017) for studying pre-equilibrium dynamics in Bjorken-expanding plasmas. It builds on the concept of "hydrodynamization" observed in holographic calculations by Heller et al. (2011) and in kinetic theory by Keegan et al. (2015) and Kurkela et al. (2015), where viscous hydrodynamics works before local equilibrium is achieved. The paper connects to the attractor framework of Heller and Spalinski (2015) and provides explicit transport coefficients for second-order and third-order viscous hydrodynamics from the moment truncations, enabling systematic improvement of hydrodynamic descriptions of heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
