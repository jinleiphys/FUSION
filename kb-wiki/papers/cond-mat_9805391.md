# Phase Transitions Without Thermodynamic Limit,The Crucial Rôle of Possible and Impossible Fluctuations, The Treatment of Inhomogeneous Scenaria in the Microcanonical Ensemble

> Microcanonical thermodynamics (MCTh) is contrasted to canonical thermodynamics (CTh). At phase transitions of 1.order the two ensembles are NOT equivalent even in the thermodynamic limit . Energy fluctuations do not vanish and phase separations are suppressed in CTh. A proper treatment of fluctuations is neccessary. MCTh allows to address even isolated small systems where phase transitions can be clearly classified into first order and continuous ones. The microcanonical caloric curve T(E) determines the transition temperature, latent heat AND surface entropy/tension. For systems of ca.1000 Na-, K-, or Fe-atoms at 1 atm. all 3 quantities can be calculated. The three parameters approach with rising size the known bulk values. There is nothing that demands the use of the thermodynamic limit. Within microcanonical thermodynamics of finite systems there are fundamental differences between conserved extensive variables and ensemble related ones like entropy, temperature and pressure. This is discussed in detail.

# Phase Transitions Without Thermodynamic Limit

## Key claim

Microcanonical thermodynamics can classify first-order and continuous phase transitions in finite systems of only a few hundred atoms, without invoking the thermodynamic limit; the canonical ensemble suppresses the large-scale spatial inhomogeneities (phase separations) that are essential to first-order transitions, making the two ensembles inequivalent even in the thermodynamic limit.

## Method

The author uses microcanonical Monte Carlo simulations (MMMC) of fragmenting atomic clusters, where the basic particles are spherical fragments whose ground-state binding energies are taken from experiment and whose internal degrees of freedom are modeled using the known bulk specific heat of solid/liquid matter. The microcanonical caloric curve T(E) is computed at constant pressure (1 atm) for systems of 200–3000 atoms, and the back-bending region where ∂T/∂E ≤ 0 is used to extract the transition temperature, latent heat, and surface entropy via a Maxwell construction on the inverse temperature β(e).

## Key numbers

- For sodium clusters of N₀ = 1000 atoms at 1 atm: T_tr = 866 K, q_lat = 0.871 eV, σ/T_tr = 5.68 (bulk values: T_tr = 1156 K, q_lat = 0.923 eV, σ/T_tr = 7.41)
- For potassium clusters of N₀ = 1000 atoms at 1 atm: T_tr = 767 K, q_lat = 0.70 eV, σ/T_tr = 7.06 (bulk values: T_tr = 1033 K, q_lat = 0.80 eV, σ/T_tr = 7.31)
- For iron clusters of N₀ = 1000 atoms at 1 atm: T_tr = 2910 K, q_lat = 3.18 eV, σ/T_tr = 8.87 (bulk values: T_tr = 3158 K, q_lat = 3.55 eV, σ/T_tr = 17.49)
- The specific boiling entropy s_boil = q_lat/T_tr for all three metals at all cluster sizes is near 10, consistent with Trouton's rule
- The surface entropy loss Δs_surf for Na at N₀ = 1000 is 0.56 (entropy units per atom)

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical thermodynamics approach previously developed by Gross and collaborators (Gross 1990, Gross 1995) for nuclear multifragmentation, and applies it to metallic clusters to demonstrate that phase transitions are well-defined without the thermodynamic limit. The results contradict the common textbook statement that canonical and microcanonical ensembles are equivalent in the thermodynamic limit, and show that the fast scaling of transition parameters toward bulk values (also seen in Potts model calculations by Gross (1997) and explained by Hüller and Promberger (1996)) arises because canonical finite-size scaling is dominated by the trivial Laplace transform factor rather than by the physics of the transition. The work enables microscopic calculation of surface entropy and surface tension for metals, which was previously not possible, and provides a test of the MMMC method for eventual application to nuclear matter liquid-gas transitions.
