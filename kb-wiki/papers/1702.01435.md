# Characterizing the mechanism(s) of heavy element synthesis

> A review of the current state of our understanding of complete fusion reaction mechanisms is presented, from the perspective of an experimentalist. For complete fusion reactions, the overall uncertainties in predicting heavy element synthesis cross sections are examined in terms of the uncertainties associated with the calculations of capture cross sections, fusion probabilities and survival probabilities.

## Key claim

The overall uncertainty in predicting heavy element synthesis cross sections is at least 1-2 orders of magnitude, not the factor of 2-4 some researchers claim, because the uncertainties in capture cross sections, fusion probabilities, and survival probabilities compound in ways that simple agreement between postdicted and measured cross sections can mask.

## Method

The paper reviews the three-stage formalism for evaporation residue cross sections (capture, fusion, survival) and examines the uncertainties in each stage. For capture cross sections, the author compares coupled-channel model calculations to measured cross sections for reactions synthesizing heavy nuclei. For survival probabilities, the author discusses the role of Kramers effects (nuclear viscosity) and fission barrier height uncertainties. For fusion probabilities, the author surveys experimental data on the ratio of fusion-fission to quasifission and examines scaling variables such as the du Rietz fissility parameter.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of calculated to observed capture cross sections varies from 0.5 to 2 across reactions that synthesize heavy nuclei.
- For the reaction $^{26}$Mg+$^{248}$Cm forming $^{274}$Hs at 63 MeV excitation energy, the measured value of $\Gamma_n/\Gamma_{\rm total}$ for first-chance fission was $0.89 \pm 0.13$, meaning a nucleus with a vanishingly small fission barrier decayed ~90% of the time by neutron emission.
- The average deviation between calculated and known inner fission barrier heights for Th-Cf nuclei is ~0.7 MeV across various models; uncertainties in fission barrier heights are 0.5-1.0 MeV in known nuclei.
- For even-even nuclei with Z=92-98, the difference between measured and calculated inner barrier heights was 0.8 MeV.
- For the reaction $^{124}$Sn+$^{96}$Zr, theoretical estimates of $P_{\rm CN}$ range from 0.0002 to 0.56, while the measured value is 0.05.
- For the reaction $^{50}$Ti+$^{249}$Bk$\rightarrow$119, the uncertainties in predicted maximum cross sections for the 3n and 4n channels differ by a factor of 20-40; the energies of the maxima are uncertain to 3-4 MeV.
- TDHF calculations for $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U predicted $\sigma_{\rm fus}/\sigma_{\rm capture}$ as $0.09 \pm 0.07$ at 205.9 MeV and $0.16 \pm 0.06$ at 225.4 MeV, in agreement with measured ratios of $0.06 \pm 0.03$ and $0.14 \pm 0.05$, respectively.

## Context

This review extends the work of several groups who have modeled cold fusion reactions for elements 104-113, including Adamian, Feng, Swiatecki, and Loveland, showing that while postdicted cross sections agree with data over six orders of magnitude, the underlying $P_{\rm CN}$ values differ significantly among models and from measurements by Naik et al. The paper identifies the need for better measurements of fission barrier heights (building on work by Afanasjev, Bertsch, Kowal, and Baran) and for improved TDHF calculations of fusion probabilities. It enables experimentalists to prioritize measurements of capture cross sections and $P_{\rm CN}$ for specific reactions of interest, particularly for the synthesis of elements 119 and 120 and for potential use of neutron-rich radioactive beams to make new isotopes of elements 104-108.
