# Ultramicro Black Holes and Finiteness of the Electromagnetic Contribution to the Electron Mass

> It is argued that the nonintegrably singular energy density of the electron's electromagnetic field (in both the classical point-charge model and quantum electrodynamics) must entail very strong self-gravitational effects, which, via black hole phenomena at finite radii, could well cut off the otherwise infinite electromagnetic contribution to the electron's mass. The general- relativistic equations for static, spherically symmetric stellar structure are specialized to treat the self-gravitational effects of static, spheri- cally symmetric, nonnegative, localized energy densities which may exhibit nonintegrable singularities at zero radius. It is demonstrated that in many situations, including the electromagnetic ones of interest here, such a system has a black hole whose Schwarzschild radius is that where the original energy per radial distance (the spherical shell area times the original energy density) reaches the inverse of (2G). The total mass of the system is that of this black hole (which follows in the usual way from its Schwarz- schild radius) plus the integrated original energy density outside this black hole. These results produce, for the classical point-charge model of the electron, an electrostatic contribution to its mass which is many orders of magnitude larger than its measured mass. For quantum electrodynamics, how- ever, the result is an electromagnetic mass contribution which is approxi- mately equal to its bare mass -- thus about half of its measured mass.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the nonintegrable singularity of the electron's electromagnetic field energy density (in both the classical point-charge model and quantum electrodynamics) must trigger strong self-gravitational effects that produce a black hole at a finite radius, which could cut off the otherwise infinite electromagnetic contribution to the electron's mass; the author qualifies that this resolution is a "might conceivably" possibility rather than a proven result, and that the quantum electrodynamics result depends on combining a classical gravitational treatment with a quantum field theory energy density.

## Method

The author specializes the general-relativistic equations for static, spherically symmetric stellar structure to treat self-gravitational effects of a given G=0 energy density ε(r), introducing a pressure term to ensure covariant divergencelessness of the stress-energy tensor. The total gravitating mass is expressed as the black hole mass (Schwarzschild radius divided by 2G) plus the integral of the original energy density outside the black hole, where the Schwarzschild radius is determined as the radius where the energy per radial distance (4πr² times the G=0 energy density) first reaches the limiting value (2G)⁻¹. This procedure is applied to both the classical point-charge model and to an effective quantum electromagnetic energy density extracted from the lowest-order Feynman diagram for the electromagnetic contribution to the electron mass.

## Key numbers

- For the classical point-charge model of the electron, the electrostatic mass contribution is "many orders of magnitude larger than the electron's measured mass" (no specific numerical factor given).
- For quantum electrodynamics, the electromagnetic mass contribution is approximately equal to the electron's bare mass, which is "about half of its measured mass."
- The limiting value for the energy per radial distance at the Schwarzschild radius is (2G)⁻¹ (in units where c=1).
- The black hole mass equals its Schwarzschild radius divided by (2G).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing problem of the infinite electromagnetic self-energy of the electron, which appears in both the classical point-charge model and in quantum electrodynamics (where renormalization quarantines but does not eliminate the infinity). The author draws parallels to historical resolutions of infinities in physics: the Rayleigh-Jeans blackbody divergence resolved by Planck's quantum hypothesis, Lorentz's classical atomic instability resolved by Bohr's quantum theory, and the infinite Coulomb scattering cross section resolved by shielding. The paper proposes that gravity, conventionally dismissed as negligible in quantum electrodynamics due to its weakness at ordinary scales, must become dominant at the extremely short distances where the electromagnetic energy density diverges, producing black hole formation that cuts off the infinity. This approach contrasts with the renormalization program, which operates entirely within quantum electrodynamics and cannot banish the infinities. The author speculates that this gravitational mechanism could also resolve the remaining ultraviolet divergences of quantum electrodynamics (such as charge renormalization) and suggests that if gravity resolves all such divergences, the renormalizability requirement becomes a physical constraint rather than a calculational necessity, potentially resolving the long-standing problem that quantized general relativity is nonrenormalizable.
