# Cluster radioactivity in $^{114}$Ba in the HFB theory

> Cluster radioactivity in $^{114}$Ba is described as a spontaneous fission with a large mass asymmetry within the self-consistent HFB theory. A new fission valley with large octupole deformation is found in the potential energy surface. The fragment mass asymmetry of this fission mode corresponds to the expected one in cluster radioactivity with the emission of $^{16}$O predicted with a very long half-life.

## Key claim

Cluster radioactivity in $^{114}$Ba is described as a very asymmetric spontaneous fission within the self-consistent HFB theory, but the predicted half-life of $4.7 \times 10^9$ s is much longer than the experimental lower limit of $>1.2 \times 10^4$ s, meaning the decay has not been observed and may be practically unobservable.

## Method

The potential energy surface of $^{114}$Ba was calculated as a function of quadrupole and octupole moments using the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) model with the D1S Gogny force. A new fission valley with large octupole deformation was identified, and the half-life was computed in the WKB approximation using the octupole moment as the leading coordinate in the action integral, with microscopic zero-point energies along the fission path.

## Key numbers

- The asymmetric fission barrier height is 23 MeV, corresponding to a typical cluster radioactivity barrier in the actinide region.
- The saddle point is located at quadrupole moment $Q_2 = 16$ b and octupole moment $Q_3 = 12$ b$^{3/2}$.
- The calculated half-life for cluster radioactivity in $^{114}$Ba is $t_{1/2} = 4.7 \times 10^9$ s.
- The experimental lower limit on the half-life from Guglielmetti et al. (1995, 1996) is $t_{1/2} > 1.2 \times 10^4$ s, and from Oganessian et al. (1994) is $t_{1/2} > 10^3$ s.
- The light fragment produced at scission is $^{16}$O and the heavy residue is $^{98}$Cd, differing by two protons from the expected $^{14}$C and $^{100}$Sn fragments.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous fully microscopic approach (Warda 2002; Robledo and Warda 2008a, 2008b) that described cluster radioactivity in actinides as very asymmetric spontaneous fission, reproducing experimental half-lives with good accuracy. The application to $^{114}$Ba tests whether the same model can describe cluster radioactivity in the $^{100}$Sn region, where the doubly-magic heavy fragment condition differs from the $^{208}$Pb region. The results enable future experimental searches to target a specific half-life prediction, though the predicted value is far above current experimental sensitivity.
