# Assessing the joint effect of temperature and magnetic field on the neutron star equation of state

> In this work, we study the effect of strong magnetic fields on the equation of state (EoS) of warm, homogeneous, Neutron Star (NS) matter in beta equilibrium. NS matter is described within a relativistic mean field (RMF) approximation, including both models with non-linear meson terms or with density dependent nucleon-meson couplings. We first study the effect of magnetic fields and finite temperature on the EoS separately, finding that the effect of the latter to be significantly stronger than the one of the former. We then study the combined effect of magnetic fields and temperature on the internal composition. We show how both factors cause an increase in the proton fraction at low density and that, as long as the temperatures considered are not higher than 10 MeV, the effect of the magnetic field on the proton fraction is not small enough to be neglected.

# Assessing the joint effect of temperature and magnetic field on the neutron star equation of state

## Key claim

The effect of strong magnetic fields on the equation of state of warm neutron star matter is significantly smaller than the effect of finite temperature, but the magnetic field's influence on the proton fraction remains non-negligible at temperatures not higher than 10 MeV, particularly at low densities. The authors find that for temperatures around 50 MeV, the magnetic field effects on composition are almost completely washed out.

## Method

The authors model homogeneous neutron star matter in beta equilibrium using a relativistic mean field (RMF) approximation, comparing four different RMF models: NL3, NL3ωρ, EOS18 (all with non-linear meson terms), and SPG(M4) (with density-dependent nucleon-meson couplings). They compute the equation of state and particle fractions for warm magnetized matter by including Landau level quantization for charged particles (protons, electrons, muons) and Fermi-Dirac statistics for finite temperature, neglecting the anomalous magnetic moment of particles.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field values considered: B* = 10^3 (B = 4.4 × 10^16 G) and B* = 3 × 10^4 (B = 1.32 × 10^18 G), where B* = B/B^c_e with B^c_e = 4.414 × 10^13 G
- At zero temperature, the relative variation of energy per baryon due to magnetic field is less than 1% for B* = 10^3 and less than 10% above saturation density for B* = 3 × 10^4, but can reach 40-50% below saturation density
- The variation of pressure caused by T = 1 MeV is one order of magnitude larger than the variation caused by the highest magnetic field considered (B* = 3 × 10^4)
- At T = 1 MeV, magnetic fields below 10^17 G give a variation of the proton fraction of about 5-10% at half saturation density
- At T = 10 MeV, magnetic fields below 10^17 G give a variation of the proton fraction of less than 2% at half saturation density
- At T = 50 MeV, a 2% effect on the proton fraction requires magnetic fields B ≳ 10^18 G at densities below half saturation density
- The approximation from Raithel (2019) for finite temperature EoS has a maximum error smaller than 10% for T = 1 MeV at ρ ∼ 0.01 fm^-3, but errors exceed 10% below saturation density for T ≳ 10 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous studies of magnetized neutron star matter by Rabhi et al. (2011), who considered magnetic fields one order of magnitude stronger with fixed charge fraction rather than beta equilibrium, and by Fang et al. (2017), who found that for temperatures above ∼100 keV the magnetic field effect becomes negligible. The study also builds on zero-temperature magnetized matter calculations by Wang et al. (2022) and Scurto et al. (2023), and on warm non-magnetized matter studies by Tonetto et al. (2022). The results enable more accurate modeling of proto-neutron stars and binary neutron star merger remnants, where both finite temperature and strong magnetic fields are present, by clarifying when the magnetic field contribution can be safely neglected.
