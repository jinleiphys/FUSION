# Fusion and reactions of $α$+$^8$Be in the Hoyle resonance and associated resonances region

> The fusion of $α$ and $^8$Be to produce a $^{12}$C nucleus is a crucial process in nucleosynthesis. In the laboratory, this process can only be studied theoretically as a $^8$Be target or projectile cannot be prepared experimentally. We use the potential scattering theory in the coupled-channel formalism to study such a process in terms of the collision between the $α$ particle on a deformed $^8$Be nucleus, both on resonance and off resonance in the Hoyle resonance and associated resonances region. The experimental $^{12}$C energy levels and widths constrain the nuclear potential to suggest the need to include a parity-dependent surface potential component that is more attractive for even-$L$ positive-parity partial waves than for odd-$L$ negative-parity partial waves. As a consequence, the radial dependence of the total potentials for the set of \{0$^+$, 2$^+$, 4$^+$\} resonances of ${}^{12}$C exhibit a double-hump behavior, possessing two local energy minima and a doublet of each of the ${}^{12}$C \{0$^+$, 2$^+$, 4$^+$\} resonances in the Hoyle and associated resonances region. We examine the approximate agreement of the theoretical results with experiment and suggest the search for the as-yet unobserved lower-energy 2${}^+_2$ and 4${}_1^+$ resonances to test the double-hump potential description. In addition, for practical astrophysical applications, we evaluate and estimate the astrophysical $S(E_{\rm c.m.})$-factor for the $α$+$^8$Be $\to$ $^{12}$C$(0^{+*})$ $\to$ $^{12}$C$(2_1^+)$ + $γ$ reaction for $E_{\rm c.m.}$ $<$ 1.0 MeV.

## Key claim

The authors use potential scattering theory in a coupled-channel formalism to model the fusion of α and ⁸Be into ¹²C, constrained by experimental ¹²C energy levels and widths, and they find that a parity-dependent surface potential component is needed to reproduce the data. The abstract glosses over the fact that the theoretical results only show "approximate agreement" with experiment and that the existence of predicted lower-energy 2₂⁺ and 4₁⁺ resonances remains unconfirmed.

## Method

The authors performed coupled-channel scattering calculations using the FRESCO code, treating ⁸Be as a deformed prolate nucleus with deformation parameter β₂ = 0.6 and modeling the α+⁸Be interaction with an optical model potential that includes a parity-dependent surface term Vπ(L)(r). The potential parameters were iteratively adjusted to reproduce the experimental energies and α-decay widths of the Hoyle resonance (0₂⁺ at Ex = 7.648 MeV) and other low-lying natural-parity resonances of ¹²C up to Ex ≈ 15 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Hoyle resonance energy: Ec.m. = 0.281 MeV (Ex = 7.648 MeV relative to ¹²C ground state)
- α-width of the Hoyle resonance from the model: Γα ≈ Γζ = 8.0 eV, compared to the most recent measured value of 8.5 eV (Fre14)
- Potential well depth selected as starting point: Vo ≈ 11 MeV, chosen from a sequence of depths (4.4, 11, 22, 38, and 59 MeV)
- Deformation parameter for ⁸Be: β₂ = 0.6 (Abe94)
- Nuclear potential radius parameter: rv = 1.469 fm
- Imaginary potential strength: Wo = 1.5 eV
- Parity-dependent surface potential strength: Vπo = 2.5 MeV, with radius rπ = 2.26 fm and diffuseness aπ = 0.37 fm
- The s-wave reaction cross section at the Hoyle resonance was parametrized with Eζ = 0.2809338737 MeV and Γζ = 8.0 eV

## Context

This work extends the potential scattering approach to the α+⁸Be fusion reaction, which cannot be studied experimentally due to the short lifetime of ⁸Be. It complements structure studies using the adiabatic hyperspherical method with complex scaling by Jensen and collaborators (AR07, AR08) and the microscopic R-matrix calculations by Neff and Feldmeier (Fel14), while also building on earlier coupled-channel and CDCC calculations by Ogata et al. (Oga09) and Suno et al. (suno15). The results enable predictions of the astrophysical S(Ec.m.)-factor for the α+⁸Be → ¹²C(0⁺*) → ¹²C(2₁⁺) + γ reaction at Ec.m. < 1.0 MeV and suggest experimental searches for the predicted but unobserved lower-energy 2₂⁺ and 4₁⁺ resonances.
