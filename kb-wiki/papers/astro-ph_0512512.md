# Stable quark matter in cosmic rays?

> Stable lumps of quark matter may be present in cosmic rays at a flux level, which can be detected by high precision cosmic ray experiments sensitive to anomalous "nuclei" with high mass-to-charge ratio. The properties of these lumps, called strangelets, are described, and so is the production and propagation of strangelets in cosmic rays. Two experiments underway which are sensitive to a strangelet flux in the predicted range are briefly described. Finally it is summarized how strangelets circumvent the acceleration problem encountered by conventional candidates for ultra-high energy cosmic rays and move the Greisen-Zatsepin-Kuzmin cutoff to energies well above the observed maximum energies.

## Key claim

Stable lumps of strange quark matter (strangelets) may be present in cosmic rays at a flux level detectable by high-precision experiments, but the predicted flux depends on highly uncertain astrophysical parameters such as the galactic strange star collision rate, the mass released per collision, and the strangelet baryon number distribution, all of which are uncertain at the order-of-magnitude level.

## Method

The paper calculates the expected cosmic ray strangelet flux by solving a steady-state propagation equation that includes source terms from supernova acceleration, diffusion, escape from the Galaxy, energy losses (ionization, Coulomb, pion production), and spallation. The source term assumes strangelets are produced in binary strange star collisions with a canonical galactic mass injection rate of 10^{-10} solar masses per year, and are subsequently accelerated in supernova shocks to a rigidity powerlaw spectrum with index -2.2. The propagation model uses the leaky box approximation with escape times and spallation cross sections derived from studies of ordinary cosmic ray nuclei, scaled to strangelet properties.

## Key numbers

- Bulk strange quark matter is bound by 56 MeV per baryon relative to iron (930 MeV per baryon) for the MIT bag model parameters m_s = 150 MeV and B^{1/4} = 145 MeV.
- For those parameters, strangelets with baryon number A > 23 are stable relative to nuclei.
- The mass formula for strangelets is M(A) = 874 MeV A + 77 MeV A^{2/3} + 232 MeV A^{1/3}.
- The charge of non-color-flavor-locked strangelets is Z = 0.1 (m_s/150 MeV)^2 A for A << 700, and Z = 8 (m_s/150 MeV)^2 A^{1/3} for A >> 700.
- For color-flavor locked strangelets, Z = 0.3 (m_s/150 MeV) A^{2/3}.
- The galactic strange star collision rate is 83.0_{-66.1}^{+209.1} Myr^{-1} at 95% confidence, corresponding to one collision every 3,000 to 60,000 years.
- The canonical galactic strangelet mass production rate is 10^{-10} solar masses per year.
- The total strangelet flux at Earth or Moon is approximately F_total ≈ 2 × 10^5 m^{-2} yr^{-1} sterad^{-1} A^{-1.067} Z^{-0.6} Φ_{500}^{-0.6} Λ, where Λ is a scaling factor of order unity and Φ_{500} is the solar modulation potential in units of 500 MeV.
- For CFL strangelets with Z = 0.3 A^{2/3}, the flux becomes F_total ≈ 4 × 10^5 m^{-2} yr^{-1} sterad^{-1} A^{-1.467} Φ_{500}^{-0.6} Λ.
- The LSSS experiment expects to reach a sensitivity of 10^{-17} over a wide mass range using 15 grams of lunar soil from Apollo 11.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for strangelet production in binary strange star collisions originally developed by Madsen (2001) and provides a detailed propagation model that supersedes the numerical study of Medina-Tanco (1996). The predicted flux levels are consistent with upper limits from previous searches reviewed by Sandweiss (2004) and are high enough to motivate two ongoing experiments: the Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer (AMS-02) on the International Space Station, which can probe strangelets with charge up to Z ≈ 30, and the Lunar Soil Strangelet Search (LSSS) at Yale, which searches for Z = 8 strangelets in lunar soil. The paper also argues that strangelets could explain ultra-high energy cosmic rays beyond the Greisen-Zatsepin-Kuzmin cutoff, because their charge has no upper bound (allowing higher acceleration energies than nuclei) and their photo-disintegration threshold requires higher Lorentz factors than for ordinary nuclei.
