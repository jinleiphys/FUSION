# Bottom-up isotropization in classical-statistical lattice gauge theory

> We compute nonequilibrium dynamics for classical-statistical SU(2) pure gauge theory on a lattice. We consider anisotropic initial conditions with high occupation numbers in the transverse plane on a characteristic scale ~ Q_s. This is used to investigate the very early stages of the thermalization process in the context of heavy-ion collisions. We find Weibel or "primary" instabilities with growth rates similar to those obtained from previous treatments employing anisotropic distributions of hard modes (particles) in the weak coupling limit. We observe "secondary" growth rates for higher-momentum modes reaching substantially larger values and we analyse them in terms of resummed loop diagrams beyond the hard-loop approximation. We find that a coarse grained pressure isotropizes "bottom-up" with a characteristic inverse rate of gamma^{-1} ~ 1 - 2 fm/c for coarse graining momentum scales of p < 1 GeV choosing an initial energy density for RHIC of epsilon = 30 GeV/fm^3. The nonequilibrium spatial Wilson loop is found to exhibit an area law and to become isotropic on a similar time scale.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a coarse-grained pressure isotropizes "bottom-up" on a time scale of 1–2 fm/c for coarse-graining momentum scales below 1 GeV, but this result depends on choosing an initial energy density appropriate for RHIC (30 GeV/fm³) and applies only to the very early stages of thermalization where expansion can be neglected.

## Method

The authors performed classical-statistical simulations of SU(2) pure gauge theory on a lattice, solving the leapfrog-type equations of motion in temporal axial gauge. They initialized the system with anisotropic Gaussian distributions having high occupation numbers in the transverse plane on a characteristic scale Δ (identified with the saturation scale Qs) and a δ(pz)-like distribution in the longitudinal direction. Expectation values were obtained by Monte Carlo sampling over initial configurations and averaging the results from individual runs.

## Key numbers

- Inverse maximum primary growth rate for |A(t,p)|²: γ_max.pr.⁻¹ ≃ 4 ε^(−1/4), corresponding to 1.1 fm/c for ε = 30 GeV/fm³ and 2.7 fm/c for ε = 1 GeV/fm³
- Inverse maximum secondary growth rate for |A(t,p)|²: γ_max.sec.⁻¹ ≃ 1.3 ε^(−1/4), corresponding to 0.4 fm/c for ε = 30 GeV/fm³ and 0.8 fm/c for ε = 1 GeV/fm³
- Coarse-grained pressure isotropization inverse rate: γ⁻¹ ≃ 1–2 fm/c for coarse-graining momentum scales p ≲ 1 GeV with initial energy density ε = 30 GeV/fm³
- To obtain inverse growth rates of about 0.1 fm/c would require an energy density of ε = 300 TeV/fm³
- Primary growth rates were measured on 96³ and 128³ lattices using the same lattice spacing
- Secondary growth rates are larger than the highest primary rate by a factor of approximately three to four

## Context

This work extends previous studies of plasma instabilities in non-equilibrium gauge theories that relied on the hard-loop approximation (Mrowczynski 1988) or effective Wong-Yang-Mills equations, as well as earlier "bottom-up thermalization" scenarios (Baier et al. 2001) that assumed no plasma instabilities occur. The classical-statistical approach allows nonperturbative studies without requiring a clear separation between hard and soft scales, which is necessary for the high occupation numbers present in heavy-ion experiments. The results support the possibility that an isotropic coarse-grained pressure may be achieved faster than complete local thermal equilibrium, potentially explaining the success of hydrodynamic models at very early times.
