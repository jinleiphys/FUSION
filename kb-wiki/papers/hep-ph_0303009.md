# Quark Number Susceptibility, Thermodynamic Sum Rule, and Hard Thermal Loop Approximation

> The quark number susceptibility, associated with the conserved quark number density, is closely related to the baryon and charge fluctuations in the quark-gluon plasma, which might serve as signature for the quark-gluon plasma formation in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. Beside QCD lattice simulations, the quark number susceptibility has been calculated recently using a resummed perturbation theory (Hard Thermal Loop resummation). In the present work we show, based on general arguments, that the computation of this quantity neglecting Hard Thermal Loop vertices contradicts the Ward identity and violates the thermodynamic sum rule following from the quark number conservation. We further show that the Hard Thermal Loop perturbation theory is consistent with the thermodynamic sum rule.

# Quark Number Susceptibility, Thermodynamic Sum Rule, and Hard Thermal Loop Approximation

## Key claim

The computation of quark number susceptibility using Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) resummed propagators but neglecting HTL vertices (i.e., using only resummed propagators with bare vertices) violates the Ward identity and the thermodynamic sum rule following from quark number conservation; only the full HTL perturbation theory (HTLpt) that includes effective 3-point and 4-point vertices related to the propagator by Ward identities is consistent with the thermodynamic sum rule.

## Method

The authors derived the general expression for the vector meson self-energy at zero momentum using the spectral representation of the HTL-resummed quark propagator, including both quasiparticle poles (normal and plasmino branches) and Landau damping contributions. They then computed the quark number susceptibility from the thermodynamic sum rule, which relates the static susceptibility to the zero-momentum limit of the dynamical correlation function, and compared the results obtained with and without the inclusion of effective HTL vertices. The calculation used the HTL effective quark mass \( m_q = g(T)T/\sqrt{6} \) and a 2-loop running coupling constant with renormalization scale choices between \( 2\pi T \) and \( 4\pi T \).

## Key numbers

- The 1-loop HTLpt result (including effective vertices) agrees well with recent lattice data from Gavai et al. (2003), shown as a band in Fig. 2.
- The contribution of the plasmino branch to the total quark number susceptibility is less than 1 percent of the total.
- The leading order perturbative result is given as \( \chi_q/\chi_f = (1 - 2\alpha_s/\pi + \cdots) \).
- The calculation used the ratio \( T_c/\Lambda_{\overline{MS}} = 0.49 \) for the temperature scale.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of quark number susceptibility calculations beyond lattice simulations (Gottlieb et al., Gavai et al.) and perturbative QCD (Vuorinen, 2002) by examining the consistency of different HTL approaches. It directly addresses the discrepancy between the 1-loop HTLpt result of Chakraborty et al. (2002), which agrees with lattice data, and the 2-loop approximately self-consistent Phi-derivable HTL resummation of Rebhan et al. (2002), which overestimates lattice data. The authors argue that the neglect of HTL vertices in the Phi-derivable approach violates the Ward identity and thermodynamic sum rule, while HTLpt satisfies them. This work enables future 2-loop HTLpt calculations of the quark number susceptibility, which would be needed to correctly incorporate leading-order perturbative effects while maintaining consistency with conservation laws.
