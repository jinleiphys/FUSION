# Phenomenology of $n$-${\bar n}$ Oscillations Revisited

> We revisit the phenomenology of $n$-${\bar n}$ oscillations in the presence of external magnetic fields, highlighting the role of spin. We show, contrary to long-held belief, that the $n$-${\bar n}$ transition rate need not be suppressed, opening new opportunities for its empirical study.

## Key claim

Contrary to long-held belief, the neutron-antineutron ($n$-${\bar n}$) oscillation rate need not be suppressed by external magnetic fields, because a neutron and an antineutron of opposite spin projection have the same energy in a magnetic field, opening new experimental possibilities for empirical study.

## Method

The authors develop a $4\times 4$ effective Hamiltonian framework for $n$-${\bar n}$ oscillations that explicitly incorporates the spin degrees of freedom of both the neutron and antineutron, extending the traditional $2\times 2$ formalism. They impose constraints from Hermiticity, CPT invariance, and CP invariance to determine the general form of the mass matrix, then analyze concrete examples including static magnetic fields with transverse components and time-dependent oscillatory magnetic fields (magnetic resonance configurations).

## Key numbers

- Neutron magnetic moment magnitude: $|\mu_n| \approx 60$ neV/T
- Current limit on proton decay $p\to e^+ \pi^0$: partial half-life exceeds $8.2 \times 10^{33}$ years at 90% C.L.
- Current limit on $n$-${\bar n}$ oscillation lifetime: $\tau_{n {\bar n}} \ge 0.86 \times 10^{8}$ s at 90% C.L. (Baldo-Ceolin 1994)
- Estimated bottle experiment sensitivity: $\tau_{n {\bar n}} \sim 2\times 10^{8}$ s, using $N_{\rm fill}\sim 10^7$, $N_{\rm trial}\sim 10^5$, storage time $\langle t^2 \rangle^{1/2} \sim 400$ s, and ${\bar N} \le 2.3$ at 90% C.L.
- QCD scale used for dimensional analysis: $\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 200$ MeV

## Context

This work extends the seminal $2\times 2$ effective Hamiltonian framework for $n$-${\bar n}$ oscillations developed by Mohapatra (1980) and Cowsik (1980), which explicitly suppressed the role of spin. The authors show that the traditional conclusion that magnetic fields quench $n$-${\bar n}$ oscillations is incomplete, and that spin-dependent effects, analogous to those in magnetic resonance physics (Rabi 1937), can remove this quenching. This enables new experimental approaches such as studying $n$-${\bar n}$ oscillations in magnetic traps or bottles currently under development for neutron lifetime measurements (Leung 2008, Walstrom 2009, Salvat 2013).
