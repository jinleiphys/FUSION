# Report of the Topical Group on Neutrino Properties for Snowmass 2021

> Neutrinos are the most elusive among the known elementary particles, because of their feeble interactions with ordinary matter. They are also the most mysterious, because of their tiny masses that suggest a novel mass generating mechanism, their unknown Dirac or Majorana nature, and their big quantum mixing leading to large-amplitude flavor oscillations. This Topical Group focuses on neutrino properties that are not directly investigated in other Topical Groups of the Neutrino Frontier: in particular, the absolute value of the neutrino masses, the Dirac or Majorana nature of neutrinos, their electromagnetic properties, their lifetime, and hypothetical exotic properties.

# Report of the Topical Group on Neutrino Properties for Snowmass 2021

## Key claim
The absolute neutrino mass scale, the Dirac or Majorana nature of neutrinos, their electromagnetic properties, lifetime, and hypothetical exotic properties are the focus of this Topical Group, with the understanding that these properties are not directly investigated in other Topical Groups of the Neutrino Frontier. The paper presents a community consensus rather than new experimental results, and the claims about future sensitivity depend on successful completion of planned ton-scale experiments and continued research and development.

## Method
The paper is a community planning document (Snowmass 2021 white paper) that surveys the status and prospects of experimental and theoretical efforts on neutrino properties. It reviews direct kinematic neutrino mass measurements via tritium beta decay (KATRIN, Project 8) and electron capture in holmium-163 (ECHo, HOLMES), neutrinoless double beta decay searches across multiple isotopes and detection technologies, and studies of neutrino electromagnetic properties via neutrino-electron elastic scattering and coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CEvNS). The report synthesizes input from multiple white papers, letters of interest, and community discussions to identify priorities and synergies.

## Key numbers
- KATRIN has set the world's best direct limit on the neutrino mass scale: m_beta < 0.8 eV (90% C.L.) based on its first calendar year of operations in 2019
- The coming ton-scale generation of neutrinoless double beta decay experiments will probe effective Majorana neutrino masses m_bb as small as 18 meV in discovery mode
- Project 8 has inferred a preliminary CRES-based neutrino mass limit of m_beta < 185 eV (90% C.L.) from first tritium spectra
- The Q-value of holmium-163 electron capture is 2.83 keV, established via Penning-trap mass-difference measurements
- Tritium has a Q-value of 18.57 keV and a half-life of 12.3 years
- Holmium-163 has a Q-value of 2.833(34) keV and a half-life of 4570 years
- KATRIN's design sensitivity is 0.2 eV (90% C.L.)
- Project 8 has measured a background limit of < 3 x 10^{-10} eV^{-1}s^{-1} (90% C.L.) from cosmic ray delta electrons in Phase II

## Context
This report extends the work of the 2014 P5 report, which identified neutrino mass physics as a key Science Driver, and the 2015 Nuclear-Physics Long-Range Plan, which prioritized a US-led ton-scale neutrinoless double beta decay experiment. It builds on the discovery of neutrino oscillations by Super-Kamiokande (1998) and SNO (2002) that established neutrinos have mass, and on the experimental programs of KATRIN, Project 8, ECHo, and HOLMES for direct mass measurements. The report enables future planning for the next generation of experiments, including ton-scale neutrinoless double beta decay searches that will fully explore the inverted mass ordering parameter space, and next-generation tritium and holmium-163 experiments targeting sub-eV sensitivity. It also identifies synergies with cosmology (CF7), theory (NF08/TF11), collider searches for heavy neutral leptons (EF09), and instrumentation development (IF).
