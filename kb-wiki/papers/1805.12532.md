# $\boldsymbolη$-$\boldsymbol{^4{\rm He}}$ interaction from the $\boldsymbol{d d \to η^4{\rm He}}$ reaction near threshold

> We analyze the data on the total cross sections for the $dd \to η^4{\rm He}$ reaction close to threshold and look for possible $η^4 {\rm He}$ bound states. We develop a framework in which the $η^4{\rm He}$ optical potential is the key ingredient, rather than parameterizing the scattering matrix, as is usually done. The strength of this potential, together with some production parameters, are fitted to the available experimental data. The relationship of the scattering matrix to the optical potential is established using the Bethe-Salpeter equation and the $η^4{\rm He}$ loop function incorporates the range of the interaction given by the experimental $^4 {\rm He}$ density. However, when we look for poles of the scattering matrix, we get poles in the bound region, poles in the positive energy region or no poles at all. If we further restrict the results with constraints from a theoretical model with all its uncertainties the bound states are not allowed. However, we find a bump structure in $|T|^2$ of the $η^4{\rm He} \to η^4{\rm He}$ amplitude below threshold for the remaining solutions.

## Key claim

The analysis of $dd \to \eta^4{\rm He}$ total cross section data cannot confirm nor rule out the existence of $\eta^4{\rm He}$ bound states; when theoretical constraints from a model of the $N^*(1535)$ resonance in the nuclear medium are applied, the solutions that lead to bound states are rejected, but a broad bump structure in $|T|^2$ of the $\eta^4{\rm He} \to \eta^4{\rm He}$ amplitude persists below threshold for the remaining solutions.

## Method

The authors fit an $\eta^4{\rm He}$ optical potential $V$ (with real and imaginary parts) and a production parameter $A$ to 12 experimental data points on the $dd \to \eta^4{\rm He}$ total cross section up to an excess energy $Q = 8.5$ MeV. The $\eta^4{\rm He}$ scattering $T$-matrix is obtained from the potential via the Bethe-Salpeter equation, using a loop function $G$ that incorporates the $^4{\rm He}$ density via a Gaussian form factor with $\beta^2 = 12.1$ GeV$^{-2}$. Random Gaussian resampling of the data is used to generate distributions of fitted parameters, and the resulting potentials are then constrained by a theoretical model for the in-medium $\eta N$ amplitude based on the $N^*(1535)$ resonance, with all model parameters varied within their uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- Fitted optical potential centroid values: ${\rm Re}(V) = (-12.3 \pm 18.4) \times 10^{-2}$ MeV$^{-1}$, ${\rm Im}(V) = (-13.7 \pm 5.4) \times 10^{-2}$ MeV$^{-1}$.
- Derived $\eta N$ scattering length: $a'_{\eta N} = [-(0.39 \pm 0.19) - i(0.23 \pm 0.12)]$ fm.
- Derived $\eta^4{\rm He}$ scattering length: $a_{\eta^4{\rm He}} = [(2.11 \pm 1.07) - i(1.21 \pm 0.67)]$ fm.
- For the centroid potential, the $T$-matrix pole lies in the unbound region at $E = 10.0$ MeV with $\Gamma = 13.3$ MeV (energy measured from $\eta^4{\rm He}$ threshold).
- For ${\rm Re}(V) = -0.2$ MeV$^{-1}$, ${\rm Im}(V) = -0.1$ MeV$^{-1}$, a bound pole appears at $E = -0.4$ MeV with $\Gamma = 16.2$ MeV.
- For ${\rm Re}(V) = -0.24$ MeV$^{-1}$, ${\rm Im}(V) = -0.1$ MeV$^{-1}$, a bound pole appears at $E = -4.5$ MeV with $\Gamma = 19.2$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Xie (2016) on the $pd \to \eta^3{\rm He}$ reaction to the $dd \to \eta^4{\rm He}$ system, using an optical potential fitted to data rather than a parametrized scattering matrix. It parallels the phenomenological analysis of Ikeno (2017), who also used optical potentials for the $\eta^4{\rm He}$ interaction, and is constrained by the experimental search for $\eta^4{\rm He}$ bound states at WASA-at-COSY reported by Skurzok (2018), which excluded most bound-state predictions. The results show that the $dd \to \eta^4{\rm He}$ data alone cannot determine whether bound states exist, but when combined with theoretical constraints from the $N^*(1535)$ model of Chiang (1991), bound-state solutions are eliminated while a subthreshold bump in the scattering amplitude remains, motivating further experimental searches.
