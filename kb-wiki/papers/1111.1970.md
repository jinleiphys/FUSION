# The cluster-core model for halo-structure of light nuclei at the drip lines

> Nuclei at both the neutron- and proton-drip lines are studied. In the cluster-core model, the halo-structure of all the observed and proposed cases of neutron- or proton-halos is investigated in terms of simple potential energy surfaces calculated as the sum of binding energies, Coulomb repulsion, nuclear proximity attraction and the centrifugal potential for all the possible cluster+core configurations of a nucleus. The clusters of neutrons and protons are taken to be unbound, with additional Coulomb energy added for proton-clusters. The model predictions agree with the available experimental studies but show some differences with the nucleon separation energy hypothesis, particularly for proton-halo nuclei. Of particular interest are the halo-structures of $^{11}N$ and $^{20}Mg$. The calculated potential energy surfaces are also useful to identify the new magic numbers and molecular structures in exotic nuclei. In particular, N=6 is a possible new magic number for very neutron-deficient nuclei, but Z=N=2 and Z=8 seem to remain magic even for such nuclei, near the drip line.

# The cluster-core model for halo-structure of light nuclei at the drip lines

## Key claim

The cluster-core model (CCM), which calculates potential energy surfaces for all possible cluster+core configurations of a nucleus, can identify halo structures a priori for both neutron-rich and proton-rich light nuclei near the drip lines, but the model's predictions differ from the nucleon separation energy hypothesis in several cases, particularly for proton-halo nuclei. The authors note that for nuclei with neutron separation energies much larger than 1 MeV, such as $^{22}O$ and $^{24}F$, the halo designation may instead indicate collective soft dipole excitations, though this has not been realized experimentally.

## Method

The authors calculated potential energy surfaces (PES) for all possible cluster-core configurations of each nucleus as the sum of experimental binding energies, Coulomb repulsion, nuclear proximity attraction, and centrifugal potential, using touching configurations with R = R1 + R2. For neutron clusters, binding energies were taken as x times the one-neutron mass excess (8.0713 MeV), and for proton clusters, as x times the one-proton mass excess (7.2880 MeV) minus a Coulomb energy term (a_c = 0.7053 MeV), treating all nucleons in clusters as unbound. The nuclear radii R0 were taken from experimental or theoretical data for Z ≤ 10 and from theoretical estimates for Z > 10, with the neutron radius approximated as the same as the proton root-mean-square radius (0.8 fm).

## Key numbers

- Neutron separation energy threshold for halo designation: S1n or S2n less than 1 MeV (compared to 6-8 MeV for stable nuclei)
- One-neutron mass excess used for neutron-cluster binding: Δmn = 8.0713 MeV
- One-proton mass excess used for proton-cluster binding: Δmp = 7.2880 MeV
- Coulomb energy coefficient for proton clusters: ac = 0.7053 MeV
- Nuclear radius constant for touching configurations: R0 = 1.15 fm
- Proton root-mean-square radius used for neutron radius approximation: 0.8 fm
- $^{11}N$ is unstable against all proton-cluster decays (1p, 2p, and 3p), with Q > 0 for all three decay channels
- $^{20}Mg$ has both S1p and S2p much greater than 1 MeV, yet the CCM predicts a 2p-halo structure
- $^{8}He$ has both S1n and S2n much greater than 1 MeV, yet is a well-studied 2n-halo nucleus
- $^{12}Be$ and $^{27}F$ have S1n as the lowest neutron separation energy, but the PES shows the 2n+core configuration lying clearly lowest
- $^{29}F$ has S2n lowest compared to S1n and S3n, but the PES shows minima at 3n+ and 4n+core almost as deep as for 2n+core

## Context

This work extends the cluster-core model previously developed by Gupta et al. (1995, 2000) for neutron-rich light nuclei to include proton-rich nuclei, adding Coulomb energy for proton clusters. The model contrasts with the nucleon separation energy hypothesis (where S1n, S2n, S1p, or S2p less than 1 MeV indicates a halo) and with few-body theoretical calculations by Thompson (1999) and others. The CCM predictions for $^{20}Mg$ disagree with the 4p+$^{16}O$ configuration proposed by Chulkov (1996) based on Coulomb displacement energies, instead supporting the 2p-halo configuration found in relativistic mean-field calculations by Ren (1996). The identification of N=6 as a possible new magic number for very neutron-deficient nuclei near the drip line, while N=8 remains magic for nuclei not too far from beta-stability, opens questions for further investigation using the CCM and other methods such as those of Ozawa (2000) and Otsuka (2001).
