# Form factor and model dependence in neutrino-nucleus cross section predictions

> To achieve its design goals, the next generation of neutrino-oscillation accelerator experiments requires percent-level predictions of neutrino-nucleus cross sections supplemented by robust estimates of the theoretical uncertainties involved. The latter arise from both approximations in solving the nuclear many-body problem and in the determination of the single- and few-nucleon quantities taken as input by many-body methods. To quantify both types of uncertainty, we compute flux-averaged double-differential cross sections using the Green's function Monte Carlo and spectral function methods as well as different parameterizations of the nucleon axial form factors based on either deuterium bubble-chamber data or lattice quantum chromodynamics calculations. The cross-section results are compared with available experimental data from the MiniBooNE and T2K collaborations. We also discuss the uncertainties associated with $N\rightarrow Δ$ transition form factors that enter the two-body current operator. We quantify the relations between neutrino-nucleus cross section and nucleon form factor uncertainties. These relations enable us to determine the form factor precision targets required to achieve a given cross-section precision.

## Key claim

The paper quantifies both nuclear-model uncertainties (by comparing Green's Function Monte Carlo and spectral function methods) and nucleon axial form factor uncertainties (by comparing parameterizations based on deuterium bubble-chamber data versus lattice QCD calculations) in neutrino-nucleus cross section predictions, finding that the choice of axial form factor parameterization can significantly impact cross section results even when sophisticated many-body treatments are used.

## Method

The authors computed flux-averaged double-differential charged-current neutrino-nucleus cross sections for carbon-12 using two complementary many-body approaches: the non-relativistic Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC) method with the Argonne v18 nucleon-nucleon potential and Illinois-7 three-nucleon force, and the relativistic spectral function (SF) method based on variational Monte Carlo overlaps. They incorporated different parameterizations of the nucleon axial form factor, including a dipole parameterization with axial mass M_A ~ 1 GeV from deuterium bubble-chamber data and parameterizations from lattice QCD calculations, as well as a model-independent z-expansion. The two-body current operator included contributions from pion exchange, seagull, pion-pole, and Delta-isobar excitation mechanisms, with the Delta propagator including an energy-dependent decay width and medium modifications.

## Key numbers

- GFMC response functions were calculated for momentum transfers in the range 100-700 MeV in steps of 100 MeV, with extrapolation to |q| > 700 MeV using scaling features
- Fermi momentum for carbon-12 taken as k_F = 225 MeV
- For the Delta transition form factor C_5^A, the parameterization uses M_{AΔ} = 1.05 GeV
- The Delta decay width uses (4 f_{πNΔ})^2/(4π) = 0.38
- Cutoff parameters for the Delta current: Λ_{πNΔ} = 1150 MeV and Λ_π = 1300 MeV
- The vector form factor C_3^V uses M_V = 0.84 GeV
- Nuclear saturation density ρ_0 = 0.16 fm^3 used for medium modifications of the Delta propagator

## Context

This work extends the comparison between GFMC and spectral function methods previously applied to electron-nucleus scattering (Rocco 2018, 2019) to the neutrino sector, and builds on earlier GFMC results for neutrino-nucleus cross sections (Lovato 2020). It directly addresses the long-standing discrepancy where MiniBooNE data required M_A ~ 1.2 GeV in relativistic Fermi Gas models while deuterium data gave M_A ~ 1 GeV, by showing that sophisticated many-body treatments with two-body currents can accommodate the lower M_A value. The results enable quantitative precision targets for future lattice QCD calculations of nucleon axial form factors, connecting to the lattice QCD community's efforts (Park 2021, Djukanovic 2022) and providing input for event generators like GENIE used in oscillation analyses.
