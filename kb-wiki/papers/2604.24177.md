# Selected Topics in Quark-Hadron Physics: From Scalar Nonets to Topological Glueballs

> This contribution reviews recent progress in the low-lying scalar mesons and glueballs. We propose a new classification for the scalar nonet that includes $f_0(980)$ and $a_0(980)$ as the lowest states, while we identify $f_0(1500)$ as a primary glueball candidate. We demonstrate that the production yields of these states in heavy-ion collisions are mutually consistent across statistical, coalescence, and S-matrix frameworks. To investigate their internal structure, we move beyond standard phenomenology by describing glueballs as topological solitons. This approach yields an energy spectrum in excellent agreement with lattice QCD and experimental data, while interpreting $f_0(2470)$ as a tightly bound glueballonium to explain its anomalously long lifetime. This non-perturbative framework provides a predictive basis for the future experimental verification of exotic scalar states.

# Selected Topics in Quark-Hadron Physics: From Scalar Nonets to Topological Glueballs

## Key claim
The paper proposes a new classification for the scalar nonet that excludes broad resonances like f0(500) and K*0(700), instead placing f0(980) and a0(980) as the lowest states, while identifying f0(1500) as a primary glueball candidate. The authors claim that production yields of these states in heavy-ion collisions are mutually consistent across statistical, coalescence, and S-matrix frameworks, but this consistency depends on assuming an S-wave gg (two-gluon) structure for f0(1500) rather than P-wave n-nbar or S-wave tetraquark configurations.

## Method
The authors employed the statistical model to calculate hadron yields at hadronization temperature TH = 162 (156) MeV for RHIC (LHC) parameters, then applied the quark coalescence model using harmonic oscillator wave functions from a non-relativistic Schrodinger equation to account for internal structure. They further validated results using the S-matrix formulation where the energy derivative of scattering phase shifts serves as an effective density of states. For the internal structure of glueballs, they used the Skyrme-Faddeev model to describe glueballs as topological solitons (Hopfions) with topological charge Q, quantizing these Hopfions as rigid bodies to construct energy spectra.

## Key numbers
- Hadronization temperature TH = 162 MeV for RHIC and 156 MeV for LHC, with volume VH = 2100 fm3 (RHIC) and 5380 fm3 (LHC), baryon chemical potential μB = 24 MeV (RHIC) and 0 MeV (LHC), strangeness chemical potential μS = 10 MeV (RHIC) and 0 MeV (LHC)
- Calculated yields for the proposed scalar nonet mesons fall predominantly within the range of 10-1 to 100
- By identifying the Q=1 Hopfion with f0(1500), the coupling constant κ/e = 5.54 MeV reproduces the mass of f0(2470) via the Q=2 configuration
- The model predicts a currently unobserved Q=2 state at 2814 MeV
- Incorporating quantum corrections, κe3 = 88.5 GeV by identifying the lowest Q=1 state with the tensor glueball candidate fJ(2220)
- The f0(2470) is identified as a tightly bound Q=2 state with binding energy of 577 MeV, while the predicted state at 2814 MeV has binding energy of 230 MeV
- If f0(1710) is adopted as the primary glueball candidate instead of f0(1500), the coupling constant becomes κ/e = 6.31 MeV

## Context
This work extends the long-standing debate on scalar meson classification dating back to Jaffe (1976) on tetraquark models and the puzzle of the mass hierarchy mf0(500) < mK*0(700) < mf0(980) ≃ ma0(980) that contradicts naive quark model predictions. The production yield analysis builds on the statistical model framework of Andronic (2005) and the coalescence model methodology from ExHIC (2010, 2011, 2017). The topological soliton approach for glueballs extends the Skyrme-Faddeev model developed by Faddeev (1996, 1998) and the Hopfion description from Amari (2025), providing a framework that can be tested against lattice QCD results from Morningstar (1999) and future experimental searches at RHIC and LHC for the predicted glueballonium states.
