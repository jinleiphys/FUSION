# Experimental signals for broken axial symmetry in excited heavy nuclei from the valley of stability

> An increasing number of experimental data indicates the breaking of axial symmetry in many heavy nuclei already in the valley of stability: Multiple Coulomb excitation analysed in a rotation invariant way, gamma transition rates and energies in odd nuclei, mass predictions, the splitting of Giant Resonances (GR), the collective enhancement of nuclear level densities and Maxwellian averaged neutron capture cross sections. For the interpretation of these experimental observations the axial symmetry breaking shows up in nearly all heavy nuclei as predicted by Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations [1] ; this indicates a nuclear Jahn-Teller effect. We show that nearly no parameters remain free to be adjusted by separate fitting to level density or giant resonance data, if advance information on nuclear deformations, radii etc. are taken from such calculations with the force parameters already fixed. The data analysis and interpretation have to include the quantum mechanical requirement of zero point oscillations and the distinction between static vs. dynamic symmetry breaking has to be regarded.

# Experimental signals for broken axial symmetry in excited heavy nuclei from the valley of stability

## Key claim

Nearly all heavy nuclei in the valley of stability exhibit broken axial symmetry (triaxiality), as predicted by Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations, but the distinction between static and dynamic symmetry breaking must be regarded, and zero-point oscillations must be included in data analysis; the authors show that nearly no free parameters remain for separate fitting to level density or giant resonance data if advance information on nuclear deformations and radii is taken from such calculations with force parameters already fixed.

## Method

The authors performed a global survey of experimental observables, including multiple Coulomb excitation data analyzed via rotation invariants, gamma transition rates and energies in odd nuclei, ground-state mass predictions, giant resonance splitting, collective enhancement of nuclear level densities, and Maxwellian-averaged neutron capture cross sections, and compared them to predictions from triaxial HFB calculations using the Gogny D1S interaction (Delaroche et al., 2010). For the giant dipole resonance, a triple Lorentzian (TLO) procedure was applied with pole energies from the HFB calculations and a single global width parameter, while level densities were computed assuming only R_pi symmetry (rotation by 180 degrees) rather than full axial symmetry.

## Key numbers

- For isotopes 148-154Sm, the predicted beta-deformation values from HFB calculations were reduced by 40%, 30%, 20%, and 10% respectively, using a correction factor of 0.4 + delta/20 for nuclei within delta <= 10 nucleons of a shell closure.
- For 204Pb and 206Pb, Coulomb reorientation measurements yielded gamma values of 43(8) degrees and 33(6) degrees, indicating static triaxiality with a tendency toward oblateness.
- The triple Lorentzian fit to the isovector giant dipole resonance used a single global width parameter c_w = 0.045(3) for the relation Gamma_i = c_w * E_i^1.6, valid for all 23 nuclei examined across a wide mass range.
- The central resonance energy E_0 was fixed via the liquid drop model with an adjusted effective nucleon mass m_eff c^2 = 800 MeV.
- Maxwellian-averaged neutron capture cross sections at <E_n> = 30 keV were predicted for more than 100 spin-0 target nuclei with A > 50, showing good agreement with compiled experimental data.

## Context

This work extends the triaxial HFB calculations of Delaroche et al. (2010) and the rotation-invariant Coulomb excitation analyses of the Rochester-Warsaw collaboration (Cline, 1986; Wu et al., 1995, 1996) to a broader range of observables including level densities and giant resonances. It contradicts the common assumption of axial symmetry in heavy nuclei, which has been used in many textbook treatments (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975) and in the Finite-Range Liquid-Drop Model (Moller et al., 2008). The results enable parameter-free predictions of neutron capture cross sections for nuclear astrophysics and advanced nuclear systems, and suggest that the nuclear Jahn-Teller effect (Jahn and Teller, 1937; Reinhard and Otten, 1984) may be responsible for symmetry breaking in heavy nuclei.
