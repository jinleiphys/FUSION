# Indication of a Differential Freeze-out in Proton-Proton and Heavy-Ion Collisions at RHIC and LHC energies

> The experimental data from the RHIC and LHC experiments of invariant pT spectra in A+A and p + p collisions are analysed with Tsallis distributions in different approaches. The information about the freeze-out surface in terms of freeze-out volume, temperature, chemical potential and radial flow velocity for different particle species are obtained. Further, these parameters are studied as a function of the mass of the secondary particles. A mass-dependent differential freeze-out is observed which does not seem to distinguish between particles and their antiparticles. Further a mass-hierarchy in the radial flow is observed, meaning heavier particles suffer lower radial flow. Tsallis distribution function at finite chemical potential is used to study the mass dependence of chemical potential. The peripheral heavy-ion and proton-proton collisions at the same energies seem to be equivalent in terms of the extracted thermodynamic parameters.

## Key claim

The paper claims a mass-dependent differential freeze-out in both proton-proton and peripheral heavy-ion collisions, meaning that different particle species (pions, kaons, protons) freeze out at different temperatures and volumes depending on their mass, but this effect does not distinguish between particles and their antiparticles. The authors hedge that the extracted thermodynamic parameters from peripheral A+A collisions and p+p collisions at the same center-of-mass energy appear equivalent, but note that the volume parameters from the simplest Tsallis fit (without radial flow) are "unphysical" for p+p collisions, being larger than for peripheral A+A collisions.

## Method

The authors fit three forms of the Tsallis non-extensive distribution function to experimental invariant transverse momentum (pT) spectra of pions, kaons, and protons (and their antiparticles) from RHIC Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV and LHC Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV, as well as p+p collisions at both energies. The three forms are: (1) a Tsallis distribution without radial flow and with chemical potential set to zero, (2) a Tsallis distribution with finite chemical potential, and (3) a Tsallis distribution including radial flow velocity via an analytic expansion to first order in (q-1). Fits are performed for pT up to 3 GeV/c for A+A and 2.5 GeV/c for p+p collisions, extracting freeze-out volume, temperature, chemical potential, radial flow velocity, and the non-extensive parameter q for each particle species.

## Key numbers

- For most peripheral Au+Au at 200 GeV, the freeze-out volume for pions is 2242.35 ± 18.23 fm^3, for kaons 223.358 ± 0.010 fm^3, and for protons 45.62 ± 2.45 fm^3 (from the Tsallis fit without radial flow or chemical potential).
- For most peripheral Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV, the freeze-out temperature for pions is 0.082 ± 0.001 GeV, for kaons 0.133 ± 0.003 GeV, and for protons 0.238 ± 0.012 GeV (from the same fit).
- The radial flow velocity extracted from the Tsallis fit with radial flow for most peripheral Au+Au at 200 GeV is 0.475 ± 0.013 for pions, 0.363 ± 0.008 for kaons, and 0.147 ± 0.018 for protons.
- For p+p collisions at 2.76 TeV, the radial flow velocity is 0.601 ± 0.013 for pions, 0.433 ± 0.016 for kaons, and 0.310 ± 0.014 for protons.
- The non-extensive parameter q for most peripheral Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV is 1.156 ± 0.002 for pions, 1.120 ± 0.004 for kaons, and 1.052 ± 0.009 for protons (from the fit without radial flow).
- The chemical potential extracted for protons in most peripheral Au+Au at 200 GeV is 0.310 ± 0.190 GeV, and for antiprotons is 0.437 ± 0.103 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the use of Tsallis non-extensive statistics to study freeze-out conditions in high-energy collisions, building on prior studies of particle spectra using Tsallis distributions by Cleymans (2011) and Bhattacharyya (2015). The results contradict a single freeze-out scenario and instead support a mass-dependent differential freeze-out, consistent with earlier findings by Wei (2015) using a different approach. The observed mass hierarchy in radial flow (heavier particles having lower radial flow) aligns with hydrodynamic descriptions of fireball evolution, and the equivalence of thermodynamic parameters between peripheral A+A and p+p collisions suggests a common thermodynamic nature, enabling future comparisons of small and large collision systems.
