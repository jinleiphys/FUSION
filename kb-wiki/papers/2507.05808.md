# Neural Unfolding of the Chiral Magnetic Effect in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The search for the chiral magnetic effect (CME) in relativistic heavy-ion collisions (HICs) is challenged by significant background contamination. We present a novel deep learning approach based on a U-Net architecture to time-reversely unfold the dynamics of CME-related charge separation, enabling the reconstruction of the physics signal across the entire evolution of HICs. Trained on the events simulated by a multi-phase transport model with different cases of CME settings, our model learns to recover the charge separation based on final-state transverse momentum distributions at either the quark-gloun plasma freeze-out or hadronic freeze-out. This devises a methodological tool for the study of CME and underscores the promise of deep learning approaches in retrieving physics signals in HICs.

# Neural Unfolding of the Chiral Magnetic Effect in Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a U-Net deep learning architecture can time-reversely reconstruct the charge separation signal of the chiral magnetic effect from final-state transverse momentum distributions, but only for simulated AMPT events with artificially implanted charge separation; the method's accuracy depends on the input stage (parton freeze-out vs. hadron freeze-out) and degrades for event-by-event predictions at early times due to error accumulation.

## Method

The authors modified the AMPT (a multi-phase transport) model to implement a global charge separation scheme by swapping a fraction of transverse momentum components between upward- and downward-moving quarks of opposite charge, simulating CME-like initial conditions. They trained a U-Net with time embeddings on 5,000 simulated Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV (impact parameter 8 fm) to predict the difference between transverse momentum distributions at adjacent time steps, then iteratively reconstructed earlier distributions by adding predicted differences to later-stage inputs. Three cases were tested: input from parton distributions at QGP freeze-out with fixed 10% charge separation, input from final-state hadron distributions with fixed 10% separation, and input from hadron distributions with mixed separation strengths from 1% to 10%.

## Key numbers

- Initial charge separation fraction of 10% was reduced to approximately 3% at QGP freeze-out due to partonic interactions (cross section 3 mb)
- Prediction accuracy for event-averaged charge separation exceeded 96% at each time step when using parton distributions at QGP freeze-out as input (Case 1)
- Prediction accuracy reached over 97% at the initial QGP stage when using final-state hadron distributions with fixed 10% separation (Case 2)
- Minimum accuracy of 87% at the earliest time step when using final-state hadron distributions with mixed separation strengths from 1% to 10% (Case 3)
- Dataset consisted of 5,000 training events and 10,000 testing events for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV with impact parameter b = 8 fm
- Transverse momentum distributions were represented as 16 x 16 pixel images with kinematic cuts: pseudorapidity |eta| < 1.0, transverse momentum 0.15 < p_T < 2 GeV/c for hadrons and 0.075 < p_T < 1 GeV/c for partons
- Partonic interactions with cross section sigma = 3 mb suppressed an initial charge separation of 10% down to 1-2% in final-state hadrons

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to detect the chiral magnetic effect in heavy-ion collisions, which have been hampered by background contamination from elliptic flow and nuclear structure differences (as seen in the RHIC isobar collision program). The approach builds on a previous supervised learning method using convolutional neural networks (CME meter) that identified charge separation in final-state pion spectra but could not track signal evolution. The authors propose that integrating their method with chiral kinetic theory models (such as the Chiral Anomaly Transport model) and PointNet architectures could enable direct connection of unfolded early-time charge separation to its dynamical origin in future work.
