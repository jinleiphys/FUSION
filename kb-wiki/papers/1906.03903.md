# Neutron production induced by $α$-decay with Geant4

> Neutron production induced by ($α$,n) reactions is important in various scenarios. One of the most relevant ones is related to underground dark matter experiments, where the neutrons produced by the $α$-decay from radioactive contaminants in the detector materials can generate an irreducible background, limiting the sensitivity of the experiment. A precise estimate of the background due to these neutrons is crucial for the experiments currently taking data and for the design of the next generation detectors. In this work, we prove that Geant4 can be used to calculate neutron yields and energy spectra induced by $α$-decay. These neutrons are produced according to the information compiled in data libraries originally written in the ENDF-6 format. In this article we also review the different databases available, showing the differences and similarities among them. Finally, we compare several Geant4 neutron production yields and spectra with experimental data and other codes.

## Key claim

Geant4, using the ParticleHP module and ENDF-6 format data libraries, can calculate neutron yields and energy spectra from (α,n) reactions induced by α-decay, but the accuracy depends strongly on which nuclear data library is used; the JENDL/AN-2005 library reproduces experimental yields within 10% in most cases, while TENDL-based libraries (including Geant4 with TENDL-2017) systematically overestimate yields by about 40% on average.

## Method

The authors built a Geant4 application that transports α particles through a material volume using electromagnetic processes from the G4EmStandardPhysics_option4 package (stopping powers from ICRU 49 below 8 MeV and Bethe-Bloch above) and nuclear reactions from the G4ParticleHP package using ENDF-6 format incident-α data libraries. Neutron yields were obtained by tallying secondary neutrons produced during α transport, using a generic biasing scheme with a bias factor FB to enhance nuclear cross sections and reduce computation time, with statistical weights assigned to secondary neutrons to correct for the enhancement. The results were verified by comparing the tallied neutron yield Y with the yield Yr calculated from the convolution of the α particle flux with the neutron production cross section, and by comparing neutron energy spectra at fixed α energies with the original ENDF-6 data.

## Key numbers

- For 10 MeV α particles in 13C using JENDL/AN-2005 with bias factor FB = 103, the ratio Y/Yr = 0.997(1) with a maximum allowed step length of 10-4 mm.
- For 10 MeV α particles in 13C using JENDL/AN-2005 with FB = 104, the yield Y = 109.2 neutrons per 107 initial α particles with 0.041% uncertainty.
- For 10 MeV α particles in 14N using JENDL/AN-2005 with FB = 103 and Smax = 10-4 mm, Y/Yr = 0.995(2).
- For the 232Th decay series in Be, the measured yield is 117.7(20) neutrons per 106 α-decays; Geant4 with JENDL/AN-2005 gives 120, Geant4 with TENDL-2017 gives 132, and NeuCBOT gives 137.
- For the 232Th decay series in C, the measured yield is 0.2305(32) neutrons per 106 α-decays; Geant4 with JENDL/AN-2005 gives 0.232, Geant4 with TENDL-2017 gives 0.389, and NeuCBOT gives 0.364.
- For the 232Th decay series in Si, the measured yield is 0.4336(52) neutrons per 106 α-decays; Geant4 with JENDL/AN-2005 gives 0.424, Geant4 with TENDL-2017 gives 0.507, and NeuCBOT gives 0.574.
- The 15N(α,Xn) cross section from TENDL libraries is two orders of magnitude larger than from JENDL/AN-2005.

## Context

This work extends comparisons between (α,n) yield codes performed by Fernandes et al. (2014) and by Westerdale and Meyers (2017), adding Geant4 results to the existing codes NeuCBOT, SOURCES, NEDIS, and the USD code. The study enables the use of Geant4 for calculating neutron backgrounds in underground dark matter experiments such as DarkSide-50 and DarkSide-20k, where neutrons from α-decay of uranium and thorium chain contaminants produce an irreducible background that limits WIMP search sensitivity. The paper also identifies that the JENDL/AN-2005 library is the only publicly available evaluated ENDF-6 format library for incident α particles, while TENDL libraries (TENDL-2014, TENDL-2015, TENDL-2017) are based on TALYS code calculations and show systematic discrepancies with experimental data.
