# Effective boost and "point-form" approach

> Triangle Feynman diagrams can be considered as describing form factors of states bound by a zero-range interaction. These form factors are calculated for scalar particles and compared to point-form and non-relativistic results. By examining the expressions of the complete calculation in different frames, we obtain an effective boost transformation which can be compared to the relativistic kinematical one underlying the present point-form calculations, as well as to the Galilean boost. The analytic expressions obtained in this simple model allow a qualitative check of certain results obtained in similar studies. In particular, a mismatch is pointed out between recent practical applications of the point-form approach and the one originally proposed by Dirac.

## Key claim

The point-form approach to calculating form factors, as recently applied to systems like the deuteron and nucleon, fails to reproduce exact results from Feynman diagrams because it describes initial and final states on two different quantization surfaces, leading to an effective boost that is closer to the non-relativistic Galilean boost than to the relativistic kinematical one.

## Method

The authors calculate elastic form factors for a bound state of two scalar particles with equal mass \(m\) interacting via a zero-range interaction, using the exact triangle Feynman diagram (equivalent to a Bethe-Salpeter calculation). They compare these exact results to those obtained from the point-form approach and from a non-relativistic calculation, for both a scalar probe (\(F_0\)) and a vector (charge) probe (\(F_1\)), at two total masses \(M = 1.6\,m\) and \(M = 0.1\,m\), over a range of momentum transfers \(Q^2/m^2\) from 0.01 to 100.

## Key numbers

- For \(M = 1.6\,m\) at \(Q^2/m^2 = 100\), the exact vector form factor \(F_1\) is 0.191, the point-form result is 0.082, and the non-relativistic result is 0.210.
- For \(M = 0.1\,m\) at \(Q^2/m^2 = 100\), the exact vector form factor \(F_1\) is 0.315, the point-form result is 0.00078, and the non-relativistic result is 0.465.
- For \(M = 0.1\,m\) at \(Q^2/m^2 = 0.01\), the exact scalar form factor \(F_0\) is 1.498, the point-form result is 0.015, and the non-relativistic result is 0.999.
- The point-form boost transformation includes a factor \(2\sqrt{m^2+\vec{k}^2}/M\) multiplying \(\vec{P}/2\) in the spectator momentum transformation, which is absent in the exact calculation and in the non-relativistic (Galilean) boost.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Desplanques (2000) on the Wick-Cutkosky model (Wick 1954, Cutkosky 1954) to a simpler zero-range interaction model, confirming that point-form calculations of form factors (as applied to the deuteron by Klink (1998) and to the nucleon by Wagenbrunn (1999)) fail to reproduce exact results, especially for scalar probes and at large \(Q^2\) or small total mass \(M\). The authors show that the effective boost in the exact calculation is closer to the Galilean boost than to the relativistic kinematical boost used in point-form calculations, and they identify the mismatch as arising from describing initial and final states on different quantization surfaces. This enables future work to remedy the failure by adding two-body currents or by choosing a quantization surface \(\lambda^\mu = (P_i+P_f)^\mu/\sqrt{(P_i+P_f)^2}\) that is symmetric between initial and final states.
