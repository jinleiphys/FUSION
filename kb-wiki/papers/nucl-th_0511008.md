# Correlation Functions: Getting into Shape

> The ability to measure characteristics of source shapes using non-identical particle correlations is discussed. Both strong-interaction induced and Coulomb induced correlations are shown to provide sensitivity to source shapes. By decomposing correlation functions with spherical or Cartesian harmonics, details of the shapes can be especially well isolated.

# Correlation Functions: Getting into Shape

## Key claim
Non-identical particle correlations, driven by both Coulomb and strong interactions, can be used to extract detailed shape information about the source function in heavy-ion collisions, provided the correlation functions are decomposed using spherical or Cartesian harmonics to isolate specific multipole moments.

## Method
The author derives the relationship between the two-particle correlation function R(q) and the source function S(r) via the squared relative wave function |φ(q,r)|², treating the problem as an inversion. Classical and quantum Coulomb wave functions are compared for their sensitivity to source shapes, and the formalism is extended using spherical harmonic and Cartesian harmonic expansions (Eqs. 1-4) to decompose both the correlation function and source function into multipole coefficients that are related one-to-one by kernel functions K_ℓ(q,r).

## Key numbers
- For ππ correlations, the Bohr radius a₀ = 1/μe² = 390 fm is much larger than typical source sizes, making Coulomb effects a simple Gamow factor with no shape information.
- Proton-kaon correlation functions for a 4×4×8 fm Gaussian source show shape sensitivity near Q = 2q ≈ 100 MeV/c.
- In the classical Coulomb limit, the correlation function approaches unity at large q as 1 − ⟨1/r⟩ μe²/q².
- The blast-wave model used for pπ correlations had temperature 120 MeV, radius 13 fm, and collective velocity 0.7c.

## Context
This work extends the angle-integrated correlation imaging techniques of Brown and Danielewicz (2000), Panitkin (2000), and Verde (2002) to higher multipole moments, enabling three-dimensional source shape reconstruction. The Cartesian harmonic formalism builds on the work of Danielewicz (2004) and Applequist (1989). The paper enables systematic studies of source shapes for many particle species at RHIC, including Σπ correlations, to test thermalization and collective flow models through mass-dependent trends in source sizes and shapes.
