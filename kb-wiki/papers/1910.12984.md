# Convenient location of a near-threshold proton-emitting resonance in $^{11}$B

> The presence of cluster-like narrow resonances in the vicinity of reaction/decay thresholds is a ubiquitous phenomenon with profound consequences. We argue that the continuum coupling, present in the open quantum system description of the atomic nucleus, can profoundly impact the nature of near-threshold states. In this Letter, we discuss the structure of the recently observed near-threshold resonance in $^{11}$B, whose very existence explains the puzzling beta-delayed proton emission of the neutron-rich $^{11}$Be.

# Convenient location of a near-threshold proton-emitting resonance in $^{11}$B

## Key claim

The narrow proton-emitting resonance in $^{11}$B at 11.425 MeV, whose existence explains the puzzling beta-delayed proton emission of the neutron-rich $^{11}$Be, is not accidental but arises from continuum coupling effects that produce a collective "aligned eigenstate" near the proton emission threshold; the authors argue that this state is most likely $J^\pi = 1/2^+$ rather than $3/2^+$, based on shell model embedded in the continuum (SMEC) calculations.

## Method

The authors employed the shell model embedded in the continuum (SMEC), dividing the Hilbert space into subspaces containing 0 and 1 particle in the scattering continuum, and constructed an energy-dependent effective Hamiltonian that includes continuum coupling terms. Calculations were performed for $J^\pi = 1/2^+$ and $3/2^+$ states in $^{11}$B using the WBP$-$ interaction in the full $psd$ model space, with Woods-Saxon potentials for radial wave functions, coupling to one-proton channels [$^{10}$Be($0^+$) $\otimes p(s_{1/2})$] and [$^{10}$Be($0^+$) $\otimes p(d_{3/2})$] and one-neutron channels [$^{10}$B($3^+$) $\otimes n(d_{5/2})$]. The continuum-coupling correlation energy was computed as a function of proton or neutron energy to identify the energy at which the strongest collectivization occurs.

## Key numbers

- The observed resonance in $^{11}$B has energy $E = 11.425(20)$ MeV, with proton width $\Gamma_p = 12(5)$ keV, located 197(20) keV above the one-proton emission threshold and 29(20) keV below the one-neutron emission threshold.
- For the $1/2^+_3$ SMEC eigenstate, the strongest collectivization is predicted at $E_p^* \simeq 142$ keV, close to the experimental resonance energy.
- Changing the Woods-Saxon radius $R_0$ by 10% modifies $E_p^*$ by approximately 6 keV.
- For the $3/2^+_3$ eigenstate, the minimum of continuum-coupling correlation energy occurs at $E_p^* \simeq 860$ keV, approximately 630 keV above the one-neutron emission threshold.
- For the $3/2^+_4$ eigenstate, the minimum is seen at $E_p^* \simeq 1300$ keV.
- The $5/2^+$ resonance at $E = 11.600(20)$ MeV has a very small neutron decay width $\Gamma_n \approx 4$ keV, and the SMEC calculation predicts the minimum of correlation energy at $E_n^* = 113$ keV for the $5/2^+_6$ eigenstate.
- Three exceptional points exist in the energy interval between 88 and 92 keV for the $1/2^+_3$ state.

## Context

This work extends the concept of near-threshold collectivity previously developed by Okolowicz et al. (2012, 2013) using the SMEC framework, which posits that continuum coupling near branch point singularities at particle emission thresholds induces collective mixing of shell-model eigenstates into a single "aligned eigenstate." The paper explains the experimental findings of Ayyad et al. (2019), who discovered the 11.425 MeV resonance in $^{11}$B and measured its unexpectedly high beta-delayed proton decay strength from $^{11}$Be, and supports the interpretation of Riisager et al. (2014) that the decay involves a quasi-free decay of the $^{11}$Be halo neutron into a single-proton state coupled to the $^{10}$Be core. The results enable future experimental studies such as $^{10}$Be($p,p$)$^{10}$Be and $^{8}$Be($^{3}$H,$^{3}$H)$^{8}$Be to clarify the nature of the resonance, and $^{10}$B($d,p$)$^{11}$Be studies to understand nearby neutron reaction channels.
