# Multifragmentation, Clustering, and Coalescence in Nuclear Collisions

> Nuclear collisions at intermediate, relativistic, and ultra-relativistic energies offer unique opportunities to study in detail manifold fragmentation and clustering phenomena in dense nuclear matter. At intermediate energies, the well known processes of nuclear multifragmentation -- the disintegration of bulk nuclear matter in clusters of a wide range of sizes and masses -- allow the study of the critical point of the equation of state of nuclear matter. At very high energies, ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions offer a glimpse at the substructure of hadronic matter by crossing the phase boundary to the quark-gluon plasma. The hadronization of the quark-gluon plasma created in the fireball of a ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collision can be considered, again, as a clustering process. We will present two models which allow the simulation of nuclear multifragmentation and the hadronization via the formation of clusters in an interacting gas of quarks, and will discuss the importance of clustering to our understanding of hadronization in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that clustering and fragmentation phenomena, which are well-studied in nuclear matter at intermediate energies (multifragmentation as a liquid-gas phase transition), also provide a crucial mechanism for understanding hadronization in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, specifically through parton recombination and coalescence in addition to traditional parton fragmentation.

## Method

The authors present two molecular dynamics models: Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) for nuclear matter, where nucleons are modelled as Gaussian wavepackets with realistic potential interactions and a Pauli potential to mimic Fermi statistics; and quark Molecular Dynamics (qMD) for subnuclear degrees of freedom, where quarks and antiquarks are treated as classical particles carrying colour charge and interacting via a confining potential that increases linearly with distance. The qMD model is used to simulate the hadronization of an expanding quark-gluon plasma fireball, with clusters of two or three quarks mapped onto hadronic states according to their masses and quantum numbers.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density is given as \(\rho_0 = 112\) MeV/fm\(^3\).
- Strong clustering in QMD simulations is observed at diluted densities \(\rho \approx 0.1 \rho_0\).
- Intermediate-energy nuclear collisions for multifragmentation studies occur at about 100–500 MeV per nucleon.
- Ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collision energies are \(\sqrt{s}_{NN} \approx 7\)–18 GeV (CERN-SPS) and \(\sqrt{s}_{NN} \approx 130\)–200 GeV (BNL-RHIC).
- For a Pb+Pb collision at SPS energies (\(\sqrt{s}_{NN} = 17.3\) GeV), qMD simulations show hadronization is complete after an eigenzeit \(\approx 15\) fm/c.
- At RHIC, the proton-to-pion ratio surpasses 1 at transverse momenta of 2–3 GeV, and parton clustering explains this anomaly for \(p_\perp \sim 2\)–3 GeV.
- The transition from parton clustering to fragmentation dominance occurs at \(p_\perp > 4\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the study of clustering phenomena from the liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear multifragmentation, as studied by Elliott et al. (2002) and KleineBerkenbusch et al. (2001), to the hadronization of the quark-gluon plasma. The qMD model builds on earlier work by Hofmann et al. (1999) and Scherer et al. (2001) to simulate the microscopic dynamics of hadronization. The paper connects these models to experimental puzzles at RHIC, such as the proton/pion anomaly identified by Vitev (2001) and elliptic flow analyses by Lin (2002) and Voloshin (2002), which are resolved by parton recombination models from Fries et al. (2003), Greco et al. (2003), and Molnar et al. (2003). The work also discusses antimatter clustering, with anti-deuteron and anti-helium production observed by the STAR collaboration (Adler et al., 2001) fitting coalescence models.
