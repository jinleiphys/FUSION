# The Rate of the Proton-Proton Reaction

> We re-evaluate the matrix element for the proton-proton reaction which is important for stellar-evolution calculations and for the solar-neutrino problem. We self-consistently determine the effect of vacuum polarization on the matrix element by first correcting the low-energy scattering data to account for vacuum polarization. We then calculate the proton-proton wave function by integrating the Schrodinger equation with vacuum polarization included. We use improved data for proton-proton scattering and for the deuteron wave function. We evaluate the uncertainties that are due to experimental error and estimate those that are due to theoretical inadequacies. We estimate the theoretical uncertainty by using six different deuteron potentials and five different proton-proton potentials. Vacuum polarization decreases the calculated value by $0.6_{-0.4}^{+0.1}$\%. The complete result is $Λ^2=6.92\times(1\pm0.002^{+0.014}_{-0.009})$ where the first uncertainty is due to experimental errors and the second uncertainty is due to theoretical uncertainties. Our value of $Λ^2$ is 2\% smaller than the value obtained in 1969 by Bahcall and May. The improved calculations of the rate of the $pp$ reaction described here increase slightly the predicted event rates for the chlorine and the Kamiokande solar-neutrino experiments.

# The Rate of the Proton-Proton Reaction

## Key claim

The square of the overlap integral for the proton-proton reaction is $\Lambda^2 = 6.92 \times (1 \pm 0.002^{+0.014}_{-0.009})$, which is 2% smaller than the 1969 value from Bahcall and May, with vacuum polarization decreasing the calculated value by $0.6^{+0.1}_{-0.4}$% when treated self-consistently.

## Method

The authors calculated the $pp$ matrix element by integrating the Schrodinger equation for five different proton-proton nuclear potentials (exponential, Yukawa, Gaussian, square-well, and repulsive-core) and six different deuteron wave functions (Argonne v14, Reid soft-core, Urbana v14, super-soft-core, Argonne v18, and McGee), all constrained to reproduce the same measured scattering parameters. Vacuum polarization was included self-consistently by first correcting the low-energy scattering data to account for the Uehling potential, then incorporating the Uehling potential into the Schrodinger equation for the $pp$ wave function.

## Key numbers

- $\Lambda^2 = 6.92 \times (1 \pm 0.002^{+0.014}_{-0.009})$ for the final result including vacuum polarization, where the first uncertainty is experimental (0.2%) and the second is theoretical
- Without vacuum polarization, $\Lambda^2 = 6.96 \times (1 \pm 0.002 \pm 0.010)$
- Vacuum polarization decreases $\Lambda^2$ by $0.6^{+0.1}_{-0.4}$%
- The low-energy cross-section factor $S_{pp}(0) = 3.89 \times 10^{-25} (1 \pm 0.011)$ MeV-barns
- The effective-range approximation gives $\Lambda_{eff}^2(0) = 6.975$ using $a_p = -7.8196(26)$ fm, $\rho_p = 2.790(14)$ fm, $E_d = 2.224575(9)$ MeV, $\eta_d = 0.0256(4)$, and $\rho_d = 1.759(5)$
- The experimental uncertainty in $\Lambda^2$ is 0.2%, coming predominantly from uncertainties in $\rho_d$ and $\rho_p$
- The theoretical uncertainty from $pp$ potential shape is $\pm 0.5\%$ and from deuteron wave function shape is $\pm 0.5\%$
- About 40% of the overlap integrand comes from radii $r \leq 5$ fm and only about 2% from $r \leq 1$ fm

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of Bahcall and May (1969), who found $\Lambda^2 = 7.08(1 \pm 0.025)$, by using improved scattering data and a self-consistent treatment of vacuum polarization that had been partially included but not isolated in prior calculations by Bohannon and Heller (1977) and Gould (1991). The result is about 2% smaller than the Bahcall and May value, primarily due to the high deuteron binding energy ($E_d = 2.267$ MeV) used in the McGee (1966) wave function adopted by Bahcall and May. The authors also address the anomalously high value $\Lambda^2 = 7.39$ from Gould and Guessoum (1990), showing it can only be obtained using input data inconsistent with experimental measurements. The improved calculation slightly increases predicted event rates for the chlorine and Kamiokande solar-neutrino experiments, and the authors note in a companion paper (Kamionkowski and Bahcall 1993) that vacuum polarization decreases rates for all other $pp$ chain and CNO cycle reactions by less than 5%.
