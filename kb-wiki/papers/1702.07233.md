# An overview of experimental results from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions at the CERN LHC: bulk properties and dynamical evolution

> The first collisions of lead nuclei, delivered by the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at the end of 2010, at a centre-of-mass energy per nucleon pair $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV, marked the beginning of a new era in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion physics. Following the Run 1 period, LHC also successfully delivered PbPb collisions at the collision energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 5.02 TeV at the end of 2015. The study of the properties of the produced hot and dense strongly-interacting matter at these unprecedented energies is experimentally pursued by all four big LHC experiments, ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, and LHCb. This review presents selected experimental results from heavy-ion collisions delivered during the first three years of the LHC operation focusing on the bulk matter properties and the dynamical evolution of the created system. It also presents the first results from Run 2 heavy-ion data at the highest energy, as well as from the studies of the reference pp and pPb systems, which are an integral part of the heavy-ion programme.

## Key claim

This review argues that the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) produced at the LHC behaves like a strongly-interacting, almost perfect liquid with a very low shear viscosity to entropy density ratio, and that similar collective signatures observed in small systems (pp and pPb) may also have a hydrodynamic origin, though this interpretation remains under active debate with alternative explanations based on initial-state gluon saturation.

## Method

The authors compiled and compared selected experimental results from the ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, and LHCb collaborations for PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV, as well as pp and pPb reference collisions. They focused on bulk observables including charged-particle multiplicity, transverse energy, anisotropic flow coefficients (v_n) extracted from Fourier decomposition of azimuthal particle correlations, baryon-to-meson ratios, direct photon spectra, and chemical freezeout parameters from statistical model fits to particle yields.

## Key numbers

- Charged-particle pseudorapidity density per participant pair in central PbPb collisions follows a power law s_NN^alpha with alpha = 0.16 for AA collisions, compared to alpha = 0.1 for pp and pA systems
- Total charged particle multiplicity for 0-5% central PbPb events at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV reaches N_ch = 17165 +/- 772 when integrated over the full eta range
- Energy density in central PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV is epsilon = 12-14 GeV/fm^3, roughly 2-3 times higher than at RHIC and well above the critical energy density of about 0.7 GeV/fm^3
- Direct photon effective temperature extracted from exponential fit at low pT (0.8-2.2 GeV/c) is T_eff = 297 +/- 12 (stat) +/- 41 (syst) MeV, about 40% higher than at RHIC
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio for the QGP is eta/s ~ 0.20, close to the theoretical lower bound of about 0.08 from AdS/CFT
- Elliptic flow v_2 increases by 30% from the highest RHIC energy to LHC energies
- Chemical freezeout temperature for PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV is T_ch ~ 156 MeV with baryochemical potential mu_B ~ 1 MeV
- The v_2 coefficients from multi-particle correlations v_2{4}, v_2{6}, and v_2{8} agree within 1%, indicating strong suppression of non-flow effects

## Context

This review extends the heavy-ion physics program from RHIC (where AuAu collisions first revealed the near-side ridge and baryon anomaly) to the higher energies available at the LHC, covering the first three years of LHC operation plus initial Run 2 results. The measurements enable quantitative tests of hydrodynamic models (Noronha-Hostler 2015, Niemi 2015) and initial-state models including MC-KLN, MC-Glauber, and IP-Glasma (Schenke 2012), with the IP-Glasma model based on Color Glass Condensate theory being particularly successful in describing the full spectrum of anisotropic flow coefficients. The observation of similar collective signatures in high-multiplicity pp and pPb collisions raises open questions about thermalization in small systems and whether hydrodynamics or initial-state effects are responsible.
