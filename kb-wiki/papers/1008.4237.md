# Structure and three-body decay of $^9$Be resonances

> The complex-rotated hyperspherical adiabatic method is used to study the decay of low-lying $^9$Be resonances into one neutron and two $α$-particles. We investigate the six resonances above the break-up threshold and below 6 MeV: $1/2^\pm$, $3/2^\pm$ and $5/2^\pm$. The short-distance properties of each resonance are studied, and the different angular momentum and parity configurations of the $^8$Be and $^5$He two-body substructures are determined. We compute the branching ratio for sequential decay via the $^8$Be ground state which qualitatively is consistent with measurements. We extract the momentum distributions after decay directly into the three-body continuum from the large-distance asymptotic structures. The kinematically complete results are presented as Dalitz plots as well as projections on given neutron and $α$-energy. The distributions are discussed and in most cases found to agree with available experimental data.

# Structure and three-body decay of $^9$Be resonances

## Key claim

The complex-rotated hyperspherical adiabatic method can reproduce the energies and widths of the six low-lying $^9$Be resonances above the $\alpha\alpha n$ threshold, but the computed widths for most states exceed the measured values by roughly a factor of two, suggesting that a many-body configuration at small distances reduces the branching ratio into the three-body cluster structure. The authors argue that the large experimental widths for the $1/2^{\pm}$ states may be artifacts of $R$-matrix analyses that assume sequential decay too strongly.

## Method

The authors solve the three-body problem for $^9$Be as an $\alpha+\alpha+n$ system using complex-rotated hyperspherical coordinates, where only the hyperradius $\rho$ is scaled by a complex rotation angle $\theta$. They employ the adiabatic expansion method, first solving the angular Faddeev equations at fixed $\rho$ to obtain adiabatic potentials, then solving the coupled hyperradial equations. The Ali-Bodmer potential (with Coulomb) is used for the $\alpha-\alpha$ interaction and the Cobis potential for the $\alpha-n$ interaction; a short-range three-body Gaussian potential $V_{3b} = S\exp(-\rho^2/b^2)$ is adjusted individually for each resonance to reproduce the experimental energy.

## Key numbers

- Ground state $3/2^-$: calculated energy $-1.60$ MeV (experimental $-1.574$ MeV) relative to the $\alpha\alpha n$ threshold, with three-body strength $S = 2.5$ MeV and range $b = 5$ fm
- $1/2^+$ resonance: calculated energy $0.11$ MeV, width $\simeq 0.1$ MeV (experimental $E_R = 0.110 \pm 0.020$ MeV, $\Gamma_R = 0.214 \pm 0.005$ MeV)
- $5/2^-$ resonance: calculated energy $0.86$ MeV, width $7.0 \times 10^{-4}$ MeV (experimental $E_R = 0.855 \pm 0.013$ MeV, $\Gamma_R = (7.8 \pm 1.3) \times 10^{-4}$ MeV)
- $1/2^-$ resonance: calculated energy $1.25$ MeV, width $0.65$ MeV (experimental $E_R = 1.21 \pm 0.12$ MeV, $\Gamma_R = 1.10 \pm 0.12$ MeV)
- $5/2^+$ resonance: calculated energy $1.46$ MeV, width $0.34$ MeV (experimental $E_R = 1.475 \pm 0.009$ MeV, $\Gamma_R = 0.282 \pm 0.011$ MeV)
- $3/2^+$ resonance: calculated energy $3.12$ MeV, width $1.74$ MeV (experimental $E_R = 3.130 \pm 0.025$ MeV, $\Gamma_R = 0.743 \pm 0.055$ MeV)
- $3/2^-$ resonance: calculated energy $2.65$ MeV, width $0.93$ MeV (experimental $E_R = 4.02 \pm 0.01$ MeV, $\Gamma_R = 1.33 \pm 0.36$ MeV)
- The $1/2^+$ resonance lies only $18$ keV above the $^8$Be ground state resonance at $918$ keV
- At large distances, the lowest adiabatic potential for all resonances approaches the $^8$Be($0^+$) resonance energy of $\sim 0.1$ MeV

## Context

This work extends the three-body description of $^9$Be beyond earlier cluster models (Kunz 1960, Tang 1962, Hiura 1963) and more sophisticated approaches such as the Resonating Group Model (Zahn 1976), Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (Kanada-En'yo 1995), and the Microscopic Multicluster Model (Arai 2003). It builds on the authors' previous study (Alvarez-Rodriguez 2008) by providing comprehensive momentum distributions and Dalitz plots for all six resonances, enabling direct comparison with experimental data. The results enable improved modeling of the $\alpha(\alpha n,\gamma)^9$Be reaction and subsequent $^9$Be($\alpha,n$)$^{12}$C in astrophysical nucleosynthesis, particularly for bridging the $A=5,8$ instability gaps.
