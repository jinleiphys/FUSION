# The role of ν_τ ultrahigh energy astrophysics in Km^3 detectors

> We show that the expected $ ν_τ $ signals, by their secondary tau tracks, in Km^3 detectors at highest cosmic ray energy window $ 1.7\cdot 10^{21} eV \gt E_τ \gt 1.6 x 10^{17} eV$, must overcome the corresponding $ ν_μ $ (or muonic) ones. Indeed, the Lorentz-boosted tau range length grows (linearly) above muon range, for $ E_τ \RAISE 1.6 x 10^8 GeV$ and reaches its maxima extension, $ R_{τ_{\max}} \simeq 191 km$, at energy $E_τ \simeq 3.8 x 10^9 GeV$. At this peak the tau range is nearly 20 times the corresponding muon range (at the same energy) implying a similar ratio in $ ν_τ $ over $ ν_μ $ detectability. This dominance, however may lead (at present most abundant $ ν_τ $ model fluxes) to just a rare spectacular event a year (if flavor mixing occurs). Lower energetic $ τ$ and $ ν_τ $ signals $ (\barν_e e\to \barν_τ τ, ν_τ N\to ...) $ at energy range ($ 10^5 ÷10^7 GeV$) may be more easily observed in km^3 detectors at a rate of a few $ (\barν_e e\to \barν_τ τ) $ to tens event $ (ν_τ N\to τ+ $ anything) a year.

## Key claim

The paper argues that at ultrahigh energies (above approximately 1.6 × 10^8 GeV), tau neutrinos will produce detectable signals in km^3 detectors that dominate over muon neutrino signals, but this dominance is conditional on the actual flux of tau neutrinos being comparable to other flavors; even under optimistic flux models, the event rate may be only about one spectacular event per year.

## Method

The author calculates the range of tau leptons in water and rock as a function of energy by combining three limiting factors: the radiation length (bremsstrahlung and pair production), the boosted tau lifetime range, and the electroweak interaction range with nucleons. These ranges are compared to the corresponding muon ranges at the same energies to determine the energy window where tau tracks exceed muon tracks. The analysis uses standard energy-loss equations and cross-section parameterizations from the literature, scaling from known muon behavior by the lepton mass ratio.

## Key numbers

- Tau range reaches maximum extension R_τmax ≈ 191 km at energy E_τ ≈ 3.8 × 10^9 GeV (in rock with density ρ_r = 3)
- At this peak, tau range is nearly 20 times the corresponding muon range at the same energy
- Critical energy where tau range first exceeds muon range: E_τ1 = 1.65 × 10^8 GeV in rock, with corresponding range R_τ1 ≈ 8.2 km
- Critical energy where tau range equals muon range again at highest energies: E_τ2 ≈ 1.7 × 10^12 GeV, with range R_τ2 ≈ 16 km
- Boosted tau lifetime range: R_τo = 5 km × (E_τ / 10^8 GeV)
- For horizontal neutrinos in sea water (ρ_r = 1): R_τmax ≈ 418 km, E_τmax ≈ 8.36 × 10^9 GeV
- Expected tau event rate from CR-4 model: near unity per year for the longest tau tracks at maximal range extension
- At the W^- resonance energy (E_ν ≈ 6.3 × 10^15 eV), tau range is only R_τ ≈ 71 m, compared to several km for muons

## Context

This work extends earlier considerations of tau neutrino detection by Learned and Pakvasa (1997), who noted the "double bang" signature of tau events at PeV energies, to much higher energies (10^17 to 10^21 eV) where the Lorentz-boosted tau lifetime makes tau tracks longer than muon tracks. The paper uses neutrino flux models from Stecker and Salamon (AGN-SS) and from Yoshida and Teshima (CR-2 and CR-4), as well as cross-section parameterizations from Gandhi, Quigg, Reno, and Sarcevic. The results enable future km^3 neutrino telescopes to identify the energy window where tau neutrinos become the dominant detectable signal, and suggest that even a single tau event per year could provide the first direct evidence for the tau neutrino's existence.
