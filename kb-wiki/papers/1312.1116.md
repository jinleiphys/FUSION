# Coupling of (ultra-)relativistic atomic nuclei with photons

> The coupling of photons with (ultra-) relativistic atomic nuclei is presented in two particular circumstances: very high electromagnetic fields and very short photon pulses. We consider a typical situation where the (bare) nuclei (fully stripped of electrons) are accelerated to energies ~1TeV per nucleon (according to the state of the art at LHC, for instance) and photon sources like petawatt lasers \simeq1eV -radiation (envisaged by ELI-NP project, for instance), or free-electron laser ~10keV -radiation, or synchrotron sources, etc. In these circumstances the nuclear scale energy can be attained, with very high field intensities. In particular, we analyse the nuclear transitions induced by the radiation, including both one- and two-photon processes, as well as the polarization-driven transitions which may lead to giant dipole resonances. The nuclear (electrical) polarization concept is introduced. It is shown that the perturbation theory for photo-nuclear reactions is applicable, although the field intensity is high, since the corresponding interaction energy is low and the interaction time (pulse duration) is short. It is also shown that the description of the giant nuclear dipole resonance requires the dynamics of the nuclear electrical polarization degrees of freedom.

## Key claim

The paper argues that photo-nuclear reactions, including giant dipole resonances, can be induced by colliding high-intensity photon pulses from lasers or free-electron lasers with ultra-relativistic bare atomic nuclei, but only under the condition that the interaction energy remains low and the pulse duration short enough that standard perturbation theory remains applicable despite the high field intensity.

## Method

The authors analyze the Doppler up-shift of photon frequency in the rest frame of an ultra-relativistic nucleus (energy ~1 TeV per nucleon) for head-on collisions with photons from petawatt lasers (~1 eV), free-electron lasers (~10 keV), or synchrotron sources. They derive the interaction Hamiltonian for one- and two-photon nuclear transitions within the dipole approximation, estimate transition rates using first- and second-order perturbation theory, and introduce a classical harmonic-oscillator model for nuclear electrical polarization to describe the giant dipole resonance, including a resonance factor and dielectric function.

## Key numbers

- Nucleus energy: ~1 TeV per nucleon (LHC state of the art), giving Lorentz factor gamma ~ 10^3 (nucleon rest energy ~1 GeV).
- For a 1 eV laser photon, the Doppler up-shift yields ~2 keV photons in the nucleus rest frame; for a 10 keV free-electron laser, it yields ~20 MeV photons.
- Laser parameters used: photon energy 1 eV, pulse energy 50 J, pulse duration 50 fs, power 1 petawatt (10^15 W), intensity ~4 x 10^20 W/cm^2, electric field ~10^9 statvolt/cm.
- In the nucleus rest frame, including light aberration, the intensity reaches ~3 x 10^24 W/cm^2 and electric field ~10^14 statvolt/cm.
- Estimated one-photon transition rate: P_ab ~ 10^12 s^-1 (for 10 MeV photons, pulse frequency uncertainty ~10^16 s^-1).
- Estimated two-photon contribution is negligible because qA_0 ~ 1 eV, far below the ~100 MeV needed to compete with one-photon processes.
- Plasma frequency estimate for nucleons: hbar omega_p ~ Z^(1/2) MeV; characteristic frequency hbar omega_c ~ 10 A^(1/6) MeV; resonance frequency Omega_0 ~ 10 MeV.
- Pair production cross-section: sigma_pair ~ 10^(-28) Z^2 cm^2; nuclear cross-section ~10^(-26) cm^2; ratio sigma_pair / a^2 ~ 10^(-2) Z^2, reaching ~10^2 for heavy nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the concept of using relativistic heavy ions to up-shift photon frequencies, previously discussed for hydrogen-like ions by Bessonov and Kim (1995) and for plasma mirrors by Bulanov, Esirkepov, and Tajima (2003). It also builds on known peripheral photon-ion interactions in ultra-peripheral collisions (Bertulani, Klein, and Nystrand, 2005). The paper enables future experimental studies at facilities like ELI-NP and the LHC, where high-intensity laser pulses and relativistic heavy-ion beams could be combined to study photo-nuclear reactions, giant dipole resonances, and possibly vacuum polarization effects in a regime of very high fields and very short pulses.
