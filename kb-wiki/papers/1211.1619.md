# N-quantum calculation of the hydrogen atom with one-photon exchange

> The N-quantum approach (NQA) to quantum field theory uses the complete and irreducible set of in or out fields, including in or out fields for bound states, as standard building blocks to construct solutions to quantum field theories. In particular, introducing in (or out) fields for the bound states allows a new way to calculate energy levels and wave functions for the bound states that is both covariant and effectively 3-dimensional. This method is independent of the Bethe-Salpeter equation. In contrast to the Bethe-Salpeter equation, all solutions of the NQA are normalizable and correspond to physical bound states. In this paper we use the NQA in one-loop approximation to calculate states of the relativistic hydrogen atom and analogous two-body systems to illustrate how our new method works. With additional terms in the in field expansion we find systematic corrections beyond the Coulomb interaction.

## Key claim

The N-quantum approach (NQA) provides a new covariant and effectively three-dimensional method for calculating bound-state energy levels and wave functions that is independent of the Bethe-Salpeter equation, but in this one-loop calculation the authors do not carry the work to the level necessary to resolve the proton radius discrepancy found in the Pohl et al. and Antognini et al. experiments; they present only a one-loop approximation that reduces to the Dirac equation for large proton mass and ignore magnetic interactions and renormalization effects.

## Method

The authors expand interacting fields (electron, muon, proton, photon) in normal-ordered series of asymptotic *in* fields, including *in* fields for each hydrogen bound state, and insert these expansions into the operator equations of motion. After re-normal-ordering and equating coefficients of linearly independent normal-ordered terms, they obtain coupled integral equations for the bound-state amplitudes (wave functions) in momentum space, keeping only one-loop (three-field) terms in the Haag expansion. They solve the resulting equation numerically in Coulomb gauge for the hydrogen atom at rest, using a partial-wave expansion of the 4×4 matrix wave function that satisfies the subsidiary condition from the on-shell proton constraint.

## Key numbers

- The mass ratio for muonic hydrogen is \(m_{\mu}/M_p \approx 1/10\), compared to \(m_e/M_p \approx 1/2000\) for ordinary hydrogen.
- The bound-state equation is solved with binding energy \(\epsilon_i < 0\) where the bound-state mass \(m_b = m_1 + m_2 + \epsilon_i\).
- The one-loop approximation reduces to the Dirac equation in the large-\(m_2\) limit, where the factor \((\not p + m_2)^T / 2E_{p'}\) in the potential becomes \((1+\gamma^0)/2\).

## Context

This work extends the NQA framework previously described by Greenberg and collaborators (Greenberg, 1990s) and by Ray and others, and is motivated by the proton radius discrepancy between ordinary hydrogen and muonic hydrogen measurements by Pohl et al. (2010) and Antognini et al. (2013). The method is independent of the Bethe-Salpeter equation (Salpeter, 1951) and avoids its spurious solutions and negative-norm amplitudes; it relates to the spectator equation of Gross (1982) and to work by Källén (1950s) and Yang and Feldman (1950s). The next step is to include higher-order terms systematically to produce a unified calculation of all QED corrections up to a given order, potentially addressing the muonic hydrogen anomaly.
