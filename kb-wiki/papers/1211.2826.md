# Color Transparency: past, present and future

> We review a unique prediction of Quantum Chromo Dynamics, called color transparency (CT), where the final (and/or initial) state interactions of hadrons with the nuclear medium must vanish for exclusive processes at high momentum transfers. We retrace the progress of our understanding of this phenomenon, which began with the discovery of the $J/ψ$ meson, followed by the discovery of high energy CT phenomena, the recent developments in the investigations of the onset of CT at intermediate energies and the directions for future studies.

# Color Transparency: past, present and future

## Key claim

Color transparency (CT), the vanishing of final and/or initial state interactions of hadrons with the nuclear medium in exclusive processes at high momentum transfers, is a unique prediction of Quantum Chromo Dynamics, but its observation at intermediate energies is strongly masked by expansion effects of the small-size wave packets, requiring careful treatment of the space-time evolution of the interacting system.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical foundations of CT, beginning with the Low-Nussinov two-gluon exchange model and the dipole formalism where the inelastic cross section of a small quark-antiquark dipole scattering off a nucleon is expressed in terms of the gluon and sea-quark distributions. It then examines experimental evidence from three classes of processes: (1) coherent diffractive dissociation of 500 GeV pions into di-jets on carbon and platinum targets (Fermilab E791), (2) photoproduction of J/ψ on H, Be, Fe, and Pb targets at 80–190 GeV (Fermilab), and (3) exclusive vector meson electroproduction at HERA. For intermediate energies, the review discusses the quantum diffusion model and the baryonic basis expansion approach to describe the time development of point-like configurations, applied to large-angle A(p,2p) measurements at BNL (6–14.4 GeV incident proton momenta).

## Key numbers

- For the E791 di-jet experiment: the per-nucleon cross section exponent α = 1.61 ± 0.08 for the process π + A → "two jets" + A, compared to the CT prediction of σ ∝ A^1.54 and the soft diffraction value α ∼ 2/3.
- For J/ψ photoproduction at Fermilab (80–190 GeV): the coherent production exponent α = 1.4 ± 0.06 ± 0.04, and the incoherent (quasi-elastic) exponent α = 0.94 ± 0.02 ± 0.03.
- The coherence length for nucleon knockout is estimated as l_c ∼ 0.4–0.6 fm × p_h (with p_h in GeV), requiring p_N ≥ 4 GeV for significant CT effects, corresponding to Q^2 ≥ 8 GeV^2 for the (e,e'p) reaction.
- The dipole-nucleon cross section formula predicts a growth with energy: for Q^2 ∼ 3 GeV^2 (J/ψ photoproduction) the cross section grows as s^0.4, and for Q^2 ∼ 40 GeV^2 (Υ photoproduction) as s^0.8.
- The di-jet cross section scales as dσ/d^2k_t^2 ∝ 1/k_t^8, observed for k_t ≥ 1.5 GeV.
- The t-slope convergence for ρ and J/ψ electroproduction at high Q^2 is shown, with data from Chekanov (2004, 2007) compared to predictions from Frankfurt (1997).

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of CT from the original two-gluon exchange model of Gunion and Soper (1976) and the early arguments by Frankfurt (1975) that the nucleon becomes transparent to hadrons built of heavy quarks. It builds on the factorization proofs for exclusive processes by Collins (1996) and the dipole formalism developed by Frankfurt (1993, 2000) and Brodsky (1994). The review directly addresses the debate between the Brodsky-Farrar mechanism (small-size configurations dominating at high Q^2) and the Landshoff mechanism (three-gluon exchange with Sudakov suppression), as discussed by Mueller (1982) and Radyushkin (2004). The intermediate-energy results from BNL (Carroll 1988, Leksanov 2001, Aclander 2004) show a non-monotonic energy dependence of nuclear transparency that challenges simple CT expectations and motivates future studies at Jefferson Lab and LHC ultra-peripheral collisions.
