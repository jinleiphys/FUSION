# Line Tension Reshapes Nucleation at Surface Edges: A Generalized Theory for Nanopore Activation

> Heterogeneous nucleation at surface edges is pervasive across nature and industry, yet the role of line tension, arising from asymmetric capillary interactions at geometric singularities, remains poorly understood. Herein we develop a generalized nucleation theory that explicitly incorporates line tension induced by edge pinning, thereby extending classical frameworks to account for nanoscale confinement and interfacial asymmetry. Through analytical treatment of droplet formation within geometrically defined nanopores, we derive a closed-form expression for the edge-pinned line tension as a function of Laplace pressure, pore geometry, and wettability. This formulation reveals that line tension can significantly reshape the nucleation energy landscape, introducing nontrivial dependencies on contact angle and pore morphology. Our results uncover a tunable, geometry-mediated mechanism for controlling nucleation barriers, offering predictive insight into phase transitions in confined environments and suggesting new strategies for design in applications ranging from nanofluidics to crystallization control.

# Line Tension Reshapes Nucleation at Surface Edges: A Generalized Theory for Nanopore Activation

## Key claim

The paper develops a generalized nucleation theory that explicitly incorporates line tension arising from geometric confinement and edge pinning in nanopores, showing that this line tension is not an intrinsic material constant but a geometry-dependent quantity that can significantly alter nucleation energy barriers. The authors claim that this pinning-induced line tension emerges as a mechanical consequence of Laplace pressure under geometric confinement, independent of intermolecular forces, and can be tuned through pore geometry and wettability.

## Method

The authors perform an analytical treatment of quasi-equilibrium liquid droplet formation within geometrically defined nanopores with asymmetric opening angles, deriving closed-form expressions for the edge-pinned line tension as a function of Laplace pressure, pore geometry, and wettability. They minimize the Gibbs free energy change to determine critical nucleus parameters, incorporating a line tension term into the Helmholtz free energy formulation, and derive a geometric parameter f that characterizes the ratio of the critical energy barrier in heterogeneous nucleation to that of homogeneous nucleation. The model is compared against classical nucleation theory predictions for smooth conical pores without pinning effects.

## Key numbers

- For a pore geometry with phi_1 = 0 degrees and phi_2 = 30 degrees, the line tension factor reaches a minimum gamma*_min approximately -0.4 at a contact angle of theta = 125 degrees
- Assuming a critical radius of a water nucleus R_c = 50 nm and surface tension sigma_lg = 0.07 N/m, the line tension is approximately -1.4 x 10^-9 N, which is an order of magnitude larger than previously reported values of 10^-11 to 10^-10 N for water droplets on flat quartz
- The upper limit contact angle for heterogeneous nucleation in asymmetric pores is given by theta_c = 180 degrees - 0.5(phi_1 + phi_2)
- The valid contact angle range for the line-tension model is defined as (theta_0, theta_c], where theta_0 = max{90 degrees - phi_1, 90 degrees - phi_2}

## Context

This work extends classical nucleation theory (CNT) and its heterogeneous extension (hetCNT), which have relied on oversimplified assumptions of smooth, regular geometries and neglected nanoscale features such as edges and confinement. The authors address the long-standing controversy over line tension magnitude and sign, which has been debated in studies by Berg (2010), Kolarov (1968), and Toshev (1988), by showing that geometry-induced line tension can span several orders of magnitude depending on pore geometry and wettability. The framework enables predictive control of nucleation barriers in confined systems, with applications in nanofluidics, pharmaceutical crystallization, and nanofabrication, and may explain anomalous observations such as the stability of nanobubbles in cylindrical pores studied by Guillemot (2012) and shifts in ice nucleation due to nanoscale texture reported by Gurganus (2014).
