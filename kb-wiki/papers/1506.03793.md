# Nuclear fusion in the deuterated cores of inflated hot Jupiters

> Ouyed et al. (1998) proposed Deuterium (DD) fusion at the core-mantle interface of giant planets as a mechanism to explain their observed heat excess. But rather high interior temperatures (~10^5 K) and a stratified D layer are needed, making such a scenario unlikely. In this paper, we re-examine DD fusion, with the addition of screening effects pertinent to a deuterated core containing ice and some heavy elements. This alleviates the extreme temperature constraint and removes the requirement of a stratified D layer. As an application, we propose that, if their core temperatures are a few times 10^4 K and core composition is chemically inhomogeneous, the observed inflated size of some giant exoplanets ("hot Jupiters") may be linked to screened DD fusion occurring deep in the interior. Application of an analytic evolution model suggests that the amount of inflation from this effect can be important if there is sufficient rock-ice in the core, making DD fusion an effective extra internal energy source for radius inflation. The mechanism of screened DD fusion, operating in the above temperature range, is generally consistent with the trend in radius anomaly with planetary equilibrium temperature $T_{\rm eq}$, and also depends on planetary mass. Although we do not consider the effect of incident stellar flux, we expect that a minimum level of irradiation is necessary to trigger core erosion and subsequent DD fusion inside the planet. Since DD fusion is quite sensitive to the screening potential inferred from laboratory experiments, observations of inflated hot Jupiters may help constrain screening effects in the cores of giant planets.

# Nuclear Fusion in the Deuterated Cores of Inflated Hot Jupiters

## Key claim

Screened deuterium-deuterium (DD) fusion in the deuterated cores of hot Jupiters, occurring at core temperatures of a few times 10^4 K rather than the previously required ~10^5 K, can provide a persistent internal energy source sufficient to explain the observed radius anomaly, provided the core contains sufficient rock-ice material and the screening potential U is approximately 200 eV as inferred from laboratory experiments with Fe targets.

## Method

The authors re-examine DD fusion rates by incorporating screening effects from a deuterated core containing ice and heavy elements, using the enhancement factor f_scr from laboratory studies of DD fusion in solid metal targets. They compute the thermally averaged screened cross-section using a Maxwellian distribution of speeds with the Gamow peak modified by the screening potential U, and apply a one-dimensional analytic time-evolution model for planetary radius based on energy balance between fusion power and blackbody luminosity, with core mass erosion modeled as exponential decay.

## Key numbers

- Screening potential U = 200 ± 20 eV for Fe target (Kasagi 2004), with laboratory values ranging from ≤30 eV for Al to ~450 ± 50 eV for Fe (Raiola et al. 2002)
- Fusion power P_DD ≈ 10^26 erg s^-1 for U ≈ 180-220 eV at core temperatures 5×10^3 K to 5×10^4 K
- Core density of deuterium ρ_D ~ 2.2×10^-6 η_D,-4 η_ice,-1 ρ_c, where η_D,-4 is D/H ratio in units of 10^-4 and η_ice,-1 is ice mass fraction in units of 0.1
- Total energy from DD fusion E_DD ~ 2.6×10^42 erg × η_D,-4 η_ice,-1 (M_P/M_J)
- Inflation timescale τ_DD ~ 1.0 Gyr × η_D,-4 η_ice,-1 (M_J/M_P)^0.6 for Jupiter-mass planets
- Effective temperature scaling T_eff = (200 K) (M_P/M_J)^0.4
- Radius inflation ΔR ~ 0.3 R_J (1 + (P_DD/L_eq) τ)^α with α ~ 0.2, where optical depth τ can reach 10^4-10^5 for T_eq ≥ 2000 K
- Core density for Jupiter-size planet ρ_c,J ≈ 4.38 g cm^-3

## Context

This work extends the original DD fusion model of Ouyed et al. (1998) for Jupiter's heat excess, which required temperatures ~10^5 K and a stratified D layer, by incorporating screening effects that remove these extreme constraints. The mechanism is presented as an alternative to other proposed explanations for the hot Jupiter radius anomaly, including Ohmic dissipation (Laughlin et al. 2011; Huang and Cumming 2012), tidal heating (Bodenheimer et al. 2001), kinetic heating from atmospheric waves (Guillot and Showman 2002), enhanced atmospheric opacity (Burrows et al. 2007), and layered convection (Chabrier and Baraffe 2007). The model enables using exoplanet observations to constrain nuclear screening effects in a unique regime of temperature and density, and the predicted scaling of radius anomaly with equilibrium temperature T_eq depends on the optical depth at which fusion heat is deposited rather than on the fusion rate itself.
