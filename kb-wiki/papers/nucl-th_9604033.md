# Matter radii of light halo nuclei

> We re-examine the matter radii of diffuse halo nuclei, as deduced from reaction cross section measurements at high energy. Careful consideration is given to the intrinsic few-body structure of these projectiles and the adiabatic nature of the projectile-target interaction. Using $^{11}$Li, $^{11}$Be and $^{8}$B as examples we show that data require significantly larger matter radii than previously reported. The revised value for $^{11}$Li of 3.55 fm is consistent with three-body models with significant $1s$-intruder state components, which reproduce experimental $^{9}$Li momentum distributions following $^{11}$Li breakup, but were hitherto thought to be at variance with cross section data.

# Matter radii of light halo nuclei

## Key claim

The matter radii of halo nuclei deduced from high-energy reaction cross sections are significantly larger than previously reported when the intrinsic few-body structure of the projectile and the adiabatic nature of the projectile-target interaction are properly accounted for; the revised radius for $^{11}$Li of 3.55 fm is consistent with three-body models that reproduce breakup momentum distributions but were previously thought to disagree with cross section data.

## Method

The authors reanalyzed reaction cross section data for $^{11}$Li, $^{11}$Be, and $^{8}$B on a $^{12}$C target at 800 MeV/nucleon using Glauber theory in two formulations: the standard static density approximation, which treats the projectile as having a smooth density distribution, and a few-body adiabatic (frozen coordinate) treatment that explicitly accounts for the spatial correlations between the core and valence nucleons in the projectile ground state wavefunction. For each projectile, the core-target and valence nucleon-target subsystems were first calibrated against independent experimental cross section data, then the full projectile-target cross section was calculated using realistic two-body (for $^{11}$Be and $^{8}$B) and three-body (for $^{11}$Li) wavefunctions.

## Key numbers

- $^{11}$Li revised matter rms radius: $3.55 \pm 0.10$ fm, representing a 14.5% increase over the previously tabulated value of $3.10 \pm 0.17$ fm (Tanihata 1988)
- $^{11}$Be revised matter rms radius: $2.90 \pm 0.05$ fm, a 7% increase over the previous value of $2.71 \pm 0.05$ fm (Tanihata 1988)
- $^{8}$B revised matter rms radius: $2.50 \pm 0.04$ fm, a 4.6% increase over the previous value of $2.39 \pm 0.04$ fm (Tanihata 1988)
- Experimental reaction cross sections used: $\sigma(^{11}\text{Li}) = 1060 \pm 10$ mb (Kobayashi 1989), $\sigma(^{11}\text{Be}) = 942 \pm 8$ mb (Tanihata 1985), $\sigma(^{8}\text{B}) = 798 \pm 6$ mb (Tanihata, private communication)
- Core-target cross sections calculated and verified against experiment: $\sigma(^{9}\text{Li}) = 796$ mb (experiment $796 \pm 6$ mb), $\sigma(^{10}\text{Be}) = 813$ mb (experiment $813 \pm 10$ mb), $\sigma(^{7}\text{Be}) = 738$ mb (experiment $738 \pm 9$ mb)
- Nucleon-$^{12}$C cross section at 800 MeV: $\sigma(N) = 231$ mb, consistent with experiment

## Context

This work extends the earlier analysis of Takigawa et al. (1992), who demonstrated that static density calculations overestimate reaction cross sections for halo nuclei but used a simplified two-body di-neutron model. The authors also build on the adiabatic treatment of composite projectiles by Nishioka and Johnson (1980) and the few-body Glauber formalism developed by Yabana, Ogawa, and Suzuki (1992) and Al-Khalili, Thompson, and Tostevin (1995). The revised radii bring consistency between reaction cross section data and the three-body intruder state models of Thompson and Zhukov (1994) that successfully reproduce breakup momentum distributions, resolving a previous discrepancy. The results imply that a careful re-examination of all matter radii deduced from reaction cross sections for exotic nuclei is necessary, particularly for extended three-body halo systems.
