# Nuclear astrophysics from direct reactions

> Accurate nuclear reaction rates are needed for primordial nucleosynthesis and hydrostatic burning in stars. The relevant reactions are extremely difficult to measure directly in the laboratory at the small astrophysical energies. In recent years direct reactions have been developed and applied to extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors. These methods require a combination of new experimental techniques and theoretical efforts, which are the subject of this presentation.

# Nuclear astrophysics from direct reactions

## Key claim
Direct reactions (transfer, Coulomb excitation, Coulomb dissociation, charge-exchange, and knockout) can be used to extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors for reactions that are extremely difficult to measure directly in the laboratory, but the reliability of each method depends on careful treatment of higher-order effects, relativistic dynamics, and the separation of nuclear from electromagnetic contributions.

## Method
The paper reviews several direct-reaction techniques applied to nuclear astrophysics: the Trojan Horse method uses breakup reactions to induce low-energy reactions via Fermi-momentum compensation; the Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient (ANC) technique extracts the asymptotic normalization of overlap integrals from peripheral transfer reactions; Coulomb dissociation relates the breakup cross section to the photodisintegration cross section via first-order perturbation theory; charge-exchange reactions (p,n) are used to obtain Gamow-Teller matrix elements via Taddeucci's formula; and single-nucleon knockout reactions with heavy ions at intermediate energies probe single-particle occupancies and level energies in exotic nuclei.

## Key numbers
- The stopping power in proton+hydrogen collisions is proportional to $v^{\alpha}$ with $\alpha=1.35$, where $v$ is the projectile velocity.
- The $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma)^{8}$B S-factor is given by $S_{17}(E)=(22.109\ {\rm eV.b}){1+5.30E+1.65E^2+0.857E^3 \over 1+E/0.1375}$, where $E$ is the relative energy in MeV of p+$^{7}$Be in their center-of-mass.
- $^{8}$B has a small proton separation energy of approximately 140 keV.
- Neutron-rich isotopes along the r-process path have lifetimes typically of $10^{-2}$ to $10^{-1}$ s.
- In the $^{33}$Ar knockout study, excited states were measured with uncertainties of several keV, yielding a 2 orders of magnitude improvement in level energy uncertainty and a 3 orders of magnitude improvement in the uncertainty of the calculated $^{32}$Cl(p,$\gamma$)$^{33}$Ar rate.

## Context
This review extends the work of Baur (1986) on the Trojan Horse method, Mukhamezhanov and Timofeyuk (1990) on the ANC technique, and Baur, Bertulani, and Rebel (1986) on Coulomb dissociation. It addresses the long-standing laboratory electron-screening puzzle, where experimental results disagree systematically by a factor of two with the adiabatic theory (Assenbaum 1987, Rolfs 1995, 2001). The paper enables future applications at new radioactive beam facilities such as FAIR's R3B and ELISE projects, and identifies open questions including the validity of Taddeucci's formula for charge-exchange reactions and the need for microscopic treatments of continuum states in reactions near the dripline.
