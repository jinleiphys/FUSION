# Measuring the Rate of Isotropization of Quark-Gluon Plasma Using Rapidity Correlations

> We propose that rapidity dependent momentum correlations can be used to extract the shear relaxation time $τ_π$ of the medium formed in high energy nuclear collisions. The stress-energy tensor in an equilibrium quark-gluon plasma is isotropic, but in nuclear collisions it is likely very far from this state. The relaxation time $τ_π$ characterizes the rate of isotropization and is a transport coefficient as fundamental as the shear viscosity. We show that fluctuations emerging from the initial anisotropy survive to freeze-out, in excess of thermal fluctuations, influencing rapidity correlation patterns. We show that these correlations can be used to extract $τ_π$. We describe a method for calculating the rapidity dependence of two-particle momentum correlations with a second order, causal, diffusion equation that includes Langevin noise as a source of thermal fluctuations. The causality requirement introduces the relaxation time and we link the shape of the rapidity correlation pattern to its presence. Here we examine how different equations of state and temperature dependent transport coefficients in the presence of realistic hydrodynamic flow influence the estimate of $τ_π$. In comparison to RHIC data, we find that the ratio $τ_π/ν\approx 5-6$ where $ν=η/sT$ is the kinematic viscosity.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the rapidity dependence of two-particle transverse momentum correlations can be used to extract the shear relaxation time τ_π of the quark-gluon plasma, but the extracted value is a ratio τ_π/ν ≈ 5–6 (where ν = η/sT is the kinematic viscosity) rather than an absolute value, and the result depends on the chosen equation of state and temperature-dependent transport coefficients in the presence of realistic hydrodynamic flow.

## Method

The authors use linearized, second-order Müller-Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics with Langevin noise and one-dimensional longitudinal (Bjorken) expansion to derive a causal diffusion equation for correlations of transverse momentum density fluctuations in excess of thermal fluctuations. They solve this equation to obtain the rapidity dependence of two-particle momentum correlations, then compare the calculated correlation patterns to STAR data from Au+Au collisions at √s = 200 GeV, varying the parameter β = τ_π/ν to find the best fit. The time dependence of temperature, entropy density, and the transport coefficient κ is computed from coupled differential equations using a parameterization of η/s as a function of temperature from Niemi (2012).

## Key numbers

- The ratio τ_π/ν ≈ 5–6, where ν = η/sT is the kinematic viscosity, extracted from comparison to STAR Au+Au data at √s = 200 GeV.
- Best agreement with data found using β = τ_π/ν = 5.5, with a formation time τ₀ = 1.05 fm and freeze-out temperature T_FO = 150 MeV.
- The range 5 < τ_π/ν < 6 gives comparably good agreement with the data.

## Context

This work extends the method described in Gavin (2016) for calculating rapidity-dependent momentum correlations using a second-order causal diffusion equation, and applies it to extract the shear relaxation time from existing STAR data (Agakishiev 2011). The approach enables experimental determination of τ_π, a transport coefficient as fundamental as shear viscosity, and provides a computationally efficient alternative to full hydrodynamic or transport simulations that lose two-particle spatial correlation information during freeze-out.
