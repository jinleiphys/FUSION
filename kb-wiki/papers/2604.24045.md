# Nuclear non-resonant photoexcitation assisted by electron recombination

> We investigate theoretically a nuclear excitation mechanism involving absorption of non-resonant photons leveraged by the coupling to the atomic shell. The nuclear non-resonant photoexcitation is assisted by electron recombination which compensates the energy mismatch between photon and nuclear transition energies, reminiscent of parametric up-conversion in non-linear media. This third-order process proceeds via a virtual nuclear state rather than virtual electronic states, distinguishing this mechanism from the electronic bridge. We investigate the process on the example of a so-far not observed 14.2 keV hard x-ray transition in 193Pt driven by an x-ray free-electron laser. Although the calculated cross section is small, it can be compensated by the vast number of non-resonant photons from the x-ray laser pulse. By enabling nuclear excitation through non-resonant photons, this up-conversion-like mechanism suggests new directions for non-linear x-ray interactions mediated by nuclear transitions.

## Key claim

The authors propose a nuclear excitation mechanism where a non-resonant photon and a recombining electron jointly excite a nucleus via a virtual nuclear intermediate state, compensating the energy mismatch between the photon energy and the nuclear transition energy. The calculated cross section is small, but the authors argue it can be compensated by the vast number of non-resonant photons in an X-ray free-electron laser pulse, and the mechanism is distinguished from the electronic bridge by proceeding through virtual nuclear rather than virtual electronic states.

## Method

The authors calculate the third-order differential cross section for nuclear photoexcitation assisted by electron recombination, expanding the continuum electron states in partial waves and including both nucleus–photon and nucleus–electron interactions. They apply the formalism to the 14.2 keV hard X-ray transition in 193Pt, using nuclear data from the ENSDF database and electronic bound wave functions from the relativistic multi-configurational Dirac-Fock code GRASP2K. The reaction rate is obtained by integrating the cross section over the electron flux (modeled with a relativistic Fermi-Dirac distribution for a plasma at electron density 10^24 cm^-3) and the photon energy distribution (Gaussian with width of several eV), with the XFEL intensity assumed at 10^18 W/cm^2 and pulse duration 100 fs.

## Key numbers

*   The reduced transition probabilities used are B(M1,5/2^- → 3/2^-) = 0.0303 W.u. and B(M1,3/2^- → 1/2^-) = 0.165 W.u. for the 12.634 keV and 1.642 keV transitions in 193Pt, respectively.
*   For the direct NEEC process, the calculated reaction rates are λ_NEEC(200 eV) = 4.43×10^-26 s^-1, λ_NEEC(400 eV) = 2.81×10^-11 s^-1, and λ_NEEC(600 eV) = 7.58×10^-6 s^-1 at the corresponding electron temperatures.
*   Assuming a dense plasma with temperature 200 eV, total 193Pt ion number ~10^12, and laser repetition rate 100 kHz, the event rate for nuclear excitation via the proposed process can reach approximately 10^-3 s^-1 at a photon energy around 12.65 keV.
*   The photon energy is scanned from 0.05 keV to 13.25 keV with a step size of 0.05 keV; peaks appear at the resonance energies of 1.642 keV and 12.634 keV.

## Context

This work extends the framework of nuclear excitation mechanisms that couple the atomic shell to the nucleus, such as the electronic bridge (EB) mechanism (Li 2023, Cai 2021) and nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) (Gunst 2015, Yang 2025). The proposed mechanism is conceptually reminiscent of parametric up-conversion and differs from the EB by proceeding via virtual nuclear states rather than virtual electronic states. The authors note that conventional NEEC requires high-temperature plasmas to open inner-shell capture channels, whereas their scheme relaxes this constraint by introducing an additional photon, enabling efficient excitation in low-temperature plasmas and potentially reducing background contributions from collisions and atomic processes. The work suggests new directions for non-linear X-ray interactions mediated by nuclear transitions and could motivate future research with facilities such as the Shanghai high repetition rate XFEL and extreme light facility (SHINE).
