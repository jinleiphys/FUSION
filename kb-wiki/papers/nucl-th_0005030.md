# Nuclear isotope thermometry

> We discuss different aspects which could influence temperatures deduced from experimental isotopic yields in the multifragmentation process. It is shown that fluctuations due to the finite size of the system and distortions due to the decay of hot primary fragments conspire to blur the temperature determination in multifragmentation reactions. These facts suggest that caloric curves obtained through isotope thermometers, which were taken as evidence for a first-order phase transition in nuclear matter, should be investigated very carefully.

# Nuclear isotope thermometry

## Key claim

The paper argues that caloric curves obtained through isotope thermometers, which were taken as evidence for a first-order phase transition in nuclear matter, should be investigated very carefully because fluctuations due to the finite size of the system and distortions due to the decay of hot primary fragments conspire to blur the temperature determination in multifragmentation reactions.

## Method

The authors used the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) to simulate the decay of excited $^{112}Sn$ nuclei at excitation energies of $E_{0}^{*}/A = 3-10$ MeV, averaging over approximately $10^{8}$ decay partitions. They compared primary and secondary fragment yields to extract isotopic temperatures via the Albergo double-ratio formula, and developed an analytic grand-canonical treatment of temperature fluctuations to quantify corrections to the isotope thermometer. Secondary decay was treated using both a simplified Weisskopf evaporative procedure and a more detailed empirical approach incorporating experimental nuclear structure information for fragments with $Z \leq 10$.

## Key numbers

- Temperature distributions from SMM have variances $\sigma_T \approx 0.4$ MeV, fairly independent of excitation energy in the range $3 \text{ MeV} \le E_0^*/A \le 10 \text{ MeV}$
- The critical temperature for the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition is expected to be of order 15 MeV (citing Jaqaman and Muller)
- The SMM predicts a plateau of roughly constant temperature $T \approx 5$ MeV for excitation energies $E^*/A \approx 3-7$ MeV
- The correction factor $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2p}} \cdot \exp\left[\frac{q^2}{4p}\right]$ for temperature fluctuations is of order 1-2 for $^{112}Sn$ decay at temperatures $4 \leq T \leq 7$ MeV
- For the decay of $^{112}Sn$ at temperatures $4 \leq T \leq 7$ MeV, the free proton yield follows $\gamma_{PF} = 4.5$ and free neutron yield follows $\gamma_{NF} = 1.0$ in a power-law parameterization
- The reduced free excitation energy parameter $\varepsilon_0 = 8$ MeV is used in the low-energy limit of $f^* = -T^2/\varepsilon_0$

## Context

This work extends the isotope thermometry method proposed by Albergo et al. (1985) and addresses the interpretation of caloric curves from experiments such as Pochodzalla et al. (1995) that were taken as evidence for a first-order phase transition. The paper contradicts the assumption that isotopic temperatures directly reflect the breakup temperature, showing that finite-size effects and secondary decay distort the temperature determination. The results enable more careful comparisons between experimental data and multifragmentation models like the SMM (Bondorf et al.) and the Microcanonical Metropolis Monte Carlo model (Gross et al.), and suggest that empirical secondary decay treatments (as in Xi et al., 1998) are necessary for accurate temperature extraction.
