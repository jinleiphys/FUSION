# Localization in light nuclei

> We investigate the presence of spatial localization in nuclei using a method that maps the nucleon same-spin pair probability and is based on the density-matrix. The method is used to study spatial localization of light nuclei within the Hartree-Fock approximation. We show that the method provides an alternative tool for studying spatial localization in comparison to the localization observed from maxima in the nuclear mass density.

## Key claim
The localization measure derived from the density-matrix provides an alternative tool for identifying alpha-cluster substructures in light nuclei that reveals more detailed features than the total nuclear mass density alone, but the authors note that full identification of alpha clusters also requires checking correlations between the four nucleons, making the localization function only the minimum necessary condition.

## Method
The authors computed static Hartree-Fock (HF) solutions on a Cartesian three-dimensional mesh with 1 fm grid spacing using the Skyrme energy functional with the SkI3 parametrization. They applied a localization measure originally developed for electronic systems (the electron localization function, ELF) that depends on kinetic energy density, density gradients, and current density derived from the one-body density-matrix. The localization function C_{qσ}(r) was evaluated for protons and neutrons separately in light N=Z nuclei up to ^{40}Ca and for neutron-rich ^{20}C.

## Key numbers
- Grid spacing of 1 fm with a box size of (-15.5, +15.5) fm in each dimension
- The density contour at half nuclear matter density (ρ = 0.08 fm^{-3}) was used as a reference in localization plots
- For ^{4}He, perfect localization with C = 1 was found in all regions where ρ > 10^{-4} fm^{-3}
- The homogeneous Fermi gas limit yields C = 1/2, which was observed throughout most of ^{16}O and compact nuclei like ^{12}C and ^{40}Ca
- The localization function for ^{16}O showed a pronounced central dip not visible in the density profile, while the density dip at center was "barely visible"

## Context
This work extends the electron localization function (ELF) formalism developed by Becke and Edgecombe (1990) for molecular systems to nuclear physics, providing a density-matrix-based alternative to traditional cluster identification from mass density maxima. The method enables systematic study of alpha-cluster structures in light nuclei within the mean-field approximation, complementing earlier cluster-model approaches such as those by Ikeda and collaborators (2001, 2006) and the time-dependent HF studies of chain configurations by Umar and collaborators (2010). The results show that stretched isomers of ^{12}C and ^{16}O exhibit clear alpha-chain structures with C ≈ 1, while compact ground states like ^{12}C and ^{16}O show predominantly metallic binding with C ≈ 1/2.
