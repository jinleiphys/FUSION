# The scenario of two families of compact stars 2. Transition from hadronic to quark matter and explosive phenomena

> We will follow the two-families scenario described in the accompanying paper, in which compact stars having a very small radius and masses not exceeding about 1.5$M_\odot$ are made of hadrons, while more massive compact stars are quark stars. In the present paper we discuss the dynamics of the transition of a hadronic star into a quark star. We will show that the transition takes place in two phases: a very rapid one, lasting a few milliseconds, during which the central region of the star converts into quark matter and the process of conversion is accelerated by the existence of strong hydrodynamical instabilities, and a second phase, lasting about ten seconds, during which the process of conversion proceeds till the surface of the star via production and diffusion of strangeness. We will show that these two steps play a crucial role in the phenomenological implications of the model. We will discuss the possible implications of this scenario both for long and for short Gamma Ray Bursts, using the proto-magnetar model as the reference frame of our discussion. We will show that the process of quark deconfinement can be connected to specific observed features of the GRBs. In the case of long GRBs we will discuss the possibility that quark deconfinement is at the origin of the second peak present in quite a large fraction of bursts. Also we will discuss the possibility that long GRBs can take place in binary systems without being associated with a SN explosion. Concerning short GRBs, quark deconfinement can play the crucial role in limiting their duration. Finally we will shortly revisit the possible relevance of quark deconfinement in some specific type of Supernova explosions, in particular in the case of very massive progenitors.

## Key claim

The transition of a hadronic star into a quark star proceeds in two distinct phases: a rapid turbulent burning lasting a few milliseconds driven by hydrodynamical instabilities, followed by a slow diffusive burning lasting about ten seconds governed by strangeness production and diffusion, with the separation between regimes determined by the Coll's condition on the energy densities of the two phases.

## Method

The authors model the conversion of hadronic stars into quark stars by dividing the process into a turbulent regime described by relativistic hydrodynamics with an infinitely thin flame front, and a diffusive regime described by a reaction-diffusion-advection equation for strange quark concentration coupled to a thermal evolution equation. They solve the Euler equations in 3+1D using a finite volume piecewise parabolic method with a level-set approach for the flame front, and separately solve the coupled differential equations for flame front propagation and neutrino luminosity during the diffusive phase. The analysis uses the Bodmer-Witten hypothesis of absolute stability of strange quark matter and assumes the critical density for deconfinement corresponds to the density at which hyperons or kaons start forming.

## Key numbers

- Hadronic stars have masses not exceeding about 1.5 solar masses, while more massive compact stars are quark stars
- The rapid turbulent burning phase lasts a few milliseconds (approximately 4 ms in the simulation shown)
- The slow diffusive burning phase lasts about ten seconds (few tens of seconds for the specific case of a 1.5 solar mass star)
- The quark diffusion coefficient D is about 10^{-1} cm^2/s for quark chemical potential around 300 MeV and temperature around 10 MeV
- The time for conversion of down quarks into strange quarks tau is about 10^{-9} s for quark chemical potential around 300 MeV
- The width of the combustion zone delta is about 10^{-5} cm
- The laminar burning velocity v is about 10^3-10^4 cm/s
- The heat capacity of the star is C = 2 x 10^{39} (M/M_sun) (T/10^9 K) erg/K
- The initial temperature after the turbulent regime is about 5 MeV
- The total energy released during the turbulent regime Q is about 8.5 x 10^{52} erg with a decay time tau of about 3 s
- The mass left unburnt after the turbulent regime is about 0.5 solar masses within a layer about 3 km thick
- The neutrino luminosity during the diffusive regime displays a quasi-plateau feature
- The burning velocity scales as v_lf proportional to T^{-5/6}

## Context

This paper extends the two-families scenario presented in the accompanying paper (Drago and Pagliara, 2015) where compact stars below about 1.5 solar masses are hadronic and more massive ones are quark stars. It builds on the Bodmer-Witten hypothesis (Bodmer, 1971; Witten, 1984) about absolute stability of strange quark matter and the kinetic theory approach of Olinto (1987) for slow combustion. The work enables predictions for gamma-ray burst phenomenology, connecting quark deconfinement to features in both long and short GRBs, including the possibility that long GRBs can occur in binary systems without associated supernova explosions and that quark deconfinement limits the duration of short GRBs.
