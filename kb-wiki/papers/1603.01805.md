# Consistency between the monopole strength of the Hoyle state determined by structural calculation and that extracted from reaction observables

> We analyze the $α$-$^{12}$C inelastic scattering to the $0^+_2$ state of $^{12}$C, the Hoyle state, in a fully microscopic framework. With no free adjustable parameter, the inelastic cross sections at forward angles are well reproduced by the microscopic reaction calculation using the transition density of $^{12}$C obtained by the resonating group method and the nucleon-nucleon $g$ matrix interaction developed by the Melbourne group. It is thus shown that the monopole transition strength obtained by the structural calculation is consistent with that extracted from the reaction observable, suggesting no missing monopole strength of the Hoyle state.

## Key claim

The monopole transition strength of the Hoyle state (0₂⁺ of ¹²C) obtained from a resonating group method structural calculation is consistent with the strength extracted from α-¹²C inelastic scattering data, provided a fully microscopic reaction framework is used; this suggests there is no missing monopole strength, contrary to claims from earlier semi-microscopic analyses.

## Method

The authors performed a fully microscopic coupled-channels calculation of α-¹²C inelastic scattering to the 0₂⁺ state using the Melbourne g-matrix interaction with the target-density approximation, with no free adjustable parameters. The transition densities of ¹²C for six states (0₁⁺, 2₁⁺, 0₂⁺, 3₁⁻, 2₂⁺, and 0₃⁺) were taken from a three-α resonating group method calculation, and the α ground-state density was taken from electron scattering data.

## Key numbers

- At 172.5, 240, and 386 MeV incident α energy, the calculated inelastic cross sections to the 0₂⁺ state reproduce experimental data well at forward angles with no free parameters.
- The monopole transition strength from the RGM calculation is consistent with the value extracted from reaction observables; a previous antisymmetrized molecular dynamics calculation gave 6.6 e fm², while the value deduced from comparing theory with experiment was 4.5 ± 0.5 e fm², a gap of about 30%.
- The renormalization factors for the real (imaginary) part of the interaction used in the prior work (Cuo13) were 1.05 (1.27) at 240 MeV and 1.24 (1.38) at 386 MeV, whereas the present calculation requires no such adjustment.

## Context

This work resolves the "missing monopole strength" puzzle reported in earlier studies (Tak06, Kho08, Ito11, Kaw14), which used semi-microscopic treatments of the distorting and coupling potentials and found that calculated cross sections overshot the data. The fully microscopic approach with the Melbourne g-matrix and RGM densities shows consistency between structural and reaction information, and the authors note that a recent analysis by Cuo13 still had a 30% gap between the structural input and the reaction observable, requiring renormalization of the interaction strength.
