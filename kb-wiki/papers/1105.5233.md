# Instability of Compressible Drops and Jets

> We revisit the classic problem of the stability of drops and jets held by surface tension, while regarding the compressibility of bulk fluids and spatial dimensions as free parameters. By mode analysis, it is shown that there exists a critical compressibility above which the drops (and disks) become unstable for a spherical perturbation. For a given value of compressibility (and those of the surface tension and density at the equilibrium), this instability criterion provides a minimal radius below which the drop cannot be a stable equilibrium. According to the existence of the above unstable mode of drop, which corresponds to a homogeneous perturbation of cylindrical jet, the dispersion relation of Rayleigh-Plateau instability for cylinders drastically changes. In particular, we identify another critical compressibility above which the homogeneous unstable mode is predominant. The analysis is done for non-relativistic and relativistic perfect fluids, of which self-gravity is ignored.

## Key claim

The paper shows that for compressible fluids held by surface tension, there exists a critical compressibility above which spherical drops and cylindrical jets become unstable to homogeneous (spherically symmetric or uniform) perturbations, yielding a minimum stable radius; however, the author notes that for ordinary fluids like water at room temperature this minimum radius is extremely small (approximately 10⁻¹¹ m), so the instability is not expected to play a central role in typical macroscopic dynamics.

## Method

The author performs a linear mode analysis of the Euler equations (non-relativistic and relativistic) for inviscid perfect fluids, supplemented by the Young-Laplace stress balance at the surface and a kinematic boundary condition. The equilibrium is a static cylinder or sphere; perturbations are expanded in sinusoidal modes along the cylinder axis and in modified Bessel functions radially, yielding a dispersion relation whose zeros determine stability. The analysis treats the spatial dimension as a free parameter n (where the fluid occupies ℝ¹,ⁿ⁺² or ℝ¹,ⁿ⁺¹), and compressibility is parameterized by β, the reciprocal of the sound speed scaled by surface tension and density.

## Key numbers

* For non-relativistic droplets (n=2), the critical compressibility parameter is β_{c,1} = 1.225; the minimum stable radius is r_min = (2/3) σ / (ρ₀ c_s²).
* For water at 25°C (σ = 72.0 × 10⁻³ J/m², ρ₀ = 1.00 × 10³ kg/m³, c_s = 1.50 × 10³ m/s), the minimum radius is approximately 2.13 × 10⁻¹¹ m.
* For non-relativistic cylinders (n=1), β_{c,1} = 1.414; the most unstable wavenumber at this critical point is k̂_{*,1} = 0.4388 and the growth rate is ω̂_{*,1} = 0.5244.
* A second critical compressibility β_{c,2} = 1.489 (for n=1) exists above which the homogeneous (k=0) mode becomes the most unstable; the corresponding growth rate is ω̂_{*,2} = 0.6388.
* For relativistic fluids, the minimum radius generalizes to r_min = [n/(n+1)] [σ/(ε₀ c_s²)] [1 − (n+1)c_s²], where c_s² = dp/dε and the speed of light is set to unity.

## Context

This work revisits the classic stability problems of drops (Kelvin, Rayleigh) and jets (Plateau, Rayleigh, Chandrasekhar) by introducing finite compressibility, which had not been systematically studied previously. The analysis extends the incompressible results of Rayleigh (1879) and Chandrasekhar (1961) and reproduces them in the c_s → ∞ limit. The existence of a minimum stable radius is analogous to the Chandrasekhar (1964) instability of self-gravitating fluid balls when the adiabatic index exceeds 4/3, but here the restoring force is surface tension rather than gravity. The results enable future study of whether any physical systems (e.g., in nuclear or astrophysical contexts) exhibit macroscopic minimal radii where this instability becomes dynamically relevant.
