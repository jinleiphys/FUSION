# Calculation of exciton densities in SMMC

> We develop a shell-model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method to calculate densities of states with varying exciton (particle-hole) number. We then apply this method to the doubly closed-shell nucleus 40Ca in a full 0s-1d-0f-1p shell-model space and compare our results to those found using approximate analytic expressions for the partial densities. We find that the effective one-body level density is reduced by approximately 22% when a residual two-body interaction is included in the shell model calculation.

## Key claim

The effective one-body level density parameter \(g_{\rm eff}\) is reduced by approximately 22% when a residual two-body interaction is included in the shell-model calculation, but this reduction is extracted from fits to the ratio of partition functions \(Y_{N_e}(\beta)\) and is rather independent of exciton number for \(N_e \ge 2\).

## Method

The authors used shell-model Monte Carlo (SMMC) techniques to calculate the ratio of the particle-hole partition function \(Z_{N_e}(\beta)\) to the full partition function \(Z_A(\beta)\) for \({}^{40}\)Ca in a full \(0s\)-\(1d\)-\(0f\)-\(1p\) model space. They introduced a second number projection operator \(\hat{P}_{N_e}\) to project onto states with a given number of particles in the \(sd\)-shell and \(fp\)-shell, thereby isolating exciton configurations. The realistic two-body interaction was fitted to a pairing-plus-multipole Hamiltonian free from the Monte Carlo sign problem, and the partial densities of states \(\rho_{N_e}(E)\) were obtained via a saddle-point approximation from the calculated \(Z_{N_e}(\beta)\) and \(E_{N_e}(\beta)\).

## Key numbers

* The total number of SMMC states at \(\beta=0\) was \(3.834 \times 10^{16}\), compared to the exact value of \(5.095 \times 10^{16}\).
* For the non-interacting case, the effective level density parameter was \(a = 5.43\) MeV\(^{-1}\) (\(g_{\rm eff} = 3.3\) MeV\(^{-1}\)).
* For the interacting case, the effective level density parameter was \(a = 4.27\) MeV\(^{-1}\) (\(g_{\rm eff} = 2.6\) MeV\(^{-1}\)), a reduction of 22%.
* The single-particle energies used were 0.0, 5.36, 0.64, 8.21, 14.21, 10.14, and 12.07 MeV for the \(0d_{5/2}\), \(0d_{3/2}\), \(1s_{1/2}\), \(0f_{7/2}\), \(0f_{5/2}\), \(1p_{3/2}\), and \(1p_{1/2}\) orbitals, respectively.
* The fitted interaction parameters were \(g_0 = -0.63\) MeV, \(\chi_2 = -0.047\) MeV fm\(^{-4}\), \(\chi_4 = -0.001\) MeV fm\(^{-8}\), and \(\chi_6 = -0.17 \times 10^{-3}\) MeV fm\(^{-12}\).

## Context

This work extends earlier analytic models of exciton densities, such as the Williams formula (Williams 1971) and its proton-neutron counterparts, which assumed uniform single-particle level spacing and an inexact treatment of the Pauli principle. It also goes beyond approximate treatments of residual interactions, such as those by Pluhaf and Weidenmuller (1988), by performing the first full interacting shell-model calculation of partial level densities in a large model space. The method enables future studies of partial decay rates in nuclear preequilibrium emission and can be extended to larger model spaces or open-shell nuclei such as \({}^{42}\)Ca.
