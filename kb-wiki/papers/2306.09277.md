# Robust universal relations in neutron star asteroseismology

> The non-radial oscillations of the neutron stars (NSs) have been suggested as a useful tool to probe the composition of neutron star matter (NSM). With this scope in mind, we consider a large number of equations of states (EOSs) that are consistent with nuclear matter properties and pure neutron matter EOS based on a chiral effective field theory (chEFT) calculation for the low densities and perturbative QCD EOS at very high densities. This ensemble of EOSs is also consistent with astronomical observations, gravitational waves in GW170817, mass and radius measurements from Neutron star Interior Composition ExploreR (NICER). We analyze the robustness of known universal relations (URs) among the quadrupolar $f$ mode frequencies, masses and radii with such a large number of EOSs and we find a new UR that results from a strong correlation between the $f$ mode frequencies and the radii of NSs. Such a correlation is very useful in accurately determining the radius from a measurement of $f$ mode frequencies in the near future. We also show that the quadrupolar $f$ mode frequencies of NS of masses 2.0 M$_\odot$ and above lie in the range $\sim$ 2-3 kHz in this ensemble of physically realistic EOSs. A NS of mass 2M$_{\odot}$ with a low $f$ mode frequency may indicate the existence of non-nucleonic degrees of freedom.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a new universal relation (UR3) exists between the quadrupolar f-mode frequency and the radius of neutron stars, with a Pearson correlation coefficient between 0.98 and 0.99 for masses from 1.6 to 2.4 solar masses, but this result is obtained within the Cowling approximation which neglects metric perturbations and can overestimate frequencies by 10-30% compared to full general relativity.

## Method

The authors constructed two ensembles of equations of state: a nucleonic set (DDB) of approximately 14,000 EOSs based on relativistic mean field theory constrained by Bayesian analysis, and a hybrid set (DDB-Hyb) of 55,000 EOSs that use the DDB EOS below twice saturation density and perturbative QCD at very high densities, with two piecewise polytropes interpolating the intermediate region. They solved the non-radial oscillation equations within the Cowling approximation for quadrupolar (l=2) f-modes, imposing boundary conditions at the stellar center and surface to obtain the eigenvalue problem for the oscillation frequency.

## Key numbers

- Quadrupolar f-mode frequencies for neutron stars of masses 2.0 solar masses and above lie in the range 2.1-2.7 kHz for the DDB-Hyb set and 2.3-2.65 kHz for the DDB set
- For UR1 (f versus sqrt(M/R^3)): slope a = 22.27 +/- 0.023 kHz.km (DDB-Hyb) and 26.76 +/- 0.01 kHz.km (DDB), with maximum relative percentage error of 6.0% (DDB-Hyb) and 4.5% (DDB) at 90% CI
- For UR2a (omega M versus M/R): slope a = 0.6474 +/- 4.6e-5 (DDB-Hyb) and 0.6549 +/- 2.6e-5 (DDB), with maximum relative percentage error of 3.78% (DDB-Hyb) and 2.20% (DDB) at 90% CI
- For UR3 (f versus R): slope m between -0.23 and -0.21 with intercept c between 5.21 and 5.0 for masses 1.6-2.4 solar masses, giving approximately 1.5% relative residual within 90% CI
- Predicted f-mode frequency for PSR J0740+6620 (mass 2.08 +/- 0.7 solar masses, radius 12.35 +/- 0.75 km) is 2.35 kHz from UR2a and 2.36 kHz from UR2b

## Context

This work extends previous studies of universal relations in neutron star asteroseismology by Andersson and Kokkotas (1997), Benhar et al. (1998), and Doneva et al. (2013) by using a much larger ensemble of EOSs consistent with modern constraints from GW170817 and NICER observations. The new UR3 between f-mode frequency and radius enables precise radius determination from future gravitational wave detections, with the authors noting that measuring f-mode frequency within approximately 2% uncertainty would yield radius measurements with approximately 0.2 km uncertainty. The work also confirms that UR2 (involving omega M versus compactness) is more robust than UR1, which shows approximately 5% dispersion and depends on whether non-nucleonic degrees of freedom are present.
