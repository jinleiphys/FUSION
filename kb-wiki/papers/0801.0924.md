# Spin-orbit and tensor mean-field effects on spin-orbit splitting including self-consistent core polarizations

> A new strategy of fitting the coupling constants of the nuclear energy density functional is proposed, which shifts attention from ground-state bulk to single-particle properties. The latter are analyzed in terms of the bare single-particle energies and mass, shape, and spin core-polarization effects. Fit of the isoscalar spin-orbit and both isoscalar and isovector tensor coupling constants directly to the f5/2-f7/2 spin-orbit splittings in 40Ca, 56Ni, and 48Ca is proposed as a practical realization of this new programme. It is shown that this fit requires drastic changes in the isoscalar spin-orbit strength and the tensor coupling constants as compared to the commonly accepted values but it considerably and systematically improves basic single-particle properties including spin-orbit splittings and magic-gap energies. Impact of these changes on nuclear binding energies is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that fitting the isoscalar spin-orbit and both isoscalar and isovector tensor coupling constants directly to the f5/2-f7/2 spin-orbit splittings in 40Ca, 56Ni, and 48Ca requires drastic changes in the isoscalar spin-orbit strength and tensor coupling constants compared to commonly accepted values, but this fit systematically improves single-particle properties including spin-orbit splittings and magic-gap energies, even at the expense of binding energy quality.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations using the SkP, SLy4, and SkO functionals, extended with tensor terms and with time-odd coupling constants readjusted to reproduce empirical Landau parameters. They computed total energies of doubly-magic cores and their odd neighbors by blocking specific single-particle orbitals, then extracted single-particle energies as mass differences between odd and even-even nuclei, including self-consistent mass, shape, and spin core-polarization effects. The isoscalar spin-orbit coupling constant and both isoscalar and isovector tensor coupling constants were fitted to reproduce the f7/2-f5/2 spin-orbit splittings in 40Ca, 56Ni, and 48Ca.

## Key numbers

- The f7/2-f5/2 spin-orbit splitting in 40Ca: experimental value is 5.24 MeV (from the difference between the 1νf7/2 at -8.62 MeV and 1νf5/2 at -3.38 MeV)
- The f7/2-f5/2 spin-orbit splitting in 48Ca: experimental value is 8.01 MeV (from the difference between the 1νf7/2 at -9.68 MeV and 1νf5/2 at -1.67 MeV)
- The center-of-mass correction shift in 40Ca equals 0.40 MeV, which almost exactly corresponds to the entire mass polarization effect
- Bare single-particle energies for 1νf7/2 in 40Ca: -9.58 MeV; after all polarizations: -9.00 MeV; experimental: -8.62 MeV
- Bare single-particle energies for 1νf5/2 in 40Ca: -1.38 MeV; after all polarizations: -0.99 MeV; experimental: -3.38 MeV

## Context

This work extends the energy density functional approach by shifting focus from ground-state bulk properties to single-particle properties, following earlier suggestions by Rutz et al. (1999) within the relativistic mean-field approach. The study builds on recent interest in tensor interactions revived by shell-model interpretations of new magic shell-openings around N=32 by Otsuka et al. (2001, 2005) and Honma et al. (2005), and extends mean-field analyses by Dobaczewski et al. (2006), Brown et al. (2006), and Lesinski et al. (2007). The proposed refitting enables future development of spectroscopic-quality energy density functionals that correctly describe nuclear shell structure, with the understanding that precise adjustment of bulk properties must be revisited after such extensions are implemented.
