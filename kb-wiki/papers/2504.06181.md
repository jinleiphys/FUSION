# Interface modes in inspiralling neutron stars: A gravitational-wave probe of first-order phase transitions

> At the extreme densities in neutron stars, a phase transition to deconfined quark matter is anticipated. Yet masses, radii and tidal deformabilities offer only indirect measures of a first-order phase transition, requiring many detections to resolve or being ineffective observables if the discontinuity exists at lower densities. We report on a smoking-gun gravitational-wave signature of a first-order transition: the resonant tidal excitation of an interface mode. Using relativistic perturbation theory with an equation-of-state family informed by chiral effective field theory, we show that such a resonance may be detectable with next-generation interferometers and possibly already with LIGO A+ for sufficiently loud events.

# Interface modes in inspiralling neutron stars: A gravitational-wave probe of first-order phase transitions

## Key claim

The resonant tidal excitation of an interfacial *i*-mode during a neutron-star binary inspiral may provide a "smoking-gun" gravitational-wave signature of a first-order phase transition to deconfined quark matter, potentially detectable with next-generation interferometers and possibly already with LIGO A+ for sufficiently loud events, but the analysis relies on a Newtonian approximation for the phase shift that has not yet been justified by a fully relativistic treatment of the dynamical tide.

## Method

The authors use general-relativistic linear perturbation theory in the Cowling approximation (ignoring metric perturbations) to compute the eigenfrequencies, eigenfunctions, and tidal overlap integrals of interfacial *i*-modes for a family of barotropic equations of state generated by sampling the speed of sound squared, with first-order phase transitions of arbitrary location and width included. The resulting mode parameters are inserted into a Newtonian estimate of the orbital phase shift due to tidal resonance, and these phase shifts are compared against noise power spectral densities of current and future gravitational-wave detectors for an equal-mass binary at 40 Mpc.

## Key numbers

- The analytical incompressible model gives an *i*-mode frequency of approximately 686 Hz for a relative density jump of 0.1, a 1.4 solar mass star, and a 10 km radius.
- The tidal overlap integral for the *i*-mode is of order 1e-2 in the incompressible model, at least an order of magnitude larger than typical quadrupolar *g*-mode overlaps of 1e-5 to 1e-4.
- The phase-shift estimates are computed for an equal-mass binary at a luminosity distance of 40 Mpc (the inferred distance to GW170817).
- All identified *i*-mode resonances occur within the last 5 orbits before merger, based on the estimated merger frequency from 2013.
- For 1.4 solar mass stars, the majority of interface-mode resonances may be detectable at the LIGO A+ level, and nearly all would be within reach of Cosmic Explorer and the Einstein Telescope.
- For 1.8 solar mass equal-mass systems, approximately half of the models would still be detectable by next-generation instruments.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of tidal resonances in neutron-star binaries, which have focused on compositional *g*-modes (Reuter 1994, Lai 1994, Kokkotas 1995, Yu 2017, Kuan 2021, Counsell 2025) and found that their weak tidal coupling (overlaps of order 1e-5 to 1e-4) may be too small to produce a measurable gravitational-wave phase shift. The *i*-mode approach provides a direct probe of first-order phase transitions that is complementary to measurements of mass, radius, and tidal deformability, which suffer from the "masquerade problem" (Alford 2005) where phase-transition signatures can be masked or degenerate with other equation-of-state features. The work enables a new observational strategy for identifying first-order phase transitions in single gravitational-wave events, though it calls for fully relativistic dynamical-tide models to replace the Newtonian approximation used here.
