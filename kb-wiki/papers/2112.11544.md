# The Impact of Neutron Transfer Reactions on Heating and Cooling of Accreted Neutron Star Crusts

> Nuclear reactions heat and cool the crust of accreting neutron stars and need to be understood to interpret observations of X-ray bursts and of long-term cooling in transiently accreting systems. It was recently suggested that previously neglected neutron transfer reactions may play a significant role in the nuclear processes. We present results from full nuclear network calculations that now include these reactions and determine their impact on crust composition, crust impurity, heating, and cooling. We find that a large number of neutron transfer reactions indeed occur and impact crust models. In particular, we identify a new type of reaction cycle that brings a pair of nuclei across the nuclear chart into equilibrium via alternating neutron capture and neutron release, interspersed with a neutron transfer. While neutron transfer reactions lead to changes in crust model predictions, and need to be considered in future studies, previous conclusions concerning heating, cooling, and compositional evolution are remarkably robust.

## Key claim

Neutron transfer reactions do occur in large numbers in accreting neutron star crusts and affect crust composition, heating, and cooling, but previous conclusions about heating, cooling, and compositional evolution are "remarkably robust", the predicted early destruction of odd-A nuclei and drastic suppression of crust Urca cooling is not as dramatic as suggested by Chugunov (2019).

## Method

The authors performed full nuclear reaction network calculations for an accreted fluid element in a plane-parallel 1D approximation, following composition changes as a function of depth (increasing density) at constant temperature 0.5 GK. They implemented neutron transfer reaction rates using the approximation of Chugunov (2019) based on Fermi's golden rule, including all reactions with positive Q-value and timescale faster than 10^12 s at the highest densities. Two initial compositions were explored: superburst ashes (A=50-60 range) and rp-process ashes (A=20-108 range), using networks of 894 nuclei (120,928 transfer reactions) and 1400 nuclei (299,825 transfer reactions) respectively.

## Key numbers

- For superburst ashes, the first significant neutron transfer reactions (time-integrated flow above 10^-8 mole/g) set in at density ρ = 4.6 × 10^10 g cm^-3; between that density and 5.1 × 10^10 g cm^-3 there are 42 such reactions, and up to ρ = 1.5 × 10^12 g cm^-3 there are 1053 such reactions.
- The largest time-integrated reaction flow for superburst ashes is 400 mole/g for the 55Ca(22O,23O)54Ca neutron transfer reaction, setting in at ρ = 1.7 × 10^11 g cm^-3.
- For rp-process ashes, the final composition near ρ = 1.6 × 10^12 g cm^-3 consolidates into two dominant species: 70Ca (abundance Y = 1.0 × 10^-2) and 46Si (Y = 9.8 × 10^-4); without transfer reactions there were four dominant species including 40Mg (Y = 8.4 × 10^-5) and 116Se (Y = 4.4 × 10^-4).
- For rp-process ashes, at the depth of the critical 108Se branchpoint (around ρ = 3.2 × 10^11 g cm^-3), neutron transfer reactions reduce the free neutron abundance by more than an order of magnitude compared to calculations without transfer reactions.

## Context

This work extends the crust model calculations of Lau (2018) by adding neutron transfer reactions that were suggested to be important by Chugunov (2019). The results show that while neutron transfer reactions do affect crust composition and Urca cooling, their impact is more nuanced than predicted, they can both enhance and suppress odd-A abundances depending on the specific density regime and initial composition. The findings enable more accurate modeling of neutron star crust cooling curves used to probe dense matter physics, but also highlight remaining nuclear uncertainties in rp-process nucleosynthesis and neutron-drip line nuclear structure that need experimental constraints.
