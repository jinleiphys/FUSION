# UNEDF: Advanced Scientific Computing Collaboration Transforms the Low-Energy Nuclear Many-Body Problem

> The demands of cutting-edge science are driving the need for larger and faster computing resources. With the rapidly growing scale of computing systems and the prospect of technologically disruptive architectures to meet these needs, scientists face the challenge of effectively using complex computational resources to advance scientific discovery. Multidisciplinary collaborating networks of researchers with diverse scientific backgrounds are needed to address these complex challenges. The UNEDF SciDAC collaboration of nuclear theorists, applied mathematicians, and computer scientists is developing a comprehensive description of nuclei and their reactions that delivers maximum predictive power with quantified uncertainties. This paper describes UNEDF and identifies attributes that classify it as a successful computational collaboration. We illustrate significant milestones accomplished by UNEDF through integrative solutions using the most reliable theoretical approaches, most advanced algorithms, and leadership-class computational resources.

## Key claim
The UNEDF collaboration claims to be developing a comprehensive description of nuclei and their reactions that delivers maximum predictive power with quantified uncertainties, but the paper primarily describes the collaboration's structure, computational achievements, and methodological advances rather than presenting a fully validated predictive theory.

## Method
The UNEDF collaboration integrated three major theoretical approaches across the nuclear landscape: ab initio methods (Green's function Monte Carlo, coupled-cluster, no-core shell model) for light nuclei, configuration interaction techniques for medium-mass nuclei, and nuclear density functional theory for heavy nuclei. The collaboration combined these with advanced algorithms (POUNDerS optimization, ADLB load balancing) and leadership-class computing resources (Jaguar at OLCF, Intrepid at ALCF) to perform large-scale simulations. Cross-disciplinary teams of nuclear theorists, applied mathematicians, and computer scientists worked on code development, uncertainty quantification, and verification/validation procedures.

## Key numbers
- Approximately 3,000 known nuclei exist, with an estimated 6,000 nuclei that could still be created in laboratories and the Cosmos
- The MFDn calculation for carbon-14 involved diagonalization of a Hamiltonian matrix of dimension 2 billion, using 214,668 cores on Jaguar
- UNEDF projects consistently used over 60% of their Jaguar allocation for leadership-class jobs (jobs using >20% of the resource) across 2008-2011
- Combined CPU-hour utilization across Jaguar and Intrepid was nearly 20 million CPU-hours in 2008 and increased more than threefold by 2011
- For 2012, UNEDF members were granted the sixth largest allocation of the 60 INCITE projects awarded
- The missing binding energy in calcium-48 from chiral nucleon-nucleon interactions alone was 400 keV per nucleon, attributed to chiral three-nucleon forces
- The TDSLDA implementation accommodates a basis set 2-3 orders of magnitude larger than other approaches

## Context
This paper describes the UNEDF SciDAC collaboration, which extended prior work in nuclear density functional theory (such as the Skyrme and Gogny functionals) and ab initio methods by adding systematic optimization, uncertainty quantification, and high-performance computing capabilities. The collaboration's developments enable future work on predictive calculations for unstable nuclei, neutron-induced fission, and nuclear reactions relevant to astrophysics and energy applications, building on earlier efforts by Bulgac (2011) on time-dependent superfluid density functional theory and by Hagen and collaborators on coupled-cluster calculations for medium-mass nuclei.
