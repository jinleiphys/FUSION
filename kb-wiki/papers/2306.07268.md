# Derivation of transition density from the observed 4He(e,e')4He(0^+_2) form factor raising the alpha-particle monopole puzzle

> Recently, the monopole transition form factor of the electron-scattering excitation of the 0^+_2 state (E_x=20.21 MeV) of the 4He nucleus was observed over a broad momentum transfer range (0.5 < q^2 < 5.0 fm^-2) with dramatically improved preision compared with older sets of data; modern nuclear forces, including those derived from the chiral effective field theory, failed to reproduce the form factor, which is called the alpha-particle monopole puzzle. To resolve this puzzle by improving the study of spatial structure of the 0^+_2 state, we derive in this letter a possible 0^+_1 --> 0^+_2 transition density ρ_tr(r) for r > 1 fm from the observed form factor. The shape of the transition density is significantly different from that obtained theoretically in the literature

# Derivation of transition density from the observed 4He(e,e')4He(0+_2) form factor raising the alpha-particle monopole puzzle

## Key claim

The observed monopole transition form factor for the 0+_1 → 0+_2 excitation in 4He, measured with dramatically improved precision over a broad momentum transfer range (0.5 < q² < 5.0 fm⁻²), cannot be reproduced by modern nuclear forces including chiral effective field theory potentials; the author derives a possible transition density ρ_tr(r) for r ≳ 1 fm from the observed form factor, finding its shape significantly different from theoretical predictions in the literature, but notes that the observed data limited to q² ≤ 5 fm⁻² cannot provide information about the central-depression structure at r ≲ 1 fm.

## Method

The author fits the observed mass form factor |F̃_M0+(q²)|/q² from Kegel et al. (2023) as a sum of two Gaussians in q² (Eq. 2) over the range 0 ≤ q² ≤ 5.5 fm⁻², then extends this parameterization to all q² to perform a Fourier transform yielding an analytic transition density ρ_tr(r) (Eq. 8). An additional Gaussian term is introduced to artificially simulate the central depression of the transition density at r ≲ 1 fm, demonstrating that the observed data cannot constrain this inner region. The derived density is compared with the theoretical densities from Hiyama et al. (2004) using the AV8' + central 3N potential and from Bacca et al. (2013, 2015) using χEFT and AV18+UIX potentials.

## Key numbers

- The observed form factor covers momentum transfer range 0.5 ≤ q² ≤ 5.0 fm⁻², with relative error in |F_M0+(q²)|² of 4%–6% except 11% at the lowest q²
- The fitted Gaussian parameters are (a₁, a₂) = (0.092, 0.035) fm² and (b₁, b₂) = (0.54, 2.5) fm² for the two-Gaussian approximation of |F̃_M0+(q²)|/q²
- The derived transition density ρ_tr(r) from Eq. 8 has a node at r = 1.82 fm, with coefficients A₁ = 4.8 × 10⁻² fm⁻³ and A₂ = 4.0 × 10⁻⁵ fm⁻³
- The experimental monopole matrix element ⟨r²⟩_tr = 1.53 ± 0.05 fm² and transition radius R_tr = 4.56 ± 0.15 fm from Kegel et al. (2023)
- The red-solid-line density (Eq. 8) yields ⟨r²⟩_tr = 1.52 fm² and R_tr = 4.65 fm, matching experiment
- The 0+_2 state excitation energy is E_x = 20.21 MeV, with a proton width Γ_p = 0.50 MeV
- The node position of the theoretical density from Hiyama et al. (2004) is at r = 1.62 fm, and their form factor has a node at q² = 14.1 fm⁻²

## Context

This work directly addresses the "alpha-particle monopole puzzle" identified by Kegel et al. (2023), where modern ab initio calculations using realistic interactions (AV18+UIX from Bacca et al. (2013) and χEFT potentials) fail to reproduce the newly measured high-precision transition form factor, while the simplified AV8' + central 3N potential of Hiyama et al. (2004) agrees at low q² but not overall. The derived transition density provides a data-driven spatial structure for the 0+_2 state that can guide future theoretical efforts; the author suggests a strategy using bound-state approximation to search interaction parameters that reproduce the 0+_2 energy, followed by explicit 3H+p scattering calculations (such as Viviani et al. (2020)) to properly treat the Feshbach resonance nature of the state.
