# Higher-rank discrete symmetries in the IBM. II Octahedral shapes: Dynamical symmetries

> The symmetries of the sdg-IBM, the interacting boson model with s, d and g bosons, are studied as regards the occurrence of shapes with octahedral symmetry. It is shown that no sdg-IBM Hamiltonian with a dynamical symmetry displays in its classical limit an isolated minimum with octahedral shape. However, a degenerate minimum that includes a shape with octahedral symmetry can be obtained from a Hamiltonian that is transitional between two limits, U_g(9) x U_d(5) and SO_sg(10) x U_d(5), and the conditions for its existence are derived. An isolated minimum with octahedral shape, either an octahedron or a cube, may arise through a modification of two-body interactions between the g bosons. Comments on the observational consequences of this construction are made.

## Key claim

No sdg-IBM Hamiltonian with a dynamical symmetry displays an isolated minimum with octahedral shape in its classical limit; however, a degenerate minimum that includes a shape with octahedral symmetry can be obtained from a Hamiltonian transitional between the U_g(9) × U_d(5) and SO_sg(10) × U_d(5) limits, and an isolated minimum (octahedron or cube) may arise through modification of two-body interactions between the g bosons.

## Method

The authors studied the classical limit of the sdg-IBM by computing the expectation value of rotationally invariant Hamiltonians in coherent states parameterized by deformation parameters β₂, β₄, γ₂, γ₄, and δ₄. They analyzed the energy surfaces of Hamiltonians with dynamical symmetries associated with the algebraic lattice U(15) ⊃ U_d(5) ⊗ U_sg(10), focusing on the U_g(9) and SO_sg(10) limits and their transitional Hamiltonian. A catastrophe analysis of the energy surface was performed to derive conditions for minima at zero quadrupole and non-zero hexadecapole deformation, and additional g-boson interactions were introduced to create δ₄ dependence and isolate octahedral minima.

## Key numbers

- The hexadecapole deformation at the minimum for the transitional Hamiltonian example is β₄* ≈ 0.34 (for N=5 bosons, with parameters ε_d − ε_s = 1200 keV, ε_g − ε_s = 1500 keV, b_d = 40 keV, c_d = 10 keV, b_sg = 150 keV, b_g = 25 keV, c_g = c = 5 keV).
- A repulsive g-boson interaction v²_gggg = 500 keV produces three degenerate isolated minima with octahedral symmetry at δ₄* = 0 (octahedron), δ₄* = arccos(1/6) ≈ 84.4°, and δ₄* = π (cube).
- The condition for a minimum with β₂* = 0 and β₄* ≠ 0 requires the inequality −4(N−1)b_sg < ε_g − ε_s + 8b_g + 20(c_g + c) < 4(N−1)b_sg (assuming a_d = a_sg = a_g = 0).
- The second stability condition for the same minimum is 2ε_d − ε_s − ε_g + 8(b_d − b_g) + 4(3c_d − 5c_g − 2c) > 0 (assuming a_d = a_sg = a_g = 0).

## Context

This paper extends the analysis of octahedral shapes in the sdg-IBM initiated in Part I (Isacker, 2015) by focusing on Hamiltonians with dynamical symmetries, rather than the most general two-body Hamiltonian considered previously. The work builds on the mean-field studies of higher-rank discrete symmetries by Li and Dudek (1994) and the Strasbourg group (Dudek, 2002; 2003; 2006; Rouvel, 2014), who showed the possible occurrence of tetrahedral, octahedral, and icosahedral symmetries through combinations of deformations. The results enable the construction of sdg-IBM Hamiltonians that produce stable octahedral shapes, providing a framework for experimental searches for such symmetries in nuclei, as pursued by Curien (2010; 2011) and Jentschel (2010).
