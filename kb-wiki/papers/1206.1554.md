# Derivation of fluid dynamics from kinetic theory with the 14--moment approximation

> We review the traditional derivation of the fluid-dynamical equations from kinetic theory according to Israel and Stewart. We show that their procedure to close the fluid-dynamical equations of motion is not unique. Their approach contains two approximations, the first being the so-called 14-moment approximation to truncate the single-particle distribution function. The second consists in the choice of equations of motion for the dissipative currents. Israel and Stewart used the second moment of the Boltzmann equation, but this is not the only possible choice. In fact, there are infinitely many moments of the Boltzmann equation which can serve as equations of motion for the dissipative currents. All resulting equations of motion have the same form, but the transport coefficients are different in each case.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the traditional Israel-Stewart derivation of fluid dynamics from kinetic theory using the 14-moment approximation is not unique: any moment of the Boltzmann equation can serve as the equation of motion for the dissipative currents, and while all resulting equations have the same form, the transport coefficients differ in each case.

## Method

The authors expand the single-particle distribution function around local equilibrium using an orthogonal basis of irreducible tensors formed from particle four-momentum, then apply the 14-moment approximation to truncate this expansion. They derive the exact equations of motion for arbitrary scalar, vector, and tensor moments of the Boltzmann equation, and explicitly compute the transport coefficients for a classical gas of massless particles with constant cross section using two different choices of moments: the one used by Israel and Stewart (the second moment) and an alternative choice from Denicol (2010).

## Key numbers

- For a classical gas of massless particles with constant cross section, the shear viscosity coefficient obtained from the Israel-Stewart choice of moment differs from that obtained using the alternative moment choice from Denicol (2010).
- The paper uses natural units where \(\hbar = k_B = c = 1\) throughout.
- The metric tensor is \(g^{\mu \nu} = \text{diag}(+,-,-,-)\).
- The projection operator \(\Delta^{\mu\nu}\) satisfies \(\Delta^{\mu}_{\mu} = 3\).

## Context

This work extends the moment method originally developed by Grad (1949) for non-relativistic systems and later generalized to relativistic systems by Israel and Stewart (1976, 1979). The paper explicitly demonstrates the ambiguity in the 14-moment approximation that was previously noted in Denicol (2010, 2012), and shows that the Israel-Stewart equations do not always agree with numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation as found by Huovinen (2008), Molnar (2009), and El (2009). The resolution of this ambiguity by including higher moments of the single-particle distribution function is addressed in a companion paper by Denicol (2012).
