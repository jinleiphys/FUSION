# Impact of QCD Energy Evolution on Observables in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We study how the inclusion of energy dependence as dictated by quantum chromodynamic (QCD) small-$x$ evolution equations affects key observables in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Specifically, we incorporate JIMWLK evolution into the IP-Glasma framework, which serves as the initial condition for a simulation pipeline that includes viscous relativistic hydrodynamics and a hadronic afterburner. This approach enables a consistent modeling of highly energetic nuclei across varying Bjorken-$x$ values, which are relevant for different collision energies and rapidity regions. In comparison to the standard IP-Glasma setup without small-$x$ evolution, we observe pronounced changes in particle multiplicities and spectral distributions, especially in smaller systems and at the highest available energies. We further explore effects on anisotropic flow observables and correlations between mean transverse momentum and elliptic flow. Our findings underscore the critical role of nonlinear QCD evolution in accurately modeling the early stages of heavy-ion collisions, as well as its implications for extracting transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

Including JIMWLK small‑x evolution into the IP‑Glasma framework changes particle multiplicities, spectral distributions, and anisotropic flow observables compared to the standard IP‑Glasma setup that uses a static saturation‑scale parametrization, with the most pronounced effects seen in smaller collision systems and at the highest available energies; the authors note that the JIMWLK‑evolved geometry produces flatter multiplicity distributions and reduced flow harmonics, and that the differences are largest for peripheral collisions.

## Method

The authors start from the IP‑Glasma model, which uses Wilson lines obtained from the McLerran‑Venugopalan model at x₀ = 0.01, and then evolve those Wilson lines to the appropriate Bjorken‑x values by numerically solving the JIMWLK evolution equation with a running‑coupling prescription and an infrared regulator. The resulting initial energy‑momentum tensor is fed into a 2+1D viscous relativistic hydrodynamic simulation (MUSIC) with shear and bulk viscosities tuned to RHIC Au+Au data, followed by a hadronic afterburner (UrQMD). They compare this “+JIMWLK” setup to a baseline “Qₛ(x)” setup in which the energy dependence is included only through the x‑dependent saturation scale from the IP‑Sat model, with all other parameters fixed.

## Key numbers

- The normalization factor K for the energy‑momentum tensor is fixed to 0.115 for the JIMWLK setup, 0.106 for the Qₛ(x) setup at RHIC, and 0.095 for the Qₛ(x) setup at LHC, by matching to the 0–5% most central Au+Au multiplicity at √sNN = 200 GeV.
- The hydrodynamic evolution starts at τ₀ = 0.4 fm/c.
- The shear viscosity parameters used are η₀ = 0.14, T₀ = 0.18 GeV, b = –4 GeV⁻¹; the bulk viscosity parameters are (ζ/s)max = 0.123, λζ = –0.12, wζ = 0.05 GeV.
- The infrared regulator m in the JIMWLK kernel is 0.4 GeV; the coordinate‑space ΛQCD is 0.04 GeV.
- The mean transverse momentum ⟨p_T⟩ used to determine the x value is 0.5 GeV at RHIC and 0.65 GeV at LHC.
- The JIMWLK evolution is run from x₀ = 0.01 down to x = ⟨p_T⟩/√sNN (midrapidity, y=0), which corresponds to x ≈ 0.0025 at RHIC 200 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the IP‑Glasma framework (Schenke et al., 2012) by incorporating JIMWLK evolution, which had previously been used to study rapidity dependence of initial geometry (Schenke et al., 2016; Schenke et al., 2022; McDonald et al., 2023; Mäntysaari et al., 2024) but not for midrapidity observables across multiple collision systems. The JIMWLK‑evolved initial conditions produce smoother nuclear profiles at small x, consistent with earlier studies of geometry evolution (Schlichting et al., 2014; Mäntysaari et al., 2022). The results enable more precise extraction of QGP transport properties by reducing the model uncertainty from the energy‑dependence of the initial state, and they provide predictions for O+O and Ne+Ne collisions that can be tested against upcoming LHC and RHIC data.
