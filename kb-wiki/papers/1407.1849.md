# Gravitational collisions and the quark-gluon plasma

> This thesis addresses the thermalisation of heavy-ion collisions within the context of the AdS/CFT duality. The first part clarifies the numerical set-up and studies the relaxation of far-from-equilibrium modes in homogeneous systems. Less trivially we then study colliding shock waves and uncover a transparent regime where the strongly coupled shocks initially pass right through each other. Furthermore, in this regime the later plasma relaxation is insensitive to the longitudinal profile of the shock, implying in particular a universal rapidity shape at strong coupling and high collision energies. Lastly, we study radial expansion in a boost-invariant set-up, allowing us to find good agreement with head-on collisions performed at the LHC accelerator. As a secondary goal of this thesis, a special effort is made to clearly expose numerical computations by providing commented Mathematica notebooks for most calculations presented. Furthermore, we provide interpolating functions of the geometries computed, which can be of use in other projects.

## Key claim

This thesis uses the AdS/CFT duality to model the thermalization of strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collisions, finding that shock waves can initially pass through each other in a "transparent" regime, and that the subsequent plasma relaxation is insensitive to the longitudinal profile of the shock, implying a universal rapidity shape at strong coupling and high collision energies.

## Method

The author solves Einstein's equations numerically in a five-dimensional anti-de-Sitter (AdS) spacetime using the characteristic formulation with Eddington-Finkelstein coordinates, where the metric is written as a nested set of linear ordinary differential equations. Three complementary approaches are employed: homogeneous systems with time-dependent anisotropy to study relaxation of far-from-equilibrium modes; colliding planar shock waves with various longitudinal profiles to study transparency and rapidity distributions; and boost-invariant setups with radial expansion to model transverse dynamics and compare with LHC data.

## Key numbers

- LHC collisions create about 26,000 particles per event, with a typical collision lasting 10 fm/c within a sphere of radius 15 fm
- At RHIC, each proton and neutron has an energy of 100 GeV, giving a Lorentz contraction factor of one hundred; at LHC, the energy is 1.38 TeV, giving a Lorentz factor of more than one thousand
- The shear viscosity from AdS/CFT is η = s/4π, where s is the entropy density
- The energy density E is constant in the homogeneous setting and related to the normalizable mode a₄ by E = -3a₄/4
- The pressure anisotropy ΔP(t) = P_L(t) - P_T(t) equals 3b₄(t), where b₄(t) is the normalizable mode from the near-boundary expansion

## Context

This work extends earlier holographic studies of black hole formation in AdS as a model for thermalization (Horowitz 1999, Chesler 2008, Chesler 2010, Heller 2011), pushing these to more realistic settings with radial expansion and comparison to experimental data. The thesis uses the characteristic formulation pioneered by Chesler and Yaffe (2008, 2013) and builds on the fluid/gravity correspondence (Bhattacharyya 2008, Hubeny 2011). The results enable fully dynamical simulations from far-from-equilibrium through viscous hydrodynamics to hadronic cascades and final particle spectra, with the model fitting LHC data surprisingly well despite being more constrained than previous attempts.
